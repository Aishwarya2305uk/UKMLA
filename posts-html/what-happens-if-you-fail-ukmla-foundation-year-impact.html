If you are worried about what happens if you fail UKMLA, the honest answer is that a fail is not a career-ending event, but it is a timing event — and for a UK final-year medical student, timing is precisely what your Foundation Programme place depends on. This guide is written specifically for UK medical students in their final year who are less concerned with the mechanics of resitting (we cover those separately) and much more concerned with a narrower, more urgent question: if I fail the AKT or the CPSA, what actually happens to my Foundation Year 1 (FY1) start date, my place in the August cohort, and my Preference-Informed Allocation (PIA) outcome?

This article deliberately does not re-explain how many attempts you get, how the appeals process works, or what mitigating circumstances evidence looks like — that ground is already covered thoroughly in our UKMLA resits rules and limits guide, and you should read that first if you need the mechanics. What follows here is the downstream career-timeline consequence: what a fail does to your graduation, your Foundation Programme application, and your realistic options for getting to FY1 on time, delayed, or via an alternative route.

It is worth saying plainly, before anything else: thousands of UK final-year students sit the UKMLA every year, and a meaningful proportion of them fail at least one component on their first attempt. You are not alone in facing this, and the system you are entering — medical school progression boards, the GMC, and the UKFPO — has handled this exact situation many times before. The purpose of this guide is to replace vague anxiety about "what happens if you fail UKMLA" with a concrete, realistic map of the actual consequences, so you can act on facts rather than fear.

Quick Answer: What Happens If You Fail the UKMLA

In short, failing the UKMLA does not stop you resitting, but it does typically stop you graduating and starting FY1 on the standard August timeline until you pass, because UK medical schools build UKMLA success into their final-year progression and graduation requirements as approved by the General Medical Council (GMC). Your Foundation Programme allocation itself — which school and post you have been matched to through PIA — is not directly affected by your UKMLA result, since PIA is a preference-and-lottery process rather than a score-ranked one. What changes is whether you can actually take up that allocated post, because taking it up depends on having graduated and secured provisional GMC registration first.

Practically, this means the most common consequence of a UKMLA fail close to the summer sitting is a delayed graduation and a delayed or lost FY1 start for that year's August cohort, rather than an automatic "deferred slot" being held open for you. Deferred entry to Foundation training is generally only granted for defined statutory reasons — such as a serious health condition, or maternity, paternity, or adoption leave — not routinely for candidates who are still resitting an exam. If you are in this position, your most productive next step is not more revision in isolation; it is contacting your medical school's exams office and the UK Foundation Programme Office (UKFPO) immediately, in parallel, so you understand exactly which of the options described below applies to you.

Because individual medical schools set some of their own progression and resit policies alongside the GMC's overarching requirements, and because UKFPO/deanery practice can also change from year to year, the specifics in this guide should be treated as a general map of the landscape rather than a guarantee of what will happen in your individual case. Always verify your own situation directly with your medical school and the UKFPO.

Why a UKMLA Fail Affects Your Foundation Year

To really understand what happens if you fail UKMLA as a final-year student, you need to understand why the exam and the Foundation Programme are structurally linked in the first place, rather than being two separate hurdles that happen to sit close together in your calendar.

Passing the UKMLA Is a Graduation Requirement, Not Just an Exam

The single fact that explains almost everything in this article is that the UKMLA is not an optional add-on assessment sitting alongside your medical degree — it is woven into your medical school's final-year progression requirements. UK medical schools have built passing both components of the UKMLA (the Applied Knowledge Test, or AKT, and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment, or CPSA) into what a student must achieve to be certified as having met the requirements for their primary medical qualification. This structure exists because the GMC requires that every UK medical graduate demonstrates, through the UKMLA, that they meet the same minimum common threshold of safe practice, regardless of which of the UK's medical schools they attended.

The practical consequence is that a UKMLA fail is not simply "a bad exam result" in isolation — it is a gap in your graduation requirements. Until that gap is closed by a pass, your medical school cannot confirm you have completed your degree, and without a confirmed degree completion and a UKMLA pass, you cannot apply for the provisional GMC registration that is legally required before you can start working as an FY1 doctor. This is why the UKMLA sits at the very centre of the timeline between finishing medical school and starting your medical career, and why a fail anywhere in that chain has a ripple effect on everything downstream.

It also helps to be clear about who this specific consequence applies to. This particular chain — UKMLA result, graduation requirement, Foundation Programme start — is specific to UK medical students sitting the UKMLA as part of an undergraduate or graduate-entry medical degree. International medical graduates (IMGs) sitting the UKMLA as part of the route to GMC registration face a different set of consequences from a fail, since they are not tied to a single medical school's progression board or a Foundation Programme start date in the same way — that audience and its specific implications are outside the scope of this guide.

How the AKT and CPSA Interact With Your Final-Year Timetable

Most UK medical schools schedule their finals so that the UKMLA components align with the standard sitting windows set out in the national UKMLA key dates and sitting schedule, with the AKT typically sat in spring of the final year and the CPSA following shortly after. This scheduling is deliberate: it is designed to leave enough time between results and the August FY1 start date for the ordinary registration and onboarding process to happen smoothly if you pass first time. The problem is that this buffer was built around a first-attempt pass. A fail compresses or eliminates that buffer, because a resit sitting has to happen after the standard year's main sitting window, and results then have to be processed, and only after a pass can the registration paperwork begin.

The UKMLA reports results by component, so if you pass the AKT but fail the CPSA (or vice versa), you only need to resit the failed component — this is a genuine mitigation built into the system and worth remembering if you are in this position, since it usually means a materially smaller gap to close than failing both. Even so, any resit adds weeks to months onto a timeline that was already tight, and the tighter your medical school's own summer resit window is relative to the August start date, the more likely a fail is to actually move your start date rather than just cause a stressful few weeks.

Your UKMLA results and scoring breakdown will show exactly which component you failed and, in most cases, some indication of where marks were lost. Treat this as your starting point for resit planning as much as your starting point for timeline planning — the two matter equally, since a rushed resit that fails again causes a far bigger delay than a well-prepared resit that takes a few extra weeks to arrange.

PIA Allocation Versus Actually Taking Up Your Post

One of the most common misunderstandings about what happens if you fail UKMLA is the assumption that a fail immediately jeopardises your Foundation School allocation itself. It is worth being precise about a distinction that causes a lot of unnecessary worry: your Foundation Programme allocation and your ability to take up that allocation are two separate things. Since the introduction of Preference-Informed Allocation (PIA), Foundation School and post allocation is determined by a combination of your ranked preferences and a lottery mechanism — it is not ranked by academic performance, and your UKMLA result does not feed into the PIA ranking calculation itself. This means a UKMLA fail, on its own, does not retrospectively change which Foundation School or post you were matched to.

What it does affect is whether you are actually eligible to walk into that post on day one. Taking up an allocated FY1 post requires, among other things, that you have graduated with your primary medical qualification, that you hold provisional GMC registration, and that you have cleared the employing NHS Trust's pre-employment checks. A UKMLA fail that is still unresolved by the time these steps need to happen creates a genuine conflict: you have an allocation, but you cannot yet fulfil the eligibility conditions attached to it. What Actually Happens to Your Foundation Programme Application

This section sets out, scenario by scenario, exactly what happens if you fail UKMLA at different points relative to the August start date, since the practical outcome depends heavily on timing rather than being a single fixed rule.

If You Resit and Pass Before the August Cohort Deadline

The best-case scenario, and the most common one for candidates who fail a single component, is resitting within the summer window and passing in time to complete GMC registration before the August start date. In this scenario, your allocated post typically remains yours, provided your medical school can confirm your graduation and the GMC can process your provisional registration application quickly enough once your pass is confirmed. This is exactly why speed matters so much here: every week saved on the administrative side is a week of buffer against the fixed August start date that Foundation Schools generally cannot move for an individual doctor.

If you are in this position, your priority list should be, in order: sit your resit as early as your medical school's resit schedule allows; ask your exams office for the fastest possible route to formal confirmation of graduation once you pass; and submit your provisional GMC registration application the same day that confirmation is available, with every supporting document (degree confirmation, identity, right to work, fitness to practise declaration) already prepared in advance so nothing else causes additional delay.

If Your Resit Falls After the August Start Date

A harder scenario is where your medical school's resit sitting, or the subsequent processing and registration steps, simply cannot be completed before the August cohort begins. In this situation, the most common outcome is that you are unable to take up your allocated FY1 post in that year's August intake. This is not usually framed as your post being "held open" indefinitely — Foundation Schools work to fixed staffing and induction schedules built around the national start date, and cannot generally accommodate an open-ended wait for an individual candidate's registration status to resolve.

What tends to happen instead is a conversation between you, your medical school, and the UKFPO about your options for the following recruitment cycle or an alternative entry route once you have passed. This might mean joining a later start point if your Foundation School and deanery can accommodate one, applying afresh into the following year's main recruitment round, or exploring the F2 Stand-alone programme described below if your specific circumstances and timing fit its eligibility rules. Which of these applies to you is genuinely case-by-case, depends on your specific medical school's policy and your Foundation School's capacity, and is a conversation you should have directly with both bodies as soon as you know a resit will run late — not something to try to resolve alone from general guidance like this article.

Deferred Entry: What It Actually Covers

Many worried students assume that what happens if you fail UKMLA close to the start date is an automatic offer of "deferred entry" as a routine safety net available to anyone whose UKMLA result is delayed. In practice, deferred entry to the Foundation Programme is generally reserved for defined statutory circumstances — significant, well-evidenced health conditions, or maternity, paternity, and adoption leave being the clearest examples — rather than being granted automatically because a candidate is still resitting an exam. This distinction matters enormously for how you should plan: do not assume a deferral will simply be offered to you if your UKMLA outcome is unresolved close to August. If your situation also involves a genuine health or caring-responsibility dimension alongside the exam timing, raise both strands with your medical school and the UKFPO together, since the statutory grounds and the exam-related timing may need to be considered separately even if they are happening at the same time in your life.

Because deferral policy sits partly with individual medical schools and partly with the UKFPO and deanery, and because the criteria can be reviewed and updated between recruitment cycles, always check the current position directly rather than relying on what a previous year's cohort experienced. This is exactly the kind of detail where "it varies and can change" is the honest answer, not a way of avoiding the question.

The Academic Foundation Programme and a UKMLA Fail

The question of what happens if you fail UKMLA takes on an extra dimension for AFP applicants specifically, given how limited and competitive these posts are. If you applied for or were offered an Academic Foundation Programme (AFP) post — the research, education, or leadership-track variant of the standard Foundation Programme — a UKMLA fail creates the same underlying eligibility problem as it does for a standard post, because AFP posts still require graduation and provisional GMC registration before you can start. AFP posts are also more competitively allocated in the first place, which can make the practical consequences of a delayed start feel higher-stakes, since AFP places are limited and highly sought after. Our sibling guide on the UKMLA and the Academic Foundation Programme explains how AFP allocation and academic tracks work in full; if you are an AFP applicant facing a resit, read that alongside this article and contact your AFP unit directly, since AFP-specific timing and capacity questions are best answered by the academic unit itself rather than assumed from general Foundation Programme guidance.

What To Do First If You Have Failed the UKMLA

Knowing what happens if you fail UKMLA in theory is only half the picture — the other half is knowing exactly what to do in the days immediately after your result, since the actions you take in that window have the biggest effect on the outcome.

Step One: Contact Your Medical School's Exams Office Immediately

Your medical school's exams office is your first and most important point of contact, because they hold the specific information that matters most to your individual situation: your school's own resit sitting dates, how a resit interacts with your school's confirmation-of-graduation process, and what pastoral or academic support is available to you as a resitting student. Do not wait for a scheduled meeting or a routine catch-up — email or call as soon as you have your result, explain that you want to understand the fastest realistic path to a resit and graduation, and ask specifically about the interaction between your resit timing and the FY1 start date.

Many medical schools have handled this exact situation before and have an established process for supporting resitting final-year students through to graduation and registration as quickly as possible. Being proactive, rather than waiting to be contacted, is consistently the difference between students who resolve this smoothly and those who lose additional time to avoidable delay.

Step Two: Contact the UKFPO or Your Foundation School Directly

In parallel, not afterwards, contact the UKFPO or your allocated Foundation School to explain your situation. They can tell you what their process is for candidates whose registration is delayed by a resit, what the realistic range of outcomes looks like for your specific Foundation School, and whether any alternative start point or route exists for your circumstances. Because this process sits partly outside your medical school's control, getting both conversations moving at the same time — rather than sequentially — is the single most effective thing you can do to protect your options.

Step Three: Understand Your Specific Resit Window

Ask your exams office for the exact date of your resit sitting, the expected date of results, and the realistic timeline for your school to confirm graduation once you pass. Compare this timeline honestly against the FY1 start date and the practical lead time the GMC and your prospective employing Trust need for registration and pre-employment checks. This is the single calculation that determines which of the scenarios above applies to you, and it is far more useful to have this mapped out explicitly and in writing from your school than to estimate it yourself from general information.

Step Four: Prepare Your Registration Documentation in Advance

Whatever the outcome of your resit, having your provisional GMC registration documentation ready in advance — degree confirmation paperwork, identity documents, right to work evidence, and your fitness to practise declaration — means that the moment your pass is confirmed, you lose no additional time to administrative preparation. This is a genuinely controllable factor in an otherwise uncertain situation, and it is worth treating as seriously as your academic resit preparation itself.

Step Five: Address the Exam Itself, Not Just the Logistics

None of the timeline management above substitutes for giving your resit the preparation it needs. A second fail compounds every one of the timing problems described in this article, so use your result feedback report to understand exactly where you lost marks, and build a focused resit plan around your specific weak areas rather than generically repeating your first attempt's revision. How the 2026 Foundation Programme Recruitment Changes Affect This

It is reasonable to ask whether recent recruitment reforms change what happens if you fail UKMLA compared with previous years. Foundation Programme recruitment has seen genuine policy changes in recent cycles, including a shift towards prioritising UK medical graduates and doctors with significant NHS experience at the offer stage. These changes matter for context, but they do not remove the underlying graduation-and-registration dependency described throughout this article — being a prioritised UK graduate under the current rules still requires you to have actually graduated and secured provisional registration before you can take up a post. Our sibling guide on the 2026 Foundation Programme recruitment changes explains these prioritisation rules and the current PIA process in full detail; read it alongside this article if you want to understand how the broader recruitment landscape interacts with an individual UKMLA resit situation.

Because recruitment policy is reviewed and adjusted between cycles, and because the interaction between school-level progression policy and national PIA rules can shift as a result, treat any given year's rules as the current position rather than a permanent fixture, and check directly with the UKFPO for the cycle relevant to your own graduation year.

Alternative Routes If Your Timeline Slips

For the smaller number of students whose timeline slips by months rather than weeks, what happens if you fail UKMLA and cannot resolve it before August comes down to which of the following alternative routes is realistically available to you.

The Following Year's Main Recruitment Round

If your resit and registration cannot realistically be completed before the current year's August start, reapplying through the following year's main UKFPO recruitment round is one established route back onto the standard Foundation Programme pathway. This means going through PIA again for the next cycle, but this time with your UKMLA fully passed and your registration already in place well before applications open, removing the timing risk that caused the original disruption.

F2 Stand-alone Programme

The F2 Stand-alone programme is a genuine, established route back into Foundation training for doctors who deferred, took time out, or otherwise did not progress directly from FY1 to FY2 on the standard timeline. It allows an eligible doctor to complete an FY2-equivalent year in clinical and non-clinical settings and receive a Foundation Programme certificate of completion on finishing it. Eligibility rules and the number of places available vary by year and by deanery, so this is very much a "check current eligibility directly" situation rather than a guaranteed fallback — but it is worth knowing this route exists if your timeline has slipped by a full year rather than a few weeks.

Locum, Trust Grade, or Clinical Support Roles While You Wait

Some final-year graduates whose FY1 start has been delayed take a temporary clinical support role, locum post, or similar NHS-adjacent position while they wait for the next recruitment cycle, particularly once they have passed the UKMLA and secured full or provisional registration but missed the current year's start window. This is a personal decision that depends on your financial situation, your registration status, and what roles are realistically open to you, and it is worth discussing with your medical school's careers service and the UKFPO before committing to a specific option.

The Financial and Practical Knock-On Effects

Beyond the Foundation Programme itself, what happens if you fail UKMLA also has quieter, easily-missed practical consequences worth planning for early.

Student Finance and Loan Repayment Timing

A delayed graduation and a delayed FY1 start have knock-on effects beyond the Foundation Programme itself. Student loan repayment terms, NHS bursary arrangements, and university accommodation or tenancy commitments are all typically built around an expected graduation date and an expected first NHS salary arriving in August. If your graduation slips because of a UKMLA resit, it is worth contacting your university's student finance office and your loan provider proactively to understand whether repayment start dates, continuing bursary eligibility, or any final-year funding you are still drawing on are affected. This is a genuinely practical, easily overlooked consequence of a delayed pass that is worth sorting out early rather than discovering it as a surprise months later.

Accommodation, Employment Contracts, and Moving Costs

Many final-year students sign accommodation contracts, give notice on student housing, or make moving arrangements around the assumption of an August FY1 start in a specific city or region. If your start is delayed following a UKMLA fail, revisit these commitments as soon as you have a realistic sense of your new timeline, since breaking or extending a tenancy, or reorganising a house move, is far easier to manage with weeks of notice than with days. If you had already accepted a specific Trust's post and made plans around it, your Foundation School can advise on whether that specific post remains available to you once your registration is resolved, or whether you will need to be matched to a different post or a different intake.

International and Visa-Sponsored Students

If you are studying in the UK on a student visa and intend to switch to a Health and Care Worker visa to begin FY1, a delayed UKMLA pass has an additional layer of urgency, because visa sponsorship timelines are tied to your confirmed employment start date and your student visa's expiry. A delayed graduation can compress the window in which your student visa remains valid before your new sponsorship needs to begin, so international students facing a UKMLA resit should contact both their university's international student support office and their prospective employing Trust's sponsorship team immediately, in addition to the medical school and UKFPO contacts described above. Visa timing is not something to leave until your resit result is confirmed — start the conversation as soon as you know a resit is likely, so any necessary visa extension or variation can be arranged with as much lead time as possible.

Why the Timeline Is So Tight in the First Place

Part of understanding what happens if you fail UKMLA is understanding why the consequence is a compressed timeline rather than simply "extra time to try again," because this context makes the urgency of acting quickly much clearer. Foundation Schools plan induction, rota-building, and clinical supervision cover around a single, fixed national start date each August, coordinated across every Trust in every deanery in the country. This is not a bureaucratic preference — it reflects the reality that FY1 doctors are absorbed into live clinical rotas, and a Trust cannot simply hold a rota slot open indefinitely for one individual doctor without affecting supervision ratios and staffing for the whole department.

At the same time, the GMC's provisional registration process, while efficient, still requires document verification, fitness to practise checks, and confirmation of your medical qualification — none of which can be meaningfully compressed below a certain minimum processing time, however urgently you need it done. This is why the sequence of events (resit sitting, results, graduation confirmation, GMC registration, Trust pre-employment checks) has so little slack in it even in the best-case scenario, and why a fail anywhere in that chain tends to have an outsized effect on the final outcome. Understanding this structural tightness is exactly why the advice throughout this guide is to act in parallel across every contact point — your medical school, the GMC, and the UKFPO or Foundation School — rather than working through each step sequentially and hoping there is enough time left over.

Frequently Asked Questions

The questions below address the specific, practical concerns UK final-year students most often raise about what happens if you fail UKMLA, each answered as a self-contained response.

Does failing the UKMLA affect my Foundation Programme allocation ranking?

No, not directly. Foundation Programme allocation under Preference-Informed Allocation (PIA) is based on your ranked preferences combined with a lottery mechanism, not on your UKMLA score or result. A UKMLA fail does not retrospectively change which Foundation School or post you were matched to. What it can affect is whether you are eligible to actually take up that allocated post on the standard start date, because taking up a post requires graduation and provisional GMC registration, both of which depend on a UKMLA pass.

Will I definitely lose my Foundation Programme place if I fail and need to resit?

Not necessarily. If your resit sitting, results, and registration can all be completed before the August start date, many candidates keep their allocated post and start on time, particularly if only one UKMLA component needs resitting. The place becomes genuinely at risk when the resit and registration timeline cannot be completed before the fixed start date, in which case the realistic outcome is usually a delayed start through an alternative route rather than an automatically held-open place. Always confirm your own specific timeline with your medical school and the UKFPO rather than assuming either outcome.

Can I get a deferred start date just because I am resitting the UKMLA?

Deferred entry to the Foundation Programme is generally reserved for defined statutory circumstances, such as a significant health condition or maternity, paternity, or adoption leave, rather than being granted routinely for candidates who are still resitting an exam. If your situation involves genuine statutory grounds alongside your UKMLA timing, raise both with your medical school and the UKFPO, but do not assume a deferral will be offered purely because your result is delayed.

What is the fastest thing I can do after failing to protect my FY1 start date?

Contact your medical school's exams office and the UKFPO or your Foundation School at the same time, not one after the other, and ask both for the exact dates and requirements relevant to your situation. Then prepare every piece of your provisional GMC registration documentation in advance so there is zero additional delay once your resit pass is confirmed. These two actions, done immediately and in parallel, are the single biggest factors in whether a fail becomes a minor delay or a lost year.

If I fail one component but pass the other, do I have to resit both?

No. The UKMLA reports pass/fail separately for the AKT and the CPSA, so if you pass one component and fail the other, you only need to resit the component you failed. This significantly reduces both the academic burden and the timeline risk compared with failing both components, since there is usually less ground to make up before your medical school can confirm graduation.

Does a UKMLA fail affect my eligibility for the Academic Foundation Programme?

The same underlying dependency applies: AFP posts, like standard Foundation posts, require graduation and provisional GMC registration before you can start, so an unresolved UKMLA fail creates the same timing risk for an AFP offer as it does for a standard post. Because AFP places are more competitively allocated and more limited in number, contact your AFP academic unit directly and as early as possible if your UKMLA result is delayed, since AFP-specific capacity and timing questions are best answered by that unit rather than inferred from standard Foundation Programme guidance.

Where can I find official information on Foundation Programme eligibility and start dates?

The UK Foundation Programme Office publishes official recruitment, eligibility, and programme guidance directly at foundationprogramme.nhs.uk, and the GMC publishes the definitive rules on the MLA assessments themselves, including how the AKT and CPSA fit into UK medical graduates' registration requirements. Always check both sources directly for the current cycle's specific rules, since dates, eligibility criteria, and deferral policy can be reviewed and updated between recruitment years.

Will a UKMLA fail show up on my Foundation Programme application or affect future job prospects?

Your Foundation Programme application itself is not scored or ranked using your UKMLA result, so a fail does not lower your standing in the PIA process in the way an exam-ranked system might. Once you have passed and are working as a foundation doctor, your UKMLA result is not something employers or training programmes routinely see or ask about — what matters from that point onward is your performance in post, documented through your ARCP outcomes and workplace-based assessments. A resolved UKMLA fail, on its own, is not something that follows you through your career once you have passed and progressed normally.

Should I tell my Foundation School or Trust that I failed and am resitting?

Yes — proactive, early disclosure to your Foundation School and, once relevant, your employing Trust is strongly advisable rather than something to avoid or delay. Foundation Schools and Trusts have handled resitting candidates before and can only help you navigate registration timing, alternative start points, or F2 Stand-alone eligibility if they know your situation in good time. Trying to manage the situation quietly and hoping it resolves itself before anyone needs to know tends to remove options rather than protect them, since many of the alternative routes described in this guide depend on early conversations, not late ones. It is also worth remembering that the UKMLA you are resitting is built to the same shared MLA standard that IMGs meet via PLAB — our explainer on whether UKMLA is replacing PLAB covers how the two routes fit together, useful context if you are ever asked to explain your resit to a non-UK audience or a future international post.

Conclusion

Understanding what happens if you fail UKMLA as a UK final-year student comes down to one core relationship: your Foundation Programme allocation itself is not directly changed by your result, but your ability to take up that allocation depends entirely on resolving the fail through graduation and provisional GMC registration before your Foundation School's fixed start date. Acting immediately — contacting your medical school's exams office and the UKFPO in parallel, understanding your exact resit window, and preparing your registration paperwork in advance — is what most reliably turns a stressful result into a manageable delay rather than a lost year.
