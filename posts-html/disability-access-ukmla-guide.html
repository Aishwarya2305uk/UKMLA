Understanding your rights around UKMLA disability access adjustments is essential whether you are a UK medical student or an international medical graduate preparing for any component of the UK Medical Licensing Assessment. The process is less complicated than many candidates fear, but it requires proactive planning, the right documentation, and an understanding of who to contact and when. This guide covers the legal framework, every type of available adjustment, the evidence required for different conditions, step-by-step application processes for both UK students and IMGs, and what happens if your application is declined.

The Legal Framework for UKMLA Disability Access Adjustments

The foundation for all examination adjustments in the UK is the Equality Act 2010. This legislation defines disability broadly: a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on normal day-to-day activities. Crucially, this definition encompasses not just visible physical disabilities but also neurodevelopmental conditions (dyslexia, ADHD, autism spectrum conditions), chronic pain, mental health conditions, chronic illness, hearing and visual impairment, and many other health conditions.

What the Equality Act Requires

Under the Equality Act, examining bodies — including the GMC and medical schools — are required to make reasonable adjustments to their services, including examinations, to give disabled people the same access as non-disabled people. Critically, the Act places the duty to make adjustments on the institution, not the individual. You should not have to fight for adjustments you are legally entitled to receive.

The Act does not define what is "reasonable" — this is determined on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the nature and severity of the candidate's difficulty, the cost and practicability of the adjustment, and whether the adjustment would undermine the purpose of the assessment. In practice, adjustments that have negligible cost and are commonly granted in other settings are almost always considered reasonable.

GMC Obligations

The GMC has a legal obligation under the Equality Act to make reasonable adjustments for candidates sitting PLAB 1 (AKT) and PLAB 2 (CPSA). Their published approach emphasises exploring whether an adjustment would remove the barriers created by the candidate's condition, and they assess each application individually based on the evidence provided. The GMC's approach to reasonable adjustments is publicly documented at gmc-uk.org and is regularly updated to reflect current good practice.

The GMC is also bound by the Public Sector Equality Duty, which requires public bodies to actively advance equality — not merely to respond reactively to discrimination claims. This means the GMC should be actively supportive of adjustment applications that are well-evidenced.

How to Request Adjustments: Step-by-Step for UK Medical Students

For students sitting the UKMLA through their medical school (i.e., the medical school-delivered AKT), the adjustment application is typically made through your university's disability services rather than directly through the GMC or exam provider.

Step 1: Register With Your University Disability Service

As early as possible in your medical training — ideally at the start of Year 1 — register with your university's disability or accessibility support service. This is the gateway to all exam adjustments and academic support. Do not wait until finals year.

Step 2: Obtain and Submit Evidence

You will need documentation confirming your condition and its impact on academic performance. The type of evidence required varies by condition (see the evidence table below). Submit this to disability services, who will then confirm your adjustment entitlement.

Step 3: Confirm Adjustments Each Exam Period

Most universities require you to confirm your adjustment requirements each academic year, and to notify disability services before each exam period. Do not assume adjustments automatically transfer from one year to the next without confirmation.

Step 4: Liaise With the Exam Unit

For high-stakes university examinations, your disability services team communicates your adjustments to the exam unit. You may need to sign a consent form allowing this disclosure.

How to Request Adjustments: Step-by-Step for IMG Candidates (GMC Portal)

For IMG candidates sitting PLAB 1 (AKT) or PLAB 2 (CPSA) through the GMC, the adjustment application process is managed directly by the GMC:

Step 1: Identify Your Needs Early

Identify your adjustment needs before booking your exam — ideally three to six months in advance. The GMC recommends applying as early as possible because processing times and evidence gathering can take weeks to months.

Step 2: Contact the GMC Adjustments Team

Email or contact the GMC's customer experience team, specifying that you are applying for reasonable adjustments for PLAB 1 or PLAB 2. The GMC's guidance documents on their website detail the current contact route and form requirements.

Step 3: Submit Supporting Evidence

Provide the evidence specified for your condition type (see table below). The GMC requires an original letter, certificate, or medical report from an appropriate professional confirming the nature of your health needs and the functional impact of your condition in an exam environment. Do not submit a diagnosis letter alone — functional impact on exam performance is essential.

Step 4: GMC Decision

The GMC reviews applications on a case-by-case basis and notifies you of the outcome. If adjustments are approved, they will be applied to your exam sitting. If your application is not approved, you have the right to appeal (see below).

How to Obtain an Educational Psychologist Report

An Educational Psychologist (EP) report is the standard evidence required for dyslexia, specific learning differences (SpLD), and often ADHD. Here is how to access one:

Through Your University

Most UK universities have access to EP assessments through their disability services, either with in-house psychologists or via referral to an approved external EP. Waiting times at universities can be two to six months, particularly at the start of the academic year. Apply early.

Privately

Private EP assessments typically cost £450 to £700. Ensure the assessor is a qualified educational psychologist registered with the Health and Care Professions Council (HCPC). The report must include standardised psychometric assessments (e.g., WAIS-IV, WIAT-III) and must specify recommended examination adjustments. A letter from a tutor saying you find reading difficult is not a substitute.

NHS Access

Direct referral for educational psychology assessment through the NHS is limited for adults. However, if you have an ADHD assessment through NHS psychiatry services, the assessment report will typically be sufficient evidence for ADHD-related adjustments without a separate EP report.

Timeline: How Far in Advance Should You Apply?

The GMC and most medical schools strongly recommend applying for adjustments at least three to six months before your exam date. This timeline accounts for:

  • Time to gather and obtain supporting evidence (EP report: 1 to 3 months; specialist letter: 4 to 12 weeks)
  • Administrative processing by the GMC or university disability service (2 to 6 weeks)
  • Any requests for additional evidence or clarification (adds 2 to 4 weeks)
  • Time to appeal if the initial application is not approved (4 to 8 weeks)

Applying late — for example, within four weeks of the exam — significantly risks adjustments not being in place for your sitting. The GMC and exam providers cannot guarantee adjustment implementation on short notice. If you have a new diagnosis or a deterioration in an existing condition close to your exam date, contact the relevant body immediately and explain the circumstances.

What Happens if Your Adjustment Application Is Rejected?

If the GMC or your medical school's exam unit does not approve your adjustment request, you have the right to challenge this decision. The process typically involves:

Step 1: Request Reasons in Writing

Ask for the specific reasons why your application was not approved. Was the evidence insufficient? Did the institution not consider the adjustment reasonable? Understanding the specific ground for rejection tells you what to address in your appeal.

Step 2: Appeal Internally

The GMC and medical schools have internal appeals processes for adjustment decisions. Submit additional evidence if the original evidence was deemed insufficient. Consider obtaining a supplementary letter from your specialist that specifically addresses the functional impact of your condition in a timed examination setting — this is the most common deficiency in initial applications.

Step 3: Seek External Support

If your internal appeal is unsuccessful, seek advice from the British Medical Association (BMA) student support team, your student union's academic advice service, or a disability rights organisation such as Disability Rights UK. The Equality and Human Rights Commission can advise on whether the institution's decision is compliant with the Equality Act.

In serious cases where the institution's decision appears to constitute disability discrimination, legal advice from a solicitor specialising in education or disability law may be appropriate. However, this step is rarely required as most well-evidenced applications are approved on appeal.

Adjusting Your Revision Strategy for Your Specific Disability

Adjustments in the exam are only part of the picture — your revision approach should also be tailored to how your brain works most effectively.

Dyslexia and Reading Difficulties

Use text-to-speech software (Natural Reader, Speechify, or the built-in accessibility features of iOS/Android) to listen to study materials rather than reading everything visually. Create visual mind maps and diagrams rather than linear notes. For SBA practice, use question banks that allow you to adjust font size and spacing. Colour-code your notes by system or theme to aid retrieval.

ADHD

Break revision into short, focused blocks (25 minutes maximum) with mandatory physical breaks. Use the Pomodoro technique as described in our time management guide. Remove digital distractions during study blocks (website blockers, phone in another room). Use external accountability — study groups, study partner check-ins, or a tutor — to maintain focus and consistency.

Autism Spectrum Conditions

Build a highly structured, predictable revision timetable to reduce cognitive load from scheduling decisions. Practise exams in conditions that match your likely exam environment as closely as possible. If you have been granted a separate exam room, visit the test centre in advance if possible to reduce novelty stress on exam day.

Visual Impairment

Configure your screen for maximum accessibility: high contrast settings, large font size, and screen magnification. If you use a screen reader, practise using it with question bank software specifically, as the accessibility of different platforms varies.

Chronic Pain and Fatigue Conditions

Build rest periods into your revision schedule as a fixed feature, not a luxury. Do not attempt marathon revision sessions — cognitive performance under chronic pain is maximised with shorter, more frequent sessions. Plan your revision schedule around your best-functioning time of day.

Disclosure: What to Tell Your Medical School and the GMC

Disclosure of disability or health conditions is a nuanced area that causes significant anxiety for many medical students and IMGs. The key principles are:

For Examination Adjustments

You must disclose the relevant information to obtain adjustments — there is no other route. However, you control what you disclose and to whom. Your disability services team at university operates under strict confidentiality obligations. Information you share to obtain adjustments is not automatically shared with clinical placement providers or your future employer without your consent.

GMC Health Declarations

The GMC registration process includes a health declaration. You are required to declare conditions that may affect your fitness to practise. This is not the same as the adjustment application process — they are separate. A learning difference (dyslexia, ADHD) does not typically affect fitness to practise and does not need to be declared unless it has caused a patient safety incident or significantly impaired your clinical practice. Mental health conditions should be declared if they are ongoing and potentially relevant to fitness to practise — your occupational health or treating clinician can advise you on this. When in doubt, seek advice from the BMA or a medical defence organisation before declaring anything you are uncertain about.

Resources for Disabled Medical Students and IMGs

Several organisations offer specific support for medical students and doctors with disabilities:

  • BMA (bma.org.uk): The BMA has dedicated guidance on reasonable adjustments throughout medical education and employment. Their "Welcomed and Valued" resource is particularly helpful.
  • Medical Defence Organisations (MDU, MPS): Can advise on health declaration obligations and fitness to practise questions.
  • Disability Rights UK (disabilityrightsuk.org): General disability rights advice including education and examinations.
  • Student Minds: Mental health support specific to students, including medical students.
  • British Dyslexia Association (bdadyslexia.org.uk): Specific guidance on dyslexia in educational settings.
  • ADHD UK: Support and resources for adults with ADHD navigating higher education and professional training.
  • Doctors with Disabilities Initiative: A peer support and advocacy network for doctors with disabilities in the UK.

Returning to Medicine After Illness or Disability

Some candidates sitting the UKMLA are returning to medicine after a period of illness, disability-related absence, or health-related interruption to training. The GMC and medical schools have specific processes for this:

If you have had an interruption to study, your medical school will typically work with you through their Fitness to Study process to agree a supported return plan. If your health condition has caused a fitness to practise concern to be raised, the GMC has a separate health-related fitness to practise process. The BMA strongly advises seeking independent advice from a medical defence organisation before engaging with any fitness to practise process.

Returning doctors often find that their clinical knowledge has drifted during their absence. If you have been away from clinical practice for more than 12 months, consider whether a knowledge refresher or clinical placement before sitting the UKMLA is appropriate. Our guide on UKMLA preparation strategies includes options for candidates returning after a break.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Disability Access Adjustments

What qualifies as a disability for UKMLA adjustment purposes?

Under the Equality Act 2010, a disability is a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term (12 months or more) adverse effect on normal day-to-day activities. This is a broad definition and includes dyslexia, ADHD, autism, visual and hearing impairment, chronic pain, mental health conditions, long-term illnesses, and many other conditions. You do not need to consider yourself "disabled" — if your condition meets the legal definition and creates a barrier in exams, you are entitled to adjustments.

Does requesting adjustments mean I am admitting I am not capable of being a doctor?

No. Reasonable adjustments are specifically designed to allow disabled candidates to demonstrate their knowledge and clinical ability on a level playing field with non-disabled candidates. They do not lower the pass standard — they remove barriers to accessing the assessment. Many highly effective doctors have adjustments throughout their training and careers.

How long does an extra time application take to process?

GMC processing times vary. Allow a minimum of six to eight weeks for a straightforward application with complete evidence. More complex cases or applications requiring additional evidence may take longer. Apply at least three to six months before your exam date to allow sufficient buffer.

Can I apply for adjustments if I was only recently diagnosed?

Yes. A recent diagnosis does not invalidate an application. The Equality Act protects you from the point of diagnosis. If your condition meets the definition of disability and you have appropriate supporting evidence from a qualified professional, you are entitled to request adjustments regardless of when you were diagnosed.

Are adjustments available for both the AKT and CPSA?

Yes. Reasonable adjustments are available for both the AKT (PLAB 1) and CPSA (PLAB 2) components of the UKMLA. Some adjustments are specific to one component — for example, screen reader access is relevant to the computer-based AKT, while adjustments for the CPSA may involve modified station formats or communication accommodations. Apply separately for each component if needed.

What if I develop a new health condition between passing the AKT and sitting the CPSA?

Apply for adjustments for the CPSA as soon as possible after diagnosis. Explain the timing in your application and include evidence from your treating clinician. The GMC will consider your application based on current need, not on whether you had adjustments for a previous component.

Is my adjustment information kept confidential?

Yes. Information submitted in support of a reasonable adjustment application is handled in accordance with data protection law (UK GDPR) and is used only for the purpose of processing your adjustment request. It is not shared with employers, clinical placement providers, or other institutions without your explicit consent. University disability services also operate under strict confidentiality obligations.

Can I get adjustments if I do not have a formal diagnosis but suspect I have a learning difference?

A formal diagnosis is typically required for adjustment applications. However, if you are in the process of being assessed (e.g., awaiting an ADHD assessment), contact your disability services team or the GMC to explain the situation. In some cases, interim adjustments can be made pending a formal diagnosis, particularly where there is a documented history of difficulty and supporting professional correspondence.

What should I do if my adjustment is not in place on exam day?

Contact the exam invigilator or test centre manager immediately and explain that adjustments should be in place for your sitting. Carry a copy of your adjustment confirmation letter from the GMC or your university. If the adjustment cannot be implemented on the day, request written confirmation of the failure and contact the GMC or your medical school immediately after the exam to report what occurred. Document everything. You may be entitled to a resit under appropriate conditions or to have the circumstances taken into account in the assessment of your performance.

Conclusion

Every candidate who qualifies for UKMLA disability access adjustments under the Equality Act 2010 is legally entitled to receive them, and both the GMC and medical schools are obligated to provide reasonable adjustments that remove barriers to accessing the assessment. The process requires proactive planning, appropriate documentation, and early application — but it is not adversarial. Most well-evidenced applications are approved. Use the evidence table and step-by-step guides in this article to prepare your application, apply at least three to six months before your exam date, and seek support from the BMA, your university disability service, or a medical defence organisation if you encounter difficulties.
