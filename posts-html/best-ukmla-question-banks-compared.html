How the UKMLA AKT actually works, and why that matters for choosing a question bank

The Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA) is the GMC's standard for entry to the UK medical register, sat by two groups: UK medical students graduating from a UK medical school, and international medical graduates who sit PLAB, which the GMC has designed to comply with MLA requirements. Both groups are tested against the same underlying MLA content map, and both take assessments built to the same delivery requirements, according to the GMC's official MLA page.

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) is the written component of the MLA - a single-best-answer question paper testing applied clinical knowledge, built centrally by medical schools collaborating through the Medical Schools Council and quality-assured by the GMC against a published set of AKT requirements. The clinical and professional skills assessment (CPSA) is the practical counterpart, assessed through a circuit of stations rather than multiple-choice questions - which is exactly why almost every question bank on the market focuses on AKT-style practice, and why CPSA preparation needs a different kind of resource. For a full breakdown of station types and timing, see our AKT exam pattern guide and our dedicated piece on preparing for the AKT format.

Two structural facts matter enormously when judging any question bank's "realism":

  • The real exam uses Angoff-based standard setting. A panel of judges rates every question on the probability that a "just passing" candidate would answer it correctly, and those ratings are combined to set the pass mark for that sitting. This means the pass mark moves slightly between sittings depending on the specific questions used - a question bank cannot replicate this process, so no bank's internal "pass mark" is a reliable predictor of your real result. See our companion piece on the UKMLA AKT pass mark and pass rate explained for more detail on how this works in practice.
  • Delivery is centrally governed. Every MS AKT sitting is built to a common format, test standard and delivery method overseen by the GMC, according to the GMC's own guidance for medical schools. A question bank can approximate the style and difficulty of SBA questions, but it is being written independently of that central process, so stylistic drift from the real paper is inevitable to some degree, however good the bank.

None of this means question banks are pointless - far from it, they remain the single most efficient way to drill applied knowledge at scale. It does mean "closest to the real exam" is a matter of degree, not an absolute claim any provider can honestly make, and you should treat marketing language about "identical to the real exam" with healthy scepticism.

Individual provider write-ups

Quesmed: the closest thing to an all-in-one UKMLA platform

Quesmed was built from the ground up around the UKMLA rather than retrofitted from an older finals question bank, and it shows in how tightly its content, mocks and flashcards track the MLA framework. Beyond its core SBA bank, it packages in textbook-style notes, flashcards, roughly a dozen curated UKMLA mock exams, five PSA-style mocks, and more than 50 university-specific mock tests aimed at earlier-year students still building foundations. It also extends into MRCP and MSRA preparation, which matters if you are looking past the UKMLA to postgraduate exams.

The trade-off is that Quesmed's "one platform, all resources" approach means you are paying for more than a pure question bank - useful if you want everything in one login, less useful if you already have notes and flashcards elsewhere and just want raw SBA volume. Pricing is tiered by plan length and bundle, and shifts with promotions, so check Quesmed's own pricing page for the live rate before committing. For UK final-year students juggling clinical placements and revision, the combined format tends to save time that would otherwise go into stitching together separate note and flashcard apps.

Passmedicine: the long-standing volume leader

Passmedicine has quietly become one of the largest question pools available to UK medical students, with over 11,000 single-best-answer and extended-matching questions spanning finals and UKMLA-style content. It has been a fixture of UK medical school revision for years, which means its explanations and question style have been refined against real student feedback over multiple exam cycles - a maturity advantage that newer platforms haven't had time to build.

Its pricing is comparatively affordable relative to its question count, typically in the tens of pounds for several months of access, though exact figures vary by promotion and plan length. The main consideration is that with a bank this large, it is easy to spread yourself too thin - working every question once shallowly rather than a focused subset repeatedly and properly. Used well, though, its sheer depth of coverage makes gaps in your knowledge hard to hide from.

UWorld: excellent reasoning, imperfect UK fit

UWorld built its reputation on USMLE preparation in the United States, and its UKMLA AKT product inherits the same emphasis on richly explained, clinical-reasoning-heavy questions - over 2,100 of them, each paired with a detailed rationale for every answer option, not just the correct one. The mobile app is polished, syncing seamlessly between devices, and the 7-day free trial with 25 questions and no card required makes it easy to sample before paying roughly £240-£320 (about $300-$400) for a few months of access.

The catch for UK candidates is real: some drug names, dosing conventions and management pathways in UWorld's question bank reflect US clinical practice rather than NICE, CKS, SIGN or BNF guidance, and the platform is not built with explicit MLA content-map tagging the way UK-native banks are. Candidates who use UWorld successfully for the UKMLA tend to be strong enough clinically to spot and mentally correct US-isms, treating the platform as a reasoning gym rather than a literal answer key. If that description fits you, UWorld's explanations are genuinely some of the best written in the industry. If you are earlier in training or an IMG unfamiliar with UK-specific guidance, lead with a UK-native bank instead.

Geeky Medics Qbank: strong ecosystem, newer written-exam offering

Geeky Medics built its name on free OSCE and clinical skills content before extending into a paid question bank, and that heritage still shapes its strengths. The MLA AKT Question Bank now runs to more than 3,000 SBA questions and full mock exams mapped to the MLA content map, bundled with three flashcard decks covering high-yield signs, investigations and drugs, webinar recordings, and a notebook of 350-plus concise topic summaries - all for a comparatively modest price, typically in the £20-£40 range.

Independent comparisons generally note that the Qbank itself is less mature for pure written SBA practice than long-established rivals like Passmedicine or Quesmed, since Geeky Medics' core strength has historically been clinical skills and OSCE-style content rather than exam-style multiple choice. If you are already using Geeky Medics for CPSA preparation, adding the Qbank keeps everything under one login; if written AKT practice is your priority, weigh it against more AKT-mature alternatives first.

MedRevisions: built for the PLAB-to-UKMLA IMG journey

MedRevisions positions itself explicitly at the intersection of PLAB and UKMLA preparation, which makes it a natural fit for IMGs navigating the transition between the two systems. Its bank runs to more than 5,000 questions, with a curated "Study Essential" mode that filters down to around 1,800 high-yield questions the platform's data suggests appear most frequently - the idea being to bank a passing level of preparation quickly, then broaden out to the full question pool for a stronger margin. Every answer is referenced against NICE, CKS and SIGN guidance, which matters for IMGs coming from health systems with different first-line management protocols.

Pricing starts from roughly $10-11 per month equivalent with multiple duration plans, and every account opens with a free trial giving access to 70-plus sample questions and limited AI tutor sessions with no card required. The built-in AI study assistant, which analyses your performance to flag your weakest Areas of Clinical Practice, is a genuinely useful differentiator for candidates who want a data-driven revision plan rather than working through content linearly. If you are researching this route in more depth, our UKMLA preparation guide for IMGs covers the wider transition beyond just the question bank choice.

MLA Prep: the volume-and-value option

MLA Prep is a newer entrant built specifically for the 2025-2026 UKMLA cycle, and it competes hard on price-to-content ratio. Its bank runs to more than 10,000 single-best-answer questions plus over 10,000 flashcards integrated into a spaced-repetition engine, and it offers unlimited 200-question full-length mock exams - a generous allowance compared with banks that cap mock attempts. Every answer is referenced against NICE and BNF guidance, keeping it aligned with UK clinical practice.

Its standout feature is the pricing model itself: alongside a modest weekly rate (around £4.99), MLA Prep offers a one-off lifetime purchase (around £49.99) rather than a recurring subscription, which works out considerably cheaper than most rivals over any realistic revision window of several months. The trade-off is that MLA Prep has a smaller established community and track record than long-running names like Passmedicine or Pastest, so you are trading brand maturity for cost efficiency. iatroX: the AI-adaptive and free-tier option

iatroX takes a different approach from the rest of the market: rather than a static question pool you work through linearly, it uses an AI-adaptive engine combining spaced repetition and active recall to resurface the questions where your retention is weakest. Its UKMLA content is explicitly mapped to more than 430 conditions on the 2026 GMC MLA content map, with every question grounded in NICE, CKS, BNF, SIGN or NHS guidance and explanations citing their source - a level of sourcing transparency few competitors match.

Its Socratic Tutor feature, available on the paid Pro tier, is worth calling out specifically: rather than just re-showing an explanation after a missed question, it prompts you to reason through the concept again before teaching it in the context of the source guideline, which mirrors how a good clinical supervisor would actually correct your thinking. Crucially, iatroX offers a genuinely free UKMLA qbank tier with adaptive learning built in, with the paid tier (roughly £29/month or £99/year) unlocking the Socratic Tutor and deeper analytics. For candidates on a tight budget, or those wanting to trial an AI-driven approach before committing money elsewhere, iatroX's free tier is one of the most substantive free options on the market.

Pastest: the explanation specialist

Pastest has built its long-running reputation for UK medical exam preparation on the quality of its written explanations rather than sheer question count, and that reputation largely holds for its UKMLA offering. The core bank runs to over 4,000 questions with detailed expanded explanations, revision notes, and hundreds of flashcards and spot-diagnosis cards, while its Knowledge+ upgrade extends coverage to more than 6,000 questions with added performance analytics.

Typical pricing sits around £50-£100 for three to six months, positioning it mid-to-upper range against competitors. If your main gap is understanding why an answer is right or wrong - rather than just drilling volume - Pastest's explanation depth is consistently cited as its strongest asset in independent comparisons.

AMBOSS: strong globally, narrower for the UKMLA specifically

AMBOSS is a genuinely excellent platform for candidates studying towards multiple international exams simultaneously - its "Student Life" membership bundles a huge cross-exam question library (spanning IFOM, MCCQE, SMLE, ENARM and more) with a rich integrated medical library, all under one subscription. For candidates whose plans extend beyond just the UKMLA - for example, IMGs weighing multiple licensing routes - that breadth has real value.

For a candidate whose only goal is the UKMLA AKT specifically, though, AMBOSS is a less targeted purchase than UK-native banks. Its question tagging and explanations are not built around the GMC's MLA content map, and UKMLA-specific coverage sits inside a much broader international library rather than as a dedicated product. It is worth considering if you are already an AMBOSS subscriber for other reasons, less compelling as a first purchase made solely for the UKMLA.

How to choose based on learning style, budget and timeline

Once you have the shortlist, the real decision comes down to three practical filters: how you learn, what you can spend, and how much time you have left. Treating these three factors seriously will get you to the right answer for the best question bank for UKMLA prep faster than reading another ten reviews.

By learning style

  • If you learn best from long, detailed explanations - Pastest and UWorld both invest heavily in rationale depth; either suits you well, with Pastest better aligned to UK guidance.
  • If you want an adaptive system that tells you what to study next - iatroX's spaced-repetition engine and MedRevisions' AI study assistant both actively direct your revision rather than leaving sequencing entirely to you.
  • If you prefer working systematically through a content map - Quesmed and Geeky Medics both structure content closely around MLA topics, making it easy to track coverage area by area. Our MLA content map explained guide is worth reading alongside whichever bank you choose, so you can cross-check topic coverage yourself rather than trusting the platform's tagging blindly.
  • If you learn through repetition and flashcards as much as SBAs - MLA Prep's 10,000-plus flashcard deck and Quesmed's flashcard sets both support a dual-format approach.

By budget

If cost is the binding constraint, start with iatroX's free tier to see whether the adaptive-learning format suits you, then layer in a low-cost paid option such as MLA Prep's lifetime purchase or a shorter MedRevisions plan once you know your revision window. Avoid the temptation to buy the most expensive bank assuming price signals quality - several of the cheapest options here (MLA Prep, MedRevisions, iatroX) are also among the most explicitly UKMLA-content-map-aligned, while a premium price tag like UWorld's reflects production quality rather than closer alignment to the actual UK exam.

By timeline

Your remaining revision time should shape both which bank and which subscription length you buy:

  • Six months or more out: a broad, high-volume bank (Passmedicine, MLA Prep, MedRevisions) lets you work through content systematically without running out of fresh questions.
  • Three months out: shift toward a bank with strong mock-exam functionality and start timed full papers weekly. Our three-month AKT countdown guide lays out exactly how to sequence this.
  • Final four to six weeks: prioritise a bank's mock exams and your weakest content-map areas over grinding fresh questions in topics you have already mastered - this is where an AI-adaptive tool like iatroX's weak-topic resurfacing genuinely earns its keep.

Whichever timeline you're on, cross-reference your chosen bank against the official MLA content map and syllabus rather than assuming any single provider's tagging is complete or current - the GMC itself describes the content map as a live document subject to ongoing revision.

Common mistakes candidates make when choosing and using a question bank

Over-relying on a single bank's internal statistics

It's tempting to treat a question bank's dashboard percentage as a direct proxy for your real exam readiness. It isn't. Every bank sets its own difficulty curve and scoring logic, which has no fixed relationship to the Angoff-derived pass mark used in the actual AKT. Use your qbank score as a trend over time - is it improving week on week - rather than as a literal forecast of your result.

Buying the biggest question count without checking content-map alignment

A bank advertising 10,000+ questions isn't automatically better than one with 3,000 if a meaningful share of those questions aren't mapped to the areas the UKMLA actually tests. Before buying on volume alone, check whether the provider explicitly references the GMC's MLA content map and how recently its content was updated against it.

Ignoring the official content map entirely

Some candidates work through a question bank cover to cover and assume that equals full syllabus coverage. It doesn't necessarily - question banks are written by third parties, however carefully, and can drift from or lag behind updates to the live GMC content map. Cross-check your revision periodically against the MLA content map explained guide and the official syllabus page rather than trusting any single provider's internal topic list as the final word.

Switching banks too often

Chasing a "better" bank every few weeks fragments your revision and means you never build the pattern recognition that comes from seeing a provider's question style repeatedly. Pick a primary bank within your first week of serious revision and commit to it for the bulk of your preparation; only add a second bank deliberately, for a specific gap, rather than restlessly.

Treating US-centric content as directly transferable

If you use UWorld or AMBOSS as your primary resource, actively flag and correct US-specific drug names, dosing conventions or guideline references against UK practice (NICE, CKS, SIGN, BNF) as you go. Passively absorbing US-style answers risks embedding incorrect first-line choices for the UK context.

Skipping timed, full-length mocks until the final week

Working through questions in short, untimed bursts is comfortable, but it doesn't train exam-day stamina or pacing. Start incorporating full-length timed mocks from at least a few weeks before your revision would otherwise "feel ready," so that time-pressure mistakes surface while you still have time to fix them.

Neglecting the CPSA because the qbank only covers AKT

Because almost every question bank on this list focuses on the written AKT, it's easy to let CPSA preparation slip in favour of qbank grinding, particularly since qbank progress feels more measurable. Build dedicated CPSA practice - communication skills, examination technique, professional dilemmas - into your schedule from the same point you start AKT question banking, not as an afterthought once AKT revision winds down.

Assuming a free trial question set represents the full bank's difficulty

Free trial questions are sometimes selected to showcase a platform's best or most polished content, which can misrepresent the average difficulty and explanation quality of the paid bank as a whole. Read independent reviews and, where possible, a short paid trial period alongside the free sample before committing to a longer subscription.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best question bank for UKMLA preparation?

There is no single best question bank for UKMLA for every candidate. Quesmed and Passmedicine are the strongest all-round picks with strong content-map alignment, MLA Prep and MedRevisions lead on affordability and question volume, iatroX leads on AI-adaptive tutoring, and UWorld suits candidates who want deep clinical reasoning but need to adjust for US-centric content. The right choice depends on your budget, timeline and where you are studying from.

Is Quesmed worth it for the UKMLA?

Quesmed is generally worth it for UK final-year students because it was built specifically around the MLA content map and bundles SBAs, flashcards, notes and CPSA-style practice in one subscription. IMGs who only need AKT-style written practice may find cheaper, more focused alternatives such as MLA Prep or MedRevisions equally effective.

How many questions do I need to practise before the UKMLA AKT?

Most successful candidates work through several thousand practice questions across their revision period, not just one full run of a single bank. A common approach is one primary question bank used two to three times through, supplemented by timed mocks, rather than chasing the single largest question count available.

Do UKMLA question banks match the real Angoff-marked exam format?

No question bank perfectly replicates the real AKT, because the official exam uses Angoff standard setting to fix its pass mark and is delivered under strict, centrally governed conditions. The closest banks map explicitly to the GMC's MLA content map and use single-best-answer formats similar to the real paper, but candidates should treat every qbank as practice, not a guaranteed predictor of their exact pass mark.

Is UWorld good for the UKMLA?

UWorld's UKMLA AKT product offers well-written, rationale-rich questions and a polished mobile app, and many candidates rate its explanations highly for building clinical reasoning. Its content originates from a US-focused question-writing tradition, so UK candidates sometimes encounter drug names, guidelines or terminology that need translating to NICE and BNF conventions before relying on it as a sole resource.

What is the cheapest way to prepare for the UKMLA with a question bank?

MLA Prep's one-off lifetime purchase model and MedRevisions' lower-cost monthly tiers are generally the most budget-friendly routes into a large UKMLA-specific question pool, and iatroX offers a genuinely free adaptive qbank tier. Comparing cost per month of realistic access, rather than the headline price, is the better way to judge affordability.

Can I pass the UKMLA using only free question banks?

Some candidates do pass using free resources such as iatroX's free tier or free trial questions from paid platforms, but free banks are usually smaller and less exhaustively mapped to the MLA content map. Most candidates get better content-map coverage and more realistic mock exams from at least one paid subscription during their final revision block.

Should I use more than one question bank for the UKMLA?

Using two question banks can help expose blind spots, particularly one UK-built bank for content-map alignment and one for volume or a different explanation style, but running three or more banks simultaneously tends to fragment revision time. Most candidates get more value from mastering one primary bank thoroughly and using a second only for topic-specific gaps.

Do question banks cover the CPSA as well as the AKT?

Most dedicated question banks focus on the AKT's written single-best-answer format, since the CPSA is a practical, in-person circuit of clinical and professional skills stations that cannot be replicated through multiple-choice questions. A small number of platforms, including Quesmed, offer supplementary CPSA-style station practice, but this should sit alongside dedicated communication-skills and examination practice rather than replace it.

How much does a UKMLA question bank typically cost?

Prices vary widely by provider and subscription length, typically ranging from free tiers up to roughly £50 to £100 for three to twelve months of full access, with some platforms offering one-off lifetime purchases instead of recurring subscriptions. Providers change pricing and promotions frequently, so always check the current rate on the provider's own site before buying.

Is a paid question bank necessary to pass the UKMLA AKT?

A paid question bank is not strictly necessary, but most candidates find that a structured, well-explained bank saves substantial revision time compared with assembling free questions from scattered sources. Given the AKT fee itself is a significant cost (£283 under the 2026 GMC fee schedule), many candidates view a modestly priced qbank as a reasonable investment against the risk of a resit.
