For a doctor deciding between the UK, Australia and Canada as a destination country, the honest short answer is this: each country requires passing a distinct national licensing assessment before you can register and practise, and none of the three can be substituted for another. The UKMLA vs AMC exam comparison ultimately comes down to the UK's Medical Licensing Assessment (AKT plus CPSA), Australia's AMC pathway (a computer-based knowledge test plus a separate clinical exam), and Canada's MCCQE (Part I and Part II) sitting alongside very different registration bodies, cost structures, and post-exam job markets. All three lead to a legitimate, internationally respected medical career, but the practical experience of getting there — timeline, cost, test-centre access from your home country, and how easily you land a first clinical post — differs enough that the decision deserves a proper side-by-side comparison rather than a guess based on forum chatter.

This guide sets out that comparison directly. It briefly recaps the UKMLA structure (with links to this site's dedicated exam-format posts for the full detail), then walks through the AMC pathway for Australia and the MCCQE pathway for Canada at a high, appropriately hedged level, before bringing all three together in a single comparison table covering exam structure, approximate cost, approximate timeline, test-centre accessibility, English language requirements, and how easy it typically is to find a first clinical post. It also looks at how candidates actually decide between the three destinations in practice, and at the small but real group of doctors who pursue more than one pathway at once. Throughout, figures for the AMC and MCCQE routes are presented as approximate and subject to change — candidates should always verify exact current fees and formats directly with the Australian Medical Council and the Medical Council of Canada before making financial or timeline commitments, since this site's core authority is the UK process, not Australian or Canadian regulatory specifics.

It is worth being clear from the outset why this three-way comparison matters more than it once did. A growing number of IMGs no longer default to a single destination country; instead, they research the UK, Australia and Canada in parallel, often alongside the US route covered in the UKMLA vs USMLE comparison, before committing serious time and money to any one licensing exam. Making that choice well requires comparing exam structure, cost, and timeline honestly across all three destinations rather than relying on outdated assumptions about which country is "easiest" — a claim none of the relevant regulators actually make about their own exams.

Many candidates researching this comparison arrive from a different starting point again: rather than choosing between the UK, Australia and Canada from scratch, they are Indian, Pakistani, Nigerian, or other Commonwealth-trained doctors who have already weighed a domestic postgraduate route against an international one, in the way the UKMLA vs NEET PG comparison sets out for Indian MBBS graduates choosing between NEET PG and the UK pathway. For that group, the UK-vs-Australia-vs-Canada question typically arises only after they have already decided against staying in their home country's postgraduate system, which is a genuinely separate decision from the one this guide focuses on. Therefore, readers who have not yet settled whether an international route makes sense for them at all may want to read that comparison first, before working through the UK-Australia-Canada breakdown that follows.

Furthermore, it is worth noting that "easiest" is doing a lot of unhelpful work in most informal comparisons of these three exams. None of the GMC, the AMC, or the Medical Council of Canada publishes a cross-country difficulty ranking, and for good reason: each body sets its own content map, its own pass standard, and its own clinical context, so a candidate's subjective sense of which exam felt "easier" says more about their own training background and language comfort than about any objective difference in rigour between the three assessments. Consequently, this guide deliberately avoids ranking the three exams by difficulty and instead focuses on the comparison points that actually are measurable and verifiable: structure, approximate cost, approximate timeline, test-centre access, and typical post-exam job-market experience.

The UKMLA Structure: A Brief Recap

The UKMLA is the Medical Licensing Assessment administered by the GMC, and it consists of exactly two components. The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) is a single sitting of 200 single-best-answer questions delivered at Pearson VUE test centres worldwide, and it assesses applied clinical knowledge against the official MLA content map rather than pure theoretical recall. The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) is an 18-station OSCE-style clinical exam sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, testing history-taking, examination, communication, and practical clinical skills.

This site covers the UKMLA's structure, fees, and scoring in far greater depth elsewhere, so rather than repeating that detail here, readers who want the full mechanics of the UK route should consult the complete guide to becoming a doctor in the UK and GMC registration, which sets out the entire pathway from eligibility through to a first NHS post. For the specific question of how the AKT is marked and what pass rates actually look like by candidate group, the AKT pass mark and pass rate explained is the relevant reference. Candidates who arrived at the UKMLA by way of the older PLAB numbering, or who are actively comparing the UK route against other established pathways, may also find the UKMLA vs PLAB pillar page and the dedicated UKMLA vs PLAB comparison post useful, since they cover in detail how PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 map onto today's AKT and CPSA. The GMC itself publishes the authoritative description of how the MLA assessments work in its official guide to the MLA assessments, and this remains the single source candidates should treat as definitive over any third-party summary, including this one.

What matters for this comparison is less the fine mechanics of the AKT and CPSA — covered thoroughly elsewhere on this site — and more how those two components stack up structurally against the AMC and MCCQE pathways discussed below. In broad terms, all three countries use a similar two-stage logic: a computer-based knowledge test followed by a practical clinical skills assessment. However, the specifics of format, cost, and where each stage can be sat differ meaningfully between the UK, Australia and Canada, and those differences are often more decisive for a candidate's actual choice of destination than the underlying exam content itself.

The AMC Pathway: Australia's Route to Registration

Australia's pathway for internationally trained doctors is coordinated by the Australian Medical Council (AMC), which assesses whether an IMG's qualifications and competence meet the standard required for registration with the Medical Board of Australia. Broadly, the AMC pathway for most IMGs not already eligible for a competent-authority or specialist pathway involves two main assessment stages, followed by registration.

The AMC Computer Adaptive Test (CAT)

The first stage is generally known as the AMC Computer Adaptive Test (CAT), a computer-based multiple-choice examination that assesses medical knowledge applied to clinical scenarios, broadly analogous in purpose to the UKMLA's AKT. It is delivered at computer-based test centres, and historically has been available in a number of countries internationally, though the exact list of test locations, sitting windows, and fee levels changes periodically and should always be confirmed directly on the AMC's own website rather than assumed from older sources. Candidates should treat any second-hand description of the CAT's exact question count, timing, or current fee as indicative rather than definitive, since this is precisely the kind of operational detail regulators update from time to time.

The AMC Clinical Exam

The second stage is the AMC clinical exam, a practical, multi-station assessment of clinical skills, communication, and professional judgement, broadly analogous in purpose to the UKMLA's CPSA. It has traditionally been delivered primarily at assessment centres within Australia, which is an important practical consideration for IMGs based overseas, since it generally implies at least one, and sometimes more than one, trip to Australia specifically for this stage of assessment. As with the CAT, exact current format, station count, and fee should be verified directly with the AMC, since clinical exam delivery arrangements are the kind of detail that can and does change between cohorts.

From AMC Assessment to Registration

Passing both AMC stages is generally a step towards eligibility for registration with the Medical Board of Australia, most commonly initially as general or limited registration, depending on the candidate's specific circumstances, supervision arrangements, and any additional requirements set by the Board. This registration process sits alongside, and is administratively separate from, the AMC's own assessment process, and candidates should expect a distinct application, documentation, and processing step with the Medical Board once AMC requirements are satisfied. Consequently, the realistic timeline from a first AMC booking to being registered and job-ready in Australia typically extends well beyond simply the time needed to pass the CAT and clinical exam themselves, since registration processing, visa arrangements, and — for many IMGs — securing a supervised position add further months to the overall journey.

Given how much of this pathway depends on current AMC policy, candidates seriously considering Australia should treat this section as an orientation rather than a substitute for the AMC's own published guidance, and should verify exact current fee levels, test-centre locations, and registration requirements directly with the Australian Medical Council and the Medical Board of Australia before committing time or money to this route.

It is also worth noting that not every IMG follows the standard AMC-assessment pathway described above. Doctors who qualified in certain recognised jurisdictions, or who hold specialist qualifications already recognised through a competent-authority or specialist-pathway route, may bypass some or all of the CAT and clinical exam requirements, subject to the Medical Board's own assessment of their individual circumstances. Consequently, candidates should not assume the two-stage AMC route described here automatically applies to them without first checking their own eligibility category, since Australia's registration framework is genuinely more layered than a single uniform pathway for every IMG. Additionally, supervised practice conditions attached to initial registration are common for IMGs entering through the standard pathway, meaning a newly registered doctor may spend a defined period working under a level of supervision before qualifying for less restricted registration — a further practical difference from the UK route, where GMC registration itself does not typically carry an equivalent blanket supervision requirement tied to the registration category.

The MCCQE Pathway: Canada's Route to Licensure

Canada's principal national licensing examination for physicians is the Medical Council of Canada Qualifying Examination (MCCQE), administered by the Medical Council of Canada (MCC). Similarly to the UK and Australian pathways, it is structured in two parts, though the practical route from passing the MCCQE to actually practising in Canada involves an additional layer of provincial and territorial regulation that does not have a direct equivalent in the UK system.

MCCQE Part I: The Knowledge-Based Examination

MCCQE Part I is a computer-based examination assessing the medical knowledge, clinical decision-making, and professional competencies expected of a physician beginning independent practice in Canada, and it is broadly analogous in purpose to the UKMLA's AKT and the AMC's CAT. It is generally available at computer-based test centres in a range of countries, which has historically made it one of the more internationally accessible stages across the three pathways compared here, though the precise list of eligible countries, test-centre network, and current fee should be confirmed directly with the MCC rather than assumed from prior years' information.

MCCQE Part II: The Clinical Skills Examination

MCCQE Part II assesses clinical skills, most commonly through an objective structured clinical examination format similar in spirit to the UK's CPSA and Australia's clinical exam, testing history-taking, physical examination, and clinical communication against simulated patients. The format, eligibility requirements, and delivery arrangements for Part II have evolved over recent years, and at various points MCC has changed how and where this component is delivered; consequently, candidates should check current MCC guidance directly for exact format and whether in-person travel to a Canadian test centre is required for their specific eligibility route, rather than relying on this or any other third-party summary.

From MCCQE to a Provincial Licence

Passing the MCCQE is a necessary step towards licensure, but it is not sufficient on its own — licensure in Canada is ultimately granted by the relevant provincial or territorial College of Physicians and Surgeons, not by the Medical Council of Canada directly. This means an IMG's practical pathway to practising in Canada depends significantly on which province or territory they intend to work in, since requirements, additional assessments, and the availability of practice-ready assessment programmes or residency positions for IMGs vary by jurisdiction. For many IMGs, securing a residency position or a recognised practice-ready assessment placement is a substantial additional hurdle beyond the MCCQE itself, and this step is often the single biggest source of delay in the overall Canadian pathway. Candidates should research their target province's specific college requirements directly, since generalising across all of Canada risks missing jurisdiction-specific detail that materially affects the realistic timeline.

As with the AMC pathway, this section is intended as a high-level, honestly hedged orientation rather than a definitive account of current MCCQE fees or formats. Anyone seriously pursuing the Canadian route should verify exact current detail directly with the Medical Council of Canada and their target province's medical regulatory college before making firm plans.

A further point worth flagging honestly is that many IMGs targeting Canada find the residency-matching stage, rather than the MCCQE itself, to be the most competitive part of the entire pathway. Canada runs its own national residency matching process for postgraduate training positions, and IMG applicants typically compete for a comparatively small pool of positions specifically designated for internationally trained candidates, separate from the larger pool available to Canadian medical graduates. This dynamic means that passing MCCQE Part I and Part II, while a genuine achievement, does not by itself guarantee that a candidate will secure a residency position in their preferred specialty or province, and some IMGs spend one or more additional years building Canadian clinical experience, research involvement, or reapplying to the match before securing a position. Candidates should research current match statistics and provincial IMG quotas directly through the relevant Canadian match organisation, since these figures shift from year to year and vary considerably by specialty.

How Candidates Typically Decide Between the UK, Australia and Canada

In practice, the decision between these three destinations is rarely made on exam structure alone. Several recurring factors shape how real candidates choose, and naming them explicitly is more useful than pretending the decision is purely technical.

  • Family ties and existing connections. Candidates with relatives, a spouse's job opportunities, or an existing support network already established in one of the three countries frequently weight that factor above exam cost or timeline differences, since relocating close to family materially affects quality of life during what is already a demanding transition.
  • Cost of living in the destination country. Beyond the exam and registration fees themselves, the ongoing cost of living — housing, healthcare, taxation, and general affordability — varies significantly between the UK, Australia and Canada, and between cities within each country, and candidates budgeting realistically should research current cost-of-living data for their likely city of practice, not just the headline exam costs compared above.
  • Long-term immigration pathway. Each country's route from a work visa to permanent residency, and eventually citizenship, differs in structure, points criteria, and processing time. Candidates who prioritise a clear, well-understood long-term immigration pathway sometimes let this factor outweigh a marginally faster or cheaper exam route, since permanent settlement plans span decades, not months.
  • Existing diaspora community. A strong existing diaspora and professional network from a candidate's home country — whether Indian, Pakistani, Nigerian, Filipino, or otherwise — in a particular city or region often eases the practical and cultural transition considerably, and candidates frequently factor in where their compatriots have already established themselves professionally.
  • English-speaking test availability in the home country. Practical test-centre access matters more than candidates often expect before researching it properly. A route whose knowledge test is available at a computer-based centre close to home, without requiring international travel just to sit the first stage, is meaningfully more attractive to many candidates than a route requiring an expensive trip abroad even for the earliest assessment stage.

Consequently, two candidates with near-identical academic backgrounds can reasonably make different choices, and neither is wrong — the "right" destination genuinely depends on a mix of professional and personal priorities that goes well beyond which exam has the lower headline fee.

In addition to the factors above, many candidates also weigh how each country's health system is structured day to day, since this affects working life far more than the licensing exam itself does. The NHS is a single, nationally coordinated public system with standardised training grades and pay scales, which some candidates find reassuring for its predictability, while Australia's mixed public-private system and Canada's provincially administered healthcare system each offer a different balance of autonomy, private-practice opportunity, and regional variation in working conditions. Similarly, candidates who have already worked or trained under a particular country's clinical guidelines — for example, doctors from Commonwealth countries whose undergraduate curricula were historically modelled on UK practice — sometimes find one system's clinical conventions more immediately familiar than another's, which can meaningfully shorten the adjustment period after registration regardless of how the licensing exams themselves compare on paper.

Pursuing More Than One Pathway: Parallel and Sequential Approaches

Some doctors choose not to commit to a single destination early, instead pursuing more than one of the UKMLA, AMC, or MCCQE pathways in parallel or in sequence, as a deliberate hedge against uncertainty in any one country's job market, registration processing, or visa policy. This is logistically possible, since nothing in principle prevents a candidate from holding a UKMLA pass alongside AMC or MCCQE credentials, but it carries real, often underestimated costs that deserve honest consideration before committing to a dual-track strategy.

The most obvious cost is financial: pursuing two national licensing pathways roughly doubles direct exam and registration spending, and when travel for in-person clinical exam components — Manchester for the UK, Australia for the AMC clinical exam, and potentially Canada for MCCQE Part II — is added, the combined cost of a genuine dual-track approach can be substantial. Time is the scarcer resource in practice, however. Preparing properly for the UK's guideline-specific AKT and CPSA content draws on a different knowledge base than preparing for the AMC or MCCQE equivalents, even though all three test broadly similar core clinical competence, and most candidates find they cannot productively prepare for two full assessment cycles at full intensity simultaneously. The more sustainable pattern, in practice, is to sequence the two pathways — completing one substantially before beginning serious preparation for the other — rather than interleaving study for both from day one.

One practical question candidates often ask is whether credential-verification work can be reused across pathways. Primary source verification of medical degrees, transcripts, and internship completion is typically required by each country's regulator independently, and while the underlying documents are the same, each regulator generally runs its own verification process through its own preferred service, meaning the administrative work of gathering, certifying, and submitting documentation usually cannot simply be copied from one application to another. Consequently, candidates planning a dual-track approach should budget for genuinely separate verification and application administration for each pathway, rather than assuming that having already been verified once for one country meaningfully speeds up the equivalent process for another. English language test scores, by contrast, are sometimes more portable in principle — a strong IELTS or OET result can potentially satisfy more than one country's requirement — but candidates should confirm this directly against each regulator's current accepted-test list, since score bands and accepted test versions differ by country and are not automatically interchangeable.

Ultimately, whether a dual-track strategy makes sense depends on a candidate's tolerance for spreading preparation time thin, their financial capacity to fund two parallel administrative processes, and how much they value keeping multiple destination doors open against the efficiency of committing fully to the single route that best fits their circumstances. Neither approach is inherently wiser — it is a genuine trade-off between optionality and focus that each candidate needs to weigh against their own priorities.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the UKMLA harder than the AMC exam?

There is no independently verified, official ranking of UKMLA versus AMC difficulty, since the two assessments are set by different regulators using different content maps, formats, and standard-setting methods. What can be said honestly is that the two pathways test broadly similar core clinical competence through a comparable two-stage structure — a knowledge test followed by a clinical skills exam — but comparing exact "difficulty" across them is not something either regulator publishes data to support.

Which is cheaper, UKMLA or MCCQE?

On available evidence, the UKMLA route tends to be the less expensive of the two once core exam and registration fees are combined, though exact current MCCQE and provincial licensing fees should always be confirmed directly with the Medical Council of Canada and the relevant provincial college, since fee schedules change periodically. Candidates should also factor in travel costs for any required in-person components — Manchester for the UK's CPSA, and potentially a Canadian test centre for MCCQE Part II — when comparing genuine total cost rather than headline exam fees alone.

Can I sit the AMC exam outside Australia?

The AMC's computer-based knowledge test (the CAT) has historically been available at test centres in a number of countries outside Australia, though the exact current list of locations should be confirmed directly with the AMC. The AMC clinical exam, however, has traditionally been delivered primarily within Australia, meaning most IMGs should expect to travel to Australia specifically for that stage regardless of where they sit the knowledge test.

Do I need to travel to Canada for the MCCQE?

MCCQE Part I is generally available at computer-based test centres in several countries, reducing the need for international travel for that stage alone. MCCQE Part II's delivery arrangements have varied over recent years, and depending on current MCC policy and a candidate's specific eligibility route, travel to a Canadian test centre may or may not be required — this should be confirmed directly with the Medical Council of Canada rather than assumed from prior years' arrangements.

Which route is fastest: UK, Australia, or Canada?

On the evidence available, the UK route is generally the fastest of the three from a first exam booking to starting a clinical post, often achievable in under two years for a well-prepared candidate, partly because there is no centralised job-matching process standing between GMC registration and applying for NHS posts. Both the Australian and Canadian routes typically involve additional registration or placement-seeking steps — such as securing a supervised position in Australia or a residency/practice-ready assessment placement in Canada — that tend to extend the realistic overall timeline.

Can I use the same documents for UKMLA, AMC, and MCCQE applications?

Generally, no in a fully interchangeable sense. Each regulator — the GMC, the AMC, and the Medical Council of Canada together with the relevant provincial college — typically runs its own independent primary source verification process, even though the underlying degree certificates, transcripts, and internship records are the same documents. Candidates pursuing more than one pathway should budget time and administrative effort for separate verification and application processes with each body rather than assuming one verification satisfies all three.

Is it realistic to prepare for UKMLA and AMC or MCCQE at the same time?

It is logistically possible but demanding, since each pathway's knowledge test is mapped to a different national clinical guideline set and each clinical skills exam has its own station format and expectations. Most candidates who genuinely want to keep more than one destination open find it more sustainable to sequence their preparation — completing one pathway substantially before committing to serious preparation for another — rather than attempting to peak for two full assessment cycles simultaneously.

Does passing the UKMLA, AMC, or MCCQE guarantee a job?

No, for any of the three. Passing the UKMLA makes an IMG eligible for GMC registration and, from there, eligible to apply directly for NHS posts, which is a comparatively direct process without a centralised matching algorithm. Passing the AMC pathway or the MCCQE similarly makes a candidate eligible for registration or licensure, but securing an actual clinical post in Australia or Canada typically still depends on separate job-seeking, supervised-position arrangements, or — in Canada's case — a competitive residency or practice-ready assessment process.

Conclusion

Comparing UKMLA and AMC, or comparing UKMLA and MCCQE, is ultimately a comparison of three legitimate but structurally different national licensing systems, each leading to a respected medical career, but each demanding a different mix of cost, time, and administrative persistence. The UK route tends to offer the fastest and least expensive path from exam to first clinical post, while the AMC and MCCQE pathways in Australia and Canada often involve additional registration or placement-seeking steps that lengthen the realistic timeline, though both remain well-established, credible routes for IMGs who prioritise those destinations for personal, professional, or immigration reasons. Whichever country a candidate ultimately chooses, the decision deserves the same standard of accurate, current, regulator-verified information, rather than assumptions carried over from forums or outdated blog posts. Readers who have concluded that the UK route fits their circumstances best should confirm their standing against the GMC's requirements before booking anything.
