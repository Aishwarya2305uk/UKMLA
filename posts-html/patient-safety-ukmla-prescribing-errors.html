Patient safety is not just a theme in the UKMLA — it is the entire purpose of the assessment. Every question in the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and every station in the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) exists because the General Medical Council wants to be certain that every doctor entering independent UK practice can keep patients safe. Prescribing errors, near-miss reporting, duty of candour, and the professional response to mistakes are not peripheral topics — they appear directly in AKT questions, underpin CPSA communication scenarios, and form a thread running through the Good Medical Practice framework the UKMLA is built on.

This guide explains everything you need to know about patient safety for the UKMLA: the types of prescribing errors the AKT tests, the near-miss reporting systems you must understand, how to handle disclosure scenarios in the CPSA, specific NHS Never Events, medication reconciliation, and the professional and legal duties that sit beneath all of it.

Why Patient Safety Is Central to the UKMLA Blueprint

The MLA Content Map groups competencies into broad clinical domains, but patient safety cuts across every one of them. The GMC's decision to make the UKMLA the gateway to independent practice reflects a specific policy concern: that junior doctors entering the NHS must be able to recognise when things go wrong, respond proportionately, and communicate honestly — not just pass written exams about clinical knowledge.

In practical terms, the AKT regularly presents prescribing scenarios where you must identify what has gone wrong (wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong route, drug interaction, contraindication) and choose the correct response. It also presents near-miss scenarios — situations where harm did not reach the patient — and tests whether you know the appropriate reporting pathway. In the CPSA, patient safety appears most visibly in communication stations where a patient or relative asks about an error that has occurred or nearly occurred.

Understanding patient safety well gives you marks in multiple domains simultaneously. It is one of the highest-leverage revision areas in the entire UKMLA, and one where preparation in this topic pays dividends across many question types.

Prescribing Errors: The Full Classification

A prescribing error is defined as a clinically meaningful error in a prescription that results — or could result — in harm to a patient if the prescription is administered as written. The UK definition, published in a landmark study by Dornan et al. for the GMC, identifies prescribing errors as distinct from transcription errors, dispensing errors, and administration errors, though all four types are tested in the AKT.

The Dornan study found that one in every eight drug prescriptions written in UK teaching hospitals contained an error, and that foundation doctors were responsible for the highest error rate of any grade. This is why the GMC places such emphasis on safe prescribing at this exact career transition point.

High-Risk Medications the AKT Specifically Tests

The AKT draws disproportionately from high-risk medicines associated with serious harm when errors occur. These are sometimes called SALAD drugs (Sound-Alike Look-Alike Drugs), high-alert medications, or Never Events when their misuse causes serious harm.

Anticoagulants: Warfarin, heparin (unfractionated and low molecular weight), and DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are among the commonest causes of drug-related hospital admission. AKT questions test: when anticoagulation is contraindicated (active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia); dose adjustment in renal impairment (all DOACs differ — dabigatran is renally cleared most heavily, with absolute contraindication below eGFR 30; rivaroxaban requires dose reduction below eGFR 50 for AF indication); and reversal agents (idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors, vitamin K and four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate for warfarin).

Insulin: Insulin errors are a Never Event category. The AKT tests: appropriate selection of insulin type (short-acting, intermediate, long-acting, mixed); management of hypoglycaemia; sick day rules for type 1 diabetes; management of diabetic ketoacidosis — particularly the variable rate insulin infusion (VRII) protocol; and the critical rule that "units" must never be abbreviated to "U" (can be misread as a zero, causing tenfold dose errors).

Opioids: Morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, and codeine questions focus on: dose equivalence when converting between opioids (oral morphine equivalent is the standard unit); dose reduction in renal and hepatic impairment (morphine-6-glucuronide accumulates in renal failure — use oxycodone or fentanyl instead); recognising opioid toxicity (miosis, respiratory depression, reduced GCS); naloxone as reversal (titrated carefully to avoid acute withdrawal precipitating a pain crisis); and legal requirements for controlled drug prescriptions (indelible ink, written in words and figures, full patient details, prescriber's address).

Methotrexate: The classic AKT trap. Methotrexate for rheumatoid arthritis and psoriasis is prescribed once weekly, not once daily. A once-daily prescription is a potentially fatal error — this is one of the most commonly tested prescribing safety scenarios in the AKT. Also tested: folic acid co-prescription (reduces adverse effects), monitoring requirements (FBC, LFTs, U&Es — check before each prescription), and interactions with NSAIDs and trimethoprim (both increase methotrexate toxicity by reducing renal clearance).

Lithium: Narrow therapeutic index, requiring therapeutic drug monitoring (target range 0.4–1.0 mmol/L for maintenance, checked at least every six months in stable patients). Toxicity (coarse tremor, confusion, ataxia, cardiac arrhythmias) is exacerbated by dehydration, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, and thiazide diuretics — all common AKT questions. Lithium is teratogenic (Ebstein's anomaly risk) and must be withdrawn or switched in early pregnancy.

Digoxin: Another narrow therapeutic index drug. Toxicity (nausea, xanthopsia — yellow-green visual halos, bradyarrhythmias, AV block) is precipitated by hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypercalcaemia, and renal impairment. The AKT expects you to manage these interactions and recognise toxicity patterns on ECG (non-specific ST scooping in therapeutic use; AV block and ectopics in toxicity).

Aminoglycosides: Gentamicin and amikacin are weight-based, once-daily dosing antibiotics with ototoxic and nephrotoxic potential. The AKT tests: weight-based dosing calculation; pre-dose (trough) and post-dose (peak) level monitoring; the Hartford nomogram for dosing adjustment; and the principle that gentamicin levels must be checked before the third or fourth dose in most protocols, or sooner in renal impairment.

NHS Never Events: What They Are and Why They Matter

A Never Event is a serious, largely preventable patient safety incident that should not occur if the available preventative measures have been implemented. NHS England maintains a list of Never Events — these are incidents so serious and so preventable that their occurrence signals a fundamental failure of safety systems, not just individual error.

The Never Events most relevant to the AKT include:

  • Wrong site surgery: Operating on the wrong limb, the wrong side, or the wrong patient. Prevention: WHO Surgical Safety Checklist (sign-in, time-out, sign-out).
  • Retained foreign object after surgery: A surgical swab, instrument, or guidewire left inside a patient. Prevention: count checks before and after every surgical procedure.
  • Misidentification of nasogastric tube placement: A nasogastric tube placed in the trachea or bronchus, confirmed by X-ray misinterpretation, resulting in aspiration of feed. Prevention: pH testing of aspirate (pH ≤5.5 confirms gastric placement); X-ray confirmation only when pH testing is equivocal.
  • IV administration of oral/enteral medications: Oral liquid medication accidentally administered intravenously. Prevention: oral syringes designed to be incompatible with IV cannulas (ENFit connectors).
  • IV potassium chloride administered in error: Concentrated potassium chloride solution given undiluted IV can cause fatal cardiac arrest. Prevention: removal of concentrated potassium from clinical areas; dilution protocols.
  • Insulin errors causing death or harm: Including 10-fold dose errors from "U" abbreviation, wrong insulin type, or omission in DKA management.
  • Overdose of anticoagulant: Including wrong DOAC dose, failure to check renal function before prescribing, or wrong reversal agent choice.

The AKT sometimes presents Never Event scenarios to test whether you recognise the appropriate immediate response (stop the harmful process, assess the patient, call for help, escalate, complete an incident report) and whether you understand the systemic preventive measure that the Never Event framework identifies.

Medication Reconciliation: A High-Risk Transition Point

Medication reconciliation is the process of comparing a patient's complete list of current medications against new prescriptions at care transitions — specifically at hospital admission, inpatient ward transfer, and hospital discharge. It is a major source of preventable patient harm and a recurring AKT topic.

The key transitions where the AKT tests medication reconciliation are:

Hospital admission: The admitting doctor must obtain an accurate medication history from the patient, their GP summary, community pharmacy dispensing records, and — where available — the Summary Care Record. Errors at this stage include: omitting a regular medication (omission error); continuing a medication that should be stopped (e.g., anticoagulants before surgery, metformin before contrast CT); or failing to identify a new allergy that has developed since the last admission.

NIL BY MOUTH and surgical preparation: When a patient is made nil by mouth, important decisions must be made about which regular medications to continue (e.g., antiepileptics, antihypertensives, beta-blockers) and which to withhold (e.g., oral hypoglycaemics, lithium). The AKT tests these decisions directly. General principles: most antihypertensives are continued (exception: ACE inhibitors and ARBs are often withheld on the day of major surgery); antiepileptics are always continued (risk of seizure); lithium is withheld before major surgery due to fluid shift risks; anticoagulants are managed according to bleeding risk and the specific VTE or thrombotic risk of the procedure.

Discharge: Medication reconciliation at discharge involves ensuring that the patient's take-home medications match their intended ongoing treatment plan, that any changes made during the admission are communicated to the GP, and that the patient understands which medications are new, which are changed, and which have been stopped. The AKT tests the content of a good discharge summary and the obligation to communicate medication changes clearly.

Contributing Factors to Prescribing Errors

The AKT does not just ask you to identify errors — it sometimes presents a scenario where you must recognise the systemic factors that contributed to one. This reflects the NHS patient safety culture shift from individual blame to systems thinking, championed by bodies like the Healthcare Safety Investigation Branch (HSIB) and encoded in NHS England's Patient Safety Incident Response Framework (PSIRF).

Factors the AKT acknowledges as contributing to prescribing errors include:

  • Cognitive load and interruptions: Prescribing during a busy ward round, while fatigued, or while being interrupted increases error rates. Recognise when to pause, seek a quiet environment, or ask a senior to double-check a complex prescription.
  • Illegible handwriting: The NHS has moved substantially to electronic prescribing, but handwritten drug charts still exist. Illegible dose, drug name, or frequency is a prescribing hazard that every doctor must take seriously.
  • Non-standard abbreviations: QDS, BD, TDS, OD are acceptable. But "U" for units, "mcg" versus "μg" on handwritten charts, and informal drug name abbreviations have caused serious errors.
  • Incomplete patient information: Not knowing a patient's allergy status, renal function, weight, or current medications before prescribing is a systems failure as much as an individual one.
  • Handover failures: Medications omitted during admission, ward transfer, or discharge are a leading cause of omission errors. Structured handover tools (SBAR — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) reduce this risk.
  • Electronic prescribing alert fatigue: Paradoxically, electronic prescribing systems that generate too many interaction alerts cause clinicians to override all alerts reflexively, reducing the safety benefit of the alerts themselves. The AKT acknowledges this as a systems issue.

Near-Miss Reporting: Systems You Must Know

A near miss is an error that reached the patient but caused no harm, or an error caught before it reached the patient. Near misses are at least as important as actual harms from a learning perspective — they expose system weaknesses before someone is hurt.

Datix: The most common NHS incident reporting system. When a near miss, adverse event, or patient safety incident occurs, complete a Datix incident report. The AKT may present a scenario asking what you should do — completing a Datix report is almost always the correct answer, typically after ensuring immediate patient safety.

The Yellow Card Scheme (MHRA): For reporting suspected adverse drug reactions (ADRs) — unexpected effects of a drug itself, rather than a prescribing error. The MHRA Yellow Card is especially important for new medicines (black triangle ▼ medicines require reporting of all suspected ADRs, not just serious ones). All healthcare professionals and patients can submit Yellow Cards.

SHOT (Serious Hazards of Transfusion): The UK haemovigilance scheme for transfusion-related incidents. Wrong blood in a tube, ABO-incompatible transfusion, and transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI) all require a SHOT report. This is separate from a Datix report — both may be needed.

RIDDOR (Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations): Applies to occupational health incidents — needle-stick injuries, chemical exposure, workplace accidents — not to medication errors or patient safety incidents.

Learn from Patient Safety Events (LFPSE): The NHS England national database that replaced the National Reporting and Learning System (NRLS). Serious incidents are escalated here for national learning. Trusts report automatically; individual clinicians generally report through Datix, which feeds into LFPSE.

Duty of Candour: The Legal and Professional Framework

The duty of candour is both a professional and statutory obligation. For the UKMLA, it appears most directly in CPSA communication scenarios involving disclosure, but it also underpins correct answers to many AKT ethics questions.

Professional duty of candour (GMC): Good Medical Practice states that when things go wrong, doctors must be open and honest with patients, offer an apology, explain what happened, and explain what will be done to prevent recurrence. This applies even when the doctor was not personally responsible for the error.

Statutory duty of candour (Regulation 20, Health and Social Care Act 2008): This applies to NHS trusts and independent providers. When a patient experiences a "notifiable safety incident" (unintended or unexpected incident resulting in death, severe harm, moderate harm, or prolonged psychological harm), the provider must notify the patient or representative, apologise, and provide a written account.

Key points for the UKMLA:

  • Apologise even if you are not certain who was at fault
  • An apology is not an admission of legal liability (Compensation Act 2006)
  • Do not wait for a formal investigation before disclosing that something went wrong
  • Document the conversation clearly in the notes, including date, time, who was present, and what was said
  • Inform your senior or supervisor as soon as a significant error is identified
  • Do not attempt to hide, alter, or minimise a patient safety incident — doing so constitutes dishonesty and is a fitness-to-practise matter

Handling Patient Safety in CPSA Communication Stations

The CPSA includes communication stations that directly test patient safety disclosure and apology. These are among the most challenging stations because they require not just knowledge but emotional attunement, clear structure, and genuine empathy under time pressure.

Typical CPSA patient safety scenarios include: a patient who received the wrong medication; a relative asking about a delay in diagnosis; a patient asking why they were not told about a known complication before a procedure; or a colleague asking you to countersign a document you know is inaccurate.

The CPSA mark scheme rewards:

Opening: Establish rapport, confirm who you are talking to, clarify what they already know, and set the agenda. Do not launch into explanation before you understand what the patient wants from the conversation. A simple "Before I explain what happened, can I ask — what have you already been told?" is high-value in these scenarios.

Explanation: Use plain English, avoid jargon, and give a factual account of what is known. Do not speculate beyond the evidence. Use pauses and check understanding ("Does that make sense so far?").

Acknowledgement and apology: Say sorry clearly and specifically — "I am very sorry that this happened to you." This is not an admission of legal liability. Avoid defensive language like "mistakes happen" or "these things are always a risk."

What happens next: Explain what steps have been or will be taken — incident report filed, senior informed, investigation under way if appropriate, clinical management of any harm caused.

Support offered: Offer written information, a follow-up appointment, a complaints pathway (PALS — Patient Advice and Liaison Service), and independent advocacy if appropriate.

Documentation: Tell the patient that you will document this conversation and provide a written summary if they wish.

Common mistakes: excessive apologising without explaining what happened; explaining what happened without apologising; over-promising certainty about outcomes; failing to offer a clear next step; becoming defensive when the patient expresses anger; and leaving without checking if the patient has further questions.

The Professional Pathway When You Witness an Error

The AKT presents scenarios where you observe — but did not cause — a patient safety incident. The correct response follows a clear pathway:

  1. Immediate patient safety first: If the patient is in immediate danger, act first — stop an incorrect infusion, call for help, administer reversal agents if trained to do so.
  2. Inform the patient: Subject to clinical state, the patient should be told as soon as it is safe to do so. Apology should come from the most senior clinician available.
  3. Inform your senior: Report to your supervisor promptly. Do not attempt to manage a significant incident alone, and do not collude with concealment.
  4. Complete a Datix report: Within the timeframe specified by your trust's policies. Datix reports are learning tools, not disciplinary documents.
  5. Document accurately: Write a contemporaneous, factual account in the patient's notes. Never alter existing entries — mark late additions clearly with date, time, and signature.
  6. Seek support: Involvement in a patient safety incident is distressing. Access occupational health, pastoral support, or speak to your educational supervisor.

Vulnerable Populations and Specific Prescribing Considerations

Paediatric prescribing: All doses in children must be calculated per kilogram of body weight, using the BNF for Children (BNFC). Using adult doses in children — or using a child's dose in a small adult — is a serious error. Neonates, infants, and older children are pharmacologically distinct groups; metabolism (particularly hepatic) and renal clearance differ substantially from adults and from each other.

Elderly patients: Polypharmacy (five or more regular medications) is extremely common in older patients and is a major driver of drug interactions, falls, and adverse events. The STOPP/START tool (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions / Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment) and the Beers criteria are used to identify inappropriate prescriptions in older adults. Renal function declines with age — always check eGFR before prescribing renally-cleared drugs in elderly patients, and remember that creatinine-based eGFR may overestimate renal function in sarcopenic elderly patients.

Pregnant and breastfeeding women: Absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy: methotrexate (teratogenic, abortifacient), valproate (neural tube defects, neurodevelopmental harm — MHRA Valproate Pregnancy Prevention Programme), retinoids (isotretinoin, acitretin — severe teratogenicity), warfarin (first trimester — embryopathy; near term — fetal haemorrhage), tetracyclines (tooth discolouration, bone effects in fetus), quinolones (animal evidence of cartilage toxicity), NSAIDs in third trimester (premature closure of ductus arteriosus), ACE inhibitors and ARBs (fetotoxicity in second and third trimester — oligohydramnios, renal tubular dysgenesis).

Patients with renal impairment: Key absolute contraindications below specific eGFR thresholds: metformin below eGFR 30 (lactic acidosis risk); NSAIDs below eGFR 30 (further nephrotoxicity, fluid retention); dabigatran below eGFR 30 (NICE guidance); gadolinium MRI contrast below eGFR 30 (risk of nephrogenic systemic fibrosis with older macrocyclic agents — newer agents have better safety profiles but caution remains).

Patient Safety Culture: The GMC and NHS Policy Context

The UKMLA's patient safety content reflects a broader NHS safety culture policy shift. Key documents the AKT expects familiarity with:

An Organisation with a Memory (2000, DoH): Established the evidence base for learning from patient safety incidents and created the National Patient Safety Agency (NPSA). Its core insight — that individual blame suppresses reporting and prevents systemic learning — underpins all modern NHS patient safety policy.

The Francis Report (2013): Following the Mid Staffordshire NHS Foundation Trust scandal, this report's recommendations shaped the duty of candour regulations and the culture of speaking up as a professional obligation. The report's central finding — that a culture of deference to hierarchy prevented staff from raising concerns about unsafe care — directly informs the UKMLA's emphasis on escalation and whistleblowing.

Freedom to Speak Up (2015): Led to the creation of Freedom to Speak Up Guardians in every NHS trust. The UKMLA expects you to know that every trust has a Freedom to Speak Up Guardian, that raising concerns through them is a protected activity, and that you cannot be penalised for raising a legitimate patient safety concern in good faith. Section 43B of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (Public Interest Disclosure Act) provides legal protection for whistleblowers.

NHS Patient Safety Strategy (2019): Sets the national ambition to reduce avoidable harm in the NHS by 50% by 2025. Introduces the Patient Safety Incident Response Framework (PSIRF) — the framework for how NHS trusts investigate and respond to patient safety incidents, replacing the previous Serious Incident Framework.

AKT Question Patterns on Patient Safety

Recognising recurring AKT question structures helps you approach patient safety questions efficiently under timed conditions:

"What should you do first?" pattern: A drug error has just been discovered. Options ask you to rank five responses. Correct approach: ensure immediate patient safety → inform the patient → inform your senior → complete an incident report. Completing a Datix report is correct but not first.

"Is this prescription safe?" pattern: You are shown a drug chart or prescription and asked whether it is appropriate. Apply knowledge of contraindications, dose ranges, drug interactions, and monitoring requirements. These questions test pharmacology and professional practice simultaneously.

"What does the patient have a right to know?" pattern: Ethics questions dressed as patient safety questions. The correct answer almost always involves honesty, transparency, and the duty of candour — particularly when the patient is asking directly about what happened.

"How should you escalate?" pattern: A colleague repeatedly makes a prescribing error, or an unsafe environment puts patients at risk. The correct escalation pathway: raise concerns with your direct supervisor first; then clinical governance channels; ultimately — if all else fails — the GMC or the appropriate regulatory body. Freedom to Speak Up Guardian is another avenue.

Frequently Asked Questions About Patient Safety in the UKMLA

Does the AKT test specific drug doses or just principles?
Both. The AKT includes dose-range questions for common drugs (particularly antibiotics, analgesics, and anticoagulants), but the more common pattern is identifying whether a prescription is appropriate for the given clinical context — which is a principles question. The BNF is your authoritative reference for doses, and the AKT expects you to know how to use it.

Is the duty of candour relevant to the AKT or only the CPSA?
Both. In the AKT it appears in ethics and professional practice questions. In the CPSA it is the central theme of disclosure communication stations. Candidates who understand the professional and statutory framework score well in both.

What should I do if my senior tells me not to report an incident?
Your professional obligation to report a significant patient safety incident does not disappear because a senior asks you not to. Escalate to the clinical governance team, the medical director, or — in serious cases — the GMC. The Freedom to Speak Up Guardian is another avenue. Suppressing an incident report at a senior's request puts you in professional and potentially legal difficulty.

Can apologising to a patient be used against me in a negligence claim?
No. The Compensation Act 2006 clarifies that an apology does not constitute an admission of liability. Clinicians are actively encouraged by the GMC, NHS England, and medical defence organisations to apologise when things go wrong. Failing to apologise when appropriate is more likely to generate or inflame a complaint.

What is the difference between a serious incident and a Datix report?
A Datix report can be filed for any incident, near miss, or unsafe condition — most will be low or moderate severity. A Serious Incident is a specific NHS category for events meeting defined severity thresholds (death, severe harm, Never Events) triggering a formal Root Cause Analysis (RCA). All SIs generate Datix reports, but not all Datix reports become SIs.

How does the Datix system work in practice?
Datix is an online form completed by any member of staff who witnesses or is involved in a patient safety incident. The form captures: who was involved, what happened, when and where, what the outcome was, what immediate actions were taken, and what you believe caused the incident. It is not a blame document — NHS policy explicitly states that staff should not face disciplinary action for filing a Datix report in good faith. The report is reviewed by the clinical governance team, categorised by severity, and used to identify patterns that inform quality improvement.

What is root cause analysis and when is it used?
Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is a structured investigation technique used for serious incidents. It seeks to identify the underlying systemic causes — not just the immediate trigger — of a patient safety event. RCA uses tools like the "five whys" (asking "why?" repeatedly to drill down from surface cause to root cause) and the fishbone/Ishikawa diagram (categorising causes by system: staff, environment, equipment, communication, policies). The UKMLA does not require detailed knowledge of RCA methodology, but does expect you to understand that serious incidents trigger formal investigation rather than informal review.

Revision Strategy for Patient Safety in the UKMLA

Patient safety content in the UKMLA is best revised in three integrated layers:

Layer 1 — Professional framework: Know Good Medical Practice, the duty of candour, Freedom to Speak Up, and the principle of systems thinking in patient safety. These provide the ethical architecture for all the clinical content.

Layer 2 — Reporting systems: Datix for ward incidents, Yellow Card for suspected ADRs, SHOT for transfusion reactions, RIDDOR for occupational incidents, and the escalation pathway for serious concerns about a colleague or environment. Know which system applies to which situation.

Layer 3 — Clinical prescribing safety: High-risk drugs, dose adjustment in renal and hepatic impairment, common drug interactions, Never Events, medication reconciliation, and the safe prescribing checklist. This requires systematic revision by drug class, using the BNF and a question bank.

Use a question bank to practise prescribing scenarios regularly throughout your revision period. Patient safety questions reward candidates who have internalised the professional framework and the clinical content simultaneously.

Conclusion: Patient Safety Is Not Revision — It Is Practice

The UKMLA's emphasis on patient safety reflects a core reality of early medical practice: you will make mistakes, and how you respond to them matters as much as whether you made them. The assessment tests not just knowledge of prescribing rules but the professional habits of mind — vigilance, transparency, appropriate escalation, and honest communication — that distinguish a safe foundation doctor from an unsafe one.

Revise the clinical content methodically. But also recognise that patient safety in the UKMLA is not a separate topic — it is the lens through which every clinical question is ultimately asking: if you were this patient's doctor, would they be safe?

High-Risk Clinical Situations: When Patient Safety Vigilance Is Most Critical

Certain clinical transitions and environments carry disproportionately high patient safety risk and are specifically referenced in UKMLA preparation guidance. Being aware of these contexts helps you orient your clinical vigilance appropriately during foundation training and revision.

Night shifts and out-of-hours periods: Adverse events are more common during nights and weekends, reflecting reduced staffing, reduced senior supervision, and greater cumulative clinician fatigue. Foundation doctors on night shifts are making clinical decisions with less immediate senior support. The correct response is not to manage independently beyond your competency level — it is to escalate promptly and appropriately, even at the cost of waking a senior. Patient safety outweighs social discomfort every time.

Emergency department environments: High patient volume, time pressure, multiple simultaneous tasks, and frequent interruptions combine to create conditions where prescribing and clinical errors are most likely. Double-checking calculations, reading allergy bands before prescribing, and using structured handover formats (SBAR) are non-negotiable safety practices in high-pressure environments.

Chemotherapy prescribing: Cancer chemotherapy regimens are among the most complex and high-risk prescription types in clinical medicine. Cytotoxic drugs have narrow therapeutic windows; errors are frequently fatal. The NHS mandates that chemotherapy prescriptions are written by trained, credentialed prescribers only. Foundation doctors should not prescribe cytotoxic agents independently, and the AKT expects you to know this boundary clearly.

Paediatric resuscitation drug dosages: Drug doses in paediatric emergencies are weight-based and differ substantially from adult doses. The Broselow tape (length-based emergency dosing) and Resuscitation Council paediatric advanced life support (PALS) guidelines provide the dose framework. Never estimate paediatric drug doses from memory in an emergency — always verify using the appropriate reference, and call for senior paediatric support immediately.
