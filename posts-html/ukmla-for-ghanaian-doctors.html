The UKMLA for Ghanaian doctors is the single licensing route that MBChB graduates registered with the Medical and Dental Council of Ghana (MDC Ghana) must pass, alongside GMC identity, English-language and primary source verification checks, to gain full GMC registration and work in the NHS. In practical terms, this means sitting the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), verifying your MBChB and internship documents through EPIC/MyIntealth, proving your English proficiency, and paying a defined schedule of GMC fees before you can hold a licence to practise in the United Kingdom.

This guide walks through exactly what a Ghanaian doctor — whether a recent graduate from the University of Ghana Medical School, KNUST School of Medicine and Dentistry, the University of Cape Coast School of Medical Sciences, or another MDC Ghana-accredited institution — needs to know about eligibility, fees, timelines and common pitfalls on the path from MBChB to a GMC number.

Key takeaways

  • Ghanaian MBChB graduates registered with MDC Ghana are eligible for the UKMLA route, but must separately clear GMC identity checks, English testing, and EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification.
  • Ghana's housemanship (internship) is two years, and while GMC registration does not strictly require its completion before sitting the UKMLA, most applicants complete at least part of it first for clinical experience and MDC Ghana permanent registration.
  • Core costs for the 2026 GMC fee schedule are approximately: AKT £283, CPSA £1,036, provisional registration £26, full registration £481, and an English test around £180-£230 — plus travel and UK accommodation for the CPSA, which is sat only in the UK.
  • Realistic end-to-end timelines from starting document verification to a GMC number typically run 9 to 18 months, depending on English test slots, CPSA availability, and how quickly your medical school responds to verification requests.
  • Ghana sits within a wider West African and pan-African IMG landscape on this route — Nigerian, Sudanese and Zimbabwean doctors follow closely related processes, and it is worth comparing notes across these guides.
  • Common avoidable mistakes include leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification too late, underestimating CPSA visa/travel lead time, and misunderstanding which English test exemptions actually apply.

Understanding the UKMLA route for Ghanaian doctors

The UKMLA (UK Medical Licensing Assessment) is the assessment that both UK final-year medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) must pass before the General Medical Council (GMC) will grant registration with a licence to practise. For a Ghanaian doctor, the UKMLA has effectively replaced the older PLAB pathway as the standard licensing exam route into the NHS, though the underlying goal — a GMC number — has not changed.

The exam has two parts: the AKT, a computer-based knowledge test, and the CPSA, a practical, station-based skills assessment. Both are set against the same UK-wide content map used for home graduates, so a Ghanaian MBChB holder is assessed to the identical standard as a UK medical student, not a lower or different one. Who regulates medical training in Ghana: MDC Ghana and the MBChB

The Medical and Dental Council of Ghana (MDC Ghana) is the statutory body that regulates the training, registration and licensing of doctors and dentists in Ghana. Every Ghanaian doctor intending to pursue the UKMLA route needs an MBChB degree from an MDC Ghana-recognised medical school and, in almost all cases, at least provisional MDC Ghana registration.

Ghana's main medical schools include:

  • University of Ghana Medical School (College of Health Sciences, Accra) — one of the country's oldest and largest medical training institutions.
  • KNUST School of Medicine and Dentistry (Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology, Kumasi) — a major training centre for the Ashanti and middle-belt regions.
  • University of Cape Coast School of Medical Sciences — a growing medical school on Ghana's coast.
  • Several newer public and private medical schools accredited by MDC Ghana, including University of Health and Allied Sciences (Ho) and University of Development Studies (Tamale).

MDC Ghana maintains an online registration portal (mdconline.mdcghana.org) where graduates apply for provisional registration as House Officers, and later for permanent registration once housemanship is complete. According to MDC Ghana's own guidance, the Council also runs registration examinations for candidates, including a policy direction toward a common national licensing examination applicable to both Ghana-trained and foreign-trained doctors — a useful parallel to keep in mind, since it mirrors the logic behind the UK's own move to a single UKMLA for home and international graduates.

If you are unfamiliar with how GMC registration works for doctors trained outside the UK generally, our GMC registration requirements for international doctors guide is a useful primer before diving into Ghana-specific detail.

Ghana's housemanship (internship) requirement

Ghana operates a mandatory two-year housemanship for MBChB graduates, a policy MDC Ghana confirmed in 2004 in consultation with the Ministry of Health. During this period, new graduates are provisionally registered with MDC Ghana as House Officers and rotate through accredited hospital placements, typically covering internal medicine, surgery, obstetrics and gynaecology, and paediatrics. Only after completing the full two years is a doctor eligible for permanent MDC Ghana registration as a medical officer (general practitioner).

This is longer than the one-year foundation-style internships used in some neighbouring countries, so Ghanaian applicants planning their UKMLA timeline should budget for it accordingly. Importantly, the GMC does not explicitly require you to have finished Ghana's full two-year housemanship before you sit the UKMLA itself — GMC eligibility criteria are centred on your primary medical qualification, English proficiency and identity verification, not on completing a specific home-country internship length. However:

  • Most Ghanaian doctors choose to complete at least a significant part of housemanship before starting the UKMLA process, both to consolidate clinical skills relevant to the CPSA and because MDC Ghana permanent registration is a valuable credential for later NHS job applications.
  • Some doctors begin English testing and document verification during housemanship, since these processes can run in parallel with clinical duties.
  • Always confirm the GMC's current eligibility position directly on gmc-uk.org, since eligibility rules are reviewed periodically and this guide should not be treated as a substitute for the primary source.

English language requirements

Although Ghana's medical education is delivered in English, the GMC generally still requires evidence of English language proficiency from IMGs unless a specific exemption applies (for example, having completed your entire primary medical qualification in a country where English is the sole official language, subject to GMC's current list and conditions). Most Ghanaian applicants sit either:

  • IELTS Academic — overall band 7.5, with a minimum of 7.0 in each of the four components (listening, reading, writing, speaking).
  • OET Medicine — grade B overall, with a minimum of B in each sub-test.

Test fees for either option are typically in the region of £180-£230, though this varies by test centre and should be confirmed directly with IELTS or OET at the time of booking. Because Ghana's own exemption status can change and is sometimes contested by applicants who assume their English-medium education automatically qualifies them, it's worth checking the GMC's current English language policy in detail rather than assuming an exemption applies.

EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification

Before the GMC will register a Ghanaian doctor, your MBChB qualification and supporting documents must be verified directly with your medical school through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials), operated by Intealth (formerly ECFMG). This is the same primary source verification process used for IMGs from every other country on the UKMLA route, and Intealth/ECFMG publishes specific instructions for applicants whose primary source is the Medical and Dental Council of Ghana, reflecting how Ghanaian medical school records and MDC Ghana registration data are checked.

Because verification depends on your medical school's administrative office responding to requests — and Ghanaian medical schools, like many overseas institutions, can take weeks or months to process these — this step is one of the most common causes of delay in the whole UKMLA journey. Start it as early as possible, ideally as soon as you have decided to pursue the UK route, rather than waiting until after you've booked exams. For a full walkthrough of how this process works across all UKMLA source countries, see our dedicated GMC EPIC verification process explained guide.

Documents checklist for Ghanaian applicants

Before starting your GMC application, gather the following:

  • MBChB degree certificate and full academic transcript from your Ghanaian medical school.
  • Provisional and/or permanent registration certificate from MDC Ghana.
  • A valid passport and additional photo ID as required by GMC identity checking.
  • Evidence of English language proficiency (IELTS Academic or OET Medicine certificate).
  • Certificate of good standing or equivalent confirmation from MDC Ghana, confirming no ongoing fitness-to-practise concerns.
  • Completed EPIC/MyIntealth application, including medical school verification requests.
  • Evidence of any postgraduate training, housemanship completion, or additional clinical experience, if applying to strengthen a later job application.
  • Proof of any name changes (marriage certificate, deed poll, etc.) if your documents do not all match exactly.

The AKT and CPSA: what Ghanaian doctors need to prepare for

The UKMLA has two components, and Ghanaian doctors should prepare differently for each, since the CPSA in particular tests skills and conventions that may differ from Ghanaian clinical training.

AKT: the Applied Knowledge Test

The AKT is a computer-based exam testing applied medical knowledge across the full UK content map — covering presentations, conditions, and clinical skills a newly qualified UK doctor is expected to know. For Ghanaian doctors, the clinical science underpinning most questions will be familiar territory from MBChB training, but two areas commonly need extra focus:

  • UK prescribing conventions — drug names, formularies (the BNF), and dosing practices sometimes differ from what is taught and practised in Ghanaian hospitals.
  • NHS-specific guidance and pathways — NICE guidelines, UK public health screening programmes, and NHS referral pathways are tested in detail and may not mirror Ghana Health Service protocols exactly.

Effective preparation typically combines UKMLA-aligned question banks, the official GMC content map, and structured revision of NICE guidance, alongside past clinical experience from housemanship rotations.

CPSA: the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment

The CPSA is a practical, circuit-based exam of stations testing history-taking, examination, clinical and procedural skills, and communication, including professional and ethical scenarios. This is where many Ghanaian and other IMG candidates report the steepest adjustment, not because of clinical competence, but because of differences in:

  • Consultation style — UK clinical communication places heavy emphasis on shared decision-making, explicit consent language, and structured signposting that may be less formalised in everyday Ghanaian clinical practice.
  • Professionalism and ethics scenarios — GMC Good Medical Practice principles, UK safeguarding frameworks, and NHS complaints/escalation processes are specifically tested and should be studied directly rather than assumed from general clinical ethics training.
  • Station timing and format — the CPSA's tightly timed circuit format rewards structured, rehearsed communication frameworks (such as calgary-cambridge-style consultation models), which benefit from deliberate practice, ideally with peers or a study group.
Tip: many Ghanaian doctors find it valuable to rehearse CPSA-style stations with colleagues who have already gone through the exam, since UK communication expectations are hard to calibrate from textbooks alone.

Preparation resources and timeline

Most successful candidates allow at least three to six months of dedicated preparation, layered on top of clinical duties, using a mix of:

  • UKMLA-specific question banks aligned to the official content map.
  • Structured CPSA station practice, ideally with feedback from someone who has sat the UK exam.
  • Formal courses or study groups, some of which run specifically for African IMGs preparing for the CPSA's UK-specific format.
  • Review of the GMC's own preparation resources and sample materials published on gmc-uk.org.

Step-by-step: the registration process from Ghana to a GMC number

  1. Confirm your MBChB and MDC Ghana registration status. You'll need at least provisional MDC Ghana registration and, ideally, evidence of housemanship progress or completion.
  2. Start EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification early. Request your medical school transcripts and MDC Ghana confirmations as soon as possible — this step frequently takes the longest.
  3. Book and sit your English test. Choose IELTS Academic or OET Medicine based on test centre availability in Ghana and your preferred format; results are typically valid for a set period, so timing matters.
  4. Register with the GMC and pay the provisional registration application fee. This formally opens your GMC application file.
  5. Book and prepare for the AKT. The AKT can often be sat at approved test centres, including some outside the UK, so check current test centre locations for West Africa.
  6. Arrange UK travel and, if needed, a visa for the CPSA. Since the CPSA is currently held only at UK test centres, plan flights, accommodation and visa timelines well in advance — see our UK visa guide for international doctors for the current requirements.
  7. Sit the CPSA in the UK. Build in buffer days either side for travel disruption, and consider combining the trip with networking or hospital visits if your schedule allows.
  8. Submit your full registration application and fee. Once you've passed both AKT and CPSA and all documents are verified, apply for full GMC registration with a licence to practise.
  9. Receive your GMC number and begin job hunting. With full registration secured, you can apply for NHS posts; our relocating to the UK: a practical guide for IMG doctors post covers what comes next, from job offers to actually moving.

Realistic timeline for Ghanaian doctors

No two applications move at exactly the same pace, but a typical realistic timeline looks like this:

  • Months 1-3: Start EPIC/MyIntealth verification, gather documents, book English test.
  • Months 3-5: Sit English test, receive results, register provisionally with the GMC, begin AKT preparation.
  • Months 5-7: Sit and pass the AKT; begin arranging CPSA travel and visa.
  • Months 7-10: Travel to the UK and sit the CPSA (allow for the possibility of a resit if needed).
  • Months 9-12: Complete verification, submit full registration application, receive GMC number.
  • Months 12-18: Secure an NHS post and complete pre-employment checks, visa sponsorship, and relocation.

Overall, most Ghanaian doctors should plan for 9 to 18 months from starting the process to holding a GMC number, and longer still before actually starting an NHS post, depending on how quickly documents are verified and exam slots become available.

CPSA travel and visa considerations

Because the CPSA is currently sat only at UK test centres, every Ghanaian candidate needs a plan for getting to the UK. Key considerations include:

  • Visa route: most candidates travelling solely to sit the CPSA use a Standard Visitor visa, but requirements and processing times change, so check current guidance directly with UK Visas and Immigration before booking flights or exam slots.
  • Financial evidence: visa applications typically require proof of funds and a clear travel purpose, so keep bank statements and your CPSA booking confirmation ready.
  • Timing buffer: book your CPSA slot only once you have a realistic visa timeline, and build in a buffer in case of visa delays or exam rescheduling.
  • Accommodation and logistics: factor in UK accommodation, local transport to the test centre, and the possibility of needing a second trip if a resit is required.

Common mistakes Ghanaian doctors make on this route

  • Leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification until the last minute. Medical school administrative delays are the single biggest cause of lost time — start this the moment you decide to pursue the UK route.
  • Underestimating CPSA travel logistics. Because the CPSA is UK-only, visa delays or missed flights can cost an entire exam cycle; build in generous buffers.
  • Assuming English-medium education automatically exempts you from an English test. Check the GMC's current exemption criteria carefully rather than assuming Ghana's English-medium MBChB training qualifies automatically.
  • Not budgeting for the full cost picture. Focusing only on AKT/CPSA fees and forgetting visa, travel, accommodation and verification charges leads to funding shortfalls mid-process.
  • Treating CPSA preparation like a knowledge exam. The CPSA rewards rehearsed communication structure and UK-specific professionalism awareness, not just clinical knowledge — practise stations, don't just read about them.
  • Not keeping MDC Ghana registration current. Even if you plan to work in the UK, keeping your Ghanaian registration in good standing preserves flexibility and avoids complications with future verification requests.

Understanding the GMC's registration pathways available to Ghanaian doctors

Ghanaian doctors approaching GMC registration should understand that the UKMLA is not the only route into the register, even though it is the most common one for early-career doctors. Broadly, the GMC recognises the UKMLA route (AKT plus CPSA) for doctors seeking standard registration with a licence to practise, and a separate Portfolio Pathway for experienced specialists who can demonstrate postgraduate training and competencies equivalent to a UK specialist or GP through a body of evidence rather than sitting exams.

For most Ghanaian MBChB graduates in the early stages of their career — including those still in housemanship, or newly permanently registered with MDC Ghana — the UKMLA route is the relevant one, since the Portfolio Pathway is designed for doctors with substantial specialist experience, not new graduates. If you later progress into a Ghanaian specialty training programme, for example through the Ghana College of Physicians and Surgeons, and accumulate several years of consultant-level or near-consultant experience, the Portfolio Pathway may become a relevant alternative to sitting the UKMLA at that later career stage. Our broader GMC registration requirements for international doctors guide explains both pathways side by side in more depth.

How Ghana's route compares with other African country routes on this site

Because MDC Ghana's regulatory model — provisional registration, a defined housemanship period, then permanent registration — is broadly similar to the structures used across several African countries, it can help to read this Ghana-specific guide alongside our other African country posts. UKMLA for Nigerian doctors is the closest comparison geographically, since Nigeria's Medical and Dental Council follows a similar provisional-to-permanent registration model with a one-year internship (housemanship), shorter than Ghana's two years. Further afield, UKMLA for Sudanese doctors and UKMLA for Zimbabwean doctors are also worth reading, since all four countries share the common thread of EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification, GMC English-language testing, and a CPSA that must be sat in the UK. Differences tend to show up in local internship length, medical school naming conventions, and how each country's regulator issues certificates of good standing — details worth double-checking against your own country's regulator rather than assuming they match exactly.

Why Ghanaian doctors consider the UKMLA route in the first place

Ghana trains a steady pipeline of MBChB graduates each year across its public and private medical schools, but the country, like much of sub-Saharan Africa, faces a persistent gap between the number of doctors trained and the number retained in the domestic health system long-term. For individual doctors, the decision to pursue GMC registration and NHS employment is rarely about a single factor — it usually reflects some combination of:

  • Career structure. The NHS offers clearly defined foundation and specialty training pathways with transparent progression criteria, which some doctors find easier to plan around than less standardised routes.
  • Postgraduate qualifications. UK Royal College memberships and NHS specialty training are internationally recognised credentials that can support a doctor's career whether they eventually stay in the UK, return to Ghana, or move elsewhere.
  • Exposure to a well-resourced health system. Access to a broader range of diagnostics, subspecialty rotations, and structured supervision is often cited as a draw, independent of pay.
  • Financial considerations. NHS pay bands, while requiring adjustment for UK cost of living, are generally higher in absolute terms than Ghana Health Service salary scales, and this is a legitimate part of many doctors' calculus.
  • Family and lifestyle factors. Some doctors already have family connections in the UK, or are drawn by lifestyle and stability considerations that are highly individual.

None of these factors makes the decision automatic, and as covered above, many doctors reasonably choose to stay in Ghana, particularly if they are established in specialty training, running a private practice, or prioritising proximity to family. The UKMLA route is best understood as one legitimate option among several, not a default path every Ghanaian doctor should assume is right for them.

Financial planning: budgeting realistically for the full journey

Beyond the headline GMC fees listed above, Ghanaian doctors should build a realistic total budget that accounts for every cost category likely to arise between deciding to pursue the UKMLA and actually starting an NHS post. A reasonable planning framework includes:

  • Exam and registration fees: AKT (£283), CPSA (£1,036), provisional registration (£26), full registration (£481) — a combined core total of roughly £1,826, before any resits.
  • English testing: IELTS Academic or OET Medicine, typically £180-£230, plus any preparation courses or repeat sittings if the first attempt doesn't meet the required score.
  • EPIC/MyIntealth verification fees: charged directly by Intealth/ECFMG for processing your credentials — check the current fee schedule on their site, since this is separate from GMC fees.
  • Document and courier costs: notarisation, certified copies, and international courier fees for transcripts and certificates can add up, particularly if documents need to be resent due to processing issues.
  • UK visa fees: a Standard Visitor visa application fee, plus the immigration health surcharge if applicable to your specific visa route, and later a Skilled Worker visa and Certificate of Sponsorship fee once you have an NHS job offer.
  • Travel and accommodation: flights to and from the UK for the CPSA sitting, plus several nights of accommodation, are a cost many candidates underestimate until they start booking.
  • Ongoing annual retention fee: £481 per year with a licence to practise, or £172 without one, once you are registered — a recurring cost separate from the one-off registration fees above.

Treating these as separate budget lines, rather than a single vague "GMC costs" estimate, makes it much easier to save in a structured way and avoid being caught short partway through the process.

Building a competitive NHS application as a Ghanaian doctor

GMC registration is the gateway to NHS employment, not a guarantee of a specific job or specialty, so it pays to plan your first NHS application deliberately rather than assuming registration alone will carry you through. Most Ghanaian doctors enter the NHS either through the Foundation Programme, if they are early in their career without an accepted internship equivalent, or directly into a suitable trust-grade post if their MDC Ghana housemanship and subsequent hospital experience already meet what NHS recruiters look for.

A strong application typically combines an honest, well-structured CV covering your Ghanaian training and any Ghana Health Service experience, thorough interview preparation that demonstrates familiarity with NHS-specific guidelines and multidisciplinary working, and informal contact with Ghanaian or other IMG doctors already established in your target trust. Experience gained in Ghana's hospital system, often covering a wide range of presentations with less subspecialty support than some UK departments, is a genuine strength worth foregrounding in interviews rather than downplaying.

Once registered and working, many Ghanaian doctors spend their first one to two NHS years deliberately building UK references, sitting relevant postgraduate exams, and researching specialty training application cycles before committing to a formal specialty application, rather than applying immediately on arrival. This staged approach tends to produce stronger long-term outcomes than rushing the next step before you have a clear sense of the UK training landscape.

Documents checklist for Ghanaian applicants

Before starting your EPIC/MyIntealth request, collect: your original MBChB degree certificate and official academic transcripts; evidence of MDC Ghana provisional and, once completed, permanent registration; housemanship completion records or a supervisor's letter confirming your current rotation if still serving; a valid passport; passport-style photographs meeting GMC specifications; your English test certificate once sat; and a certificate of good standing from MDC Ghana confirming no unresolved fitness-to-practise concerns. Because MDC Ghana and university administrative offices can take several weeks to issue confirmations, request everything as early as possible rather than waiting until each document is strictly needed for the next step.

Frequently asked questions

Can Ghanaian MBChB graduates sit the UKMLA?

Yes. Ghanaian doctors holding an MBChB from a recognised Ghanaian medical school and registered (provisionally or fully) with the Medical and Dental Council of Ghana can apply for GMC registration and take the UKMLA, provided they also meet the GMC's primary source verification, English language, and identity requirements.

Do I need to complete housemanship in Ghana before applying for the UKMLA?

The GMC does not require you to complete Ghana's two-year housemanship before sitting the UKMLA itself, but most Ghanaian doctors complete at least part of housemanship first, since MDC Ghana permanent registration and clinical experience strengthen both your competence and your later NHS job applications. Always confirm current GMC eligibility rules directly on gmc-uk.org.

How much does the UKMLA cost for Ghanaian doctors in total?

Budget roughly £283 for the AKT, £1,036 for the CPSA, £26 for provisional registration, £481 for full registration, plus an English test (IELTS Academic or OET Medicine) at approximately £180-£230. Together, exam and registration fees alone typically total around £1,800-£2,100, excluding travel, accommodation and visa costs, based on the GMC fee schedule effective 1 April 2026.

What English language test do Ghanaian doctors need for GMC registration?

Most Ghanaian applicants need IELTS Academic (overall 7.5, minimum 7.0 in each component) or OET Medicine (grade B overall, minimum B in each sub-test), unless they qualify for an automatic exemption. Because Ghana's medical training is conducted in English, some applicants may query whether they need a test at all, but the GMC generally still requires evidence unless a specific exemption applies, so check gmc-uk.org for the current policy.

Do Ghanaian doctors need to use EPIC or MyIntealth for primary source verification?

Yes. The GMC requires primary source verification of your MBChB and medical school credentials through EPIC/MyIntealth (formerly ECFMG), the same process used for IMGs from other countries. ECFMG publishes Ghana-specific instructions for applicants of the Medical and Dental Council of Ghana, so allow extra lead time to request transcripts and confirmations from your medical school.

Where do Ghanaian doctors sit the CPSA?

The CPSA is currently held only at UK test centres, so Ghanaian candidates must travel to the UK to sit it. This means budgeting for a visit visa (or aligning travel with another visa route), flights, and UK accommodation on top of the exam fee itself.

Is the UKMLA harder for Ghanaian doctors than for UK medical students?

The exam content and pass standard are identical for everyone, but IMGs including Ghanaian doctors often need more preparation time for the CPSA's UK-specific communication and consultation style, and for AKT content areas like UK prescribing conventions and NHS-specific guidelines that differ from Ghanaian clinical training.

How long does the whole process take from Ghana to a GMC number?

Most Ghanaian doctors should realistically plan for 9 to 18 months from starting document verification to receiving full GMC registration, depending on English test scheduling, CPSA availability, and how quickly EPIC/MyIntealth verification is completed by your medical school and MDC Ghana.

Can I keep my MDC Ghana registration while working in the UK?

Many Ghanaian doctors retain their MDC Ghana registration for future flexibility, though requirements for maintaining it (such as continuing professional development or periodic renewal) are set by MDC Ghana, not the GMC. Confirm current retention rules directly with MDC Ghana before you travel.

What is the difference between the UKMLA route and PLAB for Ghanaian doctors?

PLAB was the previous two-part route for IMGs; the UKMLA (AKT plus CPSA) has now replaced it as the single unified licensing assessment for both UK medical students and IMGs, including Ghanaian doctors. The overall goal, GMC registration, is the same, but the exam structure, fee schedule and content map now follow the UKMLA framework.

Do I need a visa to sit the CPSA in the UK?

Most Ghanaian candidates need a valid UK visa to travel for the CPSA, typically a Standard Visitor visa unless they already hold a different UK visa. Check current UK Visas and Immigration requirements well ahead of booking, since processing times and documentary requirements can change.

Should a Ghanaian doctor stay in Ghana or pursue the UKMLA route to the UK?

This depends on personal priorities: staying in Ghana offers faster full registration, lower upfront cost and proximity to family, while the UKMLA route to the NHS offers structured training, internationally recognised qualifications and generally higher pay, at the cost of significant upfront fees, travel, and time away from home. Many doctors complete housemanship in Ghana first, then pursue UKMLA registration.
