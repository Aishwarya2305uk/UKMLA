Breaking bad news CPSA stations are among the most consistently examined — and most consistently misjudged — communication scenarios in the entire UK Medical Licensing Assessment. Whether the news concerns a new cancer diagnosis, an unexpected abnormal scan, or a life-limiting prognosis, examiners are testing something far more specific than sympathy: a learnable, structured approach to delivering difficult information safely, clearly, and with genuine regard for the patient in front of you. This guide sets out exactly what the breaking-bad-news station tests, why it recurs across almost every specialty rather than sitting inside psychiatry alone, the SPIKES framework examiners expect you to demonstrate, the scenario types you are likely to meet, the mistakes that cost the most marks, and a realistic practice plan for both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for PLAB 2.

Unlike a single-system physical examination or a focused history-taking task, a breaking-bad-news station cannot be passed through memorised facts alone. It rewards candidates who can hold structure and empathy simultaneously — pacing the conversation, reading the patient's reaction, and adapting in real time. This is precisely why it appears, in one form or another, in cardiology, respiratory medicine, oncology, obstetrics, general practice, and surgical stations, not only in psychiatric ones. If you have already read our overview of what the CPSA actually tests or worked through the clinical examination stations, this post picks up where those leave off, focusing entirely on the breaking-bad-news station type as its own distinct communication-skills category. It sits alongside — but is meaningfully different from — the general run-through of the MLA content map and the wider UKMLA preparation guide, both of which touch on communication skills only briefly.

Quick Answer: What Does a Breaking Bad News CPSA Station Actually Test?

A breaking bad news CPSA station tests your ability to deliver difficult or unexpected clinical information to a patient (or relative) using a structured, patient-centred framework — commonly the SPIKES protocol — while demonstrating empathy, checking the patient's understanding, avoiding medical jargon, and giving the patient space to react before moving forward. Examiners are not simply marking whether you said the right words; they are marking whether you built the conversation in the right order, whether you paused when the patient needed you to, and whether the patient left the station with a clear, honest, and compassionately delivered picture of their situation.

This station type recurs across the CPSA circuit because breaking bad news is not a niche skill reserved for oncologists or psychiatrists — every Foundation Year 1 doctor will, at some point in their first year of practice, need to tell a patient or family member something they did not want to hear, a reality explored further in our guide to what the Foundation Programme actually involves. The GMC's Outcomes for Graduates explicitly requires new doctors to communicate effectively in difficult situations, and the CPSA is designed to confirm that this capability exists before a candidate is let loose on the wards. For both UK medical students sitting the CPSA as an integrated final-year OSCE and IMGs sitting PLAB 2 at the GMC's Manchester Clinical Assessment Centre, a breaking-bad-news station should be treated as a near-certainty somewhere across an 18-station circuit, not a remote possibility.

Why Breaking Bad News Is One of the Most Consistently Examined CPSA Skills

Breaking bad news sits at the intersection of three things the GMC cares about most in a newly qualified doctor: safe communication, professionalism under pressure, and genuine patient-centredness. Unlike a technical skill such as venepuncture, there is no single "correct" script — which is exactly why it is so heavily marked on process rather than content alone. A candidate can recite every clinically accurate fact about a diagnosis and still fail the station if they deliver it in a way that overwhelms, confuses, or distresses the patient unnecessarily.

Three factors explain why this station type recurs so reliably across UK clinical assessments:

  • It cannot be faked with rote learning. Unlike drug doses or examination sequences, a breaking-bad-news conversation must be built moment-to-moment in response to what the patient says and how they react — examiners can immediately spot a candidate who is running through a memorised script rather than actually listening.
  • It maps directly onto real clinical risk. Poorly delivered bad news is a genuine, well-documented source of patient harm and complaint in the NHS — patients who leave a consultation confused about their diagnosis, or who feel their concerns were dismissed, are more likely to disengage from follow-up care or lose trust in their treating team.
  • It is specialty-agnostic. Because every specialty eventually delivers difficult news — an abnormal biopsy in general surgery, a new arrhythmia diagnosis in cardiology, a stillbirth risk in obstetrics, a terminal prognosis in respiratory medicine — the GMC treats it as a core Foundation-level competency rather than a niche skill confined to psychiatry or palliative care.

This is also why breaking bad news deserves its own dedicated preparation, distinct from general communication-station practice. A candidate who has only rehearsed history-taking frameworks often reverts, under pressure, to a rapid-fire question-and-answer style that is completely wrong for a bad-news scenario — the two skills need to be trained separately. The psychiatric variant of this station is covered in more depth in our guide to psychiatry communication stations, but the version of breaking bad news discussed in this guide applies equally to medical, surgical, and obstetric scenarios.

The SPIKES Protocol: A Structured Framework for Breaking Bad News

The SPIKES protocol is the most widely taught and internationally recognised structured framework for breaking bad news in clinical practice, originally developed for oncology consultations and now used across UK medical education as the default model for any breaking-bad-news scenario. SPIKES stands for Setting, Perception, Invitation, Knowledge, Emotions, and Strategy/Summary — six sequential steps that, taken together, transform an inherently difficult conversation into a structured, learnable clinical skill. Examiners assessing a breaking bad news CPSA station are, in effect, checking whether each of these six steps was present, in roughly the right order, and executed with genuine attention to the patient's reactions.

Step 1: Setting — Preparing the Physical and Interpersonal Environment

The first step is to prepare the setting before a single word of bad news is spoken. In a CPSA station, this means acknowledging the environment even within the constraints of an eight-minute circuit: ensuring privacy, sitting down rather than standing over the patient, making eye contact, silencing distractions, and checking who else the patient would like present. Examiners specifically look for candidates who take a moment to set the scene rather than launching straight into clinical content the instant they enter the room.

A strong opening for this step sounds like: "Thank you for coming in today. Before we start, is it alright if we sit down together? Would you like anyone else to be here with you for this conversation?" This single opening move signals to the examiner that you understand the conversation is about to be difficult, and that you are deliberately creating the conditions for the patient to receive information calmly. It also buys you a brief moment to compose your own approach before the substantive content begins.

Step 2: Perception — Assessing What the Patient Already Knows or Suspects

Before revealing any new information, SPIKES requires you to find out what the patient already understands about their situation. This step is frequently skipped by candidates under time pressure, and it is one of the single biggest differentiators between a pass and a fail in a breaking-bad-news station. Asking a perception-check question achieves several things at once: it tells you how much groundwork you still need to do, it surfaces any misunderstandings you need to correct before adding new information, and — critically — it signals to the patient that this is a two-way conversation rather than a monologue.

Useful perception-checking phrases include: "Before I go through the results, can I check what you understand so far about why we did this test?" or "What has the team told you already about what we were looking for?" A patient's answer to this question should meaningfully shape how you deliver the knowledge step that follows — if they already suspect something serious, you can move more directly; if they have no idea, you need to build up to it more gradually.

Step 3: Invitation — Asking How Much the Patient Wants to Know

Not every patient wants every detail delivered in the same way at the same time, and the invitation step is where you find out how much information this particular patient wants, and how they want it delivered. This might mean asking directly: "Some people want to know every detail of their results and options, while others prefer just the headline and to take things one step at a time. What would be most helpful for you?" Respecting the answer — rather than defaulting to your own preferred style of delivery — is a clear marker of patient-centred communication that examiners are specifically trained to notice.

This step also matters ethically and legally: patients have a recognised right to determine how much information they receive about their own condition, and a doctor who overrides that preference, however well-intentioned, is not practising in a patient-centred way. In a CPSA station, briefly checking the invitation before proceeding to deliver the actual news demonstrates that you understand this principle in practice, not just in theory.

Step 4: Knowledge — Delivering the Information Clearly and Without Jargon

The knowledge step is where the actual bad news is delivered, and it is the step most candidates instinctively rush towards — often skipping steps one to three entirely. SPIKES teaches a specific technique here called the "warning shot": a brief preparatory sentence that signals difficult information is coming, giving the patient a moment to brace before the substantive news lands. A warning shot might sound like: "I'm afraid the results have shown something more serious than we were hoping for." This single sentence, delivered with a pause afterwards, does more work than any amount of subsequent detail — it prevents the news landing as a complete shock and gives the patient a fraction of a second to prepare.

After the warning shot, information should be delivered in small chunks, using plain English rather than medical terminology, and checking understanding after each chunk rather than delivering a single uninterrupted block of clinical detail. Examiners in a CPSA station are listening specifically for jargon-free language — a candidate who says "the biopsy has confirmed a malignancy with likely metastatic spread" has failed this step even if every word is medically accurate, because the patient in front of them may not understand what any of those words mean. The equivalent patient-centred phrasing — "the sample we took has shown cancer cells, and it looks as though it may have spread to other parts of the body" — conveys the same clinical reality in language the patient can actually process.

Step 5: Emotions — Responding to the Patient's Emotional Reaction

The emotions step is where many otherwise well-structured candidates lose marks, because it requires pausing rather than talking. After delivering difficult news, patients react — with silence, tears, anger, denial, or a flood of questions — and the correct response is to acknowledge that reaction before continuing with further information. SPIKES uses the acronym NURSE as a supplementary tool for this step: Naming the emotion ("It looks like this has come as a real shock"), Understanding it ("That's a completely understandable reaction"), Respecting the patient's coping ("You're handling a very difficult conversation"), Supporting them ("We are going to be with you through this, whatever happens next"), and Exploring further ("Can you tell me a bit about what's going through your mind right now?").

Crucially, this step also requires deliberate silence. Sitting with a patient's reaction for a few seconds without immediately filling the space with more clinical information is one of the hardest things to do under exam pressure, but it is exactly what examiners are trained to reward. A candidate who talks over a patient's distress, or who rushes to reassurance before acknowledging the emotion, is marked down even if their words are technically kind.

Step 6: Strategy and Summary — Agreeing Next Steps Together

The final SPIKES step closes the conversation by summarising what has been discussed, checking the patient has understood the key points, and agreeing a concrete plan for what happens next. This might include arranging a further appointment, involving a specialist nurse or multidisciplinary team, providing written information to take away, or simply confirming when the patient will next be seen. Ending a breaking-bad-news station without a clear plan — leaving the patient unsure what happens after they walk out of the room — is a common and costly omission.

A strong closing sounds like: "So just to summarise what we've talked about today: the results have shown [X], and the next step will be for you to see [specialist/team] on [timeframe]. I know this is a lot to take in — is there anything you'd like me to go over again, or any questions on your mind right now?" This final open question is deliberately left broad, giving the patient one more opportunity to raise whatever is actually on their mind, rather than assuming the consultation is complete simply because you have said everything you planned to say.

Common Scenario Types in Breaking Bad News CPSA Stations

Breaking-bad-news stations are deliberately varied across the CPSA circuit precisely because the skill is specialty-agnostic. Recognising the recurring scenario patterns in advance helps you rehearse the right emotional register and content for each, rather than treating every station as identical.

Breaking a New Cancer Diagnosis

This is the archetypal breaking-bad-news scenario and remains one of the most frequently rehearsed in CPSA preparation, whether the underlying case is a breast lump biopsy, a colonoscopy finding, or a lung mass identified on imaging. These stations test whether you can deliver a cancer diagnosis without euphemism (see the common mistakes section below), explain that further staging investigations and specialist referral are needed, and hold space for the intense emotional reaction a cancer diagnosis reasonably provokes — all within an eight-minute window.

Informing a Patient of a Serious Unexpected Finding

Not every breaking-bad-news scenario involves a known life-threatening diagnosis — many involve an unexpected finding discovered incidentally, such as an abnormal chest X-ray revealing a mass unrelated to the presenting complaint, an incidental renal cyst that turns out to be more complex on further review, or blood results revealing a previously undiagnosed chronic condition. These stations specifically test whether you can communicate genuine uncertainty honestly ("we don't yet know exactly what this is, but we do need to investigate it further") without either minimising the finding or catastrophising it before further tests have been done.

Discussing a Life-Limiting Prognosis

Stations involving a life-limiting or terminal prognosis — advanced heart failure, end-stage renal disease, a terminal cancer diagnosis with limited treatment options remaining — test a further layer of skill beyond the initial bad-news delivery: discussing goals of care, sensitively raising what matters most to the patient going forward, and avoiding a false-hope framing that oversells treatment options that are unlikely to be curative. These are often the most emotionally demanding stations in the entire CPSA circuit, and candidates frequently underestimate how much of the mark is earned simply by pausing, listening, and resisting the urge to fill silence with premature reassurance.

Communicating Bad News to a Relative or Next of Kin

A distinct but related scenario type involves delivering bad news not to the patient directly but to a relative — for example, informing a family member that a patient has died unexpectedly, or that a loved one's condition has deteriorated significantly. These stations test an additional skill: managing your own communication when the person receiving the news may direct anger, disbelief, or intense grief at you personally, while still following the same underlying SPIKES structure of a warning shot, checked understanding, and space for emotional reaction.

What Examiners Are Actually Scoring in a Breaking Bad News Station

Understanding the CPSA's dual marking approach — a structured checklist alongside a global rating — is essential for a breaking bad news CPSA station specifically, because the balance between the two shifts markedly compared with, say, a physical examination station. Examiners are scoring far more than the accuracy of what you say.

Structure Over Content

The checklist component typically rewards whether each SPIKES step was present: did the candidate check perception before delivering information; did they give a warning shot; did they check understanding; did they agree a plan. A candidate can be clinically accurate about every fact of the diagnosis and still miss most of the checklist marks if they deliver everything as a single unbroken monologue with no structure.

Empathy as an Observable, Markable Behaviour

Empathy in a CPSA station is not marked as a vague overall impression — it is operationalised as specific, observable behaviours: naming the patient's emotion aloud, using appropriate silence, adjusting tone and pace, and maintaining a warm but professional demeanour throughout. Examiners are trained to distinguish genuine empathic behaviour from performative phrases delivered without matching tone or pacing — saying "I understand this must be difficult" in a flat, rushed voice while already moving on to the next sentence scores poorly, because the words and the delivery contradict each other.

Checking Understanding at Multiple Points

A single check at the very end of the station ("do you have any questions?") is not sufficient. Examiners look for understanding checks woven throughout the conversation — after the warning shot, after any technical term that had to be used, and again before the final summary. This iterative checking is what most reliably distinguishes a candidate who is truly communicating from one who is simply delivering information at the patient.

Avoiding Jargon Without Sounding Patronising

Examiners specifically reward candidates who can translate clinical terminology into plain English without the translation itself sounding condescending. There is a meaningful difference between explaining a diagnosis clearly and over-simplifying it in a way that feels dismissive of the patient's intelligence — the best-scoring candidates find a register that is plain but respectful, checking rather than assuming the patient's level of health literacy.

Giving the Patient Genuine Space to React

Perhaps the single most examiner-visible marker of a strong breaking-bad-news performance is whether the candidate visibly pauses after delivering difficult news, rather than continuing to talk through the patient's reaction. This is deceptively hard to do under exam-room pressure, where silence can feel unbearable to the candidate even though it is exactly what the moment requires — and it is precisely why deliberate practice of this specific pause is one of the highest-yield things a candidate can rehearse before the exam.

Common Candidate Mistakes in Breaking Bad News CPSA Stations

Certain errors recur so consistently across CPSA and PLAB 2 circuits that examiners can often predict, within the first thirty seconds of a station, whether a candidate has genuinely rehearsed a structured approach or is improvising under pressure.

Rushing to Deliver the Diagnosis Before Establishing Perception

The single most common mistake is skipping straight to the knowledge step — delivering the diagnosis in the first sentence after walking in — without first checking what the patient already knows or wants to know. This collapses the SPIKES structure into a single step and removes the patient's opportunity to prepare psychologically for what is coming. Candidates under time pressure often justify this to themselves as being more "efficient," but examiners consistently mark it down as a structural failure rather than an efficiency gain.

Using Euphemisms That Avoid Clarity

A second widespread error is reaching for softening language that obscures rather than clarifies the actual diagnosis — phrases like "there are some concerning cells," "it's not entirely normal," or "we've found something we need to keep an eye on" when the actual finding is a confirmed cancer diagnosis. Patients frequently leave real-world consultations affected by this pattern genuinely unclear about what they have been told, and CPSA examiners are specifically trained to penalise vague euphemistic language that fails to convey the seriousness of the situation honestly. Clarity and kindness are not in tension — the SPIKES warning shot exists precisely to allow both at once.

Failing to Pause After a Difficult Moment

As covered above, continuing to speak immediately after delivering difficult news — rather than allowing even a few seconds of silence — is one of the most common and costly errors. Under exam pressure, silence feels risky to candidates, who often fill it with unnecessary additional clinical detail or premature reassurance. Examiners interpret this pattern as a failure of the emotions step, regardless of how technically accurate the surrounding content is.

Overloading the Patient With Information in One Block

Related to the euphemism problem is its opposite: candidates who, having finally delivered the core diagnosis, then continue talking at length about staging, treatment options, prognosis statistics, and next steps all in one uninterrupted block. SPIKES explicitly calls for information to be delivered in small chunks with checks in between — a single long monologue, even if every fact within it is correct, fails this principle.

Neglecting the Invitation Step Entirely

Many candidates who do remember to check perception still skip the invitation step — assuming, rather than asking, how much detail the patient wants. This is a subtler mistake than the others but is specifically listed on most CPSA checklists as a distinct, separately markable item, and its omission is a common reason a technically well-delivered station still loses marks.

Ending Without a Clear Plan or Final Opportunity for Questions

Stations that run out of time before the strategy and summary step are marked down for an incomplete structure, even if the earlier steps were executed well. Time management within the eight-minute window is therefore itself part of the skill being tested — a candidate needs enough discipline in the earlier steps to leave time for a proper close. Realistic Practice Advice for the Breaking Bad News CPSA Station

Because breaking bad news cannot be mastered through passive reading alone, realistic rehearsal with a study partner is the single most effective preparation method — considerably more effective than reading about the SPIKES protocol without practising it aloud under time pressure.

Rehearse the SPIKES Structure Aloud, Not Just in Your Head

Read the six SPIKES steps and then physically rehearse a full mock station aloud with a study partner playing the patient, using a timer set to eight minutes — the same approach recommended in our broader guide to mock exams and timed practice. Rotate roles so that both partners experience the discipline of staying silent as the "patient" and the discipline of pacing themselves as the "doctor." After each run-through, debrief specifically against each SPIKES step — did the setting step happen, was perception checked, was there a genuine warning shot — rather than giving only a vague overall impression of "that went well" or "that felt awkward."

Practise Scenario Variety, Not Just One Template

Rehearse at least the four scenario types covered above — a new cancer diagnosis, an unexpected serious finding, a life-limiting prognosis, and breaking news to a relative rather than the patient directly — since each requires a slightly different emotional register even though the underlying SPIKES skeleton stays the same. Candidates who only ever rehearse the cancer-diagnosis scenario often freeze when confronted with an unfamiliar variant on exam day, even though the framework they have learned would transfer perfectly well if they recognised the underlying pattern.

Watch for Common Phrase Pitfalls

Build a personal list of euphemisms and jargon you tend to reach for under pressure — common culprits include "it's not great news," "the cells look a bit unusual," and "we need to do some more tests" used as a way of avoiding naming a diagnosis the results have already confirmed — and consciously replace them with direct, plain-English alternatives during every practice run. Recording a mock station on video and reviewing it afterwards is particularly effective for catching these patterns, since most candidates are unaware of their own euphemistic habits until they hear themselves say them back.

Practise the Pause Specifically

Because the emotions step is so consistently under-performed, dedicate specific practice time to nothing but the pause itself: after delivering a warning shot and the core information, count silently to three before speaking again, even if it feels uncomfortable in rehearsal. This is a physical habit that needs deliberate repetition to become automatic under real exam pressure, in the same way a clinical examination sequence needs repetition to become fluent. Candidates who find the nerves of a bad-news scenario particularly overwhelming may also find it useful to read our guide on managing exam-day anxiety, since a racing pulse and dry mouth make the deliberate pause even harder to hold.

Seek Structured Feedback, Not Just a General Reaction

Whether practising with a study partner, a clinical supervisor, or in a formal preparation course, ask specifically for feedback against the SPIKES structure and the common-mistakes list above, rather than a generic "how did that feel?" debrief. IMGs preparing for PLAB 2 in particular benefit from feedback from someone familiar with UK-style CPSA marking conventions, since communication norms and directness expectations can differ meaningfully between healthcare systems — what counts as appropriately direct in one training system may read as insufficiently gentle, or conversely as evasive, under UK CPSA marking criteria.

Build Breaking Bad News Into Every Clinical Placement

UK medical students on clinical placements should ask supervisors directly for the opportunity to observe (and, where appropriate, contribute to, under supervision) real breaking-bad-news conversations, since there is no substitute for having seen the structure applied in genuine clinical practice. IMGs without current UK clinical placement access should instead prioritise structured role-play with peers and, where budget allows, a dedicated CPSA preparation course that includes trained simulated patients for communication stations specifically — not only physical examination stations, as set out in our wider preparation guide for IMGs. Candidates should also confirm their own UKMLA eligibility and booking route in good time, since PLAB 2 sitting availability at the Manchester centre can affect how far in advance a structured practice plan needs to begin.

Breaking Bad News for UK Medical Students vs. IMGs: Key Differences in Preparation

UK medical students typically encounter breaking-bad-news teaching repeatedly across their clinical years, embedded within communication skills modules, and often have some exposure to observing real conversations during clinical placements — meaning their preparation gap is usually about deliberate structured rehearsal under timed conditions rather than unfamiliarity with the underlying concept. IMGs preparing for PLAB 2, by contrast, may be entering the UK system with communication training shaped by a different healthcare culture, and should pay particular attention to the specifically British conventions around directness, patient autonomy, and information-sharing norms that UK examiners are trained to expect — for example, the strong UK emphasis on patient-led pacing (the invitation step) rather than a clinician-led decision about how much the patient should be told.

Both groups benefit from the same core preparation activities — structured SPIKES rehearsal, scenario variety, and specific feedback — but IMGs should budget additional time specifically for adjusting to UK-specific communication norms, ideally through a course or study group that includes UK-trained doctors or examiners who can flag any culturally specific expectations that differ from the candidate's prior training environment. Frequently Asked Questions About Breaking Bad News CPSA Stations

Is breaking bad news only tested in psychiatry stations?

No. While a psychiatric diagnosis such as schizophrenia or dementia is one possible breaking-bad-news scenario, the skill is tested across the CPSA circuit in scenarios drawn from oncology, general surgery, respiratory medicine, cardiology, obstetrics, and general practice. Examiners treat breaking bad news as a specialty-agnostic Foundation-level communication competency, not a niche psychiatric skill, precisely because every new doctor will need to use it regardless of which specialty they eventually work in.

What is the SPIKES protocol used for breaking bad news?

SPIKES is a six-step structured framework for breaking bad news, standing for Setting, Perception, Invitation, Knowledge, Emotions, and Strategy/Summary. It guides a clinician through preparing the environment, checking what the patient already understands, asking how much detail they want, delivering the news clearly with a warning shot, responding to their emotional reaction, and closing with a clear summary and plan. It is the most widely taught breaking-bad-news framework in UK and international medical education and underpins how CPSA examiners assess these stations.

How many CPSA stations involve breaking bad news?

The GMC does not publish an exact number, but candidates should expect at least one dedicated breaking-bad-news or serious-news-communication station across an 18-station CPSA circuit, with related communication and counselling stations appearing elsewhere. Because the skill is specialty-agnostic, elements of it can also surface within stations nominally focused on a different clinical scenario, such as discussing an abnormal investigation result during a general medicine station.

What is the biggest mistake candidates make in breaking bad news stations?

The most common and costly mistake is skipping the perception and invitation steps and moving straight to delivering the diagnosis, followed closely by using euphemisms that obscure rather than clarify the actual finding, and failing to pause after delivering difficult news. Each of these collapses the structured SPIKES approach that examiners are specifically trained to look for, regardless of how clinically accurate the underlying content is.

Do I need to memorise the exact SPIKES acronym word for word?

You do not need to recite the acronym itself during the station — examiners are marking your behaviour, not whether you say the word "SPIKES" aloud. What matters is that your conversation demonstrably follows the underlying structure: preparing the setting, checking perception, inviting the patient's preference, delivering knowledge clearly with a warning shot, responding to emotion, and closing with a summary and plan. Internalising the structure through repeated practice is far more valuable than memorising the letters.

How is breaking bad news marked differently from a physical examination station?

Physical examination stations are weighted more heavily towards a structured checklist of technical steps, with the global rating as a smaller modifying component. Breaking-bad-news stations shift this balance — while a checklist of SPIKES-aligned behaviours is still used, the global rating (reflecting empathy, patient-centredness, and overall communication quality) typically plays a larger role, since the qualities that separate a strong from a weak performance are harder to fully capture in a binary checklist.

Can international communication styles affect my CPSA breaking bad news score?

Communication norms do vary between healthcare training systems, and IMGs should be aware that UK CPSA marking specifically rewards patient-led pacing (checking how much the patient wants to know, rather than a clinician deciding this on the patient's behalf) and explicit emotional acknowledgement. Candidates trained in systems with different conventions around directness or family involvement in bad-news conversations should specifically rehearse the UK-expected structure rather than assuming their prior training style will transfer directly.

Conclusion

Breaking bad news CPSA stations reward structure as much as sentiment: examiners want to see the SPIKES framework genuinely applied — setting the scene, checking perception, inviting the patient's preference, delivering knowledge in a clear warning shot, responding to emotion with real silence, and closing with a concrete plan — rather than a well-intentioned but unstructured expression of sympathy. Both UK medical students and IMGs preparing for PLAB 2 should treat this as a specialty-agnostic core skill worth its own dedicated rehearsal, using timed mock stations, scenario variety, and honest feedback against the common mistakes outlined above.
