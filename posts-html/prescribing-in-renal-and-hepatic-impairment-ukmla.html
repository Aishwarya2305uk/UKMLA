Prescribing in renal impairment UKMLA questions, and their hepatic-impairment counterparts, are among the most reliably high-yield scenarios on the Applied Knowledge Test, precisely because they turn a broad pharmacology topic into a narrow, testable decision: given this patient's kidney or liver function, is this drug safe at the standard dose, safe only at a reduced dose, or not safe at all? Where the site's general pharmacology and prescribing safety guide maps out the full breadth of drug classes tested on the AKT, and the companion patient safety and prescribing errors guide covers what happens when a prescription goes wrong, this article deliberately narrows the lens onto one specific, high-yield sub-topic: how organ impairment changes a prescribing decision, and how to reason through it quickly and correctly under exam conditions.

This is a companion piece, not a replacement, for those two broader guides. It assumes a reader already has a general sense of core drug classes and safe-prescribing principles, and instead builds a dedicated, practical framework for the specific question style that asks, in effect, "this patient's eGFR (or liver function) is X — what do you do with this drug?" Both UK medical students consolidating renal medicine and clinical pharmacology teaching, and international medical graduates (IMGs) meeting the British National Formulary's (BNF) impairment guidance for the first time, will find this guide structured around the same core skill: using a patient's renal or hepatic function practically, rather than as an isolated laboratory number, to make a safe prescribing decision.

Quick Answer: How Do You Decide Whether a Drug Needs Dose Adjustment in Renal or Hepatic Impairment?

Prescribing in renal impairment on the UKMLA is fundamentally a three-way decision: for any given drug and any given level of kidney function, the correct answer is either to prescribe at the standard dose, to prescribe a reduced dose (or extend the dosing interval), or to avoid the drug entirely and choose an alternative. The estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), calculated from serum creatinine, is the practical number used to make this decision at the bedside, and the BNF's dedicated renal impairment appendix is the authoritative reference the AKT expects every candidate to know how to use.

The equivalent hepatic framework works differently in mechanism but identically in structure: rather than a single blood test, the AKT expects candidates to reason using the Child-Pugh classification, a composite clinical and biochemical score that grades the severity of liver disease from A (mild) to C (severe), to decide whether a hepatically metabolised drug should be given at standard dose, reduced dose, or avoided. In both systems, the underlying logic the exam rewards is the same: identify how a drug is cleared from the body, identify how impaired that clearance pathway currently is, and then decide whether the resulting risk of drug or metabolite accumulation is acceptable, manageable with a dose change, or too dangerous to accept at all.

Neither renal nor hepatic dose adjustment is really a memorisation exercise, even though a handful of specific drugs (metformin, nitrofurantoin, NSAIDs, digoxin, opioids) recur often enough in AKT questions that they are worth learning individually. The remainder of this guide works through the eGFR-based framework in detail, the classic renally adjusted or renally avoided drugs, the Child-Pugh concept and its hepatic prescribing implications, and a dedicated FAQ section addressing the specific questions candidates most often have about this topic.

The eGFR-Based Framework for Dose Adjustment in Renal Impairment

Renal impairment matters to prescribing because a very large proportion of drugs, and an even larger proportion of their active or toxic metabolites, are cleared from the body by the kidneys. When renal clearance falls, a drug dosed as though the kidneys were working normally will accumulate — sometimes gradually over days, sometimes dangerously within a single dose — to concentrations well above what was intended. The AKT tests this concept directly and repeatedly, because a foundation doctor who does not routinely check renal function before prescribing a renally cleared drug is a genuine, everyday patient safety risk.

How eGFR and Creatinine Clearance Are Used Practically

The eGFR is an estimate of how much blood the kidneys filter per minute, expressed in millilitres per minute per 1.73m² of body surface area, and it is calculated automatically by most UK laboratories from a patient's serum creatinine, age, sex, and sometimes ethnicity, using a validated equation (commonly the CKD-EPI equation in current UK practice). For the purposes of everyday prescribing decisions, the AKT expects candidates to treat the eGFR as the practical, readily available number to check before prescribing any renally cleared drug, rather than to memorise the underlying equation itself.

Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance is the second calculation candidates should recognise, and it remains important for one specific reason: several drugs' licensed renal dosing bands in the BNF, and in the product literature for certain anticoagulants and antivirals, were originally validated against Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance rather than eGFR, and the BNF explicitly notes where this distinction matters. In practice, this means that for most everyday drugs, using the reported eGFR is entirely appropriate, but for a smaller number of drugs — direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) being the most commonly tested example — the BNF and the summary of product characteristics still reference Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance, and a meticulous prescriber (and a well-prepared exam candidate) should recognise when a drug's dosing guidance is written in those specific terms.

Once a reader has an eGFR or creatinine clearance figure in front of them, the practical decision-making framework the AKT rewards runs through four steps in sequence:

  • Step 1 — identify the drug's clearance route. Is this drug (or its active metabolite) cleared predominantly by the kidney, predominantly by the liver, or by both in meaningful proportions? Renal impairment is only relevant to the first and third categories.
  • Step 2 — check the current eGFR band. The BNF's renal impairment appendix organises guidance by eGFR band, most commonly split around thresholds such as eGFR 30–59 ml/min/1.73m² (moderate impairment), eGFR 15–29 ml/min/1.73m² (severe impairment), and eGFR below 15 ml/min/1.73m² (established renal failure, often on dialysis).
  • Step 3 — look up the drug-specific guidance for that band. The BNF entry for the individual drug states, in plain terms, whether the standard dose is safe, whether a reduced dose or extended interval is required, or whether the drug should be avoided altogether at that level of renal function.
  • Step 4 — reassess if renal function changes. Because acute kidney injury can develop rapidly in an unwell inpatient, a dose that was appropriate on admission may no longer be appropriate a few days later, and the AKT expects candidates to recognise that renal function should be rechecked, not assumed static, throughout an admission.

This four-step sequence is deliberately generic, because it is meant to be applied to almost any drug a candidate encounters in a clinical vignette, rather than memorised as a fact about one specific medicine. A candidate who can reliably work through these four steps under exam pressure will usually reach the correct answer even for a drug they have never specifically revised, which is precisely the transferable skill the AKT is designed to test.

Why the BNF's Renal Impairment Appendix Is the Authoritative Reference

The British National Formulary's prescribing in renal impairment guidance, published via NICE's BNF platform, is the single reference the UKMLA implicitly expects every candidate to be comfortable navigating. It is organised so that a prescriber can look up an individual drug and see, entry by entry, whether dose adjustment is required at each eGFR band, alongside cautions specific to renal impairment such as an increased risk of hyperkalaemia, fluid overload, or drug-specific toxicity.

For UK medical students, active use of this appendix during clinical placements and question-bank revision builds exactly the habit the exam rewards: rather than trying to memorise every dose-adjustment figure for every drug in advance, a well-prepared candidate learns which broad categories of drug typically require renal dose adjustment, and then trusts the BNF itself — used quickly and confidently — to supply the precise figure at the point of prescribing. For IMGs, this appendix is frequently one of the more unfamiliar parts of UK clinical practice, since many national formularies elsewhere structure renal dosing guidance differently, or simply provide less granular eGFR-banded detail than the BNF does. Building genuine, hands-on fluency with this specific appendix — not just a general awareness that "some drugs need renal adjustment" — is one of the highest-yield uses of revision time for this topic.

It is worth being explicit, as with all guidance of this kind, that exact eGFR thresholds and dose-adjustment figures are reviewed and can change between BNF updates. This guide describes the reasoning framework the AKT expects candidates to apply and the drugs most commonly tested, but any real prescribing decision — and any exam answer requiring a specific numerical threshold — should always be checked against the current BNF entry rather than recalled from a revision article or from memory of a previous edition.

Recognising Which Patients Need This Framework Applied Most Carefully

Certain patient groups deserve particular vigilance when prescribing in renal impairment, and the AKT tests recognition of these groups directly. Elderly patients frequently have reduced renal function even with a serum creatinine that looks superficially normal, because reduced muscle mass lowers creatinine production alongside falling clearance, meaning eGFR calculations can understate the true degree of impairment in frail, sarcopenic patients. Acutely unwell inpatients — particularly those who are dehydrated, septic, or have recently received nephrotoxic drugs or intravenous contrast — can develop acute kidney injury over the course of a single admission, meaning a drug dose calculated on admission bloods may become inappropriate days later. Patients with long-standing chronic kidney disease (CKD) generally have a more predictable, slowly progressive trajectory, but still require periodic reassessment, particularly when a new nephrotoxic drug is being considered or when intercurrent illness is superimposed on stable CKD.

A relatively small number of drugs account for a disproportionate share of AKT renal-adjustment questions, and building genuine familiarity with why each one matters — not just which eGFR number triggers a change — is a far more efficient revision strategy than attempting to memorise the entire BNF renal appendix cover to cover.

Metformin: The Archetypal Renal-Adjustment Question

Metformin is arguably the single most commonly tested renal-adjustment drug on the AKT, and for good reason: it is one of the most widely prescribed medicines in UK practice, it is not hepatically metabolised, and its principal safety concern in renal impairment — lactic acidosis — is genuinely serious, even though it is rare. Because metformin relies almost entirely on renal excretion, reduced kidney function allows the drug to accumulate, and the AKT expects candidates to recognise the general pattern that metformin dosing becomes more cautious as eGFR falls, with avoidance appropriate at very low levels of renal function, rather than to recall a single memorised cut-off in isolation from the patient in front of them.

Beyond chronic dosing, metformin also features heavily in "what should be paused" style questions, particularly around iodinated contrast imaging and acute illness. A patient due to receive intravenous contrast, or one who has become acutely unwell with dehydration, vomiting, or sepsis, is at temporarily higher risk of acute kidney injury, and the standard safe-prescribing principle is to consider withholding metformin during this higher-risk window and reviewing renal function before restarting, rather than continuing it reflexively through an acute deterioration.

NSAIDs: A Renal Hazard That Compounds Other Risks

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) reduce renal blood flow by inhibiting prostaglandin-mediated vasodilation of the afferent arteriole, and in a patient whose kidneys are already relying on that prostaglandin-mediated compensation — someone who is dehydrated, has heart failure, has established CKD, or is also taking an ACE inhibitor, ARB, or diuretic — this effect can precipitate a meaningful, sometimes severe, drop in renal function. The AKT frequently tests this as a "triple whammy" scenario: an ACE inhibitor or ARB, a diuretic, and an NSAIDs prescribed together, since this specific combination is a well-recognised, avoidable cause of acute kidney injury in UK clinical practice.

Consequently, the safe-prescribing habit the exam rewards is straightforward: before prescribing or recommending an NSAID, actively check whether the patient has existing renal impairment, is on other drugs that reduce renal perfusion, or is currently dehydrated or acutely unwell, and choose paracetamol or another non-nephrotoxic analgesic option where a genuine alternative exists.

Nitrofurantoin: Efficacy and Toxicity Both Depend on Renal Function

Nitrofurantoin is a particularly instructive example because renal impairment affects it in two directions simultaneously. The drug depends on adequate renal excretion to achieve therapeutic concentrations within the urine, which is where it acts against lower urinary tract infections; when renal function is markedly reduced, urinary concentrations may fall too low to be clinically effective, while at the same time systemic concentrations of the drug and its metabolites can rise, increasing the risk of adverse effects. This dual mechanism is precisely why nitrofurantoin is a recurring "avoid at low eGFR" example in AKT antimicrobial questions, and why it is a useful teaching case for the broader principle that renal impairment can undermine a drug's efficacy, not only its safety.

Digoxin: A Narrow Therapeutic Index Drug Cleared by the Kidney

Digoxin is excreted largely unchanged by the kidneys, and because it has a narrow therapeutic index — meaning the gap between an effective concentration and a toxic one is small — even a modest reduction in renal clearance can push a previously stable dose into the toxic range over time. The AKT commonly tests this through a scenario in which a patient stable on digoxin develops an intercurrent illness or acute kidney injury, and the correct recognition is that digoxin toxicity should be actively considered, alongside checking a digoxin level and reviewing renal function, rather than assuming a long-standing stable dose remains automatically safe.

Opioids: Renally Dependent Metabolite Accumulation

Morphine and codeine are both metabolised to active compounds that are subsequently cleared by the kidney, and in renal impairment these metabolites — particularly morphine-6-glucuronide — can accumulate to concentrations that cause significant sedation and respiratory depression, even when the parent drug's own clearance looks only moderately affected. This is one of the clearest illustrations in the whole curriculum of why "the drug is fine, therefore the dose is fine" is an unsafe assumption: it is often the metabolite, not the parent drug, that causes harm in organ impairment.

The AKT typically expects candidates to recognise that opioid doses should be reduced and dosing intervals lengthened as renal function falls, and that opioids with comparatively less renally dependent active metabolite accumulation — commonly cited examples include oxycodone at a reduced dose, or fentanyl used cautiously under specialist guidance — may be preferred choices in significant renal impairment, always confirmed against current BNF guidance rather than assumed. This theme connects directly to the WHO pain ladder discussion covered in the site's broader pharmacology guide, but the renal-specific reasoning behind it deserves its own dedicated attention, since it is tested as a discrete AKT question style in its own right.

DOACs: Renal Dependence Varies by Individual Agent

Direct oral anticoagulants are frequently tested in renal-impairment scenarios because, unlike a class where every member behaves identically, DOACs vary meaningfully in how renally dependent their clearance actually is. Dabigatran is the most renally cleared of the commonly used DOACs and is generally the first to require dose reduction, or avoidance altogether, as renal function declines, while other agents in the class typically tolerate a somewhat broader range of renal function before the same avoidance threshold is reached, according to current BNF and licensing guidance. The safe-prescribing habit the AKT rewards here is recognising that "DOAC" is not a single uniform answer to a renal-impairment question — the specific agent matters, and renal function should always be checked, using the creatinine-clearance-based thresholds referenced in that drug's specific BNF entry, before initiating or continuing any anticoagulant in a patient with reduced kidney function.

The Child-Pugh Classification and Hepatic Prescribing Implications

Hepatic impairment presents a genuinely different challenge to renal impairment, because there is no single blood test equivalent to eGFR that captures overall liver function in one number. Instead, the AKT expects candidates to understand the concept behind the Child-Pugh classification, a composite scoring system originally developed to predict surgical risk in patients with cirrhosis, which has since become the standard practical framework clinicians use to grade the severity of chronic liver disease and to guide prescribing decisions.

How the Child-Pugh Score Is Built and What It Means for Prescribing

The Child-Pugh score combines five parameters — serum bilirubin, serum albumin, prothrombin time (or International Normalised Ratio), the presence and severity of ascites, and the presence and severity of hepatic encephalopathy — each scored from one to three points, and sums them to produce a total that places a patient into one of three classes: Child-Pugh A (mild disease, generally well-compensated), Child-Pugh B (moderate disease, a meaningful step up in risk), and Child-Pugh C (severe, decompensated disease, carrying the highest risk of drug-related harm). For AKT purposes, candidates are far better served by understanding this conceptual grading — mild, moderate, and severe hepatic impairment, each carrying progressively greater prescribing caution — than by memorising the exact point thresholds for each of the five component scores.

The practical prescribing implication of Child-Pugh grading mirrors the eGFR framework in structure, even though the underlying physiology is different: for a hepatically metabolised drug, Child-Pugh A disease may still permit a standard dose with monitoring, Child-Pugh B disease often requires a reduced dose or increased caution, and Child-Pugh C disease frequently means the drug should be avoided altogether in favour of an alternative that does not depend on hepatic metabolism, or is otherwise recognised as safer in decompensated liver disease. As with renal impairment, the BNF's hepatic impairment guidance, and the hepatic impairment section within each individual drug's BNF entry, is the authoritative reference for the specific recommendation for any given drug, and the exact wording of that guidance should always be checked directly rather than inferred.

Why Hepatic Impairment Changes Drug Handling Beyond Simple Metabolism

A liver that is significantly impaired does not only metabolise drugs more slowly; it also changes several other factors that influence how a drug behaves in the body, and the AKT rewards recognising these secondary effects as much as the primary metabolic slowdown. Reduced hepatic synthesis of albumin means that highly protein-bound drugs may circulate with a higher proportion of free, pharmacologically active drug than usual, effectively increasing potency at a given total dose. Reduced synthesis of clotting factors — reflected in a prolonged prothrombin time or raised INR, one of the five Child-Pugh components — means that drugs affecting coagulation, including anticoagulants and even some antiplatelet agents, carry an amplified bleeding risk in significant liver disease, independent of any direct effect the drug has on clotting itself. In addition, hepatic encephalopathy — a component of the Child-Pugh score in its own right — means that any drug with sedating central nervous system effects can precipitate or worsen confusion, and this single point is one of the most consistently tested hepatic-prescribing themes on the AKT.

Drugs to Avoid or Use Cautiously in Significant Liver Disease

Several specific drugs and drug classes recur often enough in AKT hepatic-impairment questions to deserve individual attention, in addition to the general Child-Pugh framework above.

Paracetamol deserves particular care in significant liver disease, and this is a genuinely nuanced, frequently misunderstood area worth stating carefully. Paracetamol itself is not absolutely contraindicated in stable, compensated chronic liver disease, and remains a first-line, comparatively safe analgesic choice for many patients with liver disease when used at standard therapeutic doses — the far greater danger, well recognised across UK clinical practice, is paracetamol overdose and its capacity to cause severe hepatotoxicity in any patient, with pre-existing liver disease, malnourishment, or chronic alcohol excess increasing susceptibility to harm at a given ingested dose. The AKT's core safe-prescribing expectation is that paracetamol dosing should be used cautiously, with attention to total daily dose and to any risk factors for reduced hepatic glutathione reserve, and that clinical toxicology teams and current BNF/TOXBASE guidance should always be the reference point for any overdose or exceptional-risk scenario, rather than an assumption drawn from general practice alone.

Sedatives, including benzodiazepines and opioids, are amongst the most important drug classes to use cautiously in significant hepatic impairment, because reduced hepatic clearance prolongs their sedative effect, and because any additional central nervous system depression risks precipitating or masking hepatic encephalopathy — a complication that can otherwise be recognised early by a change in conscious level or cognitive function. The AKT frequently tests recognition that a patient with decompensated liver disease who is prescribed a benzodiazepine or a full-dose opioid for an unrelated indication is at meaningfully increased risk of over-sedation and encephalopathy, and that when sedation cannot be avoided, the lowest effective dose, the shortest appropriate duration, and close clinical monitoring are the correct safe-prescribing response.

Warfarin and other drugs affecting coagulation require particular caution in hepatic impairment, because baseline clotting is already frequently deranged in significant liver disease, and monitoring the INR — the very parameter warfarin is titrated against — becomes harder to interpret when the liver's own synthetic function is already contributing to a prolonged baseline prothrombin time.

Statins are hepatically metabolised and carry a recognised, though generally low, risk of hepatotoxicity, and the AKT expects candidates to recognise that significant active liver disease is a caution or contraindication depending on severity, and that liver function should be checked before initiating a statin and monitored thereafter, in line with current guidance.

Methotrexate, already a well-known AKT trap for its once-weekly dosing schedule discussed in the site's patient safety guide, carries an additional, independent hepatotoxicity risk that makes significant pre-existing liver disease a further reason for caution, alongside its renal clearance considerations.

How the AKT Tests Renal and Hepatic Dose-Adjustment Reasoning

Recognising the recurring question structures used for prescribing in renal impairment and its hepatic counterpart helps candidates apply the frameworks above efficiently under timed exam conditions, rather than re-deriving the logic from scratch each time a vignette appears.

The "given this eGFR, what do you do with this drug" pattern. A vignette states a patient's renal function, sometimes as an eGFR figure and sometimes as a creatinine value the candidate must interpret, then asks which of several prescribing actions is correct for a named drug. The reliable approach is to identify the drug's clearance route first, then apply the eGFR-band reasoning described earlier in this guide, rather than guessing from a vaguely remembered rule.

The "acute deterioration in a previously stable patient" pattern. A patient established on a chronic medication develops an acute illness — sepsis, dehydration, an acute kidney injury — and the question asks whether the existing medication should be continued, paused, or dose-adjusted. This pattern rewards recognising that renal (and sometimes hepatic) function is not static, and that a dose safe on a previous admission may no longer be safe once function has changed.

The "Child-Pugh class implied by clinical findings" pattern. Rather than stating a Child-Pugh class explicitly, a vignette may describe a patient with ascites, confusion, jaundice, and a deranged INR, and expect the candidate to recognise this clinical picture as consistent with more severe, decompensated liver disease, then apply correspondingly greater prescribing caution.

The "choose the safer alternative" pattern. A patient with known renal or hepatic impairment needs treatment for a condition where several drug options exist, and the correct answer is the option whose clearance pathway is least affected by that patient's specific organ impairment, rather than the drug that might otherwise be first-line in a patient with normal organ function.

A Practical Revision Strategy for This Topic

Because prescribing in renal impairment and hepatic dose-adjustment questions reward applied reasoning over rote memorisation, an efficient revision strategy for this specific sub-topic differs slightly from revising a drug class in isolation.

Learn the framework before the drug list. A candidate who can reliably answer "how do I decide?" for an unfamiliar drug — checking clearance route, checking the current eGFR band or Child-Pugh class, and looking up the specific BNF guidance — will perform better on unfamiliar-drug questions than a candidate who has memorised a long list of familiar drugs but has no general method to fall back on.

Practise active BNF lookups for both appendices. Whenever an unfamiliar drug appears in question-bank practice, deliberately check both its renal and hepatic impairment guidance in the BNF, even if the practice question only asked about one. This builds dual fluency efficiently and mirrors real clinical practice, where both organ systems are usually checked together before prescribing.

Build a short personal list of the recurring "trap" drugs. Metformin, NSAIDs, nitrofurantoin, digoxin, opioids, and DOACs on the renal side; paracetamol, sedatives, warfarin, statins, and methotrexate on the hepatic side, are worth a dedicated, condensed personal summary, since they recur across question banks far more often than the rest of the BNF combined.

Revise this topic alongside, not instead of, the site's broader guides. This article deliberately avoids repeating the wider drug-class content already covered in the site's pharmacology and prescribing safety guide, and the professionalism and error-reporting content covered in the patient safety and prescribing errors guide. All three are best revised as a connected set, since real AKT vignettes frequently blend organ-impairment reasoning with broader pharmacology knowledge and safe-prescribing principles in a single question. Readers consolidating the underlying renal medicine content behind this guide's eGFR-based reasoning may also find it useful to revisit the site's dedicated nephrology revision guide, which covers chronic kidney disease staging and acute kidney injury in more clinical depth than is appropriate to repeat here.

It is also worth remembering that organ-impairment reasoning does not exist in isolation from the rest of a patient's presentation. A patient with reduced renal function may equally have an unrelated, but clinically important, comorbidity — a psychiatric presentation with medically unexplained symptoms, for instance, or a safeguarding concern of the kind explored in the site's guide to domestic abuse and safeguarding in adults — and the AKT increasingly favours vignettes that test several competencies within a single, realistic clinical scenario rather than one isolated fact at a time. Building genuine, connected clinical reasoning across topics, rather than revising each in a silo, is ultimately what separates strong AKT performance from a narrower, checklist-only approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

What eGFR level means a drug needs renal dose adjustment?

There is no single eGFR threshold that applies to every drug — it depends entirely on how that specific drug is cleared and how the BNF's renal impairment appendix classifies it. As a general pattern, many drugs first require caution or dose reduction once eGFR falls into the moderate impairment range (commonly cited as roughly 30–59 ml/min/1.73m²), with a smaller number requiring outright avoidance once eGFR falls further, but the exact figure for any individual drug should always be checked against its specific BNF entry rather than assumed from a general rule.

Why is metformin stopped or avoided in renal impairment?

Metformin is cleared almost entirely by the kidneys rather than the liver, so reduced renal function allows the drug to accumulate to concentrations that raise the risk of lactic acidosis, a rare but serious complication. As eGFR falls, current guidance moves from standard dosing, through increasing caution and dose reduction, to avoidance at very low levels of renal function, and metformin is also commonly paused temporarily around acute illness, dehydration, and iodinated contrast imaging because of the same underlying risk.

What is the Child-Pugh score used for in prescribing decisions?

The Child-Pugh score is a composite grading system, combining bilirubin, albumin, prothrombin time or INR, ascites, and hepatic encephalopathy, that classifies chronic liver disease severity into Child-Pugh A (mild), B (moderate), or C (severe). Clinicians use this classification as a practical guide to hepatic prescribing risk, since more severe Child-Pugh classes generally require greater dose reduction or avoidance of hepatically metabolised or sedating drugs, mirroring the way eGFR bands guide renal dose-adjustment decisions.

Is paracetamol safe to use in patients with liver disease?

Paracetamol at standard therapeutic doses is generally considered an acceptable, often preferred, analgesic choice even in stable chronic liver disease, and it is not usually a drug that needs to be avoided outright in compensated disease. However, caution is still required, particularly around total daily dose and any risk factors for reduced hepatic glutathione reserve such as malnourishment or chronic alcohol excess, since paracetamol overdose remains one of the most significant causes of severe hepatotoxicity in UK clinical practice.

Which antibiotics need renal dose adjustment on the UKMLA?

Nitrofurantoin is one of the most frequently tested examples, because reduced renal function both lowers its urinary concentration (reducing efficacy against urinary tract infections) and raises systemic drug levels (increasing toxicity risk), making it typically avoided at low eGFR. Aminoglycosides such as gentamicin are another classic example, since they are both renally excreted and independently nephrotoxic, requiring dose and interval adjustment guided by level monitoring rather than a fixed regimen.

Do opioids need dose adjustment in both renal and hepatic impairment?

Yes, though for different underlying reasons in each case. In renal impairment, active and toxic metabolites of drugs such as morphine can accumulate even when the parent drug's own clearance looks only moderately affected, requiring dose reduction or a switch to an opioid less dependent on renal clearance. In hepatic impairment, reduced metabolism prolongs the sedative and respiratory-depressant effect of most opioids, and additional caution is needed because sedation can also mask or worsen hepatic encephalopathy.

Do I need to memorise exact Child-Pugh point thresholds for the AKT?

Generally, no — the AKT places far more weight on recognising the conceptual grading (mild, moderate, and severe hepatic impairment, corresponding to Child-Pugh A, B, and C) and its prescribing implications than on recalling the exact point boundaries of each of the five component scores. Where a specific numerical answer is required, it should always be confirmed against current clinical reference material rather than memorised from a revision guide.

Conclusion

Prescribing in renal and hepatic impairment rewards a structured, transferable way of thinking far more than a long memorised drug list, which is exactly why it recurs so reliably across AKT question banks in slightly different clinical clothing each time. A candidate who can identify a drug's clearance route, apply the eGFR-based or Child-Pugh-based framework correctly, and know when to trust the BNF's renal and hepatic impairment appendices over their own memory will handle this question style confidently, whether the drug named in the vignette is one of the classic examples covered here or one they have never specifically revised. As with all clinical prescribing content on this site, this guide describes the reasoning framework and the drugs most often tested — actual prescribing decisions must always follow the current BNF, NICE guidance, and local protocols, never a revision article.
