NICE guidelines for the UKMLA AKT represent the single most important evidence base you must master before sitting the Applied Knowledge Test, because the vast majority of clinical management questions are written directly from NICE guidance rather than textbook tradition or local practice. Understanding which guidelines matter, how to read them efficiently, and where the examiners are most likely to set traps will transform your revision and dramatically improve your score.

Why NICE Guidelines Dominate the UKMLA AKT

The UKMLA AKT is explicitly designed to test a doctor's ability to apply evidence-based medicine in the UK context. The GMC blueprint aligns management questions with nationally approved guidance, and in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland that means NICE. Scotland uses SIGN for some areas, but NICE remains the primary reference for the AKT. This matters practically: if a question asks about first-line treatment for Type 2 diabetes, the correct answer is whatever NICE NG28 (updated February 2026) says, not what your consultant does in clinic or what you were taught five years ago.

NICE guidelines also matter because they are regularly updated, and the AKT reflects those updates relatively quickly. The February 2026 update to NG28 is a prime example — SGLT-2 inhibitors are now recommended alongside metformin as dual first-line therapy in Type 2 diabetes for patients with established cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease, representing a paradigm shift from previous guidance. Candidates who revised using older sources will select metformin alone and lose marks.

How to Use NICE Efficiently During Revision

Clinical Knowledge Summaries

NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries (CKS) are the fastest way to absorb guideline recommendations for common primary care conditions. Each CKS page is structured with background, diagnosis, prescribing information, and referral criteria. For the AKT, the "Prescribing information" and "When to refer" sections are highest yield. CKS exists for conditions from acne to varicose veins and is written at a level directly matching what the AKT tests. Aim to review the CKS page for every common condition rather than reading the full guideline document.

NICE Pathways

NICE Pathways provide a visual algorithmic representation of care and are excellent for understanding the stepped-care models used in mental health and the treatment escalation logic in asthma, COPD, and hypertension. When a question presents a patient at a particular stage of management and asks what to do next, the pathway diagram makes the answer immediately clear.

The British National Formulary

The BNF sits alongside NICE guidance and is the authoritative reference for drug doses, contraindications, monitoring requirements, and interactions. The AKT tests specific prescribing knowledge — doses of common drugs, contraindications in renal impairment, drugs to avoid in pregnancy — and the BNF is the source for these answers. When the BNF and NICE appear to conflict on first-line choices, the more recent NICE guideline takes precedence for the AKT.

SIGN vs NICE

SIGN (Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network) produces high-quality evidence-based guidelines that sometimes differ from NICE in thresholds, drug choices, or monitoring intervals. For the UKMLA AKT, NICE takes precedence unless the question explicitly states a Scottish context, which is rare. The most practically relevant SIGN guidelines for AKT candidates are SIGN 149 (CVD risk prevention) and SIGN 153 (bronchiolitis), but knowing the NICE equivalents will serve you better.

Cardiovascular NICE Guidelines

NG136 — Hypertension in Adults (2023 Update)

Hypertension management is among the most commonly tested topics in the AKT. Key thresholds to know: clinic BP of 140/90 mmHg or above warrants ABPM or HBPM to confirm diagnosis; ABPM daytime average of 135/85 mmHg or above confirms Stage 1 hypertension. Stage 2 is clinic BP of 160/100 or above, or ABPM of 150/95 or above. Treatment follows the A-B-C-D algorithm. For patients under 55 or of non-Black ethnicity, Step 1 is an ACE inhibitor or ARB (A drug). For patients aged 55 and over or of Black African or Caribbean origin, Step 1 is a calcium channel blocker (C drug). Step 2 combines A and C. Step 3 adds a thiazide-like diuretic — indapamide or chlortalidone preferred over bendroflumethiazide (D drug). Step 4 resistant hypertension management: if potassium is 4.5 mmol/L or below, add low-dose spironolactone; if above 4.5 mmol/L, add an alpha-blocker or beta-blocker.

Target BP in clinic is below 140/90 for most patients (below 135/85 on ABPM). For patients aged 80 or over, target is below 150/90. For those with Type 2 diabetes, the target is below 140/80 (below 130/80 if they have kidney disease, retinopathy, or cerebrovascular disease).

NG185 — Cardiovascular Disease Risk and Lipid Modification

NG185 covers lipid modification for primary and secondary prevention. QRISK3 is the recommended CVD risk calculator; a 10-year risk of 10% or above triggers a discussion about lifestyle modification and statin therapy. High-intensity statins are standard: atorvastatin 20mg for primary prevention and atorvastatin 80mg for secondary prevention. Ezetimibe is added if LDL-C remains above target on maximum tolerated statin. PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab) are reserved for familial hypercholesterolaemia or patients with established CVD who cannot achieve LDL-C targets on statin plus ezetimibe.

Atrial Fibrillation — NICE NG196

AF management is extremely high-yield. Rate control is the default strategy unless there is a reversible cause, AF is of recent onset within 48 hours, or the patient has heart failure with reduced ejection fraction where rhythm control may be preferred. Rate control targets heart rate below 110 bpm at rest (lenient strategy) or below 80 bpm if symptoms persist (strict strategy). Beta-blockers or rate-limiting calcium channel blockers are first-line for rate control; digoxin is third-line and only for sedentary patients. CHA2DS2-VASc scoring determines anticoagulation: score of 2 or above in men, or 3 or above in women, warrants anticoagulation. DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are preferred over warfarin for non-valvular AF. Warfarin remains indicated for AF with mechanical heart valves or moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis.

Respiratory NICE Guidelines

NG80 — Asthma: Diagnosis and Monitoring

The NICE asthma guideline introduced spirometry with bronchodilator reversibility testing and fractional exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO) as objective diagnostic tools, moving away from the previous symptom-and-trial approach. FeNO of 40 ppb or above is considered high and supports an eosinophilic airway inflammatory pattern. The guideline recommends completing a structured diagnostic pathway before committing to a diagnosis of asthma.

Treatment follows a stepwise approach. Step 1 is a SABA (salbutamol) reliever only. Step 2 adds a low-dose ICS (beclometasone 200 micrograms per day). Step 3 adds a LABA to form an ICS-LABA combination (such as Fostair or Seretide). If poorly controlled at Step 3, consider adding a leukotriene receptor antagonist (montelukast) or increasing the ICS dose. Step 4 involves specialist referral and consideration of add-on therapies including tiotropium, theophylline, or biologics (dupilumab, mepolizumab, benralizumab for severe eosinophilic asthma).

NG115 — COPD in Over-16s

COPD diagnosis requires spirometric confirmation: FEV1/FVC ratio below 0.70 post-bronchodilator. Severity staging uses FEV1 percentage predicted: mild (80% or above), moderate (50 to 79%), severe (30 to 49%), very severe (below 30%). First-line pharmacotherapy is a short-acting bronchodilator — SABA or SAMA (salbutamol or ipratropium) — for symptom relief. For patients with persistent breathlessness or exercise limitation, offer a long-acting bronchodilator (LABA or LAMA). ICS is only recommended in combination with LABA/LAMA if the patient has frequent exacerbations (two or more per year) and eosinophil count of 0.1 × 10⁹/L or above, or asthma features. Triple therapy (ICS-LABA-LAMA) is for those with two or more exacerbations per year on dual therapy. Long-term oxygen therapy is indicated if resting PaO2 is 7.3 kPa or below, or between 7.3 and 8.0 kPa with polycythaemia, peripheral oedema, or pulmonary hypertension. Pulmonary rehabilitation is offered to all patients with an MRC dyspnoea score of 3 or above.

Diabetes NICE Guidelines

NG28 — Type 2 Diabetes in Adults (February 2026 Update)

The February 2026 update to NG28 is the most significant recent change to NICE guidance for AKT candidates. The headline change: for adults with Type 2 diabetes who have established cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, or heart failure, SGLT-2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin) are now recommended as dual first-line therapy alongside metformin, not just as second-line add-ons. This reflects robust cardiovascular and renoprotective evidence from the EMPA-REG OUTCOME, CANVAS, DAPA-HF, and CREDENCE trials.

For patients without these high-risk features, metformin remains first-line monotherapy. HbA1c targets are individualised: 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) for those managed with lifestyle or metformin alone, and 53 mmol/mol (7.0%) for those on additional glucose-lowering drugs. GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide) are recommended as third-line therapy, or earlier if BMI is 35 or above and weight management is a priority, or if cardiovascular risk is high and SGLT-2i is contraindicated. DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, alogliptin) are an alternative second-line option, particularly in renal impairment where SGLT-2i may not be appropriate. Sulfonylureas (gliclazide) carry a risk of hypoglycaemia and are less preferred unless cost is a major consideration.

NG17 — Type 1 Diabetes in Adults

NG17 recommends structured education (DAFNE programme) for all adults with Type 1 diabetes. The HbA1c target is 48 mmol/mol (6.5%). Offer multiple daily injection regimens or continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion (insulin pump) based on individual preference and clinical need. Flash glucose monitoring (Libre) and continuous glucose monitoring are recommended for all adults with Type 1 diabetes on the NHS. Annual screening for complications includes retinopathy, nephropathy (albumin:creatinine ratio), neuropathy, and a structured foot assessment.

Mental Health NICE Guidelines

NG222 — Depression in Adults (2022)

The 2022 update to NICE depression guidance moved away from a rigid stepped-care model towards a choice-based approach. Key change: CBT and antidepressants are offered as co-equal first-line treatments for moderate-to-severe depression; neither is mandated as first-line over the other. SSRIs remain first-line pharmacotherapy. Sertraline is often preferred due to its safety profile and lower drug interaction risk. If an SSRI is not tolerated or ineffective after four to six weeks at an adequate dose, switch to a different SSRI or consider an SNRI (venlafaxine) or mirtazapine. Augmentation strategies include lithium, atypical antipsychotics (quetiapine, aripiprazole), or combining antidepressants. ECT is reserved for severe treatment-resistant depression or when rapid response is needed. Antidepressants should be continued for at least six months after remission, and at least two years for patients with recurrent episodes.

Anxiety Disorders — NICE Guidance

For generalised anxiety disorder (NG197): Step 1 is psychoeducation and self-help. Step 2 is low-intensity psychological intervention — CBT-based self-help or psychoeducational groups. Step 3 is high-intensity psychological intervention (individual CBT or applied relaxation) or drug treatment with an SSRI (sertraline first-line). Step 4 is highly specialist input including augmentation strategies. For panic disorder (NG188): CBT is preferred first-line; SSRIs are an alternative. For OCD (NG5): an SSRI (fluoxetine, sertraline, fluvoxamine, paroxetine, citalopram, or escitalopram) or clomipramine combined with ERP (exposure and response prevention). For PTSD (NG116): trauma-focused CBT or EMDR are first-line; SSRIs are second-line. For social anxiety disorder (NG133): CBT is first-line; SSRIs are an alternative.

Other High-Yield NICE Guidelines

NG51 — Sepsis: Recognition, Diagnosis and Early Management

NICE NG51 defines sepsis as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. The Sepsis 6 bundle should be delivered within one hour of recognition: take blood cultures, measure serum lactate, administer IV broad-spectrum antibiotics, commence IV fluid resuscitation (500 mL crystalloid bolus), apply high-flow oxygen, and monitor urine output with catheterisation if needed. NEWS2 is the recommended early warning scoring system. Risk stratification uses a traffic light tool: any red flag feature requires immediate senior review and antibiotics within one hour; amber features without red require senior review and antibiotics within three hours.

NG158 — Venous Thromboembolism in Over-16s

DVT investigation uses the two-level Wells score: score of 2 or above warrants proximal leg vein ultrasound. If ultrasound is negative with score of 2 or above, perform D-dimer and repeat ultrasound at six to eight days if D-dimer is positive. PE investigation uses the two-level PE Wells score. CTPA is the investigation of choice for suspected PE. V/Q scan is used when CTPA is contraindicated (contrast allergy, renal impairment, pregnancy). Treatment: DOACs (rivaroxaban or apixaban) are first-line for most patients. Anticoagulate for a minimum of three months for provoked VTE, and consider extended anticoagulation for six months or longer for unprovoked VTE after risk-benefit assessment. LMWH is traditionally preferred in cancer-associated VTE, though NICE now recommends offering DOACs in this setting as well. VTE prophylaxis in hospital: risk-assess all admissions and prescribe LMWH plus compression stockings for high-risk patients.

NG148 — Acute Kidney Injury

AKI is defined by KDIGO criteria adapted by NICE: a rise in creatinine of 26 micromol/L or above within 48 hours, a rise of 1.5 times baseline or more within seven days, or urine output below 0.5 mL/kg/hour for six hours or more. Management: identify and treat the underlying cause (pre-renal causes such as dehydration, sepsis, and cardiac failure are most common; intrinsic renal causes include glomerulonephritis and ATN; post-renal causes include obstruction). Rehydrate with crystalloid if volume-depleted. Stop nephrotoxic medications including NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, and contrast agents. Monitor electrolytes particularly potassium, fluid balance, and creatinine. Seek nephrology input for AKI Stage 3, uncertain diagnosis, or complications. AKI is a risk factor for CKD progression, so follow-up at 90 days is recommended.

NG76 — Child Maltreatment

Features raising concern for child maltreatment include bruising in non-mobile infants, bruising in unusual patterns (fingertip, ligature, or implement marks), unexplained fractures (especially posterior rib fractures and metaphyseal corner fractures), retinal haemorrhages without major trauma, burns in unusual distributions, and delayed presentations. All healthcare professionals have a statutory duty to refer concerns to Children's Social Care. Document findings clearly and do not attempt to investigate independently.

NG12 — Suspected Cancer: Recognition and Referral

NG12 sets out two-week wait referral criteria for suspected cancer. High-yield criteria include: age 40 or above with unexplained weight loss and abdominal pain (upper GI cancer); rectal bleeding without anal symptoms in patients over 50 (colorectal); haematuria without UTI (bladder cancer); dysphagia (oesophageal cancer); hoarseness persisting more than three weeks in a smoker (laryngeal cancer); unexplained haematuria in any age (renal cell carcinoma); breast lump in any woman aged 30 or above or any man. The threshold for two-week-wait referral is a risk of cancer of 3% or more based on presenting features.

NG5 — Medicines Optimisation

NG5 underpins structured medication reviews, deprescribing, and the use of STOPP/START criteria in older adults. STOPP identifies potentially inappropriate medications; START identifies potentially underused medications. Common STOPP examples: NSAIDs in eGFR below 50, anticholinergics in urinary retention, long-term PPIs without indication, benzodiazepines in falls risk. Common START examples: statins in established CVD, ACE inhibitors in heart failure with reduced EF, bisphosphonates in patients with osteoporosis on long-term corticosteroids.

NG142 — Care of the Dying Adult

NG142 covers care of the dying adult in the last days of life. Key principles include anticipatory prescribing of subcutaneous opioids for pain, midazolam for agitation or breathlessness, antiemetics (haloperidol or cyclizine) for nausea, and hyoscine butylbromide for respiratory secretions. Individualised care planning should involve the patient (if they have capacity) and their family. The Liverpool Care Pathway is no longer recommended.

How NICE Differs from the BNF

A common source of confusion is the relationship between NICE and the BNF. NICE guidelines set the clinical framework — which drug class to use, in what sequence, and for which patient population. The BNF provides the prescribing mechanics — the dose, formulation, monitoring parameters, and contraindications of individual drugs. The AKT tests both. A question might ask which drug class to initiate (NICE territory) or what monitoring to arrange for a patient starting methotrexate (BNF territory). Neither source is redundant for exam preparation.

Antimicrobial Stewardship and Infection Guidance

NICE antimicrobial prescribing pathways are essential AKT knowledge. For uncomplicated lower UTI in women: nitrofurantoin 100 mg modified release twice daily for three days if eGFR is 30 mL/min or above; trimethoprim 200 mg twice daily for seven days if nitrofurantoin is unsuitable. For community-acquired pneumonia: amoxicillin 500 mg three times daily for mild-moderate disease; add doxycycline or a macrolide if atypical organisms are suspected. For cellulitis: flucloxacillin 500 mg four times daily for uncomplicated cases. For acute sinusitis: antibiotics are not routinely recommended as the condition is usually self-limiting and viral. The core principle of antimicrobial stewardship (NG15) is to avoid prescribing antibiotics for self-limiting viral infections and to use the narrowest-spectrum agent appropriate when an antibiotic is indicated.

Frequently Asked Questions About NICE Guidelines for the UKMLA AKT

Do I need to memorise guideline numbers for the AKT?

No — the AKT does not directly test your recall of NG numbers. You need to know the clinical content and recommendations. However, knowing that NG136 covers hypertension and NG28 covers Type 2 diabetes helps you locate information quickly during revision and confirms you are working from current guidance rather than outdated textbooks.

How recent do my NICE guidelines need to be?

The AKT is updated regularly and reflects current NICE guidance at the time of sitting. Guidelines updated in the twelve months before your exam are examinable and potentially the highest-yield areas precisely because many candidates will have revised from older sources. Always check for updates to major guidelines — diabetes, hypertension, lipids, and depression — in the months before your examination date.

What if I know from clinical practice that my hospital does things differently?

For the AKT, always answer according to NICE guidance rather than local protocols. Local practice may deviate from national guidance for legitimate reasons including resource constraints, patient population characteristics, or specialist expertise, but the AKT tests what NICE says. If your trust's practice conflicts with NICE, use NICE for exam purposes.

Are SIGN guidelines ever tested in the AKT?

SIGN guidelines may occasionally inform questions, particularly for conditions where NICE has not published guidance or where the question has a specifically Scottish context. For the vast majority of AKT questions, NICE is the operative guidance. Focus your revision on NICE and supplement with SIGN only if you have remaining time.

How should I prioritise which guidelines to learn?

Prioritise by disease burden and examination frequency. Cardiovascular disease (hypertension, AF, lipids, ACS), diabetes, respiratory conditions (asthma, COPD), mental health (depression, anxiety), and common infections (sepsis, UTI, pneumonia) are tested most heavily. Within each area, focus on diagnosis, treatment initiation, escalation thresholds, and monitoring — these formats dominate AKT questions.

Is the BNF or NICE website available during the AKT?

No — the AKT is a closed-book examination. You are expected to recall prescribing information from memory. The most commonly tested facts are: first-line drug choices, standard doses of common medications, major contraindications, and monitoring requirements for drugs such as lithium, methotrexate, warfarin, amiodarone, and DMARDs.

Conclusion

Mastering NICE guidelines is the single most efficient investment of your UKMLA AKT revision time. Focus on the cardiovascular, diabetes, respiratory, and mental health guidelines covered in this article, use NICE CKS and Pathways for rapid review, and pay particular attention to recently updated guidance — especially the February 2026 NG28 update on Type 2 diabetes management.
