Respiratory medicine UKMLA questions appear in virtually every sitting of the Applied Knowledge Test, making this one of the highest-yield systems to master. From acute asthma and COPD exacerbations to pulmonary embolism and lung cancer red flags, the breadth of respiratory presentations tested reflects their frequency on the wards. This guide brings together the core guidelines — BTS/NICE/SIGN asthma, NICE NG115 COPD, BTS pleural and pneumothorax guidelines — alongside the clinical frameworks and data tables you need to answer AKT questions confidently and perform well in CPSA stations.

Respiratory Investigations: Understanding the Tools

Before diving into individual conditions, a solid grasp of respiratory investigations is essential for respiratory medicine UKMLA success. Every question about obstructive versus restrictive disease, or type 1 versus type 2 respiratory failure, hinges on being able to interpret results correctly.

Spirometry and Lung Function Tests

Spirometry is the cornerstone of respiratory diagnosis. The key ratio is FEV1/FVC. An obstructive pattern shows FEV1/FVC below 0.70 with reduced FEV1 and normal or increased FVC. A restrictive pattern shows FEV1/FVC above 0.70 but with reduced FVC and reduced TLC on full lung function testing. The DLCO (diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide) reflects gas transfer across the alveolar-capillary membrane — reduced in emphysema, pulmonary fibrosis, and pulmonary hypertension; normal or elevated in asthma and obesity hypoventilation.

ABG Interpretation Framework

Arterial blood gas interpretation follows a stepwise approach. First assess pH (normal 7.35–7.45). Then assess PaCO2 (normal 4.7–6.0 kPa) — if pH is low and CO2 is high, this is respiratory acidosis. Assess bicarbonate (HCO3, normal 22–26 mmol/L) — if pH is low and HCO3 is low, this is metabolic acidosis. Then determine compensation and calculate the A-a gradient if needed. PaO2 normal on air is 10–14 kPa; SpO2 should be above 94% (88–92% in hypercapnic COPD).

Asthma: BTS/NICE/SIGN Step Management and Acute Severity

Asthma management in respiratory medicine UKMLA questions is guided by the joint BTS/NICE/SIGN chronic asthma guideline, which introduced a major shift in 2024: the preferred pathway no longer uses SABA (short-acting beta-2 agonist) as the sole reliever. Instead, the recommended approach uses an ICS-formoterol combination inhaler as either AIR (anti-inflammatory reliever, as-needed only) or MART (maintenance and reliever therapy).

COPD: GOLD Staging, Assessment, and Management

COPD management follows NICE NG115 (updated 2019) and GOLD guidance. Diagnosis requires post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC below 0.70. Severity is classified by FEV1 % predicted (GOLD spirometric grades 1–4), but the GOLD 2023 framework now uses symptom burden (mMRC dyspnoea scale or CAT score) and exacerbation history to guide treatment in groups A to E.

COPD Assessment Tools

The mMRC (modified Medical Research Council) dyspnoea scale grades breathlessness from 0 (none except strenuous exercise) to 4 (too breathless to leave the house). The CAT (COPD Assessment Test) is an 8-item questionnaire scoring 0–40; a score of 10 or above indicates significant symptom burden. In NICE NG115, use of a LABA alone (without ICS) is now preferred as initial add-on for patients without asthmatic features.

COPD Pharmacological Management (NICE NG115)

Initial therapy: SABA or SAMA as needed. If persisting breathlessness: LABA or LAMA (or both). ICS is only added if the patient has asthmatic features/steroid responsiveness (blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/microlitre, or ≥100 with two or more exacerbations/year). Avoid ICS monotherapy in COPD. Roflumilast (PDE-4 inhibitor) may be added in severe COPD with chronic bronchitis and frequent exacerbations. Mucolytics (carbocisteine) are considered for patients with chronic productive cough. Pulmonary rehabilitation is offered to all with MRC grade 3 or above.

COPD Exacerbation Management and NIV

Acute COPD exacerbation: controlled oxygen (target SpO2 88–92%), nebulised salbutamol and ipratropium, oral prednisolone 30 mg for 5 days, antibiotics if purulent sputum (amoxicillin, doxycycline, or clarithromycin). NIV (BiPAP) is indicated for persistent type 2 respiratory failure (pH 7.25–7.35) despite initial medical therapy. Invasive ventilation is considered if NIV fails or contraindicated. Contraindications to NIV include facial trauma, copious secretions, reduced consciousness, and haemodynamic instability. Target SpO2 88–92% — avoid hyperoxia causing hypercapnia via the Haldane effect in known hypercapnic patients.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia: CURB-65 and Management

CAP diagnosis requires clinical features (cough, fever, dyspnoea, pleuritic pain, new focal signs) plus new consolidation on CXR. CURB-65 score guides site of care: Confusion (AMT ≤8), Urea >7 mmol/L, RR ≥30, BP systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60, Age ≥65. Score 0–1: home treatment; score 2: hospital assessment; score 3–5: severe, consider ITU. Antibiotic choice: mild/moderate CAP — amoxicillin 500 mg TDS (add clarithromycin for atypical cover in moderate disease). Severe CAP — co-amoxiclav + clarithromycin IV, or beta-lactam + macrolide. Common organisms: Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common), Haemophilus influenzae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae (atypical — non-productive cough, raised CRP, responds to macrolides), Legionella (Legionnaire's disease — hyponatraemia, raised LDH, urinary antigen test), Staphylococcus aureus (post-influenza). Consider TB if risk factors: weight loss, night sweats, haemoptysis, travel from endemic region.

Pulmonary Embolism: Risk Stratification and Management

PE diagnosis begins with clinical probability using the Wells PE score. Two-level Wells: score >4 = PE likely (proceed to CTPA); score ≤4 = PE unlikely (do D-dimer; if positive proceed to CTPA). D-dimer has a high negative predictive value in low-probability PE but is non-specific. CTPA is the definitive investigation. VQ scan is an alternative in renal impairment or contrast allergy, and is preferred in pregnancy (lower fetal radiation dose than CTPA). Treatment: therapeutic anticoagulation with a DOAC (apixaban or rivaroxaban preferred; LMWH + warfarin or LMWH if cancer). Duration: provoked PE 3 months; unprovoked 3–6 months minimum, consider indefinite. Massive PE with haemodynamic instability: systemic thrombolysis (alteplase 100 mg IV), or surgical embolectomy. PE in pregnancy: LMWH throughout — avoid DOACs (not licensed) and warfarin (teratogenic).

Pleural Effusion and Pneumothorax

Pleural Effusion: Transudate vs Exudate

Light's criteria distinguish transudate (failure states) from exudate (inflammatory/malignant). An effusion is an exudate if any ONE of: pleural fluid protein/serum protein >0.5; pleural fluid LDH/serum LDH >0.6; pleural fluid LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH. Transudates (protein <30 g/L): heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism, Meigs syndrome. Exudates (protein >35 g/L): pneumonia (parapneumonic/empyema), malignancy, TB, pulmonary infarction, rheumatoid arthritis.

Management: diagnostic aspiration (sample for protein, LDH, glucose, pH, cytology, MC&S, AFB). If empyema (pH <7.2, glucose <2.2, turbid/purulent): intercostal drain plus antibiotics. Malignant effusion: therapeutic aspiration or indwelling pleural catheter (IPC) for recurrent effusions. Pleurodesis for recurrent effusions in patients with good PS.

Interstitial Lung Disease

ILD encompasses a range of conditions causing progressive fibrosis or inflammation of the lung parenchyma. Key UKMLA ILD conditions:

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF): progressive dyspnoea, dry cough, fine bi-basal inspiratory crackles, finger clubbing, restrictive spirometry, reduced DLCO. HRCT shows honeycombing with basal predominance and traction bronchiectasis (UIP pattern). Treatment: antifibrotics (pirfenidone or nintedanib) slow progression; no curative therapy without transplant.

Sarcoidosis: granulomatous disease; bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL) on CXR; hypercalcaemia; uveitis; erythema nodosum + BHL = Lofgren syndrome (good prognosis). Elevated ACE. Treat with steroids if symptomatic or progressing.

Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (Extrinsic Allergic Alveolitis): type III/IV hypersensitivity to inhaled organic antigens. Farmer's lung (Micropolyspora faeni from mouldy hay), bird fancier's lung (avian proteins). Subacute/chronic: bilateral infiltrates, avoid antigen, steroids.

Obstructive Sleep Apnoea

OSA is caused by upper airway obstruction during sleep. Risk factors: obesity (BMI >30), male sex, macroglossia, retrognathia, hypothyroidism, alcohol. Symptoms: daytime somnolence (Epworth Sleepiness Scale >10 significant), witnessed apnoeas, morning headaches, nocturia, impaired concentration. The Epworth Sleepiness Scale scores likelihood of dozing in 8 situations (max 24; >10 excessive daytime sleepiness). Investigation: overnight sleep study (oximetry or polysomnography). Management: weight loss, avoid alcohol/sedatives, CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) is first-line for moderate-severe OSA (AHI >15). DVLA: patients with OSA must not drive until symptoms are controlled. Group 1 licence holders can drive if treatment effective; group 2 (HGV) require formal confirmation of compliance and treatment.

Tuberculosis in the UK

TB rates in the UK are low overall (~8/100,000) but higher in urban areas and specific populations (migrants from high-incidence countries, homeless, immunocompromised, prisoners). Pulmonary TB: productive cough >3 weeks, haemoptysis, weight loss, night sweats, fever. CXR: upper lobe shadowing, cavitation, nodules. Primary TB may show Ghon focus and Ranke complex. Diagnosis: sputum AFB smear (3 samples), culture (gold standard — 4–8 weeks), GeneXpert MTB/RIF for rapid diagnosis and rifampicin resistance. Mantoux test: ≥6 mm (BCG unvaccinated) or ≥15 mm (BCG vaccinated) is positive. IGRA (interferon gamma release assay, e.g. QuantiFERON): preferred for LTBI screening (not affected by BCG). Treatment of active TB: 2HRZE (2 months rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) then 4HR (4 months rifampicin + isoniazid). Side effects: rifampicin (red urine, hepatotoxicity, induces CYP450), isoniazid (peripheral neuropathy — give pyridoxine/B6, hepatitis), pyrazinamide (hepatotoxicity, gout), ethambutol (optic neuritis — check colour vision). DOT (directly observed therapy) for non-adherence risk.

Respiratory Pharmacology and Inhaler Devices

Understanding inhaler devices is increasingly relevant to the UKMLA, particularly in clinical skills stations where you may be asked to counsel a patient or identify the correct device. Metered-dose inhalers (MDIs) require good coordination and are used with a spacer device for all children and those who cannot coordinate actuation with inhalation. Dry powder inhalers (DPIs, e.g. Turbuhaler, Ellipta, Accuhaler) require sufficient inspiratory flow and are not suitable for patients in acute severe asthma. Soft mist inhalers (SMIs, e.g. Respimat) produce a slow-moving aerosol and require less coordination than MDIs. For acute asthma in hospital, wet nebulisers driven by air (not oxygen in COPD) deliver bronchodilators efficiently.

Inhaler technique errors are extremely common — studies suggest over 70% of patients use inhalers incorrectly. Foundation doctors are expected to check inhaler technique, prescribe spacers where indicated, and ensure patients are reviewed at least annually regarding their inhaler device and technique. Key prescription points: always prescribe inhalers by brand name as well as generic (devices differ significantly), document whether a spacer is required, and ensure the patient demonstrates technique before discharge.

Systemic corticosteroids in respiratory disease: oral prednisolone is used in acute asthma (40–50 mg for 5–7 days) and COPD exacerbations (30 mg for 5 days). Prolonged courses increase risk of adrenal suppression, osteoporosis, diabetes, and immunosuppression. Patients on long-term systemic steroids should carry a steroid emergency card. Stress dosing (hydrocortisone 100 mg IV TDS) is needed during surgery, significant illness, or vomiting if taking equivalent of prednisolone 5 mg or more daily for more than 3 weeks.

Pulmonary Hypertension and Respiratory Failure in the UKMLA

Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is defined as mean pulmonary artery pressure above 20 mmHg at right heart catheterisation. Group 1 (pulmonary arterial hypertension, PAH): idiopathic, connective tissue disease (especially systemic sclerosis), portal hypertension, HIV, congenital heart disease. Group 2: left heart disease. Group 3: lung disease (COPD, IPF). Group 4: chronic thromboembolic PH (CTEPH — investigate with V/Q scan, potentially curative with pulmonary endarterectomy). Symptoms: progressive exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, right heart failure features (oedema, ascites, raised JVP). Investigation: echocardiography (estimated RVSP), right heart catheterisation (gold standard). Treatment: Group 1 — endothelin receptor antagonists (bosentan, ambrisentan), phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil), prostacyclin analogues (epoprostenol IV, iloprost inhaled); Group 4 — riociguat, pulmonary endarterectomy.

Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): acute hypoxic respiratory failure (PaO2/FiO2 ratio below 200 mmHg) with bilateral infiltrates on imaging and no cardiogenic cause. Berlin definition 2012: onset within 1 week of known clinical insult; bilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, atelectasis, or nodules; respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure. Causes: sepsis (most common), aspiration pneumonia, major trauma, transfusion (TRALI), pancreatitis. Management: lung-protective ventilation (tidal volume 6 mL/kg ideal body weight; PEEP titrated; plateau pressure below 30 cmH2O), prone positioning (improves V/Q matching, reduces mortality), neuromuscular blockade, conservative fluid strategy. Dexamethasone reduces mortality in moderate-severe ARDS (DEXA-ARDS trial). Overall mortality remains approximately 30–40%.

Respiratory Medicine and Occupational Lung Disease

Occupational lung disease is a high-yield UKMLA topic because it requires careful occupational history taking — a skill tested in both AKT and CPSA. Asbestosis: diffuse pulmonary fibrosis from prolonged asbestos exposure (latency 15–30 years); lower zone fibrosis, pleural plaques (calcified), bilateral basal crackles; associated with mesothelioma (high risk) and SCC. Silicosis: from silica dust (quarrying, sandblasting, mining); upper zone fibrosis and nodules; "eggshell" calcification of hilar nodes; increases risk of TB. Coal worker's pneumoconiosis: progressive massive fibrosis in coalminers; upper lobe masses. Occupational asthma: causes include isocyanates (spray painting, adhesives), flour and grain dust, latex, colophony (soldering), wood dust; characteristic feature is improvement during holidays and weekends. Berylliosis: rare granulomatous lung disease resembling sarcoidosis from beryllium exposure in aerospace/electronics industries. All occupational lung diseases are notifiable under RIDDOR if acquired in the course of employment and require referral for compensation assessment (Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit).

Frequently Asked Questions About Respiratory Medicine in the UKMLA

How do I distinguish type 1 from type 2 respiratory failure on an ABG?

Type 1 respiratory failure is defined as PaO2 below 8 kPa with a normal or low PaCO2 — the lung is failing to oxygenate but can still ventilate (blow off CO2). Causes include pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, pulmonary oedema, and ARDS. Type 2 respiratory failure is PaO2 below 8 kPa with PaCO2 above 6.0 kPa — the lungs are failing to both oxygenate and ventilate. Causes include COPD, neuromuscular disease, obesity hypoventilation, and central causes. In type 2 failure, if the bicarbonate is raised (compensated), this indicates a chronic process. If pH is low with raised CO2 and normal bicarbonate, this is an acute decompensation.

What are the driving rules for patients with respiratory conditions?

Patients with OSA must not drive until symptoms are controlled with CPAP or other therapy. DVLA must be notified. For group 2 (HGV/PSV) licences, formal medical confirmation of treatment and compliance is required. Patients with severe COPD requiring oxygen therapy should discuss driving with their doctor and DVLA if concerned about fitness to drive. Patients on home oxygen should not drive while using oxygen. There are no DVLA-specific restrictions solely based on asthma diagnosis provided it is well controlled.

When is non-invasive ventilation (NIV/BiPAP) indicated in COPD?

NIV is indicated in COPD exacerbation with type 2 respiratory failure when pH is between 7.25 and 7.35 (acidotic but not severely so) despite initial medical management including controlled oxygen, bronchodilators, steroids, and antibiotics. It is also used prophylactically if pH is 7.35 and CO2 is rising. Absolute contraindications include undrained pneumothorax, facial trauma, severe haemodynamic instability, inability to protect airway, and copious secretions. If NIV fails and pH remains below 7.25, intubation and mechanical ventilation should be considered.

What is the target oxygen saturation in COPD exacerbation and why?

Target SpO2 in COPD exacerbation is 88–92%, not the standard 94–98% used in most other conditions. This is because some patients with chronic hypercapnic COPD rely on hypoxic drive to breathe — high-flow oxygen can suppress this, worsen ventilation, and cause CO2 retention via the Haldane effect (oxygen displacing CO2 from haemoglobin). Controlled oxygen is delivered via a 28% Venturi mask (2 L/min) initially with regular ABG monitoring. This target also applies in the ambulance setting — pre-alert with written instruction on the patient's notes.

How does the UKMLA test sleep apnoea?

OSA questions in the AKT typically present a middle-aged overweight male with morning headaches, daytime somnolence, snoring, and witnessed apnoeas. Questions may ask about the most appropriate investigation (overnight oximetry or polysomnography), the Epworth Sleepiness Scale, first-line treatment (CPAP), or driving restrictions. The CPSA may include a scenario about discussing DVLA notification or explaining CPAP to a patient. A common distractor is to confuse OSA (obstructive) with central sleep apnoea, which involves failure of respiratory drive rather than airway obstruction.

What is the Glasgow-Blatchford score used for in respiratory contexts?

The Glasgow-Blatchford score is not a respiratory tool — it is used in upper GI bleeding to predict need for intervention. In respiratory medicine, the relevant severity score for pneumonia is CURB-65. Confusing these is a common trap in UKMLA questions. Always identify the clinical context: breathlessness and consolidation on CXR = CURB-65; haematemesis and melaena = Glasgow-Blatchford.

Conclusion

Respiratory medicine UKMLA questions reward candidates who can apply clinical algorithms quickly and accurately. Master spirometry interpretation, the BTS/NICE/SIGN asthma step ladder (including the shift away from SABA monotherapy), COPD GOLD staging and exacerbation management with NIV thresholds, CURB-65 for pneumonia, Wells score for PE, and the chest X-ray pattern recognition table. The ABG framework — remembering the difference between type 1 and type 2 respiratory failure, and why oxygen targets differ in COPD — is a consistent AKT theme. Round out your revision with pleural disease (Light's criteria, pneumothorax BTS guidelines), ILD subtypes, TB treatment and side effects, and OSA DVLA rules.
