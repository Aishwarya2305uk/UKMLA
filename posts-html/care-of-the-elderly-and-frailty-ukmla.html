Care of the Elderly and Frailty in the UKMLA: What to Revise

Care of the elderly UKMLA questions sit at the intersection of clinical reasoning, communication skill, and pragmatic prescribing, and they appear far more often than candidates expect. With the UK population ageing rapidly and NHS wards, GP surgeries, and emergency departments increasingly dominated by frail, multimorbid older patients, the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test treats geriatric medicine and frailty as a genuinely high-yield domain rather than a niche subspecialty. Both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AKT need a structured understanding of frailty as a clinical syndrome, delirium recognition, falls assessment, polypharmacy, and pressure ulcer prevention — the core topics that make up care of the elderly UKMLA revision.

This guide sets out exactly what to revise for care of the elderly UKMLA stations, why the topic is weighted so heavily, and how each sub-topic tends to be examined. It builds on the foundations covered in our AKT format guide and complements related clinical-reasoning content across the site, including how prescribing decisions change in patients with reduced renal or hepatic function, which we cover in a dedicated dose-adjustment guide.

Quick Answer: What Does the UKMLA Test in Care of the Elderly?

Care of the elderly UKMLA content tests the recognition and management of frailty, delirium, falls, polypharmacy, and pressure ulcer risk in older adults — always through the lens of comprehensive geriatric assessment rather than isolated disease management. The AKT does not simply ask "what is dementia" or "what drug treats hypertension" in this domain; it asks candidates to weigh up a frail 84-year-old's overall trajectory, distinguish an acute confusional state from an underlying cognitive disorder, and decide whether stopping a medication is safer than continuing it.

The four pillars examined most consistently are:

  • Frailty identification — using tools such as the Clinical Frailty Scale to describe a patient's baseline resilience, not just their chronological age
  • Delirium recognition — screening approaches modelled on the 4AT and Confusion Assessment Method (CAM), causes, and safe distinction from dementia
  • Falls and fracture risk — structured multifactorial falls assessment and evidence-based prevention
  • Polypharmacy and deprescribing — structured medication review using STOPP/START-style principles

Pressure ulcer risk assessment and prevention is examined as a supporting theme running through most inpatient scenarios involving immobile or cognitively impaired older patients.

Why Care of the Elderly Is High-Yield for the UKMLA

Understanding exactly why care of the elderly UKMLA content carries so much weight helps candidates prioritise their revision time sensibly rather than treating it as an optional extra. The demographic reality of the NHS makes this one of the most heavily weighted content areas in the entire syllabus, even though it rarely gets a single dedicated lecture block in undergraduate teaching. The Office for National Statistics projects that the number of people in the UK aged 85 and over will nearly double over the next two decades, and hospital inpatient surveys consistently show that the majority of acute medical and surgical beds are occupied by patients aged over 65, many of whom are living with frailty, multimorbidity, and polypharmacy simultaneously.

Consequently, the UKMLA content map treats geriatric medicine not as an isolated specialty but as a thread that runs through cardiology, respiratory medicine, orthopaedics, psychiatry, and general practice questions alike. A candidate who cannot recognise delirium, cannot interpret a Clinical Frailty Scale score, or does not know how to approach a medication review in an 88-year-old will struggle across a wide swathe of AKT items, not just the ones explicitly labelled "geriatrics." This is precisely why the topic rewards structured revision rather than passive reading — the same reasoning framework recurs, dressed in different clinical scenarios, dozens of times across a single sitting.

For UK medical students, this content is frequently first encountered during a dedicated geriatric medicine placement, but the exam draws just as heavily on principles taught during general medicine, surgery, and psychiatry rotations. For IMGs, particularly those trained in health systems with younger population profiles or different care structures, this domain can be the least familiar part of the AKT, which makes it worth prioritising early rather than leaving until the final weeks of revision. Our population health and screening revision guide provides useful context on how UK demographic trends and preventive strategies interact with the ageing population theme tested here.

Frailty as a Clinical Syndrome, Not Just Old Age

Any serious approach to care of the elderly UKMLA revision has to begin with frailty itself, since almost every other theme in this guide is best understood as a downstream consequence of reduced physiological reserve.

Understanding Frailty as a Distinct Diagnosis

Frailty is a distinct clinical syndrome characterised by reduced physiological reserve across multiple body systems, resulting in an increased vulnerability to sudden health state changes triggered by relatively minor stressor events. This is the single most important conceptual point in the entire topic area, and it is deliberately tested again and again in different guises: frailty is not a synonym for old age, and not every older patient is frail, while some younger patients with complex chronic disease can meet frailty criteria.

The distinction matters clinically because a frail patient responds differently to illness than a robust patient of the same age. A urinary tract infection that would cause a robust 80-year-old mild discomfort might precipitate delirium, a fall, loss of mobility, and hospital admission in a frail 80-year-old. This phenomenon is sometimes described using the concept of a "reserve" that has been eroded across multiple organ systems simultaneously — cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, renal, cognitive, and immune reserve are all diminished, so a single insult can cascade into multi-system decompensation.

Two broad conceptual models underpin frailty assessment, and candidates should recognise both:

  • The phenotype model (Fried frailty phenotype) — defines frailty by the presence of three or more of five physical criteria: unintentional weight loss, self-reported exhaustion, low grip strength, slow walking speed, and low physical activity
  • The cumulative deficit model — defines frailty as an accumulation of deficits (symptoms, signs, diseases, disabilities, and abnormal test results) across a broad range, with more deficits equating to greater frailty regardless of which specific deficits are present

The UKMLA is most likely to test the cumulative deficit approach in its applied form, since this is the conceptual basis for the Clinical Frailty Scale used throughout UK hospitals.

The Clinical Frailty Scale

The Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) is a 9-point clinician-scored judgement tool that summarises a patient's overall level of fitness or frailty based on their function two weeks before the current acute illness, ranging from 1 (very fit) to 9 (terminally ill). It is the tool most commonly referenced in UK clinical practice and, accordingly, the version most likely to appear directly in AKT stems.

Candidates should understand the practical significance of the CFS beyond simply memorising the categories. A CFS score influences decisions about the intensity of investigation and treatment appropriate for a patient, informs discussions about ceiling of care, and predicts outcomes including length of hospital stay, mortality, and likelihood of discharge to a higher level of care. However, it is essential to note that the CFS should never be applied to patients with stable long-term disabilities (such as cerebral palsy or learning disability) who have never experienced a decline in function, since it is designed to measure frailty trajectory, not baseline functional level. This nuance is a favourite AKT distractor: a young adult with a lifelong learning disability who lives independently with support is not "frail" simply because they need assistance with some activities. Getting this distinction right is one of the fastest ways to pick up marks in care of the elderly UKMLA questions built around the Clinical Frailty Scale.

Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment

Comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) is a multidimensional, multidisciplinary diagnostic process used to determine the medical, psychological, and functional capabilities of a frail older person in order to develop a coordinated and integrated plan for treatment and long-term follow-up. CGA is consistently associated with improved outcomes for frail older inpatients, including increased likelihood of living independently at home at follow-up, when compared with standard medical care.

CGA is organised around four core domains, and the AKT often tests whether a candidate can correctly categorise a clinical finding into the right domain:

  • Medical — problem list, comorbidities, medication review, nutrition, continence
  • Functional — activities of daily living, mobility, falls history, occupational needs
  • Psychological — cognitive assessment, mood, anxiety, and screening for delirium
  • Social and environmental — home circumstances, carer support, financial issues, use of aids and adaptations, social network

The CGA process is typically delivered by a multidisciplinary team including geriatricians, nurses, physiotherapists, occupational therapists, pharmacists, and social workers, reflecting the reality that frailty cannot be managed by pharmacological treatment alone. A recurring exam theme is recognising when a scenario is describing CGA implicitly — for example, a case describing an MDT meeting reviewing an older inpatient's medications, mobility, mood, and discharge planning together is describing CGA even if that term is never used in the stem — spotting this pattern is a core care of the elderly UKMLA exam skill.

Delirium: Recognition and the Crucial Distinction from Dementia

Delirium recognition is arguably the single most examinable component of care of the elderly UKMLA content, precisely because missing it has immediate, serious safety consequences.

What Delirium Is and Why It Is Missed

Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness accompanied by additional cognitive change, that develops over hours to days and is caused by an underlying medical condition, substance intoxication or withdrawal, or medication. It is exceptionally common in hospitalised older adults — reported in up to a third of general medical inpatients aged over 65, and considerably more common in those admitted to intensive care or following hip fracture surgery — yet it is missed in a large proportion of cases, particularly the hypoactive subtype.

Three clinical subtypes are recognised, and the UKMLA expects candidates to recognise all three, not just the agitated form that is easiest to picture:

  • Hyperactive delirium — agitation, restlessness, hallucinations, aggression. The most easily recognised, but the least common subtype
  • Hypoactive delirium — withdrawal, lethargy, reduced responsiveness, appearing sleepy or "quiet." The most common subtype and the one most frequently missed or misattributed to tiredness or depression
  • Mixed delirium — fluctuation between hyperactive and hypoactive features, often within the same day

The core diagnostic features of delirium are an acute onset, a fluctuating course (often worse in the evening — so-called "sundowning"), inattention (difficulty maintaining or shifting focus), and either disorganised thinking or an altered level of consciousness. Any acute deterioration in an older patient's mental state should trigger active screening for delirium before any other diagnosis is assumed.

Screening Tools: The 4AT and CAM Concept

Two validated screening approaches dominate UK clinical practice and are the ones most likely to be referenced, directly or indirectly, in AKT stems.

The 4AT is a rapid four-item bedside screening tool requiring no special training, widely used across NHS wards and emergency departments as the first-line delirium screen. It assesses:

  • Alertness — normal, mild sleepiness for under 10 seconds after waking, or any clearly abnormal alertness
  • AMT4 (Abbreviated Mental Test-4) — age, date of birth, place, current year
  • Attention — asking the patient to recite the months of the year backwards
  • Acute change or fluctuating course — evidence of a significant change or fluctuation in alertness, cognition, or other mental function arising over the last 2 weeks and still evident in the last 24 hours

A 4AT score of 4 or more suggests possible delirium, with or without cognitive impairment, and warrants further clinical assessment. A score of 1 to 3 suggests possible cognitive impairment, and a score of 0 makes delirium and severe cognitive impairment unlikely (though it does not exclude milder or fluctuating presentations, so clinical suspicion should still guide further review).

The Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) is the internationally validated diagnostic algorithm underpinning most delirium screening, and it requires the presence of both:

  1. Acute onset and fluctuating course, and
  2. Inattention

plus at least one of:

  1. Disorganised thinking, or
  2. Altered level of consciousness

Candidates should understand this as a conceptual algorithm rather than memorising a rigid scoring system: delirium requires acute/fluctuating inattention, plus either disorganised thinking or altered consciousness. This structure recurs across most delirium screening tools used internationally, and grasping the logic rather than a specific instrument's exact wording is what the AKT actually rewards.

Causes of Delirium

Delirium is almost always multifactorial, arising from an interaction between predisposing factors (baseline vulnerability) and precipitating factors (the acute insult). A useful mnemonic-free approach is to systematically consider causes across body systems:

  • Infection — urinary tract infection, pneumonia, and infective exacerbations are among the most common precipitants in older inpatients
  • Metabolic disturbance — hypoglycaemia, hyperglycaemia, hyponatraemia, hypercalcaemia, uraemia, hepatic impairment, hypoxia, hypercapnia
  • Drugs — opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, corticosteroids, and any recently started or withdrawn medication (including alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal)
  • Pain — untreated or undertreated pain, especially after surgery or fracture
  • Constipation and urinary retention — frequently overlooked but easily reversible precipitants
  • Environmental change — unfamiliar surroundings, sensory deprivation (missing glasses or hearing aids), sleep disruption
  • Cardiovascular and neurological events — myocardial infarction, stroke, subdural haematoma (especially after a fall), seizure (including non-convulsive status)
  • Alcohol withdrawal — a specific and important cause requiring its own management pathway

Predisposing risk factors that make a patient more vulnerable to any given precipitant include pre-existing dementia or cognitive impairment, advanced age, frailty, sensory impairment, multiple comorbidities, and a prior history of delirium. A key AKT reasoning point is that dementia is the single strongest predisposing factor for delirium, so a patient with known dementia who develops an acute worsening of confusion should be actively screened for delirium rather than assumed to simply be experiencing disease progression.

Distinguishing Delirium from Dementia — and Why It Matters

Correctly distinguishing delirium from dementia is arguably the single highest-yield discrimination task in this entire content area, because the two conditions require entirely different immediate management: delirium demands urgent search for and treatment of a reversible underlying cause, while dementia is managed as a chronic, progressive condition that, in its advanced stages, increasingly overlaps with the goals-of-care and symptom-control principles covered in our palliative and end-of-life care revision guide. Missing an underlying delirium by assuming a confused older patient is "just demented" is a recognised and dangerous error, since it can delay treatment of a life-threatening precipitant such as sepsis. This single distinction accounts for a disproportionate share of the marks available in care of the elderly UKMLA scenarios involving acute confusion.

In practice, the two conditions frequently coexist — dementia predisposes strongly to delirium, and an older patient with known dementia can absolutely develop a superimposed delirium on top of their baseline cognitive impairment. The safest clinical (and exam) approach when faced with any acutely confused older patient, regardless of cognitive history, is to assume delirium until proven otherwise, actively search for a reversible cause, and only attribute confusion to "just dementia" once delirium has been reasonably excluded or its precipitants treated.

Managing Delirium

Management prioritises identifying and treating the underlying cause, alongside supportive, non-pharmacological measures. Core supportive strategies include reorientation (clear communication, visible clocks and calendars, familiar faces), ensuring sensory aids (glasses, hearing aids) are in use, maintaining hydration and nutrition, promoting normal sleep-wake cycles, encouraging early mobilisation, and avoiding unnecessary ward moves. Environmental modification alone reduces both the incidence and duration of delirium in at-risk inpatients.

Pharmacological treatment is reserved for patients who are severely agitated and at risk of harming themselves or others, or where non-pharmacological measures have failed and the patient is in significant distress — it is not used routinely and never as a substitute for treating the underlying cause. When medication is required, low-dose short-term antipsychotics (such as haloperidol) are typically used with caution, and are specifically avoided in Parkinson's disease and dementia with Lewy bodies, where antipsychotic sensitivity can cause severe, sometimes irreversible, deterioration. Recognising this contraindication is a classic care of the elderly UKMLA safe-prescribing point.

Falls Assessment and Prevention

Falls assessment is another cornerstone of care of the elderly UKMLA revision, since a fall is rarely a purely mechanical event in a frail patient — it is usually the visible symptom of an underlying vulnerability.

Why Falls Matter in Frail Older Adults

Falls are among the most common reasons for older adults to present to UK emergency departments, and they carry disproportionate consequences in frail patients: fractures (particularly of the hip, wrist, and vertebrae), head injury, loss of confidence, fear of falling, reduced mobility, and a well-recognised downward spiral into further deconditioning and dependency. A fall in an older person should never be dismissed as a trivial mechanical event without at least considering whether it represents a marker of an underlying acute illness, such as infection, arrhythmia, or postural hypotension.

Multifactorial Falls Risk Assessment

NICE guidance recommends a multifactorial falls risk assessment for any older person presenting after a fall, or who reports recurrent falls, or who demonstrates gait or balance abnormalities. This assessment is deliberately broad because falls are rarely due to a single cause — the key components include:

  • Gait, balance, and mobility — often formally assessed using tools such as the Timed Up and Go test
  • Muscle strength — especially lower limb strength, closely linked to sarcopenia
  • Bone health and fracture risk — osteoporosis assessment, often using FRAX or similar tools
  • Perceived functional ability and fear of falling
  • Visual impairment
  • Cognitive impairment — including undiagnosed delirium or dementia
  • Urinary incontinence — a recognised, often overlooked, falls risk factor
  • Home hazards — loose rugs, poor lighting, cluttered walkways, inappropriate footwear
  • Cardiovascular examination — including lying and standing blood pressure to detect orthostatic (postural) hypotension
  • Medication review — particularly sedatives, antihypertensives, and any drug with a known fall-associated side effect profile

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) sets out this multifactorial approach in its clinical guideline on the assessment and prevention of falls in older people, which remains the reference standard cited across UK clinical practice for this topic and a frequently quoted source in care of the elderly UKMLA revision materials.

Evidence-Based Falls Prevention

Once risk factors are identified, prevention is targeted and multifactorial rather than generic. Effective interventions include structured strength and balance training programmes (such as supervised group exercise classes specifically designed for fall prevention), home hazard assessment and modification by an occupational therapist, correction of visual impairment (updated glasses prescriptions, cataract surgery where appropriate), medication review and reduction of psychotropic or other fall-risk drugs, and treatment of postural hypotension.

Vitamin D and calcium supplementation is considered for patients found to be deficient or at high fracture risk, though it is not a substitute for the multifactorial interventions above. Falls prevention should always be considered alongside bone protection — a patient with confirmed osteoporosis or a fragility fracture history should be assessed for pharmacological fracture prevention (such as bisphosphonates) in addition to falls-specific interventions, since preventing the fall and protecting bone strength are complementary, not interchangeable, strategies.

A distinctly examinable point is that single interventions in isolation are far less effective than a multifactorial, individually tailored programme — the AKT frequently rewards recognising that a scenario describing several risk factors (poor vision, four regular medications including a benzodiazepine, reduced grip strength, and a rug in the hallway) requires a coordinated multi-domain response, not simply "refer to physiotherapy" alone.

Polypharmacy and Medication Review in Older Adults

Safe prescribing is one of the most consistently tested applied-knowledge skills in care of the elderly UKMLA questions, since the same drug that is entirely appropriate for a fit 40-year-old can be actively harmful for a frail 85-year-old.

What Polypharmacy Means and Why It Is Dangerous in Frailty

Polypharmacy is generally defined as the concurrent use of five or more regular medications, though the more clinically meaningful distinction is between appropriate polypharmacy (multiple drugs prescribed in line with best evidence to manage genuine multimorbidity, with benefits still outweighing harms) and problematic polypharmacy (where the risk of harm from drug interactions, adverse effects, or reduced quality of life outweighs the potential benefit). Frail older adults are especially vulnerable to problematic polypharmacy because of altered pharmacokinetics (reduced renal clearance, altered volume of distribution, reduced hepatic metabolism), altered pharmacodynamics (increased sensitivity to sedatives, antihypertensives, and anticholinergic drugs), and cumulative anticholinergic and sedative burden across multiple prescriptions.

Prescribing safely for frail, multimorbid patients often requires different reasoning than prescribing for younger, single-disease patients, and this connects directly to the broader dose-adjustment principles covered in our guide on how organ impairment changes prescribing decisions, since declining renal function is near-universal in advanced age even without a formal diagnosis of chronic kidney disease.

The STOPP/START Concept

Structured, explicit medication review tools underpin safe deprescribing in the UK, and the best known of these is the STOPP/START framework (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions / Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment). Candidates do not need to memorise every individual STOPP/START criterion, but should understand the underlying concept and be able to apply its logic to a clinical scenario:

  • STOPP criteria identify potentially inappropriate medications that should usually be stopped or reconsidered in older adults — classic examples include long-term benzodiazepines in patients at risk of falls or sedation, NSAIDs in patients with a history of peptic ulcer disease or significant renal impairment without gastroprotection, and any drug continued despite no ongoing clinical indication
  • START criteria identify appropriate, evidence-based medications that are often omitted in older patients despite a clear indication — such as anticoagulation in atrial fibrillation, bone protection in confirmed osteoporosis, or an ACE inhibitor in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction

The core exam lesson is that medication review in older adults is a two-directional process: it is just as much about identifying under-treatment (drugs that should have been started but were not) as it is about identifying over-treatment (drugs that should be stopped). A common AKT trap is a scenario that only tests whether a candidate can spot a drug to stop, when the correct answer actually requires recognising a missed indication for starting a beneficial treatment — a pattern that recurs constantly across care of the elderly UKMLA prescribing scenarios.

A Structured Approach to Deprescribing

A safe, structured medication review in a frail older patient typically follows a repeatable sequence: establish a complete and accurate medication history (including over-the-counter and herbal preparations), confirm the ongoing indication for every drug, assess whether the patient's goals of care and life expectancy still justify preventive medications with a long time-to-benefit (such as statins for primary prevention in advanced frailty), check for drug-drug and drug-disease interactions, consider the cumulative anticholinergic and sedative burden, and prioritise deprescribing decisions collaboratively with the patient, discussing benefits, harms, and the patient's own priorities rather than making unilateral changes.

A well-answered care of the elderly UKMLA question on this theme will explicitly weigh both directions of STOPP/START logic before committing to an answer. Deprescribing should always be done one drug at a time where possible, with a clear plan for monitoring and a fallback plan if symptoms recur, since abrupt withdrawal of some drug classes (beta-blockers, benzodiazepines, corticosteroids, antidepressants) carries its own withdrawal risk. This reflects the same careful, individualised prescribing philosophy discussed in our wider pharmacology and prescribing safety revision guide, and directly overlaps with the medication-error reasoning tested in our patient safety and prescribing errors guide.

Pressure Ulcer Risk and Prevention

Pressure ulcer prevention rounds out the care of the elderly UKMLA syllabus and is frequently tested as an incidental safety point buried inside an unrelated clinical scenario.

Recognising Who Is at Risk

Pressure ulcers (also called pressure sores or bedsores) develop when sustained pressure, shear, or friction over a bony prominence impairs local blood supply, leading to tissue ischaemia and breakdown. Frail, immobile, and cognitively impaired older patients are at disproportionately high risk, and pressure ulcer prevention is one of the clearest examples of how the four themes in this guide interconnect in practice: a frail patient with delirium who is less mobile and less able to reposition themselves, potentially on sedating medication from a poorly reviewed drug regimen, is at compounded risk.

Key risk factors include reduced mobility, impaired sensation (including neuropathy and reduced consciousness level), poor nutrition, incontinence (moisture damages skin integrity), reduced skin elasticity with age, and significant acute illness. Validated risk assessment tools such as the Waterlow score are used across NHS settings to formally quantify pressure ulcer risk and trigger appropriate preventive measures on admission and at regular intervals thereafter.

Staging and Recognition

Pressure ulcers are commonly staged from 1 to 4 based on depth and tissue involvement:

StageDescription
Stage 1Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin, usually over a bony prominence
Stage 2Partial-thickness skin loss involving the epidermis and/or dermis, presenting as a shallow open ulcer or intact/ruptured blister
Stage 3Full-thickness skin loss with visible subcutaneous fat, but bone, tendon, or muscle not exposed
Stage 4Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle

Common sites include the sacrum, heels, ischial tuberosities, and greater trochanters — essentially any bony prominence subjected to sustained pressure in a patient who is not repositioned regularly, particularly those lying supine for extended periods or sitting in a chair without adequate offloading. Recognising these sites and stages quickly is a practical, easily scored care of the elderly UKMLA skill.

Prevention Strategies

Prevention is far more effective, and far cheaper in both human and financial terms, than treatment once a pressure ulcer has developed. Core preventive measures include regular repositioning (commonly at least every two to four hours for high-risk patients, individualised to the patient's risk level), pressure-redistributing mattresses and cushions, maintaining good nutrition and hydration (protein and overall nutritional status directly influence skin integrity and wound healing capacity), meticulous skin care with attention to moisture and continence management, and early mobilisation wherever clinically possible.

Structured risk assessment on admission, with a clear repositioning and equipment plan documented and reviewed regularly, is now standard practice across NHS inpatient settings and reflects a broader patient-safety culture that treats pressure ulcers as a largely preventable harm rather than an inevitable consequence of illness or age. This preventive, whole-system mindset mirrors the wider approach to avoidable harm discussed in our prescribing errors and patient safety guide, and is a recurring theme across NHS quality improvement priorities and across care of the elderly UKMLA revision more broadly.

Bringing It Together: An Integrated Approach for the AKT

The genuinely high-scoring approach to care of the elderly UKMLA questions is to see frailty, delirium, falls, polypharmacy, and pressure ulcers not as five separate revision topics but as five expressions of the same underlying vulnerability. A frail patient (high Clinical Frailty Scale score) is more likely to develop delirium when acutely unwell, is more likely to fall as a consequence of that delirium or the medications used to manage other conditions, and is more likely to develop a pressure ulcer if that fall results in a period of reduced mobility. Recognising these interconnections is precisely what comprehensive geriatric assessment is designed to capture, and it is exactly the kind of joined-up clinical reasoning the UKMLA AKT rewards.

For UK medical students, embedding this integrated thinking early — rather than treating geriatric medicine as a single rotation to revise in isolation — pays dividends across almost every other system-based topic in the exam. For IMGs, particularly those whose prior training emphasised single-disease management in younger populations, deliberately practising multimorbidity-based AKT questions that combine two or three of these themes in one stem is one of the most efficient ways to close this specific knowledge gap. Reviewing how urological presentations are tested alongside general medical reasoning in our urology revision guide can also help, since urinary symptoms, retention, and incontinence recur constantly as both delirium precipitants and falls risk factors in this population — another reminder that care of the elderly UKMLA revision rarely stays neatly inside a single system.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between frailty and just being old in care of the elderly UKMLA questions?

Frailty is a distinct clinical syndrome defined by reduced physiological reserve across multiple body systems, making a person vulnerable to rapid health decline from relatively minor stressor events, whereas simply being old refers only to chronological age. Not every older adult is frail, and some frail patients are younger with complex chronic disease; clinicians assess frailty using structured tools such as the Clinical Frailty Scale rather than assuming it from age alone.

What score on the Clinical Frailty Scale indicates significant frailty?

A Clinical Frailty Scale score of 5 or above generally indicates at least mild frailty, with scores of 6 and 7 representing moderate and severe frailty respectively, and scores of 8 to 9 reflecting very severe frailty or a terminal trajectory. The scale is based on a patient's function roughly two weeks before their current acute illness and should not be applied to people with stable, lifelong disabilities who have never experienced a decline in baseline function.

How can doctors tell the difference between delirium and dementia at the bedside?

Delirium develops acutely over hours to days, fluctuates (often worsening at night), and characteristically impairs attention and consciousness, whereas dementia develops insidiously over months to years, follows a relatively stable day-to-day course, and preserves attention until later stages. Because dementia is the strongest single risk factor for developing delirium, any acute worsening of confusion in a patient with known dementia should prompt active screening for a new, reversible delirium rather than being dismissed as disease progression.

What tools are used to screen for delirium in UK hospitals?

The 4AT is the most widely used rapid bedside delirium screening tool in UK practice, assessing alertness, an abbreviated mental test, attention (reciting months backwards), and acute or fluctuating change, with a score of 4 or more suggesting possible delirium requiring further assessment. The Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) is the internationally validated diagnostic algorithm underpinning most screening tools, requiring acute/fluctuating inattention plus either disorganised thinking or altered consciousness.

What does a multifactorial falls risk assessment involve?

A multifactorial falls risk assessment evaluates gait and balance, muscle strength, bone health and fracture risk, vision, cognition, continence, home hazards, cardiovascular status including lying and standing blood pressure, and current medications, since falls in older adults are rarely caused by a single factor. NICE recommends this structured, multi-domain assessment for anyone who has fallen, reports recurrent falls, or shows gait or balance abnormalities, followed by individually tailored, multi-component prevention rather than a single generic intervention.

Why is polypharmacy a major focus of care of the elderly UKMLA questions?

Polypharmacy generally refers to the concurrent use of five or more regular medications, and it becomes problematic when the cumulative risk of harm outweighs the benefit, which is especially likely in frail older adults due to altered drug handling and increased drug sensitivity. STOPP/START is a structured, two-directional medication review framework: STOPP criteria flag potentially inappropriate medications that should usually be stopped, while START criteria flag beneficial, evidence-based treatments that are often missed and should be started.

How can pressure ulcers be prevented in frail older patients?

Pressure ulcer prevention centres on regular repositioning (often every two to four hours for high-risk patients), use of pressure-redistributing mattresses and cushions, good nutrition and hydration, careful skin and continence care, and early mobilisation wherever possible, guided by a formal risk assessment such as the Waterlow score completed on admission and reviewed regularly. Because immobility, delirium, and polypharmacy-related sedation frequently coexist in frail patients, pressure ulcer prevention is most effective when embedded within a broader comprehensive geriatric assessment rather than treated as an isolated nursing task.

Conclusion

Care of the elderly UKMLA content rewards candidates who understand frailty, delirium, falls, polypharmacy, and pressure ulcers as interconnected expressions of reduced physiological reserve, assessed through the structured lens of comprehensive geriatric assessment, rather than as isolated facts to memorise. Given the UK's ageing population and the NHS's heavy reliance on frailty-literate doctors across almost every specialty, mastering this domain pays off across a disproportionate share of the AKT, not just the questions explicitly labelled geriatric medicine.
