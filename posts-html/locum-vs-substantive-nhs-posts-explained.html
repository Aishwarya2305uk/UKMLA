Weighing up locum vs substantive NHS posts is one of the most consequential practical decisions a doctor makes once GMC registration and a licence to practise are in place — and it is a decision that applies equally to UK medical graduates finishing Foundation Programme and to international medical graduates (IMGs) arriving for their first NHS role. A substantive post is a permanent, employed position with a single Trust; locum work is temporary, arranged shift-by-shift or in short blocks, either through the Trust's own staff bank or via an external locum agency. Neither option is objectively "better" — each suits a different stage of career, a different appetite for flexibility versus security, and a different set of longer-term goals around training, pensions, and portfolio-building. This guide sets out, in practical and honest terms, what each employment type actually involves, who tends to benefit from which, and the specific considerations IMGs should weigh before choosing one path over the other.

This is deliberately a different question from the one covered in the site's companion guide on the Locally Employed Doctor (LED) route to full GMC registration, which focuses on the CESR/CEGPR portfolio pathway open to doctors already working substantively outside a numbered training programme — and it is also a separate question from the general process of GMC registration itself, which this guide assumes is already complete. That article is about a specific registration mechanism for experienced doctors already settled into NHS employment. This article is about something that comes earlier and applies far more broadly: the basic structural choice, available to almost any doctor at almost any career stage, between taking a permanent Trust contract and working flexibly through bank or agency locum shifts. Many doctors move between the two more than once across a career, and understanding the trade-offs clearly, before committing either way, avoids a lot of avoidable frustration later.

The sections below explain what a substantive post actually involves, what locum and bank work look like in practice, the genuine pros and cons of each for pay, flexibility, job security, training access, and pension continuity, and how IMGs specifically should think about locum work as a possible stepping stone while longer-term registration and visa arrangements are being settled — while being clear that visa sponsorship rules differ meaningfully between agency locum work and a substantive sponsored post, and that this is a point to check with a qualified immigration adviser rather than assume.

Quick Answer: Locum vs Substantive NHS Posts

The core difference in the locum vs substantive NHS posts comparison is contractual permanence. A substantive post is a permanent employment contract with a single NHS Trust, offering job security, structured access to study leave and training opportunities, continuous NHS Pension Scheme membership, and a stable base for building a long-term UK career and portfolio. Locum work — whether booked through a Trust's internal staff bank or an external locum agency — is temporary, paid by the shift or in short blocks, generally at a higher hourly rate, and offers far greater control over which shifts, specialties, and locations a doctor works, but without the same guaranteed continuity of income, training access, or pension contributions.

Neither route is inherently the "correct" choice, and a doctor's own priorities should drive the decision rather than a general assumption that one is always superior. A doctor who values predictable income, structured career progression, automatic pension continuity, and easier access to study leave and internal training opportunities is usually better served by a substantive post. A doctor who values flexibility over which shifts to accept, the ability to work around other commitments (further study, family responsibilities, a second income stream, or exploring different specialties and Trusts before settling), and who is comfortable managing their own pension and CPD arrangements more actively, may find locum or bank work genuinely preferable, at least for a defined period.

For IMGs specifically, the picture carries an additional layer of complexity: locum work is sometimes used as a practical stepping stone while a doctor is still finalising longer-term registration or securing sponsorship for a substantive post, but the visa implications of agency locum work versus a substantive, sponsored role are genuinely different and must be checked individually with a qualified immigration adviser — this guide flags where those differences matter rather than assuming a single answer applies to every doctor's circumstances. The detailed sections that follow work through each dimension of the comparison — pay, flexibility, security, training access, and pension — before turning to the IMG-specific considerations and a realistic view of how bank and locum work fit into an early-career UK strategy.

What a Substantive NHS Post Actually Involves

A substantive post is a permanent contract of employment with a single NHS Trust (or, less commonly, another NHS employer such as an integrated care board or a GP practice acting as an employer). "Substantive" simply means the post is not time-limited — it continues indefinitely, subject to the usual protections and processes of UK employment law, rather than ending automatically after a fixed term or a single shift. This is the employment model that underlies numbered training programme posts, SAS doctor roles, many Locally Employed Doctor (LED) and Trust grade posts, and consultant or GP partner positions alike, though the term "substantive" is most often used to distinguish a permanent post from a fixed-term or locum one.

Salary Structure and Pay Progression

Substantive NHS medical posts are paid according to nationally agreed pay scales set out in the relevant terms and conditions of service — separate scales exist for doctors and dentists in training, for SAS doctors, and for consultants — with pay progressing through defined increments as a doctor gains experience or moves up a training grade. This creates predictable, transparent pay progression: a doctor in a substantive post generally knows, in advance, what their salary will be for the coming year and how it is likely to change as they progress, which is considerably harder to forecast for a doctor relying primarily on locum shifts, where earnings depend on how much work is available and accepted in any given month.

Structured Access to Training, Study Leave, and Supervision

One of the most significant practical advantages of a substantive post, particularly for a doctor still early in their UK career, is structured access to training and professional development. Substantive employees are typically entitled to funded study leave (the exact allowance varies by grade and Trust, but is a standard contractual feature), access to the Trust's internal teaching programme, a named educational or clinical supervisor who tracks progress over time, and inclusion in the department's formal appraisal cycle. For a doctor in a numbered training programme, this also means the curriculum-mapped rotations and the Annual Review of Competence Progression (ARCP) process that track progress towards a Certificate of Completion of Training. Locum doctors, by contrast, do not automatically receive any of this: study leave is not a standard feature of a locum booking, and there is no guarantee of continuity of supervision from one booking to the next, since a locum doctor may work with a different team, and sometimes a different Trust, every few weeks.

Continuous NHS Pension Scheme Membership

A substantive post generally means automatic, continuous enrolment in the NHS Pension Scheme through a single employer, with contributions deducted consistently from every payslip and a single, unbroken employment record underpinning the pension calculation. This continuity matters more than it might initially seem: NHS pension benefits are calculated with reference to pensionable pay and length of service, and a clean, unbroken substantive employment record makes both easier to track and less prone to administrative gaps than pension contributions accumulated across multiple bank or agency engagements, some of which may not offer NHS Pension Scheme access at all depending on the specific arrangement.

Job Security and Standard Employment Protections

A substantive post carries the full range of standard UK employment protections: a defined notice period on either side, protection against unfair dismissal after the relevant qualifying period, statutory sick pay and NHS occupational sick pay entitlements, maternity/paternity/adoption leave entitlements, and continuous service that counts towards certain NHS benefits and entitlements that accrue with length of service. A locum engagement, particularly one booked shift-by-shift through an agency, typically carries none of these in the same form — a locum doctor is often engaged on a basis closer to a series of individual contracts for services than a single continuous employment relationship, which is precisely why locum work trades security for flexibility rather than offering both simultaneously. Substantive posts are also directly subject to the standard working-hours and rest-break rules that apply to doctors in the NHS, since a substantive rota is built and monitored by the employing department against those limits; locum bookings are equally bound by the same rules, but a doctor juggling shifts across more than one Trust or agency carries a greater share of the responsibility for tracking their own cumulative hours and rest periods, since no single rota coordinator sees the whole picture.

What Locum and Bank Work Actually Involve

Locum work is temporary medical work, arranged either through an NHS Trust's own internal staff bank or through an external locum agency, and paid by the shift, the day, or in short defined blocks rather than through a continuous salary. The two routes — bank and agency — share the same basic temporary, flexible structure but differ in some important practical respects worth understanding before choosing between them.

NHS Staff Banks vs External Locum Agencies

An NHS staff bank is a pool of doctors (and other staff) held directly by an individual Trust, or sometimes shared across a group of neighbouring Trusts, who pick up additional shifts within that Trust or Trust group on a flexible basis. Working via a Trust's own bank generally means simpler onboarding for shifts within that Trust, since the doctor is already known to the Trust's systems, and NHS Employers guidance is explicit that doctors intending to take on additional locum hours should first offer those hours to an NHS staff bank before turning to an external agency — a "fidelity to the NHS" principle intended to keep locum capacity within the NHS system itself wherever possible. An external locum agency, by contrast, is a private intermediary that places doctors into shifts across multiple Trusts, and can offer wider access to shifts across a broader geographic area or range of specialties than a single Trust's own bank, generally in exchange for an agency fee built into the arrangement. This guide does not recommend or name any specific agency — the right choice between bank and agency work depends on a doctor's own priorities around Trust familiarity, geographic flexibility, and administrative simplicity, and is worth discussing directly with a Trust's own medical staffing office when the time comes.

How Locum Shifts Are Booked and Paid

Locum shifts are typically advertised through an online booking system — either the Trust's own bank portal or an agency's platform — listing the specialty, grade, dates, and hourly or shift rate on offer. A doctor browses available shifts and accepts the ones that fit their availability and preferences, with no obligation to accept any particular shift and no guarantee that a preferred shift will remain available by the time it is selected. Payment is typically processed on a timesheet basis: the doctor submits a timesheet for hours actually worked, which is verified (often by the responsible consultant or rota coordinator) before payment is released, generally on a weekly or monthly cycle depending on the bank or agency's own processing schedule. This is a fundamentally different payment rhythm from a substantive post's fixed monthly salary, and doctors new to locum work should budget for the fact that early payments may be less predictable in timing while the administrative relationship is being established.

Flexibility Over Location, Specialty, and Working Pattern

The single biggest practical draw of locum work is control: a locum doctor generally chooses which shifts to accept, which means real control over working pattern (day shifts only, occasional nights, a specific number of hours per week), geographic location (useful for a doctor balancing family commitments, further study, or simply wanting to work in a specific city or region), and even specialty exposure, since some doctors deliberately use locum shifts across different specialties to build broader clinical experience before committing to a substantive post in a particular field. This flexibility is genuinely valuable for doctors managing competing demands — further postgraduate exams, caring responsibilities, or simply wanting a period of lower-intensity work — that a fixed substantive rota does not easily accommodate.

Higher Headline Pay, With the Trade-Offs That Come With It

Locum hourly rates, particularly for less popular shifts, unsocial hours, or hard-to-fill specialties and locations, are often higher than the equivalent substantive hourly rate once the substantive salary is converted to an hourly figure. This is a genuine and well-documented feature of the NHS locum market, driven by the same basic economics that apply to any flexible, on-demand labour market: Trusts pay a premium to fill gaps at short notice. However, this headline pay advantage needs to be weighed honestly against everything a substantive post includes that a locum rate does not — employer pension contributions, paid annual leave, sick pay, funded study leave, and job security among them — all of which have real financial value even though they do not appear as a cash figure on a locum payslip. A doctor comparing the two purely on hourly rate, without accounting for these differences, will systematically overstate how much better locum work pays in practice.

Career Progression: Locum Work vs a Substantive Post

Beyond pay and flexibility, the comparison that matters most for a doctor thinking several years ahead is career progression — how each employment type affects training access, portfolio-building, and the practicalities of applying for future posts, whether that is a numbered training programme, a CESR/CEGPR application, or eventually a consultant or GP partner role.

Structured Training and Study Leave Access

Doctors in substantive posts, and especially those in numbered training programme posts, have structured, funded access to study leave, internal departmental teaching, and often external courses relevant to their specialty, built into their contract and their programme's curriculum. Locum doctors do not receive this automatically: a locum booking is, by design, a discrete engagement to cover clinical service, not a training placement, and study leave in the formal, funded sense is not a standard feature of locum work. A doctor who spends an extended period doing locum work exclusively, without a substantive base, should therefore expect to fund and organise their own continuing professional development considerably more actively than a colleague in a substantive post, where much of this is arranged and paid for by the employer as a matter of course.

Continuity for Appraisal, Revalidation, and Portfolio-Building

Every licensed doctor in the UK must participate in annual appraisal and revalidation, whether working substantively, as a locum, or a mixture of both — this is a GMC requirement that applies regardless of employment type. However, the practicalities differ meaningfully. A doctor in a substantive post typically has one designated body (usually their employing Trust) and one appraiser who sees their practice consistently over time, making it straightforward to build a coherent annual appraisal portfolio. A doctor who works predominantly as a locum, particularly one who moves between multiple Trusts or agencies, must actively identify and maintain a single designated body for revalidation purposes, and must proactively collect supporting information — reflective notes, feedback, significant event reviews, CPD records — from every place they have worked during the appraisal year, rather than relying on one employer's systems to hold it all in one place. The GMC's own guidance on revalidation is explicit that doctors who work for multiple organisations or locum agencies must keep their designated body connection up to date in their GMC Online account and gather evidence from every setting they have worked in — a materially heavier administrative burden than the equivalent task for a substantive employee.

This continuity gap matters even more for a doctor who is, or may in future be, building a portfolio towards a CESR or CEGPR application via the route described in the site's guide to the Locally Employed Doctor pathway to full GMC registration. That guide explains how a CESR/CEGPR portfolio depends heavily on consistent, well-documented evidence — consultant references, appraisal records, and a clear curriculum-mapped account of clinical experience over time. A doctor who spends years moving between short locum bookings, without a stable base and a small number of consultants who have observed their practice consistently, will generally find it considerably harder to assemble this kind of coherent evidence trail than a doctor who has spent an equivalent period in one or two substantive posts with continuous supervision. This is not a reason to avoid locum work altogether, but it is a real, practical cost that should be weighed against its flexibility benefits by any doctor with CESR/CEGPR or ARCP-tracked training progression as a medium-term goal.

How Locum Experience Is Viewed in Future Job and Training Applications

Locum experience is genuine, legitimate clinical experience, and it counts towards a doctor's overall record when applying for future substantive posts, numbered training programmes, or a CESR/CEGPR application — it is not disregarded simply because it was not substantive. However, because locum placements are often shorter and more fragmented than a substantive post, and because supervision and references from a series of short bookings are naturally less in-depth than those from a supervisor who has worked with a doctor continuously over a year or more, locum-only experience can be somewhat harder to present coherently in a structured application, whether that is an application form for a numbered training programme or a portfolio for CESR/CEGPR. Doctors who use locum work strategically — deliberately returning to the same Trust or department repeatedly, building a relationship with a small number of consultants who can speak knowledgeably to their practice over time — tend to mitigate this disadvantage considerably compared with those who move between unconnected bookings with no continuity at all. Keeping a well-organised, continuously updated e-portfolio record of clinical activity, reflections, and supervisor feedback is one of the most effective ways a locum doctor can offset the natural fragmentation of moving between placements, since it creates a single, consistent evidence trail even when the underlying clinical settings change frequently.

Pension Considerations: NHS Pension Scheme Continuity

Pension continuity is one of the less obvious, but genuinely significant, differences between locum and substantive work, and one that is easy to underweight early in a career when retirement feels distant.

How Substantive Employment Simplifies Pension Contributions

A doctor in a substantive post is automatically enrolled in the NHS Pension Scheme through their single employer, with contributions deducted consistently every payslip and recorded against one continuous employment record. This makes pension accrual straightforward to track and administratively simple: one employer, one set of contribution records, and a clean history for the pension scheme administrators to calculate benefits against when the time comes.

How Locum and Bank Work Can Complicate Pension Continuity

Working via an NHS Trust's staff bank generally does allow continued NHS Pension Scheme membership, since the bank itself is an NHS employer arrangement — but a doctor who works across multiple banks, or supplements bank shifts with agency locum work (where NHS Pension Scheme access depends on the specific engagement and is not guaranteed in the same way), can end up with pension contributions spread across several separate records rather than one continuous one. Gaps between bookings, periods with no locum work at all, and engagements that do not offer NHS Pension Scheme access can all interrupt what would otherwise be a continuous contribution history. None of this makes locum work incompatible with a healthy NHS pension outcome — many doctors combine bank shifts with NHS Pension Scheme membership successfully — but it does mean a doctor relying heavily on locum work should check their own pension records periodically (via the NHS Pensions member self-service portal) rather than assuming contributions are accumulating as smoothly as they would in a single substantive post, and should be particularly careful to confirm whether any given agency booking offers NHS Pension Scheme access at all before assuming it does.

Practical Steps for Locum Doctors to Protect Pension Continuity

Doctors who plan to do a significant amount of locum work, whether as a deliberate long-term choice or as a bridge between substantive posts, benefit from a small number of practical habits: prioritising NHS staff bank shifts over non-pensionable agency arrangements where the choice is available and the pay difference is not decisive; checking, before accepting a booking, whether that specific engagement offers NHS Pension Scheme membership; keeping a personal record of every employer or bank worked through during a tax year, since this makes it far easier to check contribution records later; and periodically reviewing accumulated pension records through the official NHS Pensions member portal rather than assuming everything has been correctly recorded automatically. None of this is complicated, but it does require active attention that a substantive post's automatic, single-employer enrolment simply does not demand.

Locum Work for IMGs: A Stepping Stone, With Visa Caveats

For IMGs specifically, the locum vs substantive NHS posts decision carries an additional layer that UK graduates generally do not need to weigh in the same way: the interaction between employment type and immigration status. This section sets out the practical pattern many IMGs follow, while being explicit that the visa detail must be checked individually rather than assumed.

Why Locum Work Is Sometimes Used as a Bridge

It is genuinely common for an IMG who has recently completed GMC registration — a process covered in more detail in the site's guide to the pathway from passing the UKMLA to securing an NHS job — to pick up bank or locum shifts in the period immediately after registration, while a longer-term substantive post, whether that is a numbered training programme place or a Locally Employed Doctor role, is still being arranged or is waiting for its start date. Locum work in this context serves a genuinely useful bridging function: it generates income and UK clinical experience without requiring the doctor to wait, unpaid, for a substantive post to begin, and it can also help a doctor build the kind of UK-specific clinical experience and references that make a subsequent substantive application stronger.

Visa Sponsorship: Locum Agency Work vs a Substantive Sponsored Post

This is the single most important caveat for any IMG considering locum work as part of their early UK career strategy, and it deserves to be stated plainly rather than glossed over: visa sponsorship arrangements differ meaningfully between agency locum work and a substantive, sponsored NHS post, and exactly how they differ depends on a doctor's specific visa route, sponsor, and personal circumstances. A substantive post with an NHS Trust that holds a sponsor licence can support a Skilled Worker or Health and Care Worker visa in the most straightforward way most doctors picture — a single employer sponsoring a single, defined role. Locum work, particularly through an external agency, involves a different sponsorship relationship, since the doctor may be engaged by, or paid through, an entity that is not the same as the Trust where the clinical work is actually performed, and not every locum agency holds the sponsor licence category or visa route compatibility that a particular doctor's immigration status requires.

This guide deliberately does not attempt to state a single fixed rule about which arrangements do or do not work for a given visa category, because the honest answer is that it depends on the specific visa route, the specific agency or bank's own sponsorship arrangements, and the individual doctor's circumstances — and because UK immigration rules, including the salary threshold and sponsorship requirements covered in the site's dedicated guide to the 2026 visa salary threshold changes for doctors, are reviewed and updated periodically by the Home Office. The responsible, honest advice is this: any IMG considering locum or agency work as part of their visa-sponsored UK career should check their specific situation directly with a qualified immigration adviser or their sponsoring Trust's international recruitment or HR team before assuming that locum earnings, or a switch between locum and substantive work, will not affect their visa position. Getting this wrong can have serious immigration consequences, and it is not a question this guide, or any general online resource, can safely answer with a one-size-fits-all rule.

Building UK-Specific Evidence Through Locum Work

Where locum work clearly does help most IMGs, regardless of the visa detail above, is in building UK-specific clinical experience and references ahead of a substantive application. UK medical practice has its own clinical governance conventions, documentation standards, and multidisciplinary working patterns that differ from many doctors' prior training environments, and locum shifts — even short ones — provide direct, practical exposure to these systems. An IMG who uses a period of locum work deliberately, returning where possible to the same department or Trust rather than scattering shifts randomly, and proactively asking supervising consultants for structured feedback and references, can turn what might otherwise be a purely income-generating bridge into a genuinely useful stepping stone towards a stronger substantive application later, whether that application is for a numbered training post, an LED role with a longer-term view to CESR/CEGPR progression, or a direct SAS appointment.

Balancing Income Needs Against Long-Term Career Positioning

IMGs, more than most UK graduates, often face immediate financial pressures in the period after arriving in the UK — visa and relocation costs, GMC registration fees, and the general expense of establishing a new life in a new country all tend to land in a short window. Locum work's higher headline hourly pay can be genuinely useful in meeting these short-term needs. But doctors in this position should weigh that short-term benefit against the longer-term costs discussed throughout this guide — fragmented pension contributions, weaker training-portfolio continuity, and the added administrative burden of appraisal and revalidation across multiple engagements — and should treat locum work as a deliberate, time-limited bridging strategy with a clear eye on when and how they plan to move into a substantive post, rather than an indefinite default simply because it is the fastest way to start earning.

How Bank and Locum Work Fit Into a Realistic Early-Career UK Strategy

Pulling the threads of this guide together, bank and locum work are best understood not as a permanent alternative to substantive employment, but as one legitimate tool among several in a doctor's early-career UK strategy — genuinely useful in the right circumstances, and worth using deliberately rather than by default.

When Locum Work Makes the Most Sense

Locum or bank work tends to make the most practical sense in a handful of recurring situations: bridging the gap between completing GMC registration and a substantive post's start date; supplementing income around a period of intensive exam preparation or further study, where a fixed substantive rota would be too inflexible; exploring different specialties, departments, or Trusts before committing to a substantive application in a particular field; and providing extra flexibility around significant personal or family circumstances that make a fixed rota impractical for a defined period. In each of these cases, locum work solves a genuine, time-limited problem, and its flexibility outweighs the training-access and pension-continuity costs discussed above, precisely because the arrangement is not intended to be indefinite.

When a Substantive Post Is the Stronger Choice

Conversely, a substantive post is generally the stronger choice for a doctor who has decided on their preferred specialty and wants structured, curriculum-mapped progression towards it; who wants the pension continuity and long-term financial predictability that comes with continuous single-employer service; who benefits most from consistent supervision and funded study leave while building towards a CESR/CEGPR portfolio or an ARCP-tracked training programme; and, for IMGs specifically, whose visa route depends on stable, straightforward sponsorship by a single employing Trust. For most doctors, most of the time, a substantive post is the more sustainable long-term base, with locum or bank shifts used selectively around it — for extra income, extra flexibility, or extra clinical exposure — rather than the other way round.

A Blended Approach: Substantive Base With Occasional Bank Shifts

It is also worth being clear that the choice is rarely a strict either/or. Many doctors in substantive posts pick up occasional additional bank shifts — within the NHS Employers "fidelity to the NHS" principle of offering additional hours to a Trust's own staff bank first — for extra income or to gain experience in a different department, without giving up any of the security, training access, or pension continuity of their substantive role. This blended approach captures much of locum work's income and flexibility benefit while keeping the substantive post's structural advantages intact, and is, in practice, how a large proportion of NHS doctors actually structure their working lives once they have a settled substantive base.

Reassessing the Choice Over Time

Finally, it is worth stressing that this is not a once-and-for-all decision. A doctor's priorities shift across a career — flexibility may matter more early on, or during a period of intensive exam preparation or family change, while stability and pension continuity may matter more as a career, and life, settles. Doctors who periodically reassess whether their current balance of substantive and locum work still matches their actual priorities, rather than drifting into one pattern by default and never revisiting it, tend to make better long-term decisions than those who treat their first choice as permanent.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between a locum and a substantive NHS post?

A substantive post is a permanent employment contract with a single NHS Trust, while locum work is temporary, arranged shift-by-shift or in short blocks through a Trust's staff bank or an external agency. Substantive posts offer job security, structured training access, and continuous pension contributions; locum work offers greater flexibility and often higher hourly pay, but less security and no automatic access to structured training or study leave.

Does locum work pay more than a substantive NHS post?

Locum hourly rates are often higher than the equivalent substantive hourly rate, particularly for hard-to-fill shifts, but this headline figure does not include the value of employer pension contributions, paid annual leave, sick pay, and funded study leave that come automatically with a substantive post. Comparing the two purely on hourly rate overstates how much better locum work actually pays once these additional benefits are accounted for.

Can locum doctors still contribute to the NHS Pension Scheme?

Yes, in many cases, particularly when working through an NHS Trust's own staff bank, but contributions can become fragmented across multiple bookings, banks, or agencies rather than accumulating through one continuous employer record. Doctors who rely heavily on locum work should check whether each specific engagement offers NHS Pension Scheme membership and periodically review their pension records through the official NHS Pensions member portal.

Is locum work a good option for IMGs new to the NHS?

Locum work can be a genuinely useful bridge for IMGs, generating income and UK-specific clinical experience while a longer-term substantive post is being arranged. However, visa sponsorship arrangements differ between agency locum work and a substantive sponsored post, and IMGs should check their specific situation with a qualified immigration adviser before assuming locum earnings or a switch between locum and substantive work will not affect their visa position.

Does locum work count towards NHS training or a CESR/CEGPR portfolio?

Yes, locum experience is legitimate clinical experience that counts towards a doctor's overall record for future training or CESR/CEGPR applications. However, because locum placements are often shorter and more fragmented, with less continuous supervision than a substantive post, building a coherent, well-documented portfolio from locum-only experience generally takes more deliberate effort than from a stable substantive base.

Do locum doctors still need to complete GMC appraisal and revalidation?

Yes. Every licensed doctor must participate in annual appraisal and revalidation regardless of employment type. Locum doctors who work for multiple organisations or agencies must identify and maintain a single designated body and proactively collect supporting information from every place they have worked during the appraisal year, which is a heavier administrative task than for a doctor in one substantive post.

Should a doctor choose a substantive post or locum work first in their NHS career?

It depends on individual priorities and circumstances. A substantive post generally offers the more sustainable long-term base, with structured training access, pension continuity, and (for IMGs) more straightforward visa sponsorship, while locum work is often best used selectively — to bridge a gap, build flexibility, or supplement income — rather than as an indefinite long-term default.

Conclusion

Deciding between locum vs substantive NHS posts is not a question with a single right answer — it depends on a doctor's career stage, financial priorities, appetite for flexibility versus security, and, for IMGs, their specific visa and sponsorship circumstances. A substantive post offers predictable pay progression, structured access to training and study leave, continuous NHS Pension Scheme contributions, and standard employment protections, making it the stronger long-term base for most doctors building a UK career. Locum and bank work offer valuable flexibility and often higher headline pay, and can be an effective bridging strategy or supplementary option, but come with real trade-offs in job security, training continuity, and pension administration that are easy to underweight until they matter. Doctors, and especially IMGs weighing up sponsorship implications, should make this choice deliberately and revisit it as priorities change, rather than drifting into one pattern by default.
