UKMLA for Malaysian doctors means sitting the UK Medical Licensing Assessment (the AKT and the CPSA) and completing GMC registration through the standard international medical graduate (IMG) route — there is no separate or shortcut pathway for MBBS graduates trained in Malaysia. If you hold an MBBS from University of Malaya, Universiti Kebangsaan Malaysia (UKM), Universiti Sains Malaysia (USM), International Medical University (IMU), UiTM or another Malaysian medical school, you follow the same UKMLA and GMC registration process as doctors from India, Nigeria, Pakistan or anywhere else outside the UK, with a few Malaysia-specific wrinkles around housemanship, compulsory service and document verification. This guide walks through eligibility, real 2026 fees in GBP with rough Malaysian ringgit (MYR) equivalents, the step-by-step registration process, a realistic timeline, and how the UK route compares with Australia's AMC pathway and Singapore's SMC registration.

Key takeaways

  • Malaysian MBBS graduates are eligible for the UKMLA route provided their degree is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools and accepted by the GMC on a case-by-case basis.
  • You do not need to finish your MMC housemanship or Malaysia's compulsory government service before applying to the GMC, though your internship status affects whether you get provisional or full registration.
  • Core GMC fees from 1 April 2026 are: AKT £283, CPSA £1,036, provisional registration £26, full registration £481, plus an English test at roughly £180-230.
  • EPIC verification through a MyIntealth account is mandatory for GMC registration and is separate from any credential checks the Malaysian Medical Council (MMC) itself runs.
  • Most Malaysian applicants should budget 12-24 months and roughly £1,500-2,000 in GMC-related fees alone, excluding travel, visa and living costs.
  • Malaysian doctors often weigh the UK against Australia's AMC/Competent Authority Pathway or Singapore's SMC registration — the right choice depends on training capacity, pay and long-term goals, not just exam difficulty.

Eligibility for Malaysian doctors on the UKMLA route

Eligibility for the UKMLA route depends primarily on whether your primary medical qualification (PMQ) is accepted by the GMC, not on your nationality. Malaysian doctors qualify to sit the UKMLA and apply for GMC registration if their medical degree meets the GMC's structural requirements and their medical school appears in the World Directory of Medical Schools. This section breaks down what that means in practice for graduates of Malaysian universities.

MBBS from Malaysian universities and MMC registration

Most Malaysian doctors hold an MBBS awarded by a local public university (University of Malaya, UKM, USM, Universiti Putra Malaysia, Universiti Islam Antarabangsa Malaysia) or a private medical school (IMU, UiTM, Manipal University College Malaysia, Newcastle University Medicine Malaysia, Perdana University, Melaka-Manipal Medical College among others). The Malaysian Medical Council (MMC) is the statutory regulator that registers doctors to practise in Malaysia under the Medical Act 1971, and it maintains the official Malaysian Medical Council register along with a published list of recognised medical qualifications.

For the GMC's purposes, your MMC registration status in Malaysia is not itself the gatekeeper — what matters is whether the GMC accepts your underlying PMQ. The GMC's own guide to acceptable overseas qualifications confirms that qualifications are assessed case by case, and that acceptance for one applicant from a given medical school does not automatically guarantee acceptance for every other graduate of that same school. If in doubt, contact the GMC's PMQ team directly before committing to exam bookings or fees.

One structural point worth understanding early: the GMC checks that your medical programme included a minimum of roughly 5,500 hours of study spread across at least three years, with substantial in-person clinical training rather than purely online or classroom-based teaching. Malaysian MBBS programmes, which are typically five years long and include extensive hospital-based clinical postings in the final two to three years, comfortably meet this structural bar in most cases — but again, this is a general observation, not a guarantee for every individual applicant, and it does not replace an actual GMC determination on your specific qualification.

It also helps to understand why the distinction between MMC registration and GMC registration matters practically. Some Malaysian doctors assume that because they are fully registered and licensed to practise independently in Malaysia, the UK will treat that as equivalent proof of competence. It does not. The GMC treats UK registration as a separate legal process governed by UK law, and your MMC status is relevant mainly as evidence of your training and supervised practice history, not as a substitute for passing the UKMLA or meeting the GMC's own registration criteria.

Housemanship and compulsory service: what's different for Malaysian graduates

Malaysia runs its own internship and service structure that sits alongside, not inside, the GMC registration process. Under the current system, MBBS graduates typically complete a housemanship of around two years, supervised through Ministry of Health-approved hospitals, before they are eligible for full MMC registration. This is frequently followed by a period of compulsory government service, which historically has run for roughly two to three years depending on the specific policy in force at the time — Malaysia has adjusted this structure more than once in recent years, so treat any specific duration as indicative only and confirm the current rules on the Malaysian Medical Council's website before making career decisions around it.

Do not assume your MMC housemanship or compulsory service period must be finished before you can start the UKMLA process. The GMC registration route and Malaysia's domestic service obligations run on separate tracks, and many doctors begin GMC paperwork, English testing or even AKT preparation while still completing housemanship.

What does matter for the GMC is whether you have completed what it considers an "acceptable internship" — broadly, a period of supervised clinical practice equivalent to the UK Foundation Programme's first year. If you apply to the GMC before finishing an acceptable internship, you are likely to be offered provisional registration restricted to UK Foundation Programme training posts, rather than full registration. If you've already completed housemanship and hold full MMC registration, you may be assessed for full GMC registration directly, subject to passing the UKMLA and meeting all other requirements. For a broader look at how this fits together, see the site's guide to GMC registration requirements for international doctors.

English language requirements

Malaysian doctors must demonstrate English proficiency to the GMC's standard: IELTS Academic with an overall score of 7.5 and no individual section below 7.0, or OET with a grade B (350 or above) in all four components. Because English is a medium of instruction at most Malaysian medical schools, some applicants may qualify for an exemption if their entire primary medical qualification was taught and assessed in English — but this exemption is assessed by the GMC on submission of evidence, not assumed automatically, so confirm your position before skipping the test. Separately, UK immigration rules from 8 January 2026 require doctors applying for a UK work visa to demonstrate at least CEFR B2 English, which is a different (generally lower) bar than the GMC's clinical registration standard, so meeting the GMC threshold typically covers the visa requirement too.

In practice, many Malaysian applicants choose to sit an English test anyway even if they believe they might qualify for an exemption, simply because the exemption assessment can take time and adds a dependency into an already long application. Booking an IELTS or OET slot early, ideally alongside your AKT preparation, removes one variable from your timeline. Test centres for both IELTS and OET are widely available across Malaysia, including in Kuala Lumpur, Penang and Johor Bahru, so logistics are rarely the bottleneck here — the main risk is simply leaving it too late and discovering your score needs a resit close to when you wanted to submit your GMC application.

EPIC and MyIntealth: primary source verification for Malaysian graduates

Every international medical graduate applying to the GMC, regardless of home country, must have their primary medical qualification verified through the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC) service, accessed via a MyIntealth account. This is a GMC-mandated step, run by ECFMG (a division of Intealth), and it is entirely separate from any verification the Malaysian Medical Council itself might run for its own registration purposes. For a full walkthrough of how the process works, timelines and common pitfalls, see the site's dedicated GMC EPIC verification process explainer.

In practice this means creating a MyIntealth account, completing identity verification, requesting an EPIC Portfolio, and uploading your basic medical degree certificate and transcripts so your medical school can confirm them directly to ECFMG. Start this early: verification requests routed through your university's registrar can take weeks, and delays here are one of the most common reasons Malaysian applicants miss their planned AKT or CPSA sitting.

Documents checklist

  • Passport and any prior UK visa history
  • Primary medical qualification certificate (MBBS) and full academic transcripts
  • Evidence of internship/housemanship completed to date, including supervisor certification where applicable
  • MMC registration certificate (provisional or full, whichever you currently hold)
  • English test certificate (IELTS or OET) or evidence supporting an exemption claim
  • MyIntealth account with a completed EPIC Portfolio request
  • GMC Online account with all required declarations completed
  • Evidence of good standing / certificate of good standing from any authority where you have previously practised, if applicable

Requirements can change, so always cross-check your personal document list against the current guidance on gmc-uk.org's Medical Licensing Assessment pages and the site's general eligibility guide before assembling your application.

A practical tip for Malaysian applicants: keep both digital and certified physical copies of every document from the start of the process, since different stages of the journey (EPIC verification, GMC Online submission, visa application, NHS trust pre-employment checks) each ask for slightly different formats and sometimes request notarised or certified true copies rather than plain scans. Setting up an organised folder structure early — separated by GMC application, EPIC/MyIntealth, English test, and visa — saves considerable time later, particularly if you are juggling housemanship or compulsory service duties in Malaysia alongside your UK application.

Understanding the AKT and CPSA: what Malaysian candidates need to prepare for

The UKMLA has two components — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — and both must be passed to become eligible for GMC registration. Malaysian candidates sit the same exams as every other UKMLA candidate; there is no separate Malaysia-specific paper or reduced content.

AKT: format and preparation

The AKT is a computer-based multiple-choice exam testing applied medical knowledge across the GMC's published content map, covering clinical presentations, clinical skills, and professional knowledge and skills at a level expected of a newly qualified UK Foundation Year 1 doctor. Malaysian candidates who trained largely in English and have exposure to internationally benchmarked question banks are generally well placed, but the content map still reflects UK clinical guidelines (NICE, UK-specific drug formularies, UK public health structures), so pure reliance on Malaysian undergraduate curricula is not enough.

Useful preparation steps for Malaysian candidates include working through UKMLA-style question banks (several of which Malaysian medical schools, including IMU, now build into their own curricula), reviewing NICE guidance directly rather than relying on local protocols, and doing timed practice papers under exam conditions well before your sitting.

A common trap for Malaysian candidates is unconsciously answering AKT questions using Malaysian Clinical Practice Guidelines (CPG) instead of NICE guidance, particularly in areas like antibiotic choice, diabetes management thresholds and antenatal care schedules, where the two systems diverge in specific details even though the underlying clinical reasoning is similar. Building a habit of cross-checking your revision notes against the current NICE guideline for each topic, rather than trusting memory from Malaysian hospital protocols, pays off directly in AKT performance. It is also worth timing your practice sessions realistically from the start, since the AKT is a time-pressured exam and many candidates who know the material well still lose marks purely from pacing issues in early attempts.

CPSA: format and preparation

The CPSA is the clinical and practical skills component of the UKMLA, run as a circuit of stations testing history-taking, examination, communication, data interpretation and practical procedures against UK clinical scenarios and UK-style patient interactions. This is where cultural and communication-style differences most often catch Malaysian candidates out — UK stations expect explicit verbal safety-netting, direct discussion of risk and consent in a way that can feel more forthright than some Malaysian clinical training environments model. Practising full mock CPSA circuits, ideally with feedback from someone who has sat the real exam or trained in the UK system, is one of the highest-value preparation steps you can take.

Because the CPSA is currently only available at GMC-approved test centres based in the UK, Malaysian candidates should plan a dedicated trip specifically for this exam rather than assuming they can slot it around another visit.

Beyond clinical content, station etiquette matters more in the CPSA than many Malaysian candidates initially expect. UK examiners are typically looking for explicit statements of intent — telling the patient what you are about to do and why, checking understanding in plain language rather than medical jargon, and closing each consultation with a clear summary and safety-net advice. Malaysian clinical training, particularly during housemanship, often emphasises efficient, senior-led communication that can come across as too brief or too hierarchical in a CPSA station, where the marking scheme rewards patient-centred, collaborative-sounding language even under time pressure. Rehearsing full consultations out loud, ideally recorded and reviewed, or role-played with a study partner acting as the patient, is far more effective preparation than reading station mark schemes alone.

Choosing where to sit the AKT from Malaysia

The AKT is delivered through Pearson VUE test centres, and Malaysian candidates typically have a choice of sitting it at a centre within Malaysia or in a neighbouring country, depending on availability during your chosen sitting window. Availability at any given centre can vary by sitting period, so it pays to check the current list of open centres on the GMC's booking system as early as your eligibility is confirmed, rather than assuming a local slot will be available close to your preferred date. Regional candidates from Kuala Lumpur, Penang, Johor Bahru and East Malaysia have historically had reasonable access to Pearson VUE infrastructure given its wide commercial testing footprint across Southeast Asia, but exact centre lists and capacity are set by Pearson VUE and the GMC, not by this guide, so always confirm directly when you come to book.

Because the AKT can be sat locally while the CPSA cannot, many Malaysian candidates find it sensible to treat the two exams as entirely separate planning exercises: optimise your AKT sitting for convenience and cost, and treat the CPSA sitting as a dedicated UK trip that also gives you a chance to attend interviews, visit NHS trusts, or network with the Malaysian doctors' community already working in the UK.

Step-by-step registration process for Malaysian doctors

  1. Confirm PMQ acceptability. Check your Malaysian medical school's listing in the World Directory of Medical Schools and, if uncertain, email the GMC's PMQ queries team before booking anything.
  2. Set up a MyIntealth account and start EPIC verification. This is often the longest lead-time step, so start it as early as possible — ideally before you've finished preparing for the AKT.
  3. Book and pass the AKT (£283). Sat at Pearson VUE test centres, including several locations accessible from Malaysia and the wider Southeast Asia region.
  4. Meet the English language requirement via IELTS or OET (£180-230), unless you can evidence an exemption.
  5. Book and pass the CPSA (£1,036). Currently only available at UK test centres, so plan travel and accommodation around this trip.
  6. Apply for GMC registration — provisional (£26) if you haven't completed an acceptable internship, or full (£481) if you have, alongside a licence to practise.
  7. Secure a Certificate of Sponsorship and apply for your UK visa once you have a confirmed NHS job offer — see the site's UK visa guide for international doctors for the specifics.
  8. Complete pre-employment checks and relocate — DBS checks, occupational health clearance, and practical relocation logistics are covered in the site's practical relocation guide for IMG doctors.

Realistic timeline for the UKMLA route from Malaysia

Most Malaysian doctors should plan for a 12 to 24-month journey from starting EPIC verification to holding full GMC registration with a confirmed NHS start date, though highly organised candidates who pass both exams on the first attempt and secure a job offer quickly can move faster.

  • Months 1-3: MyIntealth account setup, EPIC Portfolio request, English test booked and sat, AKT preparation begins in earnest.
  • Months 3-6: AKT sat and passed; if not passed first attempt, allow additional preparation and a resit window.
  • Months 6-10: CPSA preparation, UK travel booked, CPSA sat at a UK test centre.
  • Months 9-14: GMC registration application submitted and processed; job search and interviews for NHS posts run in parallel, often via NHS trust recruitment or locum agencies.
  • Months 12-24: Certificate of Sponsorship issued, visa application processed, relocation and start date confirmed.

Delays most commonly come from EPIC verification (waiting on your Malaysian university to respond to source document requests), CPSA test centre availability, and NHS trust recruitment timelines rather than the exams themselves.

CPSA travel and visa considerations

Because the CPSA is only held at UK test centres, Malaysian candidates need a plan for at least one dedicated trip to the UK purely for the exam, on a standard visitor visa (check current UK Visas and Immigration guidance on whether a visit visa is required for Malaysian passport holders, as visa-free entry rules can change). Book flights and accommodation well in advance of your CPSA date, factor in jet lag and a buffer day before the exam, and keep copies of all GMC and exam confirmation documents accessible during travel in case of border queries about your purpose of visit.

Once you have a confirmed NHS job offer, the employer sponsors your Skilled Worker visa via a Certificate of Sponsorship, which is a separate and later step from any visit visa used for the CPSA itself. UK vs Australia vs Singapore: how Malaysian doctors weigh their options

Malaysia's geographic and professional proximity to both Australia and Singapore means many doctors seriously compare all three routes rather than committing to the UK by default. There is no universally "better" option — the right choice depends on your career stage, family circumstances, and whether you prioritise training structure, pay, or long-term registration flexibility.

Australia's pathway runs through the Australian Medical Council (AMC), and some GMC-registered doctors can use the Competent Authority Pathway (CAP) to bypass part of the standard AMC assessment. Australia currently has significant workforce shortages in regional and semi-urban areas, and anecdotal industry commentary suggests junior doctor pay and lifestyle can compare favourably to the UK, though training post availability can be more limited than in the NHS. Singapore, meanwhile, runs its own Singapore Medical Council (SMC) registration process, which Malaysian graduates sometimes find more accessible given geographic and cultural proximity, though registration and conditional pathways there have their own distinct requirements.

For a detailed side-by-side comparison of exam structure, cost and registration mechanics across these systems, see the site's UKMLA vs AMC and MCCQE comparison. Many Malaysian doctors keep more than one pathway open simultaneously in the early stages — for instance, sitting the AKT while also preparing AMC MCQ materials — before committing fully once a job offer or training place materialises on one side.

A few practical factors tend to drive the final decision for most Malaysian doctors. First is training structure: the NHS Foundation Programme and subsequent specialty training pathways are well documented and relatively predictable in length, whereas some Australian training pathways can involve longer waits for a permanent training position, particularly in competitive specialties. Second is family and travel logistics: Singapore's proximity means shorter flights, easier visits home and, for some, an easier adjustment given shared regional context, while both the UK and Australia involve a much longer-haul relocation. Third is long-term registration value: GMC registration is recognised internationally in its own right and can support later moves to other Commonwealth systems, which is one reason some doctors pursue UK registration even if their immediate plan is to work in Australia or Singapore first.

Cost is rarely the deciding factor on its own, since all three systems involve broadly comparable outlay once exam fees, travel, verification and visa costs are totalled — but the CPSA's requirement to travel to the UK specifically (rather than sitting a clinical exam locally, as some other systems allow) is worth building into your planning and savings timeline well in advance.

Life and work in the NHS after GMC registration

Once you hold full GMC registration and a licence to practise, most Malaysian doctors enter the NHS either through the UK Foundation Programme (if coming in at a junior level without an accepted internship) or directly into a suitable clinical post if their prior experience and registration status support it. NHS trusts recruit internationally through structured international recruitment programmes, direct advertised vacancies on NHS Jobs, and locum or staff-grade agencies, and Malaysian doctors with strong AKT/CPSA results and clear English certification are generally competitive candidates given the broadly similar clinical training background.

Adjusting to NHS practice involves more than passing exams. Malaysian doctors moving from a housemanship or MMC-registered role often need to adapt to the NHS's structured rota systems, its emphasis on multidisciplinary team documentation, and a generally flatter hierarchy between junior and senior doctors than some are used to in Malaysian hospitals. Most find this adjustment manageable within the first few months, particularly with a supportive induction and a proactive approach to asking questions early rather than assuming local practice mirrors home training exactly. The site's practical relocation guide for IMG doctors covers housing, banking, National Insurance registration, and other early logistics in more depth, and is worth reading well before your first NHS start date.

Specialty training and career progression after registration

Passing the UKMLA and gaining GMC registration is the beginning of an NHS career, not the end of the planning process. Malaysian doctors entering the NHS typically start in one of two ways: through the UK Foundation Programme, if they are early in their career and have not yet completed an accepted internship equivalent, or directly into a suitable trust-grade or locally employed doctor post if their MMC housemanship and subsequent clinical experience already meet the bar NHS employers look for. Both routes lead toward the same eventual goal — entry into a formal specialty training programme — but the timeline and competitiveness differ depending on which entry point applies to you.

Specialty training in the UK runs through a separate, competitive national recruitment process for each specialty, typically requiring evidence of relevant clinical experience, sometimes a postgraduate exam from the relevant Royal College, and a structured application with portfolio and interview components. Malaysian doctors with MMC-recognised housemanship and additional Malaysian hospital experience are often well placed for this stage, since Malaysia's own postgraduate training pathways (through bodies such as the National Specialist Register) share substantial common ground with UK training standards in terms of clinical exposure and supervised practice. That said, specialty training application cycles, eligibility windows and competition ratios vary significantly by specialty and by year, so this is worth researching directly through each Royal College's current guidance rather than assuming your Malaysian postgraduate progress translates automatically.

Many Malaysian doctors use their first one to two NHS years deliberately: building UK-specific references, sitting any postgraduate membership exams relevant to their target specialty, and using locally employed doctor posts to gain the specific competencies particular specialty training programmes look for, rather than applying to specialty training immediately upon registration. This staged approach tends to produce a stronger application than rushing into the first specialty recruitment cycle after arrival, particularly for competitive specialties where UK-specific clinical exposure and referee familiarity carry real weight.

Documents checklist for Malaysian applicants

Before starting your EPIC/MyIntealth verification request, it helps to gather every document you are likely to need in one place rather than requesting them one at a time as each step demands. Most Malaysian applicants need: your original MBBS degree certificate and official transcripts from your medical school; proof of MMC provisional and, where completed, full registration; housemanship completion records or a letter confirming your current stage if still serving; a valid passport; passport-style photographs meeting GMC specifications; your English test certificate (IELTS Academic or OET) once sat; and any certificate of good standing your MMC can issue confirming there are no unresolved fitness-to-practise concerns.

Because MMC-issued documents and university transcripts can take weeks to process through Malaysia's own administrative channels, request them as early as possible, ideally starting the same month you decide to pursue the UKMLA route rather than waiting until you are ready to submit your MyIntealth application. Keep both physical and scanned copies of everything, since EPIC/MyIntealth verification is document-heavy and GMC application review will separately ask for supporting evidence at the registration stage itself.

Common mistakes Malaysian doctors make on the UKMLA route

  • Leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification too late. This step depends on your university's administrative responsiveness and is outside your direct control — start it months before you plan to sit the AKT.
  • Assuming Malaysian clinical protocols map directly onto NHS guidelines and NICE pathways without reviewing them first. Many core principles are similar, but drug names, dosing conventions and referral pathways differ enough that a deliberate review before the CPSA and before starting NHS work pays off.
  • Treating the housemanship and compulsory service years as wasted time if planning to leave Malaysia. They are not: MMC-supervised housemanship builds exactly the clinical and communication skills the CPSA assesses, and completing it in full keeps your options open if your UK plans change.
  • Booking CPSA travel too close to the exam date. Malaysian candidates face a long-haul flight and significant jet lag; book well ahead, arrive with at least one full buffer day, and avoid scheduling anything demanding immediately before the exam.
  • Not deciding early between the UK, Australia and Singapore. Drifting between all three without a primary focus tends to slow progress on all of them; pick a lead route and treat the others as a genuine backup rather than splitting effort evenly.

Frequently asked questions

Can Malaysian MBBS graduates sit the UKMLA?

Yes, provided your medical school and degree are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools and the GMC accepts your primary medical qualification. Most MBBS programmes from established Malaysian universities such as University of Malaya, UKM, USM and IMU meet the GMC's structural criteria, but acceptance is assessed on a case-by-case basis, so always check gmc-uk.org before booking your exam.

Do I need to finish my MMC housemanship before applying to the GMC?

No. You can apply for GMC provisional or full registration based on your UK eligibility route without having completed Malaysia's compulsory service. However, if you have not completed an acceptable internship pattern recognised by the GMC, you may be offered provisional registration restricted to UK Foundation Programme posts rather than full registration.

What is the current MMC housemanship and compulsory service structure in Malaysia?

Malaysian MBBS graduates typically complete a two-year housemanship supervised by the Ministry of Health before being eligible for full MMC registration, followed by a period of compulsory government service. Exact durations and policy details have changed periodically, so confirm the current requirement directly on mmc.gov.my before planning your UK move.

How much does the AKT cost for Malaysian candidates?

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) costs £283, effective 1 April 2026, payable in GBP directly to the GMC regardless of where in the world you sit the exam, including test centres accessible to Malaysian candidates.

How much does the CPSA cost and where can Malaysian doctors sit it?

The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) costs £1,036 from 1 April 2026. It is only held at GMC-approved test centres, which are currently based in the UK, so most Malaysian candidates will need to budget for international travel and accommodation alongside the exam fee.

Is EPIC or MyIntealth verification required for Malaysian doctors applying to the GMC?

Yes. Primary source verification of your medical degree through the EPIC service, accessed via a MyIntealth account, is a standard GMC requirement for international medical graduates, including those trained in Malaysia. This is separate from any credential checks the Malaysian Medical Council itself may require.

What English language test do Malaysian doctors need for GMC registration?

You need IELTS Academic (overall 7.5, no section below 7.0) or OET (grade B or above in all four components), at a cost of roughly £180-230 depending on the test and location. Many Malaysian doctors are exempt from this if their entire medical degree was taught and examined in English, but this exemption must be confirmed with the GMC directly.

How long does the whole UKMLA to GMC registration process take for a Malaysian doctor?

Most Malaysian applicants should budget 12-24 months from starting EPIC verification to holding full GMC registration and a confirmed NHS job, depending on how quickly you pass the AKT and CPSA, complete English testing, and secure a Certificate of Sponsorship for your visa.

Should Malaysian doctors choose the UK, Australia or Singapore?

There is no single right answer. The UK route runs through the UKMLA and GMC registration, Australia runs through the AMC and offers a Competent Authority Pathway for some GMC-registered doctors, and Singapore has its own SMC registration routes for Malaysian graduates. Compare training capacity, pay, family ties and long-term registration goals before deciding, and note that many doctors keep more than one pathway open simultaneously.

What documents do Malaysian doctors need to start the GMC application?

You will typically need your primary medical qualification certificate, official transcripts, passport, evidence of your internship or housemanship where completed, English test results, and a completed EPIC portfolio through MyIntealth. Exact document lists can change, so always check the current checklist on gmc-uk.org before applying.

Can Malaysian doctors get provisional registration while still completing housemanship?

It depends on your circumstances and route. Some Malaysian graduates apply for GMC provisional registration to work in UK Foundation Programme posts as their route to completing an acceptable internship equivalent, rather than completing housemanship in Malaysia first. Speak to the GMC or a registration adviser about which route fits your stage of training.

How much should Malaysian doctors budget in total for GMC registration fees?

Core GMC fees alone (AKT £283, CPSA £1,036, provisional or full registration £26-£481, English test roughly £180-230) total in the region of £1,500-£2,000, before flights, accommodation, visa costs and annual retention fees of £481 (with a licence) or £172 (without) are added.
