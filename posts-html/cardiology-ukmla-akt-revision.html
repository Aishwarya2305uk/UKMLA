Cardiology UKMLA Revision: The Highest-Yield Topic in the AKT

Cardiology UKMLA revision deserves more dedicated time and attention than almost any other clinical domain in your preparation. Cardiovascular disease accounts for the largest single burden of morbidity and mortality in the United Kingdom, and correspondingly, cardiology represents one of the most heavily tested areas in the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test. Questions on hypertension, acute coronary syndromes, heart failure, atrial fibrillation, valvular disease, and pericarditis appear across multiple clinical domains and require not just factual recall but integrated clinical reasoning — knowing when to suspect a diagnosis, how to investigate it, and which guideline-concordant management steps to apply. This guide provides a comprehensive, clinically integrated review of the highest-yield cardiology topics for the UKMLA AKT, referenced to current NICE guidelines and designed to help you build the kind of deep, applicable knowledge that the MLA Content Map demands.

Hypertension: NICE NG136 and Clinical Practice

Diagnosis: Clinic Blood Pressure vs ABPM

Hypertension diagnosis in UK clinical practice follows NICE guideline NG136, updated in 2023. The diagnostic pathway begins with clinic blood pressure measurement, but a single elevated reading is not sufficient for diagnosis. If the clinic blood pressure is 140/90 mmHg or higher, NICE recommends confirming the diagnosis with ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) — a 24-hour recording of blood pressure taken at regular intervals while the patient goes about their normal daily activities. The diagnostic thresholds differ between clinic and ambulatory recordings: clinic blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or higher triggers ABPM; the ABPM diagnosis threshold for hypertension is a daytime average of 135/85 mmHg or higher.

This distinction is clinically important and frequently tested in the UKMLA. White coat hypertension — elevated blood pressure in a clinical setting that normalises outside — is common, and treating it with antihypertensives based on clinic readings alone leads to unnecessary medication and risk of hypotension. ABPM also identifies masked hypertension — normal clinic readings with elevated ambulatory readings — which carries significant cardiovascular risk and requires treatment. Home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) is an acceptable alternative to ABPM in patients who are unable to tolerate ABPM devices, with a diagnostic threshold of 135/85 mmHg mean over seven days of morning and evening readings.

Stage 1 hypertension is defined as clinic BP 140/90 to 159/99 with ABPM daytime average 135/85 to 149/94. Stage 2 hypertension is clinic BP 160/100 or higher with ABPM daytime average 150/95 or higher. Severe hypertension is clinic BP 180/120 or higher, which warrants same-day specialist assessment regardless of ABPM findings.

First-Line Antihypertensive Treatment by Age and Ethnicity

NICE NG136 provides a stepped-care antihypertensive treatment algorithm that varies by age and ethnicity, reflecting differences in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) activity between patient groups. This algorithm is one of the highest-yield memorisation targets in the entire UKMLA cardiology section, because questions frequently present a newly diagnosed hypertensive patient and ask which first-line agent is most appropriate.

Step 1 treatment: for patients under 55 years who are not of African or Caribbean family origin, first-line treatment is an ACE inhibitor (or an angiotensin receptor blocker if ACE inhibitor is not tolerated, e.g. due to cough). For patients aged 55 years or over, or those of African or Caribbean family origin at any age, first-line treatment is a calcium channel blocker (CCB). The rationale for this distinction is that younger, non-African-Caribbean patients typically have higher renin levels and respond better to RAAS blockade, whereas older patients and those of African or Caribbean origin typically have lower renin levels and respond better to volume-dependent drugs such as CCBs.

Step 2 treatment: if blood pressure target is not achieved on a single agent, combine an ACE inhibitor (or ARB) with a CCB. Step 3 treatment: add a thiazide-like diuretic (specifically indapamide or chlortalidone, not bendroflumethiazide, which is less evidence-based for cardiovascular outcomes) to the ACE inhibitor/ARB plus CCB combination. Step 4 treatment: if blood pressure remains above target despite three agents at optimal doses, add either spironolactone (if serum potassium is 4.5 mmol/L or lower), or an alpha-blocker, or a beta-blocker. The addition of spironolactone at step 4 reflects evidence that resistant hypertension is often driven by aldosterone excess.

Blood pressure targets vary by patient group: the target for patients under 80 years is less than 140/90 mmHg in clinic (less than 135/85 mmHg ambulatory); for patients 80 years or over, the target is less than 150/90 mmHg in clinic. Patients with type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD), or established cardiovascular disease may have lower targets specified by their condition-specific guidelines.

Hypertensive Emergency vs Hypertensive Urgency

A hypertensive emergency is defined as severely elevated blood pressure (typically 180/120 mmHg or higher) with evidence of acute end-organ damage. End-organ damage in this context includes hypertensive encephalopathy (altered consciousness, seizures), acute aortic dissection, acute coronary syndrome, acute pulmonary oedema, eclampsia, and hypertensive nephropathy with acute kidney injury. Hypertensive emergencies require immediate admission and intravenous antihypertensive therapy to reduce blood pressure in a controlled manner — typically aiming for a 25% reduction in mean arterial pressure within the first hour, with further gradual reduction over 24 to 48 hours. The choice of intravenous agent depends on the specific end-organ involved: labetalol or nitroprusside for most emergencies; nitrates for acute pulmonary oedema; magnesium for eclampsia.

A hypertensive urgency is severely elevated blood pressure (180/120 mmHg or higher) without acute end-organ damage. This does not require emergency hospital admission but does require prompt review, assessment of secondary causes, and initiation or intensification of oral antihypertensive therapy. Many hypertensive urgencies can be managed in the community with close follow-up, provided the patient is not symptomatic and investigations do not reveal acute end-organ involvement.

Secondary Causes of Hypertension

Secondary hypertension — hypertension caused by an identifiable underlying condition — accounts for approximately 5 to 10% of hypertension cases but is disproportionately represented in UKMLA questions because it requires specific diagnostic recognition and targeted management distinct from primary hypertension. Key secondary causes to know include: renal parenchymal disease (most common secondary cause — assess with eGFR, urine ACR, renal ultrasound); renovascular hypertension due to renal artery stenosis (particularly in patients with peripheral vascular disease, flash pulmonary oedema, or BP refractory to treatment — assess with Doppler ultrasound or CT angiography; note that ACE inhibitors and ARBs are relatively contraindicated in bilateral renal artery stenosis due to risk of acute kidney injury); primary hyperaldosteronism/Conn's syndrome (hypertension with hypokalaemia, elevated aldosterone:renin ratio — treat with surgery or spironolactone); phaeochromocytoma (episodic headache, sweating, and palpitations — measure urinary or plasma metanephrines, treat with alpha-blockade before beta-blockade); obstructive sleep apnoea (consider in obese patients with refractory hypertension); and coarctation of the aorta (radio-femoral delay, hypertension in upper limbs with normal or low BP in lower limbs — more commonly diagnosed in younger patients).

Chronic Heart Failure: HFrEF vs HFpEF

Classification and Diagnosis

Heart failure is classified primarily by left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF), which determines both prognosis and treatment approach. Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) is defined as LVEF less than 40%, reflecting impaired systolic function. Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) is defined as LVEF 50% or higher with evidence of diastolic dysfunction, elevated filling pressures, or structural cardiac changes on echocardiography. Heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF) occupies the intermediate range of 41 to 49%. The majority of evidence-based drug therapy with proven mortality benefit in heart failure applies specifically to HFrEF; treatment of HFpEF focuses on symptom management and treatment of underlying contributors.

NYHA (New York Heart Association) functional classification grades heart failure severity by symptom burden: Class I (no symptoms with ordinary activity), Class II (mild symptoms with moderate exertion), Class III (marked limitation with minimal exertion), Class IV (symptoms at rest or minimal activity). NYHA class is used to guide treatment thresholds (e.g. for device therapy) and to document progression or response to treatment.

Brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) and N-terminal pro-BNP (NT-proBNP) are biomarkers of ventricular wall stress and are elevated in heart failure. NICE NG106 recommends BNP or NT-proBNP measurement as part of the initial assessment pathway: NT-proBNP greater than 2000 pg/mL (or BNP greater than 400 pg/mL) warrants urgent echocardiography within 2 weeks; values of 400 to 2000 pg/mL (NT-proBNP) or 100 to 400 pg/mL (BNP) warrant echocardiography within 6 weeks. Normal natriuretic peptide levels make significant left ventricular systolic dysfunction unlikely.

Four Drug Pillars for HFrEF: NICE NG106 Updated

The pharmacological management of HFrEF has been transformed by a series of landmark trials, and NICE NG106 now recommends four drug classes as the cornerstone of HFrEF therapy, each with evidence of mortality benefit. These four pillars — often remembered as the foundational quadruple therapy for HFrEF — are: ACE inhibitor (or ARB if ACE inhibitor is not tolerated); beta-blocker; mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA, specifically spironolactone or eplerenone); and SGLT-2 inhibitor (specifically dapagliflozin or empagliflozin, which have demonstrated significant mortality and hospitalisation benefit in HFrEF regardless of diabetes status).

The sequencing of these drugs has evolved: current guidance recommends initiating all four classes at low doses and up-titrating as tolerated, rather than fully titrating one drug before adding the next. This approach reflects evidence that any dose of each drug class confers mortality benefit, and that reaching four-drug therapy at lower doses is preferable to reaching high doses of fewer drugs. ACE inhibitors and ARBs should not be combined due to risk of acute kidney injury and hyperkalaemia. Sacubitril/valsartan (an ARNI — angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitor) can replace the ACE inhibitor or ARB in patients who remain symptomatic despite optimised therapy, and has shown superior mortality reduction to enalapril in the PARADIGM-HF trial.

Device therapy thresholds: Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICD) are recommended for HFrEF patients with LVEF 35% or less who remain at risk of sudden cardiac death despite optimal medical therapy. Cardiac resynchronisation therapy (CRT) is recommended for patients with LVEF 35% or less and QRS duration 120 ms or longer (particularly left bundle branch block morphology), to synchronise ventricular contraction and improve cardiac output. CRT-D devices combine CRT and ICD functions and are used when both criteria are met.

Acute Coronary Syndromes

STEMI: Primary PCI and Time-Critical Management

ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) is a medical emergency caused by complete occlusion of a coronary artery, producing full-thickness myocardial ischaemia and necrosis. Immediate reperfusion is the therapeutic priority. Primary percutaneous coronary intervention (primary PCI) is the preferred reperfusion strategy when it can be delivered with a door-to-balloon time of less than 90 minutes from first medical contact (or 120 minutes in exceptional circumstances where transfer is required). If primary PCI cannot be delivered within this timeframe, thrombolysis is an alternative reperfusion strategy, with alteplase or tenecteplase being the preferred agents.

The antiplatelet regimen for STEMI is dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): aspirin 300 mg loading dose (followed by 75 mg daily) plus a P2Y12 inhibitor. Ticagrelor (180 mg loading, 90 mg twice daily) is preferred over clopidogrel for most STEMI patients, based on the PLATO trial demonstrating superior mortality reduction. Prasugrel (60 mg loading, 10 mg daily) is an alternative in patients undergoing PCI who have not received prior thrombolysis and do not have a high bleeding risk. Anticoagulation with unfractionated heparin or bivalirudin is used during PCI.

NSTEMI and Unstable Angina: GRACE Score and Risk Stratification

Non-ST-elevation ACS (NSTEMI and unstable angina) requires risk stratification to guide the urgency and approach of further management. The GRACE (Global Registry of Acute Coronary Events) score is the recommended risk stratification tool, incorporating age, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, creatinine, Killip class, ST-deviation on ECG, elevated cardiac biomarkers, and cardiac arrest at presentation. A GRACE score above 140 identifies high-risk patients who should receive early invasive coronary angiography within 24 hours. Intermediate-risk patients (GRACE 109 to 140) should undergo angiography within 72 hours. Low-risk patients may undergo non-invasive investigation first.

Anticoagulation for NSTEMI/UA: fondaparinux (a factor Xa inhibitor) is the preferred anticoagulant for most NSTEMI patients based on the OASIS-5 trial showing comparable efficacy to enoxaparin with a lower bleeding risk. Unfractionated heparin or enoxaparin may be used as alternatives, particularly in patients proceeding urgently to angiography. DAPT as for STEMI is initiated: aspirin plus ticagrelor or clopidogrel, with the choice guided by bleeding risk and the likelihood of PCI versus CABG.

Secondary Prevention After ACS

Secondary prevention after ACS is a high-yield topic in UKMLA questions, because it requires knowledge of multiple drug classes and their evidence base. All post-ACS patients should receive: dual antiplatelet therapy for twelve months (typically aspirin 75 mg plus ticagrelor 90 mg twice daily, then aspirin alone long-term); high-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80 mg) regardless of baseline LDL level; ACE inhibitor (for all post-MI patients, regardless of LVEF, with dose titrated to maximum tolerated); and beta-blocker (for all post-MI patients, particularly those with reduced LVEF, for at least twelve months). Ezetimibe and PCSK9 inhibitors may be added if LDL target of less than 1.4 mmol/L is not achieved on maximal statin therapy. Aldosterone antagonists are added for patients post-MI with LVEF less than 40% and either diabetes or symptomatic heart failure, based on the EPHESUS trial.

Atrial Fibrillation: NICE NG196 2021

Rate Control vs Rhythm Control

Atrial fibrillation (AF) management requires decisions about two parallel questions: rate control (slowing the ventricular response to AF) and rhythm control (restoring and maintaining sinus rhythm). NICE NG196, published in 2021, updated the guidance significantly, particularly regarding the earlier use of rhythm control. Rate control is appropriate for most patients with permanent AF and for those in whom rhythm control is not being pursued. Rate control targets a resting heart rate of less than 80 bpm in most patients (or less than 110 bpm if the patient is asymptomatic and has preserved LV function). Rate control agents include beta-blockers (first-line in most patients), calcium channel blockers (diltiazem or verapamil — contraindicated in HFrEF), and digoxin (third-line, less effective during exercise).

Rhythm control is recommended for patients with symptomatic AF and those with AF of recent onset (less than 48 hours or confirmed onset more than 4 weeks ago after adequate anticoagulation). NG196 importantly updated guidance to recommend that rhythm control be considered earlier in the disease course — including for stable patients — rather than only for those who have failed rate control. This reflects evidence from the EAST-AFNET 4 trial that early rhythm control reduces cardiovascular events. Pharmacological cardioversion options include flecainide (in patients without structural heart disease), amiodarone, and sotalol. Electrical cardioversion (DC cardioversion) can be used for both acute and elective rhythm restoration.

Stroke Risk Assessment: CHA2DS2-VASc

AF carries a significantly elevated risk of thromboembolic stroke due to left atrial stasis and clot formation. Stroke risk is quantified using the CHA2DS2-VASc score, which assigns points for: Congestive heart failure (1 point); Hypertension (1 point); Age 75 or over (2 points); Diabetes mellitus (1 point); Stroke or TIA history (2 points); Vascular disease — peripheral arterial disease or prior MI (1 point); Age 65 to 74 (1 point); Sex category female (1 point). NICE NG196 recommends anticoagulation for men with a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 2 or more, and for women with a score of 3 or more (since the female sex point alone does not confer increased risk in the absence of other risk factors). Anticoagulation with a direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC — apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, or edoxaban) is preferred over warfarin for most patients with non-valvular AF.

DOAC Choice and HAS-BLED

The choice between individual DOACs is guided by patient-specific factors including renal function, dosing convenience, and specific clinical context. Apixaban and rivaroxaban are twice daily and once daily respectively; both are renally cleared and require dose adjustment in chronic kidney disease. Dabigatran is renally cleared to a greater extent than other DOACs and is avoided in patients with eGFR less than 30 mL/min/1.73m2. The HAS-BLED score estimates bleeding risk in anticoagulated AF patients (Hypertension uncontrolled; Abnormal renal or liver function; Stroke history; Bleeding history or predisposition; Labile INR; Elderly — age over 65; Drugs or alcohol). A high HAS-BLED score does not mandate withholding anticoagulation — it identifies modifiable bleeding risk factors that should be addressed — but it should prompt regular reassessment and caution with concomitant antiplatelet agents.

Acute Haemodynamic Compromise in AF

Acute AF with haemodynamic compromise — defined as systolic BP less than 90 mmHg, heart rate greater than 150 bpm, acute pulmonary oedema, or myocardial ischaemia — is a medical emergency requiring immediate electrical cardioversion under sedation. If the onset of AF is within 48 hours or the patient has been adequately anticoagulated for at least four weeks, cardioversion can be performed without the risk of dislodging a left atrial thrombus. If the onset is unclear and the patient has not been anticoagulated, transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE) should be performed before cardioversion to exclude left atrial thrombus, or anticoagulation should be commenced and maintained for at least four weeks before elective cardioversion.

AF Ablation

Catheter ablation (pulmonary vein isolation) is an increasingly widely used rhythm control strategy for symptomatic AF, and is recommended by NICE NG196 for patients with symptomatic paroxysmal or persistent AF in whom antiarrhythmic drug therapy has failed or is not preferred. Ablation is more effective than antiarrhythmic drugs for maintaining sinus rhythm, particularly in paroxysmal AF, but has procedural risks including cardiac tamponade, pulmonary vein stenosis, and thromboembolic events. Anticoagulation should be continued for at least two months after ablation regardless of CHA2DS2-VASc score, and long-term anticoagulation should be guided by the score thereafter.

Stable Angina: Medical Management

Stable angina is caused by fixed coronary artery stenosis limiting myocardial blood supply during exercise. Medical management aims to relieve symptoms and reduce cardiovascular risk. Short-acting glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) spray or sublingual tablet is used for acute symptom relief. Regular anti-anginal therapy begins with a beta-blocker (reducing heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand) as first-line, with a rate-limiting calcium channel blocker (diltiazem or verapamil) as an alternative in patients intolerant of beta-blockers. If symptoms persist on monotherapy, a long-acting nitrate or nicorandil can be added. Ranolazine — a late sodium current inhibitor that reduces myocardial oxygen demand without affecting heart rate or blood pressure — is an option for patients whose symptoms are inadequately controlled and who are not suitable for revascularisation. Ivabradine can be used to reduce heart rate in patients with sinus rhythm who cannot tolerate beta-blockers. All patients with stable angina should receive secondary prevention with aspirin and a statin, and ACE inhibitor if they have concurrent hypertension, heart failure, or diabetes.

Valvular Heart Disease

Aortic Stenosis

Aortic stenosis (AS) is the most common valvular lesion in the developed world, caused predominantly by calcific degeneration in older adults. The classic triad of symptoms is exertional syncope, exertional chest pain (angina), and dyspnoea or heart failure — each carrying a progressively worsening prognosis, with a median survival of 5, 3, and 2 years respectively without intervention. The characteristic murmur is an ejection systolic (crescendo-decrescendo) murmur heard loudest at the aortic area (right second intercostal space) and radiating to the carotids. Peripheral signs include a slow-rising, low-volume (plateau) pulse, a narrow pulse pressure, and a palpable systolic thrill at the aortic area. The ECG may show left ventricular hypertrophy changes.

Echocardiography is the primary investigation for AS, defining severity by aortic valve area (severe: less than 1.0 cm2 or less than 0.6 cm2/m2 indexed) and mean pressure gradient (severe: greater than 40 mmHg) and peak jet velocity (severe: greater than 4 m/s). Aortic valve replacement (AVR) is the definitive treatment for symptomatic severe AS, either surgical (SAVR) or transcatheter (TAVI/TAVR). TAVI is now preferred for high-risk surgical patients and is increasingly used for intermediate and lower surgical risk, based on landmark trials (PARTNER series, CoreValve studies). Medical management of AS is limited — there is no disease-modifying pharmacotherapy — but hypertension and heart failure comorbidities should be managed, with caution to avoid preload reduction in severe AS.

Mitral Regurgitation

Mitral regurgitation (MR) causes a pansystolic murmur that is classically loudest at the cardiac apex and radiates to the axilla (for posterior leaflet prolapse) or to the base (for anterior leaflet prolapse). Common causes include mitral valve prolapse (most common in the developed world), rheumatic heart disease, infective endocarditis, dilated cardiomyopathy (functional MR), and ischaemic heart disease (papillary muscle dysfunction). Acute severe MR — for example from papillary muscle rupture post-STEMI — presents with acute pulmonary oedema and haemodynamic collapse, requiring emergency surgical or transcatheter intervention. Chronic MR is managed medically until evidence of LV decompensation (LVEF less than 60%, LV end-systolic diameter greater than 45 mm) or severe symptoms, at which point surgery (valve repair or replacement) is recommended before irreversible LV damage occurs.

Mitral Stenosis

Mitral stenosis (MS) is now predominantly a disease of rheumatic heart disease and is more common in patients from regions where rheumatic fever is prevalent. The haemodynamic consequence is obstruction to left atrial emptying, leading to elevated left atrial pressure, pulmonary hypertension, and eventually right heart failure. Characteristic features include: malar flush (butterfly distribution of facial redness due to low cardiac output), AF (due to left atrial dilation), an opening snap (caused by the domed mitral leaflets opening under elevated left atrial pressure — the closer the opening snap to S2, the more severe the stenosis), and a mid-diastolic, low-pitched rumbling murmur heard best in the left lateral decubitus position with the bell of the stethoscope at the apex. Investigations: echocardiography defines the valve area (severe: less than 1.0 cm2), gradient (severe: mean gradient greater than 10 mmHg at normal heart rate), and Wilkins score (predicts suitability for balloon mitral valvotomy). Intervention options include percutaneous mitral valvotomy (for suitable valve morphology) or surgical repair/replacement.

Aortic Regurgitation

Aortic regurgitation (AR) is characterised by leakage of blood back into the left ventricle from the aorta during diastole, causing left ventricular volume overload and dilation. The classic peripheral signs reflect the wide pulse pressure: collapsing (water-hammer) pulse (rapidly rising and falling); wide pulse pressure; head bobbing with each systole (de Musset's sign); visible carotid pulsation (Corrigan's sign); pistol-shot femoral pulses (Traube's sign); and diastolic murmur over the femoral artery on light compression (Duroziez's sign). The murmur of AR is an early diastolic, high-pitched, blowing murmur heard best at the left sternal edge (third and fourth intercostal spaces) with the patient sitting forward and in held expiration. Acute severe AR — from aortic dissection or infective endocarditis — presents with pulmonary oedema and requires emergency surgical intervention.

Pericarditis

Diagnosis and ECG Features

Acute pericarditis is inflammation of the pericardium, most commonly caused by viral infection (coxsackie B virus, echovirus, Epstein-Barr virus), or less commonly by bacterial infection, autoimmune disease, malignancy, uraemia, or post-myocardial infarction (Dressler's syndrome). The characteristic clinical features are sharp, pleuritic chest pain that is relieved by sitting forward and leaning forward, and a pericardial friction rub on auscultation (a scratching, superficial sound best heard at the left sternal edge with the patient leaning forward). The classic ECG changes are saddle-shaped (diffuse, concave) ST elevation across multiple leads without reciprocal depression (except in lead aVR) — contrasting with the regional, convex ST elevation of STEMI — together with PR segment depression, which reflects atrial involvement and is highly specific for pericarditis.

Management and Complications

First-line treatment for acute pericarditis is a combination of aspirin or an NSAID (typically ibuprofen 600 mg three times daily) plus colchicine (0.5 mg twice daily, or 0.5 mg once daily in patients under 70 kg), for a total of at least three months. The addition of colchicine to NSAIDs has been shown in the COPE and ICAP trials to significantly reduce recurrence rate. Corticosteroids are reserved for patients in whom NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated or ineffective, and should be used at the lowest effective dose to minimise the risk of recurrence associated with steroid use. Physical activity restriction is recommended until symptoms resolve, which is particularly important for athletes.

Complications of pericarditis include pericardial effusion, which in its most severe form can lead to cardiac tamponade — a life-threatening condition in which pericardial fluid compresses the heart chambers, reducing cardiac output. Tamponade presents with Beck's triad: hypotension, raised jugular venous pressure, and muffled heart sounds. The ECG shows electrical alternans (beat-to-beat alternation in QRS axis). Emergency pericardiocentesis is required. Constrictive pericarditis is a late complication in which fibrous thickening of the pericardium restricts cardiac filling, causing symptoms of right heart failure.

Frequently Asked Questions About Cardiology in the UKMLA

How many cardiology questions appear in the UKMLA AKT?

While the exact distribution of questions by domain is not publicly disclosed for each sitting, cardiovascular medicine consistently appears as one of the most heavily represented content areas in the UKMLA AKT, reflecting the prevalence and clinical importance of cardiovascular disease in UK medical practice. Candidates should expect a significant proportion of AKT questions to involve cardiovascular presentations either as the primary topic or as an important component of a multisystem clinical scenario. All major cardiovascular topics — hypertension, ACS, heart failure, AF, valvular disease, and cardiac emergencies — are within scope.

What is the most commonly tested antihypertensive prescribing scenario?

The most commonly tested prescribing scenario in hypertension involves selecting the correct first-line agent based on age and ethnicity. Remember the key rule: under 55 and non-African-Caribbean gets ACE inhibitor; over 55 or African-Caribbean gets CCB. Questions also frequently test the step-up approach, the choice of step 4 agent (spironolactone for potassium 4.5 mmol/L or below), and prescribing cautions — particularly avoiding ACE inhibitors in bilateral renal artery stenosis, in pregnancy, and in patients with hyperkalaemia or significant CKD.

How do I distinguish between the four pillars of HFrEF treatment in exam questions?

UKMLA questions on HFrEF drug therapy typically present a patient on one or two of the four pillars and ask which additional drug should be added, or they present a contraindication to one drug and ask which substitute to use. The key facts to remember are: all four drugs (ACE-I/ARB, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT-2 inhibitor) are indicated in HFrEF with mortality benefit; ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) replaces ACE-I/ARB if the patient remains symptomatic; ivabradine is added only if the heart rate is above 75 bpm in sinus rhythm despite maximal beta-blocker; digoxin has neutral mortality benefit and is used for symptom control only. MRA is contraindicated in patients with significant hyperkalaemia (K+ above 5.0 mmol/L) or eGFR below 30.

What ECG findings distinguish pericarditis from STEMI?

This is a high-yield distinction that is frequently tested. In pericarditis, ST elevation is diffuse (affecting most leads simultaneously), concave (saddle-shaped), and not associated with reciprocal ST depression in other leads (except aVR). PR depression is present in the same leads as ST elevation. There are no Q waves and no hyperacute T waves. In STEMI, ST elevation is regional (confined to the territory of one coronary artery), convex (tombstone-shaped), associated with reciprocal ST depression in opposing leads, and may be accompanied by Q wave development and hyperacute T waves in the early phase. The clinical context also helps: pericarditis pain is sharp, pleuritic, and relieved by sitting forward; STEMI pain is crushing, central, and not positional. Always treat as STEMI until proven otherwise if clinical doubt exists, as the consequences of missing an STEMI are more severe than those of treating pericarditis as STEMI.

What are the key drug contraindications in heart failure that appear in UKMLA questions?

Several contraindications in heart failure are frequently tested. Calcium channel blockers with negative inotropy (diltiazem, verapamil) are contraindicated in HFrEF because they worsen contractile function — only amlodipine and felodipine are safe CCBs in HFrEF. NSAIDs worsen heart failure by causing sodium retention and fluid overload, and by antagonising the effects of ACE inhibitors and diuretics. Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone) cause fluid retention and are contraindicated in heart failure. Metformin requires dose reduction and monitoring in heart failure with significant renal impairment, and is contraindicated in severe renal failure. Do not combine ACE inhibitor with ARB due to excess risk of AKI and hyperkalaemia without additional cardiovascular benefit (ONTARGET trial).

Conclusion: Master Cardiology to Maximise Your UKMLA Score

Cardiology represents one of the most productive investments of revision time in UKMLA preparation.
