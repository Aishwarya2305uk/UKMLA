The MLA content map is the official blueprint that defines exactly what knowledge, skills, and professional behaviours are assessed in every UKMLA examination — and understanding it is the most important strategic decision any candidate can make when planning their preparation. Published and maintained by the General Medical Council (GMC), the MLA content map provides the definitive framework against which all Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) questions are written and all Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) stations are designed. If you are preparing for the UKMLA or the PLAB examination (which has been aligned to the same standard since August 2024), the MLA content map is not an optional supplement — it is the foundational document that should govern every hour of your revision.

Unlike a traditional medical school curriculum, the MLA content map does not prescribe how content should be taught. It specifies the standard that must be demonstrated in the assessment. This distinction matters practically: two candidates who studied at different institutions, using different textbooks and teaching styles, will both be assessed against the same content map. The candidate who knows the content map well and has deliberately mapped their revision to it will have a structural advantage over the candidate who prepares using a generic approach without reference to the official blueprint.

This guide explains the structure of the MLA content map in full, covers the major changes introduced in the September 2026 update, details the highest-yield areas by frequency, clarifies common misconceptions, and provides a practical framework for using the content map as the cornerstone of your UKMLA preparation.

What the MLA Content Map Contains: The Four Pillars

The MLA content map is organised around four main domains, sometimes referred to as pillars. Each pillar represents a distinct dimension of clinical competency, and both the AKT and CPSA assess across all four pillars — though the balance between them differs between the written and clinical components of the exam.

Pillar 1: Clinical Presentations

Clinical presentations are the patient-facing symptoms or signs that a doctor must be able to assess, investigate, and manage. The content map lists the presentations that may appear in UKMLA questions, organised loosely by body system. Examples include chest pain, shortness of breath, headache, haematuria, weight loss, altered consciousness, limb pain, skin rash, fever, and many dozens more. The September 2026 update expanded the presentations list significantly, with particular additions in women's health (including menstrual irregularities, pelvic pain, and pregnancy-related presentations), paediatrics, and mental health.

Each presentation in the content map can be tested in any clinical context — an important change introduced in September 2026 that removed the previous mapping grid that restricted which presentations could appear in which specialty setting. Chest pain might now appear in the context of a community pharmacy encounter, a GP consultation, an emergency department setting, or an inpatient ward — any of these contexts is valid in the new framework. This change rewards candidates who can apply their knowledge flexibly across settings rather than memorising condition-setting pairs.

Pillar 2: Clinical Conditions

The clinical conditions pillar lists the specific diagnoses and disease entities that are examinable in the UKMLA. The September 2026 update grew this list from approximately 311 conditions to approximately 430, representing a 38% expansion in the number of individually examinable conditions. The additions were concentrated in several areas:

  • Women's health: Expanded coverage of gynaecological conditions, obstetric complications, contraception, and menopause
  • Genetics: Introduction of clinical genetics presentations including chromosomal disorders, genetic counselling scenarios, and inherited conditions
  • Transgender health: New inclusions covering gender dysphoria, hormone therapy considerations, and gender-affirming care
  • Social determinants: Conditions and presentations related to housing, poverty, substance use, and health inequalities
  • Sepsis and infection: Updated sepsis guidance aligned with current NICE and UK Sepsis Trust protocols

For exam preparation, the clinical conditions list is the most direct guide to what can be tested. Every condition on the list is examinable; conditions not on the list cannot appear in the exam. This makes the list a practical prioritisation tool: study everything on the list, and you have theoretically covered the examinable universe. In practice, some conditions appear with much higher frequency than others — see the high-yield areas section below.

Pillar 3: Professional Behaviours

The professional behaviours pillar covers the ethical, legal, and interpersonal dimensions of medical practice. This is not a peripheral add-on to the clinical content — professional behaviours are explicitly assessed in both the AKT (through scenario-based questions about ethical dilemmas, consent, confidentiality, and duty of candour) and the CPSA (through examiner observation of how candidates interact with simulated patients and navigate professional scenarios).

Key areas within the professional behaviours pillar include: consent and capacity (including the Mental Capacity Act 2005, Gillick competence, and best-interests decision-making); confidentiality and information governance; safeguarding (child protection and adult safeguarding frameworks); duty of candour (the professional obligation to be open and honest with patients); communication skills (breaking bad news, shared decision-making, handling complaints); and patient safety (recognising and responding to deteriorating patients, prescribing safety, incident reporting). A candidate who scores consistently in the bottom quartile on professional behaviour questions is at significant risk of failing the AKT, even if clinical knowledge scores are strong.

Pillar 4: Practical Skills

The practical skills pillar lists the procedural and examination skills that are directly assessed in the CPSA and may be tested in AKT questions through description or interpretation scenarios. The skills list includes: clinical examination of major body systems (cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, neurological, musculoskeletal); basic life support and advanced life support; venepuncture and cannulation; urethral catheterisation; wound assessment and basic wound management; interpretation of ECGs, blood results, imaging, and spirometry; and safe prescribing including drug calculations.

For AKT preparation, the practical skills pillar means candidates must be able to interpret the results of clinical skills, not just perform them. For CPSA preparation, it means candidates must be able to demonstrate these skills competently with a simulated patient under examiner observation. Practical skills questions in the AKT often take the form of interpreting an ECG tracing, reading a spirometry result, or selecting the correct management step following a described examination finding.

The Three Underpinning Themes

Cutting across all four pillars of the MLA content map are three underpinning themes that the GMC expects to permeate all aspects of clinical practice and therefore all aspects of the UKMLA. These themes are not assessed in isolation — they appear embedded within clinical presentations, condition management, and professional behaviour scenarios throughout both assessment components.

Patient Safety

Patient safety is the primary underpinning theme and reflects the UKMLA's core purpose: ensuring that every licensed doctor is safe to practise. In the AKT, patient safety is assessed through questions about recognising clinical deterioration, appropriate prescribing (including safe dose calculation, drug interactions, and allergy management), escalation pathways, and systemic safety practices such as the World Health Organization surgical safety checklist. In the CPSA, patient safety is assessed behaviourally — does the candidate wash their hands, verify patient identity, explain what they are doing, and escalate concerns appropriately?

Safeguarding

Safeguarding reflects the doctor's statutory and professional duty to protect vulnerable patients — children and adults — from abuse, neglect, and exploitation. Safeguarding questions appear in both AKT and CPSA, typically as scenario-based cases in which a candidate must identify safeguarding concerns, understand the relevant legal framework (Children Act 1989, Mental Capacity Act 2005, Care Act 2014), and take appropriate action. A common AKT format presents a patient with inconsistent history, unusual injury patterns, or circumstances that raise concern, asking the candidate what to do next.

Prescribing

Safe prescribing is an explicit underpinning theme because prescribing errors represent one of the most common and preventable sources of patient harm in UK hospitals. Prescribing-themed questions appear throughout the AKT and may include drug calculations, drug-drug interactions, contraindications in pregnancy or renal impairment, appropriate routes of administration, and NICE-guideline-aligned first-line treatment selection. Many medical schools and preparation courses treat prescribing as a distinct revision category for the UKMLA, and this is well-justified given its prevalence in the exam.

Always check the publication date and content map alignment of any resource you use.

How Questions Are Mapped to the Content Map

Every single AKT question is mapped to at least one item in the MLA content map before it is approved for use. This mapping process is part of the GMC's quality assurance framework and ensures that no question appears in the examination unless it tests content that is explicitly within the examinable blueprint. The question-writing and review process involves practising clinicians at Foundation Programme level as well as senior clinical educators, ensuring that questions reflect the realistic clinical challenges of a Foundation Year 1 doctor.

Understanding the mapping process has practical implications for preparation. It means you can use the content map defensively: any topic not listed in the content map is not examinable. You do not need to prepare for advanced specialist knowledge, cutting-edge research findings, or conditions that appear only in tertiary subspecialty practice. Your preparation should be bounded by the content map on all sides.

The Content Map Versus the PLAB Syllabus

Before August 2024, PLAB had its own content framework that differed from the MLA content map. After August 2024, all PLAB examinations (both PLAB 1 / AKT and PLAB 2 / CPSA) are assessed against the MLA content map. This means the content map is now the definitive guide for PLAB candidates as well as UKMLA candidates. If you are preparing for PLAB 1 or PLAB 2 in 2025 or 2026, you are preparing for an examination assessed against the MLA content map. Any preparation resource that refers to a separate PLAB syllabus is using outdated terminology; the content they describe should be the MLA content map or it may not be current.

Top 10 Highest-Yield Areas by Frequency

While the content map formally gives equal status to all listed conditions and presentations, analysis of UKMLA and PLAB question distributions reveals that certain clinical areas appear with significantly higher frequency than others. Based on published question bank analyses and community feedback from recent candidates, the highest-yield areas include:

  1. Cardiovascular medicine — ACS management, heart failure, arrhythmias, hypertension, anticoagulation
  2. Respiratory medicine — COPD, asthma, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, lung cancer
  3. Gastroenterology and hepatology — GI bleeding, IBD, liver disease, abdominal pain assessment
  4. Neurology — Stroke, TIA, headache differentials, seizure management, meningitis
  5. Mental health — Depression, psychosis, anxiety, eating disorders, capacity assessment
  6. Pharmacology and prescribing safety — Drug interactions, dose calculations, prescribing in special populations
  7. Obstetrics and gynaecology — Pregnancy emergencies, ectopic pregnancy, contraception, gynaecological cancers
  8. Paediatrics — Developmental milestones, common childhood illnesses, safeguarding in paediatric contexts
  9. Musculoskeletal medicine — Back pain, fracture management, inflammatory arthritides
  10. Renal medicine and urology — AKI, CKD, UTIs, haematuria, electrolyte disturbances

How to Use the Content Map for Revision

The most effective way to use the MLA content map is as both a planning tool and a progress tracker. At the start of your preparation period, download the latest version of the content map from the GMC website. Work through the clinical conditions list systematically, marking each condition as: fully confident, partially confident, or not yet studied. This gives you a visual representation of your preparation landscape and identifies priority gaps.

For each condition, your revision should cover: the typical clinical presentation (how the patient presents to a Foundation doctor); the initial investigations (what you would order first); the immediate management (initial stabilisation and treatment); definitive management (which may involve specialist referral); and key complications and their management. This five-part framework for each condition, applied systematically across the content map, builds the kind of broad, applied knowledge that the AKT rewards.

For CPSA preparation, use the practical skills list within the content map to audit your clinical skill competency. For each listed skill, practise to the standard expected in an OSCE setting: correct technique, appropriate communication, patient safety awareness, and professional behaviour throughout. Role-playing each station type with a partner and videoing your performance for self-review are highly effective preparation methods.

Common Misconceptions About the MLA Content Map

Several persistent misconceptions about the content map circulate in candidate communities and can lead to misdirected preparation. The most common include: believing that only conditions listed under a specific specialty section can appear in that specialty's clinical context (false since September 2026, when the mapping grid was removed); assuming that the content map is identical to a standard medical textbook's table of contents (it is not — the map is specifically scoped to Foundation-level practice, omitting many advanced specialist conditions); and thinking that the content map changes every year (in practice, major revisions are infrequent, with the September 2026 update being only the second major revision since launch).

Frequently Asked Questions About the MLA Content Map

Where can I download the official MLA content map?

The official MLA content map is available as a free PDF download from the GMC website at gmc-uk.org/education/medical-licensing-assessment/mla-content-map. The GMC publishes both English and Welsh language versions. Always ensure you download the most recent version — the September 2026 update supersedes all earlier versions for assessments from September 2026 onwards. The document is available without registration or payment; be cautious of third-party websites charging fees for a document that is freely available from the GMC.

Does the September 2026 content map update affect candidates who already passed their AKT?

No. If you have already passed the AKT under the previous content map, your result is valid and is not affected by the September 2026 update. AKT results are valid for five years from the date of passing. You will only sit under the new content map if your next assessment (whether AKT, CPSA, or a re-sit) takes place from September 2026 onward. Candidates mid-pathway should check whether their remaining component will fall under the new map and update their preparation accordingly.

How many conditions are in the September 2026 content map?

The September 2026 update expanded the list of examinable clinical conditions from approximately 311 to approximately 430. This represents a 38% increase in the number of individually listed conditions. The most significant expansions were in women's health, genetics, transgender health, and social determinants of health. The full conditions list is available in the downloadable PDF content map from the GMC website.

Are rare conditions likely to appear in the UKMLA?

Conditions in the content map are listed because they represent clinically important presentations that a Foundation doctor may plausibly encounter. The content map does not contain highly specialised, rare subspecialty conditions — it is bounded by the Foundation Programme threshold. Within the conditions that are listed, some are more common in the exam than others (see the high-yield areas section above), but any listed condition is theoretically examinable. A condition listed in the content map that is clinically rare may still appear as an AKT question because it tests important clinical reasoning (e.g., recognising an urgent referral need) even if the underlying condition is uncommon.

Is the MLA content map the same for both UK students and IMGs?

Yes. The MLA content map is a single unified document that applies to all UKMLA assessments — both the AKT and CPSA taken by UK medical students, and the PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 taken by IMGs. This is fundamental to the UKMLA's purpose: creating a single consistent standard for all routes to UK medical licensure. The same conditions, presentations, professional behaviours, and practical skills are examinable regardless of which route a candidate takes to licensure.

Conclusion

The MLA content map is not just an administrative document — it is the single most important resource available to any UKMLA or PLAB candidate. By defining precisely what is and is not examinable, it gives candidates the ability to bound their preparation rationally, prioritise high-yield areas, and audit their knowledge and skill coverage systematically. The September 2026 update has expanded the content significantly, making early familiarity with the updated map more important than ever for candidates preparing for assessments from September 2026 onward.

The candidates who use the content map most effectively are those who treat it as a living document throughout their preparation — not something to glance at once and set aside, but a regular reference point for checking coverage, identifying gaps, and tracking progress. Combined with a high-quality question bank, systematic clinical knowledge revision, and regular OSCE practice, mastery of the MLA content map framework gives candidates the structural foundation for UKMLA success.
