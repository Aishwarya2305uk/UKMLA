UKMLA preparation for IMGs (International Medical Graduates) requires a fundamentally different approach to that of UK-trained medical students — not because the exam content differs, but because the pathway to sitting it is longer, the administrative steps are more complex, and the cultural and clinical context of UK practice may be unfamiliar. This comprehensive guide takes you from the very beginning of the IMG journey through to post-UKMLA job search, covering EPIC/MyIntealth verification, English language testing, NHS cultural adaptation, and the resources that have the strongest track record for IMG candidates.

The IMG Pathway to UKMLA: An Overview

For international medical graduates, the route to practising medicine in the UK follows a defined sequence. Understanding the full pipeline before starting any individual step prevents costly delays and helps you plan your finances, timeline, and life arrangements realistically.

The standard IMG pathway runs as follows:

  1. Credential verification: MyIntealth (formerly EPIC) verification of your primary medical degree
  2. English language test: OET (Grade B in all components) or IELTS Academic (7.5 overall, minimum 7.0 per band)
  3. UKMLA AKT (PLAB 1 equivalent): 200 SBA questions, two papers, Pearson VUE centres
  4. UKMLA CPSA (PLAB 2 equivalent): OSCE-style clinical and professional skills assessment in Manchester or London
  5. GMC registration: Application following both UKMLA passes
  6. Employment: Foundation equivalent, clinical fellow post, or specialty training application

Each step has its own timeline, cost, and documentation requirements. The sections below address each in detail.

MyIntealth Credential Verification: Step-by-Step Guide

EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) was retired in 2024 and replaced by MyIntealth, operated by Intealth (the parent organisation of ECFMG). This is now the required verification platform for GMC registration. Here is the process:

Step 1: Create Your MyIntealth Account

Visit myintealth.org and create an account using your personal email. Do not use institutional emails that may become inactive.

Step 2: Submit Your Credentials

Upload your primary medical degree certificate and transcripts. You will need to provide your medical school's details so MyIntealth can contact them directly for verification. Some medical schools are pre-registered with the system; others require manual contact.

Step 3: Medical School Response

This is the main bottleneck. Some medical schools respond within two to three weeks; others — particularly in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa — may take two to four months. Contact your medical school's registrar or academic affairs office in parallel to alert them to expect a verification request.

Step 4: GMC Application Window

Once verification is complete, the GMC opens a 90-day window for you to submit your full registration application. Do not let this window lapse — the application must be completed within 90 days or you restart the process.

Common issues to anticipate: mismatch between your name on your degree and your passport (obtain an affidavit early), medical schools that require payment for verification, and delays during academic holidays at your institution.

Ordering Medical Documents for GMC Registration

Beyond your primary degree, the GMC may require additional documents depending on your country of graduation and post-graduation history. Commonly required items include:

  • Primary medical qualification certificate (original or certified copy)
  • Academic transcripts (year by year)
  • Internship completion certificate or proof of post-qualification experience
  • Good Standing Certificate from your home country's medical council
  • Specialist qualification certificates (if applicable)
  • Language test certificate
  • Passport or national ID

Obtain certified translations for any documents not in English. The GMC specifies that translations must be certified by a professional translation service — not by a bilingual friend or colleague. Start gathering these documents simultaneously with your MyIntealth verification to save months of waiting time.

Understanding the UK NHS Context for UKMLA Preparation

The UKMLA AKT and CPSA are built around the GMC's Outcomes for Graduates and the content of UK clinical practice. For IMGs from systems with different pharmacopoeia, referral structures, or clinical hierarchies, this requires deliberate adaptation.

Primary vs Secondary vs Tertiary Care

The NHS operates a strict referral-based tiered system. Most patients first see a GP (primary care). The GP refers to secondary care (hospital consultants). Tertiary care handles highly specialised conditions via secondary care referral. Many AKT questions assume you know which level of care is appropriate for a given presentation — a question about a patient with suspected cancer, for example, expects you to know the two-week-wait urgent referral pathway, not to manage it yourself in primary care.

Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Culture

UK clinical practice places enormous emphasis on collaborative decision-making through MDT meetings, especially in oncology, mental health, and complex chronic disease management. CPSA stations may explicitly test your ability to communicate with other healthcare professionals and to acknowledge the limits of your role.

Consent, Capacity, and Autonomy

The UK legal and ethical framework is built on the Mental Capacity Act 2005 and Montgomery v Lanarkshire (2015) on consent. Autonomy is paramount: a competent adult can refuse any treatment. This differs markedly from paternalistic models still common in some healthcare systems. Know the four-part capacity assessment, the best interests framework for those lacking capacity, and the Gillick competence standard for under-16s.

UK Prescribing for IMG Candidates

Drug naming conventions differ internationally. The UK uses International Non-proprietary Names (INN) throughout the BNF (British National Formulary). If your training used brand names or different national naming systems, you must learn INN equivalents for common drugs. Examples: paracetamol (not acetaminophen), adrenaline (not epinephrine, though both are now acceptable in AKT), furosemide (not frusemide).

The BNF is available free online (bnf.nice.org.uk) and as an NHS app. IMG candidates should spend at least two weeks working systematically through the most common drug classes: antihypertensives, antibiotics, anticoagulants, analgesics, psychiatric medications, and diabetic agents. Pay particular attention to UK-specific contraindications and interaction warnings, which may differ from your home country's formulary.

NICE guidelines (available at nice.org.uk) govern most clinical management decisions in the AKT. Questions on management typically have one NICE-concordant answer. Familiarise yourself with the most frequently tested guidelines: NG185 (ACS), NG136 (heart failure), NG28 (type 2 diabetes), NG12 (stroke), and NG89 (depression).

Key Differences for IMGs From Specific Medical Systems

While all IMGs face adaptation challenges, certain country-specific differences recur frequently in UKMLA preparation courses:

Indian Medical Graduates

Common differences: familiarity with generic drug names but occasional brand-name confusion; strong in pharmacology but may need to adjust to UK epidemiology (e.g., lower prevalence of certain tropical diseases in AKT); less experience with formal MDT documentation. NHS consent processes require particular attention.

Nigerian and West African Medical Graduates

Common differences: strong clinical examination skills but potential unfamiliarity with NHS administrative systems; community-based healthcare models differ significantly from NHS primary care; UK tropical medicine is a small part of AKT but familiarity with UK-prevalent tropical presentations (malaria in returned travellers, etc.) is required.

Pakistani Medical Graduates

Common differences: very similar examination structure to MBBS UK, so content adaptation is relatively minor; primary differences lie in NHS structure knowledge and ethical framework; mental health presentations and psychiatry management may require additional study as the cultural framing differs.

Filipino Medical Graduates

Common differences: strong English language skills (often the least significant barrier); primary care model differs substantially from UK GP system; nursing-led care and task-shifting are more prominent in Filipino healthcare, requiring adjustment to UK professional boundaries.

Top Resources for IMG UKMLA Preparation

IMGs have different resource needs from UK medical students because they often need to build foundational NHS clinical knowledge alongside exam-specific preparation. Here is a ranked guide:

Tier 1: Essential

  • UKMLA Question Banks (MedMLA, Quesmed, UWorld UKMLA): SBA question practice is the single most important preparation activity. Aim for 2,000 to 3,000 questions minimum before sitting.
  • BNF Online (bnf.nice.org.uk): Free. Essential reference for drug names, doses, interactions. Spend time navigating it actively, not just looking up individual drugs.
  • NICE Guidelines: Free at nice.org.uk. Focus on guidelines for the most common AKT-tested conditions rather than reading all guidelines comprehensively.
  • Geeky Medics: Free online platform with excellent clinical skill guides, ABG tutorials, ECG interpretation, and examination frameworks. Particularly valuable for CPSA preparation.

Tier 2: Highly Recommended

  • Oxford Handbook of Clinical Medicine: The "cheese and onion" is the most comprehensive single-volume UK clinical reference. Buy the latest edition.
  • Kumar and Clark's Clinical Medicine: The standard UK undergraduate textbook. Excellent for systematic knowledge building across all systems.
  • Pass Medicine / MedRevisions: UK-specific SBA platforms with explanations referencing NICE guidelines directly.

Tier 3: Supplementary

  • Almostadoctor.co.uk for quick clinical summaries
  • Radiopaedia for radiology interpretation practice
  • Life in the Fast Lane for ECG and critical care interpretation

Online Communities and Support Networks for IMGs

Preparation is significantly easier when you are part of a community of candidates facing the same challenges. Key communities include:

  • Facebook groups: Search for "PLAB UK preparation", "UKMLA IMGs", and country-specific groups ("Indian doctors UK", etc.). These groups share real-time information about exam dates, test centre experiences, and study tips.
  • WhatsApp groups: Often organised by country of origin or by exam sitting date. Ask in Facebook groups for current WhatsApp links.
  • Reddit r/ukmla: Less common than Facebook but increasingly active with IMG-specific posts.
  • IMG Connect (imgconnect.co.uk): Recruitment and information website with an active community forum.
  • BMA International Medical Graduates: The British Medical Association has specific resources and representation for IMG doctors at bma.org.uk/imgs.

Clinical Shadowing and NHS Experience for IMGs

The UKMLA CPSA tests clinical skills in a UK NHS context. Candidates with no prior UK clinical experience often struggle with station-specific communication styles, documentation expectations, and the NHS professional hierarchy. Obtaining clinical experience before sitting the CPSA is strongly recommended.

Clinical Attachments vs Observer Status

A clinical attachment is an informal arrangement where you shadow a clinical team, sometimes with some supervised patient contact. You are not employed and receive no payment. Attachments are arranged directly with NHS trusts — typically through the medical education department. Write formally to the medical education coordinator at your target department.

Observer status is similar but involves strictly observational roles with no patient contact. This is increasingly common as trusts manage liability for unregistered overseas practitioners.

Both types of attachment are extremely valuable for CPSA preparation. Two to four weeks of attachment in general medicine, surgery, and primary care provides essential context for the communication stations. It also allows you to observe real UK consent conversations, handover formats (SBAR), and documentation standards.

Post-UKMLA Job Search as an IMG

Once you hold GMC registration following both UKMLA components, you are eligible to apply for clinical positions in the UK NHS. The main routes for newly registered IMGs are:

Clinical Fellow Posts

Clinical fellow positions are junior doctor posts that sit outside the formal Foundation Programme and Specialty Training pathways. They are advertised individually by NHS trusts and are an excellent entry point for IMGs. Responsibilities are equivalent to those of a foundation or core trainee. Search NHS Jobs (jobs.nhs.uk) and individual trust websites. Competition is high in popular specialties and locations.

International Medical Training Initiative (IMTI)

The IMTI and related international training programmes allow qualified doctors from specific countries to complete defined training programmes in the UK. These are time-limited posts with a requirement to return to your home country, but they provide structured supervision and UK experience valuable for your career.

Locum Agency Registration

Once registered with the GMC, you can register with NHS locum agencies. Locum work provides flexible hours and good pay rates, and can be started while you build your UK experience base. However, locum posts offer less structured training and supervision than substantive posts.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Preparation for IMGs

Is PLAB still available or has it been replaced by UKMLA?

PLAB has been aligned with the UKMLA. PLAB 1 is now equivalent to the AKT (same content map, MLA standards), and PLAB 2 is now equivalent to the CPSA. The GMC refers to both as PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 for IMG candidates, and the content is based on the MLA content map. The branding has changed but the exam pathway remains two sequential components.

Can I sit the AKT outside the UK?

Yes. The AKT is available at Pearson VUE centres in multiple countries. Check the GMC website for current international availability. Note that some countries may have limited sittings per year. The CPSA (PLAB 2 equivalent) is only available in the UK, currently in Manchester and limited London dates.

How difficult is the AKT for IMG candidates compared to UK medical students?

The content is the same, but the context is different. UK medical students study within the NHS system throughout their training, making NHS-specific questions more intuitive. IMGs need to invest additional time learning NHS pathways, UK drug names (INN), NICE guidelines, and the ethical/legal framework. With dedicated study (typically four to six months), IMGs can and regularly do pass at first attempt.

How many times can I attempt the AKT if I fail?

The GMC allows up to four attempts at PLAB 1 (AKT). If you have not passed within four attempts, you must apply to the GMC for permission to make further attempts, which requires demonstrating additional preparation. See our guide on appeals and resits for more information.

Do I need UK clinical experience before sitting the AKT?

The AKT does not formally require UK clinical experience. However, experience significantly improves performance, particularly for NHS-context questions. For the CPSA, UK experience is strongly recommended as the communication and consultation style is distinctly NHS-specific.

How do I handle name discrepancies between my degree and passport?

Name discrepancies are one of the most common MyIntealth and GMC application issues. If your name on your medical degree differs from your current passport (due to marriage, transliteration differences, or abbreviated names), obtain a statutory declaration or affidavit from a notary public or solicitor confirming both names refer to the same person. Address this before submitting your MyIntealth application.

Are there IMG-specific preparation courses worth taking?

Several UK-based academies offer IMG-specific preparation programmes that combine AKT question practice with NHS context teaching. Chequers Training Academy, Aspire2PLAB, and similar organisations offer both online and in-person courses. These are not essential but can accelerate the cultural and contextual adaptation that self-study alone is slower to achieve.

What support is available for IMGs with disabilities or health conditions?

The GMC offers reasonable adjustments for the AKT and CPSA for candidates with disabilities or health conditions. This is the same process as for UK students. See our guide on disability access and adjustments in the UKMLA for full details on how to apply.

Conclusion

Effective UKMLA preparation for IMGs is a multi-year project that requires simultaneous management of credential verification, language testing, systematic content study, NHS cultural adaptation, and financial planning. The pathway is well-defined and thousands of IMGs successfully navigate it every year. The key differentiators between those who pass at first attempt and those who struggle are: starting documentation early, choosing resources aligned with UK NHS practice rather than general clinical study, obtaining clinical experience in the UK before the CPSA, and building a support community of fellow candidates.
