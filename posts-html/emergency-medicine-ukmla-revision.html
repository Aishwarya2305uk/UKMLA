Emergency medicine and acute presentations for the UKMLA represent some of the most critical and frequently examined content across both the AKT and CPSA, because the GMC's primary concern at the point of licensure is ensuring that newly qualified doctors can recognise and initiate management of life-threatening conditions safely and rapidly. Every scenario involving an acutely unwell patient — from a deteriorating ward patient triggering a crash call to a patient presenting to the emergency department in anaphylaxis — requires a structured, systematic approach that the UKMLA tests directly.

The ABCDE Approach

The ABCDE approach is the cornerstone of acute patient assessment and is the framework within which all acute emergency management is organised. It is tested across both the AKT (clinical scenarios asking what to do first or next) and the CPSA (where candidates are expected to demonstrate a structured assessment in simulated acute scenarios). The approach is iterative — reassess after every intervention before moving to the next stage.

A — Airway: Is the airway patent? Listen for stridor, gurgling, or silence. If not patent, simple manoeuvres first (jaw thrust, head-tilt chin-lift, suction), then adjuncts (oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway), then definitive airway (intubation or surgical airway via cricothyroidotomy if intubation fails). B — Breathing: Rate, depth, symmetry, oxygen saturation. Apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreathe mask (15 L/min) to any acutely unwell patient unless COPD with known type 2 respiratory failure (target SpO2 88 to 92% in type 2 RF, 94 to 98% in all others). C — Circulation: Heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time (normal under two seconds), skin colour and temperature, IV access, bloods and ECG. If haemodynamically compromised, fluid challenge (250 to 500 mL crystalloid bolus in adults, 10 mL/kg in children) and identify source of compromise. D — Disability: AVPU scale (Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive) or GCS (Eyes 4 + Verbal 5 + Motor 6 = 15). Check blood glucose — treat hypoglycaemia if present. Pupillary responses. E — Exposure: Full examination, temperature, check skin for rash (purpura, urticaria), look for obvious injuries. Maintain dignity and prevent hypothermia.

NEWS2 Scoring

NEWS2 (National Early Warning Score 2) is the UK standardised early warning scoring system recommended by NHS England for adult inpatients. Six physiological parameters are each scored 0 to 3: respiration rate, oxygen saturation (SpO2 — modified for patients with hypercapnic respiratory failure, using SpO2 Scale 2 target 88 to 92%), any supplemental oxygen, systolic blood pressure, pulse rate, and level of consciousness (using AVPU — a new confusion scores 3). Temperature is also scored. A cumulative NEWS2 score triggers escalation: score 0 to 4 is routine monitoring; score 5 to 6 or any single parameter scoring 3 requires urgent medical review; score 7 or above constitutes a medical emergency requiring immediate review by a senior clinician and critical care assessment.

Sepsis

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. NICE NG51 defines sepsis using the Sepsis 6 care bundle, which should be delivered within one hour of recognition for high-risk patients. The six elements are: take blood cultures (before antibiotics if possible but do not delay antibiotics to obtain cultures), measure serum lactate (lactate above 2 mmol/L indicates tissue hypoperfusion; above 4 mmol/L is a medical emergency), administer IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (choice depends on likely source — community-acquired infection: piperacillin-tazobactam or co-amoxiclav plus a macrolide for CAP; hospital-acquired: meropenem or piperacillin-tazobactam), commence IV fluid resuscitation (500 mL crystalloid bolus over 15 minutes for adults with suspected septic shock or hypoperfusion, then reassess), ensure adequate urine output (catheterise and target above 0.5 mL/kg/hour), and apply high-flow oxygen.

Septic shock is diagnosed when a patient with sepsis requires vasopressors to maintain a mean arterial pressure (MAP) of 65 mmHg or above, and has a serum lactate above 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Vasopressors of choice: noradrenaline is first-line. Vasopressin can be added as a second agent. Dobutamine is added if there is myocardial dysfunction causing low cardiac output. Source control — draining an abscess, removing an infected urinary catheter, or debriding infected tissue — is a critical component of sepsis management that is often underemphasised in exam preparation.

Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening generalised or systemic hypersensitivity reaction. Triggers include drugs (most commonly penicillins, NSAIDs, contrast media), foods (nuts, shellfish, dairy), insect stings, and latex. Clinical features develop rapidly (minutes to hours after exposure) and involve two or more body systems: cutaneous or mucosal changes (urticaria, angioedema, flushing), respiratory compromise (wheeze, stridor, dyspnoea), cardiovascular compromise (hypotension, tachycardia, collapse), and GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain). A severe allergic reaction involving the airway or cardiovascular system confirms anaphylaxis and demands immediate treatment.

Immediate management: call for help, lie the patient flat with legs elevated (or upright if respiratory distress predominates — do not make the patient sit up if cardiovascular compromise is the main feature). Adrenaline 0.5 mg (500 micrograms) IM into the anterolateral thigh is the first-line treatment and should be given immediately. IM not IV is used in most cases because IM administration has a safer pharmacokinetic profile in non-monitored settings; IV adrenaline requires cardiac monitoring and specialist administration. The dose can be repeated at five-minute intervals if there is no improvement. High-flow oxygen. IV fluid resuscitation (500 mL to 1000 mL crystalloid bolus). Antihistamine (chlorphenamine 10 mg IM or slow IV) — adjunct only, does not treat the life-threatening features. Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV — adjunct only, does not act immediately but may prevent protracted reactions. Observe for a minimum of six to twelve hours after resolution due to the risk of a biphasic reaction (second wave of anaphylaxis occurring four to twelve hours after initial event, in approximately 5 to 20% of cases). On discharge, prescribe two adrenaline auto-injectors (EpiPens), provide written instructions on use, and arrange follow-up with an allergist.

Acute Coronary Syndromes

ACS encompasses STEMI, NSTEMI, and unstable angina. STEMI is defined by typical ischaemic symptoms plus persistent ST elevation of at least 1 mm in two or more contiguous limb leads, or at least 2 mm in two or more contiguous precordial leads, or new left bundle branch block, plus positive troponin. NSTEMI has positive troponin without ST elevation. Unstable angina has ischaemic symptoms at rest with ECG changes but no troponin rise.

STEMI management: primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PPCI) is the preferred reperfusion strategy if it can be delivered within 120 minutes of diagnosis (door-to-balloon time target: within 90 minutes of first medical contact). If PPCI is not achievable within 120 minutes, thrombolysis (tenecteplase, alteplase) is given. Antiplatelet therapy: aspirin 300 mg loading dose plus a P2Y12 inhibitor (ticagrelor 180 mg loading dose preferred, or prasugrel, or clopidogrel if neither is available). Anticoagulation: UFH or LMWH or bivalirudin during PPCI. Oxygen: only if SpO2 below 94% (high-flow oxygen in normoxic patients is no longer recommended as it may increase myocardial injury). Morphine (or diamorphine) for pain — titrate to effect. GTN (sublingual or IV) for ongoing chest pain unless systolic BP below 90 mmHg or inferior STEMI with possible RV involvement (beware preload dependence). Beta-blocker (metoprolol or atenolol) if not contraindicated. ACE inhibitor from day one in all STEMI patients without contraindications. Statin (atorvastatin 80 mg) from day one.

NSTEMI management: risk-stratify using GRACE score (determines in-hospital and six-month mortality risk). Antiplatelet: aspirin 300 mg plus ticagrelor or clopidogrel. Anticoagulation: fondaparinux (preferred) or LMWH. Early invasive strategy (coronary angiography within 24 to 72 hours) for intermediate-to-high GRACE scores. Conservative management with optimal medical therapy for low GRACE scores.

Acute Pulmonary Oedema

Acute cardiogenic pulmonary oedema (APO) results from elevated pulmonary venous pressure causing fluid transudation into the alveoli. Causes include STEMI, acute mitral regurgitation, hypertensive emergency, or decompensated chronic heart failure. Clinical features: severe breathlessness, orthopnoea, frothy pink sputum, widespread crepitations to the mid-zones and above, and gallop rhythm on auscultation. CXR shows bat-wing perihilar oedema, Kerley B lines (horizontal interstitial lines at the lung periphery), upper lobe blood diversion, and cardiomegaly. Immediate management: sit the patient upright (reduces preload), high-flow oxygen to maintain SpO2 above 94%, IV furosemide 40 to 80 mg (venodilatory effect within minutes, diuresis over one to two hours), sublingual or IV GTN (if systolic BP above 110 mmHg) as a vasodilator to reduce preload and afterload, CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) if available — reduces the work of breathing and improves oxygenation, and has been shown in trials to reduce the need for intubation and reduce mortality. IV diamorphine was previously used as an anxiolytic and venodilator but is now used with caution — evidence base is less robust and respiratory depression is a concern. Treat the underlying cause — ACS, hypertension, arrhythmia.

Acute Severe Asthma

Acute severe asthma in adults is defined by any of: PEFR 33 to 50% of best or predicted, RR above 25 breaths/min, HR above 110 bpm, or inability to complete sentences in one breath. Life-threatening asthma features: PEFR below 33%, SpO2 below 92%, silent chest, cyanosis, feeble respiratory effort, altered consciousness, bradycardia, or hypotension. Near-fatal asthma: raised PaCO2 (above 6.0 kPa) or requiring mechanical ventilation. Management: high-flow oxygen (via non-rebreathe mask) to maintain SpO2 above 94%, back-to-back salbutamol nebulisers (2.5 to 5 mg) driven by oxygen, ipratropium bromide 500 micrograms nebuliser (added if severe or life-threatening), oral prednisolone 40 to 50 mg (or IV hydrocortisone 100 mg if unable to take orally), IV magnesium sulphate 1.2 to 2 g over twenty minutes (for severe/life-threatening asthma not responding to initial bronchodilators — single dose only). Criteria for ITU referral: deteriorating PEFR despite treatment, hypoxia, rising PaCO2, exhaustion, confusion, or respiratory arrest.

Status Epilepticus

Status epilepticus is defined as continuous seizure activity lasting more than five minutes, or two or more seizures within five minutes without return to baseline consciousness between them. Management follows a stepwise benzodiazepine ladder. Phase 1 (0 to 5 minutes): IV lorazepam 4 mg (if IV access available) or buccal midazolam 10 mg or rectal diazepam 10 mg (if no IV access). If no response after ten minutes, give a second dose of benzodiazepine. Phase 2 (10 to 20 minutes, if benzodiazepines have failed): IV levetiracetam 60 mg/kg (max 4500 mg) over five minutes, or IV phenytoin 20 mg/kg at maximum 50 mg/min with ECG monitoring (risk of cardiac arrhythmia and hypotension), or IV sodium valproate 40 mg/kg (max 3000 mg) over five minutes (avoid in women of childbearing potential). Phase 3 (20 to 40 minutes, refractory status epilepticus): call anaesthetics for rapid sequence intubation (RSI) and general anaesthesia. Treat reversible causes throughout: hypoglycaemia (check BM and give 50 mL of 50% dextrose IV or Glucogel buccally), hypoxia, hyponatraemia, alcohol withdrawal (give thiamine before glucose to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy), meningitis (give ceftriaxone empirically), or non-convulsive status epilepticus (NCSE — requires EEG for diagnosis).

Diabetic Ketoacidosis

DKA is a medical emergency characterised by the triad of ketonaemia (blood ketones above 3 mmol/L or urinary ketones 2+ or above), hyperglycaemia (blood glucose above 11 mmol/L — or known diabetes with any glucose), and metabolic acidosis (venous pH below 7.3 or bicarbonate below 15 mmol/L). Causes include new-onset T1DM, intercurrent illness, missed insulin doses, and occasionally DKA in Type 2 diabetes (particularly in certain ethnic groups on SGLT-2 inhibitors — euglycaemic DKA). The Joint British Diabetes Societies (JBDS) 2023 guidelines are the operative guidance. Key principles of DKA management:

IV fluid resuscitation: 500 mL 0.9% NaCl bolus over fifteen minutes if systolic BP below 90 mmHg. Then 1 litre 0.9% NaCl over one hour, 1 litre over two hours, 1 litre over two hours, 1 litre over four hours, 1 litre over four hours (total approximately 4 litres in first eight hours). Once blood glucose falls to 14 mmol/L, add 10% dextrose at 125 mL/hour alongside 0.9% NaCl to prevent hypoglycaemia while the fixed-rate insulin infusion continues to clear ketones. Fixed-rate insulin infusion (FRIII): 0.1 units/kg/hour of Actrapid or Novorapid in 0.9% NaCl (not in glucose). Do NOT give bolus insulin at presentation. The FRIII continues until ketones clear (below 0.3 mmol/L), pH normalises, and the patient can eat and drink — then switch to subcutaneous insulin (restart background insulin immediately when switching). Potassium replacement: DKA patients are total-body potassium depleted despite apparently normal or high serum levels (intracellular shift). Add potassium to IV fluids: if K+ 3.5 to 5.5 mmol/L, add 40 mmol KCl per litre; if K+ above 5.5 mmol/L, no added K; if K+ below 3.5 mmol/L, senior review — may need higher rate. Do not start insulin if K+ below 3.5 mmol/L until corrected. Monitoring: hourly capillary blood glucose and ketones, hourly fluid input/output, two-hourly U&E, four-hourly VBG for pH and bicarbonate. Target: ketone fall of at least 0.5 mmol/L/hour, glucose fall of at least 3 mmol/L/hour, bicarbonate rise of at least 3 mmol/L/hour.

Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State

HHS (previously HONK — hyperosmolar non-ketotic coma) occurs predominantly in Type 2 diabetes. It is characterised by severe hyperglycaemia (blood glucose typically above 30 mmol/L), marked hyperosmolality (above 320 mOsm/kg), profound dehydration, and absence of significant ketonaemia (ketones below 3 mmol/L) or acidosis. The patient is typically elderly and may present with confusion, focal neurological signs, or seizures. Management: rehydrate slowly — 0.9% NaCl as the preferred fluid; correct osmolality over 48 hours to avoid cerebral oedema. Low-dose insulin infusion or sliding scale is introduced later (once glucose fails to fall adequately with fluids alone) to avoid precipitous drops. VTE prophylaxis is essential as HHS carries a very high thrombotic risk. Patients in HHS often have significant potassium losses and require careful replacement.

Hyperkalaemia

Hyperkalaemia (K+ above 5.5 mmol/L) is a medical emergency at levels above 6.5 mmol/L, or at any level with ECG changes or symptoms. Causes include AKI/CKD, Addison's disease, drugs (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, LMWH, NSAIDs, SGLT-2 inhibitors), acidosis, and tissue destruction (rhabdomyolysis, burns, massive haemolysis). ECG changes in progressive order: peaked (tall, narrow, tented) T waves → PR interval prolongation → P wave flattening and disappearance → QRS widening → sine wave pattern → ventricular fibrillation. Management: calcium gluconate 10% 10 mL IV over two to five minutes (membrane stabilisation — acts within minutes, does not lower potassium). Insulin-dextrose: ten units Actrapid in 50 mL of 50% dextrose IV over fifteen to thirty minutes (shifts K+ into cells — lowers K+ by 0.5 to 1.5 mmol/L within fifteen to thirty minutes). Salbutamol nebuliser 10 to 20 mg (also shifts K+ into cells — additive effect with insulin). Sodium bicarbonate if acidosis is contributing. Calcium resonium (sodium or calcium polystyrene sulfonate) or patiromer — binds K+ in the gut and promotes faecal excretion (takes twelve to twenty-four hours). Furosemide if renal function is adequate to promote renal K+ excretion. Haemodialysis for severe, refractory hyperkalaemia or if end-stage renal failure.

Stroke and TIA

Stroke is a clinical diagnosis: sudden onset of focal neurological deficit, lasting more than twenty-four hours or causing death. TIA (transient ischaemic attack) causes focal neurological deficit that completely resolves within twenty-four hours. The FAST acronym (Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 999) has been superseded by BE-FAST (Balance, Eyes, Face, Arms, Speech, Time) to include posterior circulation symptoms. NIHSS (National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale) quantifies neurological deficit and guides treatment decisions.

Immediate management: CT head without contrast is the first investigation — this excludes haemorrhagic stroke (which appears hyperdense on CT) before thrombolysis. If ischaemic stroke is confirmed (hypodense area on CT, or CT normal within first few hours) and onset is within four and a half hours, IV alteplase (thrombolysis) is given at 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) — ten percent as IV bolus, remainder as infusion over sixty minutes. Mechanical thrombectomy (endovascular clot retrieval) is recommended for large vessel occlusion strokes (anterior circulation) if treatable within six hours (or up to twenty-four hours in selected patients with penumbra on imaging). Aspirin 300 mg is given within twenty-four hours of ischaemic stroke (but not for twenty-four hours post-thrombolysis) and continued for two weeks before switching to long-term antiplatelet therapy. Admit to a stroke unit — this is the single most effective stroke intervention and reduces mortality and dependency regardless of stroke type.

TIA risk is scored using the ABCD2 score: Age (60 or above = 1), Blood pressure (140/90 or above = 1), Clinical features (unilateral weakness = 2, speech disturbance without weakness = 1), Duration (60 minutes or more = 2, ten to fifty-nine minutes = 1), Diabetes (1). Score of 4 or above indicates high risk — specialist TIA clinic within 24 hours. Any TIA or minor stroke should receive aspirin 300 mg immediately, dual antiplatelet for the first three weeks (aspirin plus clopidogrel as per POINT and CHANCE trials), then long-term aspirin or clopidogrel monotherapy. Carotid imaging (Doppler or CTA) should be performed urgently for anterior circulation TIA — if carotid stenosis above 50%, carotid endarterectomy is recommended within two weeks.

Bacterial Meningitis

Bacterial meningitis presents with fever, severe headache, photophobia, neck stiffness, and altered consciousness. Non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash suggests meningococcal disease and indicates septicaemia. In a patient without contraindications, lumbar puncture is the definitive diagnostic investigation — opening pressure, CSF appearance, cell count, protein and glucose, Gram stain, and culture. Contraindications to LP: papilloedema, focal neurological deficit, GCS below 12, coagulopathy, or haemodynamic instability. If any contraindication is present: take blood cultures, give IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone 2 g IV) and dexamethasone (0.15 mg/kg QDS for four days) immediately, then perform CT head before LP. Dexamethasone reduces neurological sequelae (particularly hearing loss in Streptococcus pneumoniae meningitis) and should be given with or before the first antibiotic dose.

Major Trauma

Major trauma is assessed using the primary survey: C-ABCDE — Catastrophic haemorrhage (stop exsanguinating haemorrhage first — tourniquet or direct pressure), then Airway (with C-spine protection), Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure. Haemostatic resuscitation (damage control resuscitation) involves transfusing packed red cells, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets in a 1:1:1 ratio to manage traumatic coagulopathy. Permissive hypotension (targeting systolic BP of approximately 80 to 90 mmHg) until haemorrhage is controlled avoids diluting clotting factors. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV within three hours of major haemorrhage has been shown in the CRASH-2 trial to reduce mortality. Massive transfusion protocol (MTP) is activated for ongoing blood loss requiring more than ten units of red cells in twenty-four hours. Whole blood is increasingly used in some trauma centres. Damage control surgery involves temporising surgical intervention to control haemorrhage and contamination, followed by definitive repair once the patient is physiologically stabilised (the "lethal triad" of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy is corrected).

Frequently Asked Questions About Emergency Medicine in the UKMLA

What is the correct order of management in anaphylaxis — should I give antihistamines before adrenaline?

No. Adrenaline is always the first and most important treatment in anaphylaxis. Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts and should only be given after adrenaline. A common mistake is to reach for antihistamines first because they feel safer — this delays life-saving treatment. The mnemonic is: call for help, lie flat, adrenaline IM, then oxygen, fluids, and adjuncts.

When should I give oxygen to a COPD patient in an emergency?

In an acute emergency involving a known COPD patient, give oxygen if the patient is hypoxic, and target SpO2 88 to 92% using controlled oxygen delivery (Venturi mask at 24 to 28%). However, if the patient is in extremis (SpO2 below 85%, cyanotic, haemodynamically compromised), treat immediately with high-flow oxygen first and reassess. The risk of over-oxygenation in type 2 respiratory failure is real but should never prevent treating life-threatening hypoxia. Use ABG to guide ongoing oxygen therapy.

How do I differentiate DKA from HHS?

DKA occurs predominantly in Type 1 diabetes, with blood glucose typically 11 to 30 mmol/L, significant ketonaemia (blood ketones above 3 mmol/L), and metabolic acidosis (pH below 7.3). HHS occurs predominantly in Type 2 diabetes, with very high blood glucose (commonly above 30 mmol/L), very high osmolality (above 320 mOsm/kg), absent or mild ketonaemia, and no significant acidosis. DKA presents more acutely (hours); HHS develops over days to weeks.

What is the Sepsis 6 and when should it be used?

The Sepsis 6 is a bundle of six care elements to be delivered within one hour of recognising high-risk sepsis: take blood cultures, measure lactate, give IV antibiotics, start IV fluids (crystalloid), measure urine output, and give high-flow oxygen. It is used for patients with suspected sepsis who have red flag features (systolic BP below 90 mmHg, HR above 130 bpm, RR above 25, or NEWS2 score of 7 or above, new confusion, or lactate above 2 mmol/L). Early implementation of the Sepsis 6 has been shown to reduce mortality by 46% in UK studies.

What ECG change is most specific for pulmonary embolism?

Sinus tachycardia is the most common ECG finding in PE, but is non-specific. The S1Q3T3 pattern (deep S wave in lead I, Q wave and inverted T wave in lead III) is the classic teaching sign but is actually seen in only around 20% of PE cases. New right bundle branch block (RBBB) and right axis deviation are more specific signs of right heart strain from PE. The most specific finding, when present, is a combination of sinus tachycardia with new RBBB and right axis deviation in a patient with a clinical presentation consistent with PE.

Conclusion

Emergency medicine and acute presentations form the backbone of what the GMC expects every doctor to manage on day one of their career. The structured ABCDE approach, NEWS2 scoring, and the specific management pathways for sepsis, anaphylaxis, DKA, ACS, stroke, and status epilepticus are among the most heavily tested topics in the entire UKMLA.
