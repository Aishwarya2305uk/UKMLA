Every time the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test is sat, the pass mark for that sitting is not set by selecting a fixed percentage. It is set through a formal process called Angoff standard setting. This process is central to why the UKMLA is considered a fair, valid, and criterion-referenced assessment — one where the pass mark reflects what is clinically necessary, not where most candidates happen to score. Understanding how Angoff standard setting works helps candidates interpret their results accurately, demystifies what "meeting the standard" actually means, and provides important context for understanding the legitimacy of the examination.

What Is Standard Setting?

Standard setting is the process of determining the minimum level of performance a candidate must demonstrate to be considered competent. In licensing examinations — including the UKMLA — it answers the question: "What score corresponds to a minimally competent foundation doctor?"

This is a fundamentally different question from "What is the average score?" or "What score puts you in the top 50%?" Standard setting is criterion-referenced: the pass mark is defined by what the examination should require, not by the distribution of how candidates actually performed. This distinction matters enormously for candidates.

In a criterion-referenced assessment:

  • 100% of candidates could pass if all demonstrate the required competency
  • 100% of candidates could fail if none demonstrate the required competency
  • The pass mark can vary between sittings if papers differ in difficulty
  • Your performance is compared to a competency standard, not to your cohort

This is the philosophical foundation of the UKMLA: the purpose of the examination is not to rank candidates against each other but to verify that each candidate meets the standard required to practice safely.

The Angoff Method: Step-by-Step

The Angoff method, named after educational psychologist William Angoff who described it in 1971, is one of the most widely used and well-researched standard-setting methods in high-stakes assessment. Here is how it works for the UKMLA AKT:

Step 1 — Assemble the Standard-Setting Panel

A panel of subject matter experts is convened. For the UKMLA, this panel includes:

  • Experienced clinicians (consultants, GPs, specialty registrars at senior levels) who have direct knowledge of what a newly qualified foundation doctor should be able to do
  • Medical educators who understand the curriculum and the examination blueprint
  • Patient advocates or lay representatives who bring the patient safety perspective
  • Psychometricians (measurement specialists) who oversee the technical process

The panel typically contains 10–20 members. Their backgrounds are chosen to represent the breadth of clinical and educational expertise relevant to the examination's purpose.

Step 2 — Train the Panel

Panellists are trained to anchor their judgements to a specific reference point: the Minimally Competent Candidate (MCC), also called the borderline or minimally competent doctor.

This is a conceptual reference point — a hypothetical doctor who just barely has enough knowledge and clinical reasoning to be safe in independent practice. Not an excellent foundation doctor; not a failing student — the specific, barely-adequate doctor at the exact pass/fail boundary.

Training exercises help panellists calibrate their understanding of this reference point by reviewing examples of candidate performance, discussing cases at the boundary of pass/fail, and aligning on what "minimally competent" looks like across different clinical domains.

Step 3 — Item-by-Item Angoff Ratings (Round 1)

Each panellist reviews every question (item) in the examination individually and provides an Angoff rating for that question: the proportion of minimally competent candidates who would answer this question correctly.

This is expressed as a probability between 0.00 and 1.00. A rating of 0.70 means: "I judge that 70% of minimally competent candidates would get this question right." A rating of 0.40 means: "I judge that only 40% of minimally competent candidates would get this right — it is a harder question."

This rating is explicitly about the MCC, not about excellent candidates or poor candidates. The panellist is essentially asking: "How difficult is this question for someone who is just barely competent?"

Step 4 — Discussion and Round 2

After the first round of individual ratings, panellists share their ratings with each other and discuss items where there is significant disagreement (technically, where the variance across panellists is high). Discussion focuses on:

  • What aspects of clinical competence the question is testing
  • How accessible this information would be to a minimally competent doctor
  • Whether the question assumes knowledge that is core or peripheral to the MLA Content Map
  • The correct answer and the explanation for it

After discussion, panellists revise their ratings independently for items where they choose to. This iterative process reduces the impact of individual outlier judgements and produces a more stable and defensible standard.

Step 5 — Calculate the Angoff Standard

For each question, the mean Angoff rating across all panellists is calculated. The Angoff pass mark for the whole paper is the sum of all the per-question mean ratings.

Mathematically: if a paper has 180 questions, and the sum of all mean Angoff ratings is 112.5, then the Angoff pass mark for that paper is 112.5 out of 180 — equivalent to approximately 62.5%.

This means the pass mark varies between sittings depending on the difficulty profile of that sitting's questions, as judged by the panel. A harder paper (lower mean Angoff ratings per item) will produce a lower Angoff pass mark. An easier paper will produce a higher pass mark.

Step 6 — Psychometric Adjustment

The raw Angoff standard may be adjusted by the psychometricians after the examination has been sat, using actual candidate performance data. There are two main adjustments:

Equating: Different versions of the examination (different sittings) are not identically difficult. Equating adjustments ensure that a score in one sitting is equivalent to the same score in another sitting — so that passing in a hard sitting is no harder than passing in an easy sitting.

Item analysis: After the examination, statistical analysis of item performance may identify questions that did not function as intended — for example, questions where very high-scoring candidates (who answered everything else correctly) performed worse than low-scoring candidates, suggesting the question had a flaw. Such items may be removed from scoring, which adjusts the final pass mark.

The Role of Psychometricians in the UKMLA

Psychometricians are measurement specialists who ensure the technical quality of the examination. Their role in UKMLA standard setting includes:

Before the examination: Training the standard-setting panel; quality-checking the item pool to ensure questions meet reliability and validity standards; reviewing statistical properties of new and pre-existing items; constructing examination forms with appropriate difficulty spread.

During standard setting: Facilitating the Angoff panel process; calculating round 1 and round 2 statistics; identifying items with high inter-rater variance for targeted discussion.

After the examination: Running item analysis on candidate performance; identifying malfunctioning items (poor discrimination, potential errors in the question); applying equating adjustments; calculating reliability statistics (Cronbach's alpha — the internal consistency of the test as a whole); producing the final report for the examination board.

The psychometric quality of an examination is reflected in its reliability coefficient (Cronbach's alpha): a value above 0.80 indicates strong reliability; most large-scale medical licensing examinations target Cronbach's alpha above 0.85. A highly reliable test produces similar results for the same candidate regardless of which specific items are included — indicating that the score reflects genuine knowledge rather than random item selection.

Standard Error of Measurement: The Uncertainty Around Your Score

No test is perfectly reliable. Every test score has a degree of measurement error — the extent to which your observed score differs from your true underlying level of competency. This is quantified as the Standard Error of Measurement (SEM).

The SEM is calculated from the test's reliability coefficient and standard deviation of scores:

SEM = SD × √(1 − r)

Where SD is the standard deviation of scores and r is the reliability coefficient (Cronbach's alpha).

In practice, this means that for a candidate whose true level of competency corresponds to a score of, say, 120 on a 180-item AKT, their observed score on any given sitting might vary by approximately ±1 SEM due to measurement uncertainty alone. If the SEM is 4 points, they might score anywhere between 116 and 124 on any given day — even if their underlying competency does not change.

This has direct implications for borderline results. A candidate who scores within 1 SEM of the pass mark is in a genuinely uncertain zone — the examination cannot reliably distinguish their true level of competency from the pass standard. The UKMLA's use of a structured appeals pathway acknowledges this statistical reality.

Borderline Regression for the CPSA

While the AKT uses the Angoff method, the CPSA uses borderline regression — a method that relies on actual candidate performance rather than pre-determined item ratings. This is appropriate because OSCE stations, unlike written questions, have more variability in performance and more complex marking structures.

In borderline regression:

  1. After each CPSA station, the examiner provides a global rating of the candidate: Clear Pass, Borderline Pass, Borderline Fail, or Clear Fail.
  2. This global rating is plotted against the candidate's numerical score on that station.
  3. A regression line is fitted to the data — it predicts the numerical score associated with each global rating level.
  4. The pass mark for that station is the numerical score predicted by the regression line at the "Borderline Pass/Fail" boundary.

The station pass marks are aggregated to produce an overall CPSA pass mark. Compensatory passing allows strong performance in some stations to offset weaker performance in others, provided no station falls below a specified minimum.

How to Challenge a Result Based on Standard Setting

Candidates cannot challenge the standard-setting method itself — the methodology is established and validated by the examination authority. However, there are legitimate grounds for appeal that relate to how standard setting was applied:

Valid grounds for appeal:

  • Evidence that examination administration conditions were materially disrupted in a way that affected your performance
  • Evidence that agreed reasonable adjustments were not properly implemented
  • Evidence of an administrative error in marking or in the application of equating adjustments to your result
  • Evidence that a question was subsequently identified as flawed and should have been removed from your sitting's score

Not valid grounds for appeal:

  • Believing the pass mark was too high
  • Believing specific questions were unfair, ambiguous, or outside the syllabus (unless you can show a formal error was identified and not corrected)
  • Receiving advice from a tutor or colleague that the "correct" answer for a question was different from the marked answer
  • Performing well in medical school assessments and finding it inconsistent that you did not pass the licensing exam

Appeals must be submitted within the specified timeframe — typically 10–20 working days from the result date. Contact your medical school's academic support office or the GMC (for PLAB candidates) promptly.

What Standard Setting Means for Your Revision Strategy

Understanding Angoff standard setting has direct practical implications for how you should approach UKMLA preparation:

The pass mark is not a percentage of all candidates: Do not try to estimate "how many marks you need" by looking at what percentile most candidates score. The pass mark is set by a competency standard, not a rank. Aim to demonstrate competency across the full Content Map, not to score in a particular range relative to your peers.

Consistent coverage is more important than perfection in any one area: The Angoff ratings for the UKMLA reflect the consensus view that a minimally competent doctor must know something about the breadth of the Content Map. Questions covering very common, high-stakes presentations (chest pain, acute breathlessness, sepsis) will have high Angoff ratings — the standard expects even the minimally competent candidate to answer these. Questions covering rare, complex, or highly specialised topics will have lower Angoff ratings — the standard accepts that some candidates will not know these. Revise breadth before depth.

Your question bank percentage is not the same as your AKT score: Question bank difficulty distributions differ from AKT item difficulty distributions. A candidate scoring 65% on Passmedicine is not equivalent to a candidate who will score 65% on the AKT. Question bank performance is a directional guide, not a precise prediction. Focus on trends (is your score improving?) and domain breakdown (where are your specific gaps?) rather than treating absolute percentages as predictive.

A borderline result is genuinely uncertain territory: Due to the SEM, a candidate who scored within one SEM of the pass mark may have had a different outcome on a different day. This is an inherent statistical uncertainty, not an injustice. Acknowledge it, and if you were very close to passing, recognise that a targeted revision of your weaker domains — even a small improvement — is likely to make the difference in a resit.

The Panellist Experience: What Standard Setting Looks Like From the Inside

Medical educators and clinicians who serve on UKMLA standard-setting panels describe several recurring challenges and insights from the process:

The difficulty of thinking about the MCC: Panellists consistently report that holding the Minimally Competent Candidate concept firmly in mind — rather than asking "what would a good doctor know?" or "what do I expect my foundation trainees to know?" — is the hardest part of the task. Both of these alternative frames produce artificially high Angoff ratings, because they anchor to a higher standard than the minimal competency threshold. Training and calibration exercises help, but the challenge persists.

Disagreement among panellists is expected and productive: A panel where all Angoff ratings cluster tightly without discussion is a red flag — it suggests panellists may be deferring to each other rather than making independent judgements. Legitimate disagreement, and the structured discussion that resolves it, is where the real calibration work happens.

Panel composition affects the standard: A panel composed predominantly of senior consultants may produce higher Angoff standards (they expect more) than a panel with more foundation doctors and clinical educators (who are calibrated to what junior doctors actually know). The UKMLA deliberately includes educators and clinicians across career stages to balance these perspectives.

Context matters: Panellists report that the same question rated in isolation may receive a different Angoff rating than when it is considered alongside the patient scenario and clinical context in the question stem. This is appropriate — the Angoff rating should reflect the difficulty of the question as a whole, including its clinical context, not just the bare clinical fact it tests.

Frequently Asked Questions: Angoff Standard Setting and the UKMLA

Is the pass mark the same for every UKMLA sitting?
No. The Angoff pass mark varies between sittings because each sitting has a different set of questions, and the sum of the mean Angoff ratings across those specific questions will differ. The process is designed to ensure that the difficulty of the examination is reflected in the pass mark — a harder paper will have a lower pass mark, and an easier paper will have a higher pass mark.

Can I find out what the pass mark was for my sitting?
The pass mark may be communicated to candidates along with their results, or it may be available through your medical school's examination board. Contact your medical school's academic office for information specific to your sitting.

If a question is later removed due to a flaw, does this affect the pass mark?
Yes. Item removal changes both the total possible score and the Angoff standard (since the removed item's Angoff rating is no longer included in the sum). The net effect depends on the specific item and may raise or lower the pass mark. Candidates' scores are typically recalculated to exclude the removed item.

How long does the standard-setting process take?
A full Angoff standard-setting panel for a 180-item examination typically runs over one to two days, including training, two rounds of ratings, and discussion. Post-examination psychometric analysis and equating may take additional weeks before the final pass mark is confirmed.

Why not just use a fixed 60% pass mark for simplicity?
A fixed percentage assumes all examination papers are equally difficult, which is empirically false. Using a fixed pass mark when paper difficulty varies means that candidates sitting harder papers are unfairly disadvantaged, while candidates sitting easier papers benefit without earning it. The Angoff method adjusts for this by anchoring the pass mark to competency rather than to an arbitrary percentage.

Does the GMC publish information about the standard-setting process?
Yes. The GMC's website includes technical reports on the UKMLA examination quality and methodology. The Medical Schools Council also publishes guidance on assessment standards. Candidates interested in the technical details of the process can access these documents directly through the GMC and MSC websites.

Conclusion: Standard Setting as a Foundation of Examination Fairness

The Angoff method is not perfect — no standard-setting method is. It relies on human judgement, which is fallible, and it produces a single pass mark that applies equally to all candidates regardless of the context in which they are training. But it is the most thoroughly validated, widely accepted, and operationally feasible method available for setting pass marks on large-scale written medical licensing examinations.

For UKMLA candidates, the key practical takeaway is this: you are being compared to a competency standard, not to your peers. Meeting the standard requires demonstrating applied clinical knowledge and reasoning across the breadth of the MLA Content Map. The pass mark is set before you sit (based on the Angoff ratings) to reflect what a minimally competent doctor should know — and your job is to exceed it.

Item Response Theory: The Technical Foundation Behind Modern Examinations

While the Angoff method provides the initial pass mark estimate based on expert judgement, modern medical licensing examinations use Item Response Theory (IRT) to analyse and refine examination quality and enable score equating across sittings. Understanding IRT helps explain why test developers can compare your score to the pass standard even when different examination forms contain different questions.

IRT models the relationship between a candidate's latent ability (their true level of clinical competency) and their probability of answering a given question correctly. Unlike classical test theory (which just counts right and wrong answers), IRT estimates a candidate's ability on a continuous scale by considering the specific pattern of their correct and incorrect answers in relation to each question's statistical properties.

Each AKT item has three IRT parameters:

  • Difficulty (b parameter): The ability level at which a candidate has a 50% probability of answering correctly. A high b-value = a hard question.
  • Discrimination (a parameter): How well the question differentiates between higher-ability and lower-ability candidates. A highly discriminating question (high a-value) produces a sharp transition from "mostly wrong" to "mostly right" as ability increases. A poorly discriminating question produces little difference in performance across ability levels.
  • Guessing (c parameter): The probability that a candidate with very low ability answers correctly by chance. For a five-option SBA, the theoretical minimum is 0.20 (one in five chance of guessing correctly).

IRT enables equating — the statistical procedure that ensures a score of, for example, 120 on one sitting reflects the same clinical competency level as 120 on a different sitting, regardless of paper difficulty. Equating is performed using anchor items (questions that appear in multiple sittings with known difficulty parameters) or by linking sittings through common-person groups. The practical result: pass marks from different sittings are comparable, and a candidate who sits in a harder window is not disadvantaged relative to one who sits in an easier window.

Blueprinting: How the MLA Content Map Translates Into AKT Questions

Blueprinting is the process of systematically linking examination content to the curriculum framework — in the case of the UKMLA, to the MLA Content Map. A well-blueprinted examination ensures that the breadth of the Content Map is covered in proportion to its clinical importance, and that candidates cannot predict and avoid specific content areas.

For the UKMLA AKT, blueprinting means:

  • Each of the 130 MLA presentations is represented in the item bank proportionate to its prevalence and clinical significance
  • High-frequency, high-stakes presentations (chest pain, acute breathlessness, headache, altered consciousness, sepsis) are tested more heavily than rare, specialised conditions
  • The major clinical domains (medicine, surgery, primary care, paediatrics, psychiatry, obstetrics) are each represented according to their significance in the Foundation Programme clinical environment
  • Professional, legal, and ethical content is included throughout — not as a separate domain but integrated into clinical scenarios

The practical implication for candidates: revision that follows the MLA Content Map systematically is doing exactly what the blueprint is designed to reward. Identifying which presentations you have not yet revised and addressing them deliberately is the single most direct response to how the AKT is constructed.

Validity: What the UKMLA Actually Measures

Reliability (consistency of measurement) and validity (measuring what you intend to measure) are the two fundamental quality indicators of any assessment. The Angoff standard-setting process and IRT analysis protect reliability; validity requires a separate evidence base.

The UKMLA's validity rests on several pillars:

Content validity: The MLA Content Map was developed by the GMC with extensive consultation with clinicians, educators, patients, and researchers. Its coverage of the knowledge and skills required in Foundation Programme practice has been systematically reviewed. The blueprinting process ensures the examination samples from the full content domain rather than a narrow, predictable subset.

Criterion validity: Evidence that UKMLA performance predicts real-world clinical performance outcomes — such as Foundation Programme performance, patient safety incident rates, or clinical supervisor ratings. This type of validity evidence is the strongest possible and is actively studied by the GMC's education and training directorate. Early evidence from AKT predecessor examinations (the UKMLE predecessor to the UKMLA) showed moderate correlations between AKT performance and Foundation Programme clinical assessment outcomes.

Construct validity: Evidence that the examination structure measures what it claims to measure (clinical competency) rather than extraneous factors such as test-wiseness, socioeconomic background, or familiarity with the examination format. The SBA format, standardised administration conditions, and standard-setting process all contribute to construct validity. Research on potential bias — whether specific demographic groups perform differently on the UKMLA in ways not explained by competency differences — is an active area of GMC research.

Post-Hoc Fairness Analysis: Ensuring the Examination Is Not Biased

After each UKMLA sitting, the examination authority conducts differential item functioning (DIF) analysis — a statistical procedure to identify questions that function differently for different candidate subgroups (defined by gender, educational background, first language, ethnicity, or clinical placement type) in ways not explained by overall ability differences.

A question exhibits DIF if candidates of equal overall ability perform differently on that specific question based on their group membership. For example, if male and female candidates of equal overall AKT ability perform differently on a specific gynaecology question, this would be flagged for review. DIF is not automatically a signal that the question is biased — it could reflect genuine differences in clinical exposure or legitimate curriculum differences — but it triggers expert review of the question's content, phrasing, and assumptions.

Questions with significant DIF that cannot be explained on clinical grounds are removed from scoring or flagged for revision in future sittings. This continuous fairness review is a key component of how the examination authority maintains confidence that the UKMLA measures clinical competency fairly across the diverse UK medical student population.

What "Meeting the Standard" Really Means: The Competency Threshold in Context

The Angoff standard-setting process defines the pass mark as the score expected from a "minimally competent candidate" — a doctor who just barely meets the threshold for safe independent practice. This framing sometimes concerns candidates who feel that "minimally competent" sets the bar too low.

It is important to understand what "minimally competent" means in the licensing examination context and what it does not mean:

It does not mean "barely functioning": The MCC is a conceptual anchor, not a prediction about any individual doctor's practice. Doctors who barely meet the licensing standard do not necessarily practice at a "barely adequate" level — the standard is set at the minimum required for safe entry to supervised foundation practice, where ongoing learning, supervision, and development continue.

It reflects the purpose of the examination: A licensing examination is not designed to identify the best doctors — that is the role of educational achievement records, clinical supervisor ratings, and career progression assessments. A licensing examination is designed to identify the minimum safe threshold below which a doctor should not enter practice. Setting the standard at the MCC threshold is exactly right for this purpose.

The standard represents clinical consensus: The Angoff panel's collective judgement about what the MCC should know represents the clinical community's best current answer to the question of what a newly licensed doctor in the UK must be competent in. It is not a mathematical artefact — it is a professionally-grounded statement about the knowledge required for safe practice at the point of entry to foundation training.

Understanding this helps candidates contextualise both pass and fail results. A pass means you have met the consensus standard for safe entry to practice — a meaningful achievement. A fail means your performance did not reach that standard on this sitting — a signal that additional preparation is needed before you begin independent practice, not a verdict on your long-term potential as a clinician.

The Future of Standard Setting in the UKMLA

Medical education assessment is a dynamic field, and the methods used to set standards in medical licensing examinations continue to evolve. Several developments are influencing how standard setting may change in future UKMLA iterations:

Adaptive testing: Computer-adaptive testing (CAT) algorithms select questions for each candidate based on their performance on previous questions, converging on the candidate's ability level more efficiently than a fixed-form paper. CAT has been adopted in some nursing licensing examinations (NCLEX) and is being evaluated for medical licensing. CAT would change the standard-setting landscape significantly — the item difficulty parameters from IRT become the primary determinant of difficulty rather than a fixed Angoff rating for a specific paper.

Workplace-based assessment integration: There is ongoing discussion in medical education about whether licensing assessment should extend beyond point-in-time examinations to incorporate longitudinal workplace-based assessments. This would bring the standard-setting challenge into the clinical environment, where reliability is harder to achieve than in standardised examination conditions.

Automated item generation: AI-driven item generation tools can produce AKT-style questions at scale from clinical content. As these tools improve, they could substantially expand the item bank, enabling more precise blueprinting and potentially enabling shorter, more frequent assessments that give candidates more granular feedback on their developing competency.

Transparency and Public Confidence in the UKMLA Standard

The GMC publishes reports on the UKMLA examination quality, including information on pass rates, reliability statistics, and the standard-setting methodology. This transparency serves two purposes: it allows medical schools to evaluate how their students are performing relative to the national standard, and it allows the medical profession and public to have confidence that the standard being applied is rigorous, consistent, and defensible.

Pass rates for the UKMLA are monitored at both the cohort level (what proportion of all candidates passed in a given year) and at the institutional level (how different medical schools' cohorts compare). Significant and persistent variations in pass rate between institutions are a signal that prompts investigation — either of the examination's validity for specific student populations, or of the educational preparation being provided by institutions with consistently lower pass rates.

The GMC's approach to transparency in standard setting reflects a broader principle in healthcare quality assurance: outcomes should be publicly reported, outliers should be investigated, and the standard itself should be subject to ongoing review and refinement in light of evidence about what foundation doctors actually need to know to practice safely. The Angoff standard-setting process is not a static historical artefact — it is reviewed and recalibrated periodically as the clinical environment, the Foundation Programme curriculum, and the evidence base for medical education all evolve.

For candidates, this transparency means that the pass mark you are being measured against is not arbitrary. It has been derived through a systematic, expert, iterative process and has been subject to ongoing psychometric validation. Meeting it is a meaningful achievement; not meeting it is a meaningful signal. Both outcomes provide information about your readiness for independent practice, and both deserve to be taken seriously.
