GMC Regulation of Physician Associates: What Doctors Need to Know

The GMC regulation of physician associates is now a fact of everyday clinical life in the UK, and it changes what you need to know as a doctor working alongside these colleagues on the ward, in outpatient clinics, or in theatre. Whether you are a UK medical student about to start foundation training or an international medical graduate (IMG) preparing for the UKMLA and a future NHS post, you will almost certainly work in a multidisciplinary team that includes a physician associate (PA) or an anaesthesia associate (AA). Understanding how these roles are regulated, what a named supervisor is responsible for, and where the professional boundaries sit is now a genuinely examinable and practically essential piece of knowledge — not a peripheral policy detail.

This guide sets out, in plain and strictly factual terms, what changed when the General Medical Council (GMC) took on regulatory responsibility for PAs and AAs, what the December 2026 registration deadline actually means, how supervision and scope of practice work in the new system, and what any doctor — whether newly qualified or newly registered via the GMC registration route — needs to do differently as a result. It also explains, without taking a side, why the appropriate scope of PA and AA practice remains an actively discussed topic among medical Royal Colleges and professional bodies, and how that debate connects to a wider consultation on GMC Order reform and protected medical titles.

Quick Answer: What Does GMC Regulation of Physician Associates Actually Mean?

The GMC has regulated physician associates and anaesthesia associates in the UK since 13 December 2024, with registration opening from 16 December 2024. This means PAs and AAs now sit on their own GMC registers, are held to the same core professional standards as doctors under Good Medical Practice, and can be investigated by the GMC if serious concerns are raised about their conduct or performance. From December 2026, it will become a criminal offence to practise as a PA or AA anywhere in the UK without GMC registration, closing a two-year transition window that currently allows unregistered but otherwise qualified individuals to continue working while they complete the registration process.

Crucially, GMC regulation of physician associates does not create a single, fixed national job description that tells every PA or AA exactly what tasks they may perform. Instead, the GMC takes a deliberately non-prescriptive approach: what an individual PA or AA can safely do depends on their own skills and competence, built up over time and confirmed by their named supervisor. This is the single most important practical point for any doctor working alongside a PA or AA — you cannot assume competence based on the job title alone, and you cannot assume every PA in every department can do the same things.

The Headline Facts in Brief

  • Regulation began: 13 December 2024
  • Registration opened: 16 December 2024
  • Criminal offence for unregistered practice begins: December 2026
  • Governing professional standards: Good Medical Practice (the same document that applies to doctors)
  • Scope of practice: determined individually, confirmed by a named supervisor — not fixed nationally by the GMC
  • Regulator: the GMC, the same body responsible for doctor revalidation and medical registration generally

The rest of this guide unpacks each of these points in detail, with separate sections addressing what matters most for UK medical students entering foundation training and for IMGs joining the NHS workforce, since the practical implications differ slightly depending on where you are in your career.

Why the GMC Now Regulates Physician Associates and Anaesthesia Associates

Physician associates and anaesthesia associates are healthcare professionals who work under the supervision of doctors, supporting medical teams with tasks such as taking histories, performing examinations, requesting and interpreting investigations, and contributing to management plans, within the scope agreed with their supervisor. Before December 2024, these roles existed in the NHS but had no statutory regulator — there was no legal register, no enforceable professional code specific to the roles, and no formal mechanism for a national body to investigate serious concerns about an individual's fitness to practise in the role.

Parliament brought PAs and AAs into statutory regulation via an Order made under the Health and Social Care Act 2012 framework, giving the GMC the same core regulatory powers over these professions that it already holds over doctors: setting standards for the qualifications needed to join the register, approving the curricula and assessments PA and AA students must pass, maintaining a public register that patients and employers can check, and investigating fitness-to-practise concerns. The GMC was chosen as the regulator partly because PAs and AAs work so closely within medical teams, and partly to create a single, consistent framework of professional standards across the whole team a patient encounters. The GMC sets out the full detail of its role, remit, and the reasoning behind this regulatory model on its own page on regulating physician associates and anaesthesia associates, which is the authoritative primary source for anything not covered here.

The Registration Timeline in Detail

Understanding the exact sequencing of GMC regulation of physician associates matters because it explains why some PAs and AAs you meet in clinical practice may describe themselves as "GMC registered" while others may still be completing that process.

During the transition period, an employer may still have PAs or AAs on staff who are in the process of registering rather than already registered. As a doctor supervising or working alongside a PA or AA during this window, it is reasonable — and sensible — to check the registration status of a colleague directly on the GMC's public register rather than assuming it from their job title or employment start date. After December 2026, continuing to practise as a PA or AA without being on the register will be a criminal matter, not simply a professional or employment one, which sharpens the urgency of the transition for anyone not yet registered.

What the GMC's Regulatory Role Actually Covers

As the regulator, the GMC's responsibilities for PAs and AAs mirror, in structure, its responsibilities for doctors:

  • Setting the standards of patient care and professional behaviour PAs and AAs must meet
  • Approving the curricula and setting the outcomes that PA and AA courses must deliver before graduates can join the register
  • Checking who is eligible to work as a PA or AA in the UK and maintaining the public register
  • Providing guidance and advice to PAs, AAs, employers, and supervising doctors
  • Investigating concerns that may put patient safety at risk, through fitness-to-practise processes broadly similar to those used for doctors

This means that, functionally, a PA or AA registered with the GMC is now accountable in a similar way to a doctor: patients, employers, and colleagues can raise a concern, and the GMC can investigate and, where necessary, restrict or remove that individual's registration. This is a materially different accountability structure from the pre-2024 position, where PAs and AAs were unregulated and any concerns had to be handled solely through local employment processes.

Understanding Scope of Practice: The GMC's Non-Prescriptive Approach

One of the most misunderstood aspects of GMC regulation of physician associates is the question of scope of practice — in other words, exactly what tasks a PA or AA is allowed to perform. Many doctors, understandably, expect a regulator to publish a fixed list: "a PA may do X, Y and Z, and may never do A, B or C." That is not how the GMC has approached this.

Why There Is No Single National Scope of Practice

The GMC has been explicit that it does not set a single, defined post-qualification scope of practice that determines what tasks any given PA or AA can safely carry out. The reasoning given is that safe scope of practice is inherently individual: it depends on that specific PA or AA's own skills, training history, and demonstrated competence, developed over time in their actual clinical setting, and confirmed by their named supervisor — not on a generic list that would have to apply identically to every PA in every specialty, in every hospital, regardless of their actual experience.

In practical terms, this means two PAs with the same job title, working in the same specialty, in two different hospitals, may legitimately have different scopes of practice, because one has more experience, more specific training, or a supervisor who has assessed and signed off a wider range of competencies than the other. For a doctor moving between departments, rotating through placements, or joining a new trust, this is the single fact most likely to trip you up if you assume "PA" is a role with one fixed meaning nationally.

The Role of Royal Colleges in Clarifying Scope

Because the GMC does not set a single national scope of practice, it has supported work led by individual Royal Colleges and the Academy of Medical Royal Colleges to further clarify scope-of-practice issues within specific clinical contexts. Royal Colleges are generally better placed than a generalist regulator to define what safe, appropriate PA or AA involvement looks like within a specific specialty — for example, what an AA can appropriately be asked to do in an anaesthetic list, or what a PA working in general practice or general surgery can reasonably be expected to manage independently versus under direct supervision.

This division of labour — GMC setting professional standards and registration requirements, Royal Colleges providing specialty-specific scope guidance — is a deliberate structural choice, and it means that a doctor wanting to understand what a PA in, say, emergency medicine or psychiatry can typically be expected to do should look for guidance from the relevant Royal College alongside the GMC's own materials, rather than expecting the GMC alone to have published an exhaustive answer.

The Ongoing Debate About Scope of Practice

It is worth being direct about this: the appropriate scope of PA and AA practice remains an actively discussed topic among medical Royal Colleges and professional bodies, and there has been public debate about how PA and AA roles should be defined, supervised, and limited in relation to patient safety. This guide does not take a position in that debate. What can be stated factually is the structure the GMC has put in place to manage it: individualised scope, confirmed by supervision, informed by Royal College guidance, and subject to fitness-to-practise investigation where a PA or AA is found to have practised beyond their agreed competence. Doctors should be aware that this conversation is ongoing and that guidance from Royal Colleges and NHS employers may continue to evolve as the profession matures within statutory regulation, and that the same caution around avoidable prescribing and clinical errors that applies to doctors generally applies with equal force to any team member operating close to the edge of their competence.

The Named Supervisor Model: What Changes If You Become a Supervisor

For many doctors, the most immediate practical consequence of GMC regulation of physician associates is not a change to their own registration, but a change to their responsibilities if they take on the role of named supervisor for a PA or AA. This applies to newly qualified doctors relatively early in their careers, since PAs and AAs are often supervised day-to-day by registrars, consultants, and — in some settings — more experienced senior house officers, not exclusively by consultants.

What a Named Supervisor Actually Determines

The named supervising doctor is the person who determines the range of activities a specific PA or AA can undertake, and at what level of supervision — direct, indirect, or with periodic review. This is not a one-off decision made on day one of a placement. It is an ongoing, evolving judgement based on directly observed competence, feedback from the wider team, and the PA or AA's own track record of raising concerns appropriately when a task falls outside what they have been signed off to do.

In practice, becoming a named supervisor for a PA or AA means:

  • Assessing, and periodically reassessing, what tasks the individual can safely perform
  • Setting the level of supervision required for each type of task — some may be performed independently once competence is demonstrated, others may always require direct doctor oversight
  • Being available (in person or via an agreed escalation pathway) for the PA or AA to seek help when something falls outside their agreed scope
  • Documenting competency decisions in a way that is defensible if a concern is later raised
  • Understanding that overall clinical responsibility for a patient's care remains with the supervising and wider medical team, not solely with the PA or AA

This is a genuinely new professional responsibility for many junior and middle-grade doctors, and it is worth taking seriously well before you are actually asked to supervise a PA or AA — for example, by proactively reading local trust policy on PA/AA supervision as soon as you join a new department, rather than waiting until you are asked to sign off a competency you have not thought about in advance.

The PA or AA's Own Duty to Escalate

Supervision is not a one-way responsibility. PAs and AAs have a professional duty to prioritise patient safety and to seek help if a task is outside their competence, training, or agreed scope — and this duty holds even if they had previously agreed to attempt the task. In other words, a PA who initially accepted a task cannot be criticised for later declining or escalating it if, on reflection or on encountering an unexpected complication, they recognise it is beyond what they can safely manage. This mirrors exactly the same principle that applies to doctors under Good Medical Practice: recognising the limits of your competence and escalating appropriately is a professional obligation, not a sign of weakness, and supervisors should actively support colleagues — of any professional background — who raise their hand in this way.

How to Understand What a Specific PA or AA Colleague Can and Cannot Do

Given that scope of practice is individual rather than fixed nationally, how should a doctor working alongside a PA or AA for the first time actually find out what that colleague can and cannot do? A few practical, low-friction approaches:

  • Ask directly. A simple, professional question — "What have you been signed off to do independently, and what would you want me involved in?" — is entirely appropriate and, in most well-functioning teams, expected.
  • Check local departmental competency documentation. Most trusts maintain a local record of what each PA/AA has been assessed as competent for, often tied to a specific supervisor's sign-off.
  • Speak to the named supervisor. If you are working with a PA or AA you do not know well, their named supervisor is the authoritative source on their current scope.
  • Check GMC registration status directly. Especially during the transition period before December 2026, confirming registration on the GMC's public register is a reasonable and quick step.
  • Never assume equivalence across settings. A PA who has extensive experience and a wide agreed scope in one department may have a much narrower one in a different specialty they have just joined.

This is precisely the kind of practical, structural knowledge that increasingly appears in professional-standards and workplace-based scenarios across UKMLA preparation, alongside related themes such as the professional standards doctors are expected to apply and how concerns about a colleague's practice should be escalated.

Professional Standards: How Good Medical Practice Applies to PAs and AAs

Once registered, PAs and AAs are required to follow the same core professional standards as doctors, as set out in Good Medical Practice — the GMC's foundational statement of the values, knowledge, skills, and behaviours expected of everyone on its registers. This is a deliberate design choice: rather than writing a wholly separate code of conduct for PAs and AAs, the GMC extended the existing framework doctors are already held to, adapted to reflect the PA/AA role.

Shared Obligations Under Good Medical Practice

Key obligations that now apply equally to registered PAs, AAs, and doctors include:

  • Working within the limits of your competence — recognising when a task is beyond your training or current scope, and seeking help or escalating rather than attempting it regardless
  • Clearly introducing yourself and your role to patients — a patient is entitled to know whether they are being seen by a doctor, a physician associate, or an anaesthesia associate, and PAs/AAs must not allow ambiguity about their role to persist
  • Maintaining honesty and transparency — including being open about uncertainty, and about any mistakes made
  • Prioritising patient safety above all other considerations, including workplace pressure to complete a task that has not been properly assessed as within scope
  • Participating appropriately in ongoing professional development, consistent with maintaining and expanding competence over time

For doctors, this shared standards framework has a genuinely practical upside: when you delegate a task to, or receive a handover from, a registered PA or AA, you are dealing with a colleague who is bound by broadly the same core professional obligations you are — not an unregulated team member operating under a different, unenforceable set of expectations. That said, it does not mean every clinical duty or legal responsibility is identical between the roles; prescribing rights, ordering certain investigations, and other specific clinical activities remain governed by separate, task-specific legal and employer frameworks, which is exactly why individualised, supervisor-confirmed scope of practice still matters even under a shared professional-standards umbrella.

Introducing Roles Clearly to Patients

The requirement to introduce role clearly deserves particular emphasis, because it is one of the more concrete, checkable obligations in this area. A patient who has been seen by a physician associate is entitled to know that explicitly — not to infer it, and not to be left assuming they saw a doctor when they did not. As a supervising or team-based doctor, part of supporting a good clinical environment is reinforcing this culture: making sure clinic templates, ward round introductions, and discharge documentation make the identity and role of every team member clear, rather than leaving it to chance or informal convention.

Fitness to Practise and Accountability

Because PAs and AAs are now GMC-registered, serious concerns about their conduct or performance can be formally investigated through the GMC's fitness-to-practise processes, broadly analogous to those used for doctors. This gives patients, employers, and colleagues — including supervising doctors — a route for raising concerns that did not exist in a statutory form before December 2024. As with doctor fitness-to-practise cases, individual investigations will have regard to whether the activity in question was within the individual's agreed scope of practice, and whether they had been assessed by a supervisor as competent and authorised to undertake it — reinforcing, again, why clear documentation of supervision decisions matters on the doctor's side of this relationship.

What This Means for UK Medical Students

If you are a UK medical student heading towards foundation training, GMC regulation of physician associates is likely to affect you in two main ways: first, as a trainee who will work in teams that include PAs and AAs from your very first placement; and second, relatively early in your career, as someone who may be asked to help supervise or delegate tasks to a PA or AA before you feel especially senior yourself.

Working Alongside PAs and AAs as a Junior Doctor

As a foundation year doctor, you will likely encounter PAs and AAs as established members of the ward team, sometimes with more day-to-day clinical experience in that specific environment than you have in your first few months. This can feel like an odd dynamic — a foundation doctor is the doctor on the team, with the accountability that comes with GMC registration and a medical degree, while working alongside a PA who may have years of experience in that particular department. Handling this well means being clear about role and responsibility without being dismissive of a colleague's genuine clinical experience: you can, and should, draw on a PA's departmental knowledge while still being clear that certain decisions — particularly those involving diagnosis, prescribing, and overall treatment planning — sit within the doctor's remit and accountability.

Early Supervisory Responsibility

Because named supervision of PAs and AAs is not restricted to consultants, you may find yourself contributing to supervision or competency sign-off relatively early — for example, as a senior house officer or registrar working closely with a PA on a specific task or rotation. Understanding, from the outset of your career, that scope of practice is individualised and supervisor-confirmed — not a fixed job description — puts you in a much stronger position to supervise well rather than either over-restricting a competent colleague or, conversely, assuming competence that has not actually been assessed.

Relevance to Postgraduate Training and Recruitment

This is also relevant background as you move through foundation training and into specialty applications. Workforce planning discussions increasingly reference how PA and AA roles interact with training posts and rota design, and awareness of the regulatory framework is useful context when navigating changes to foundation programme recruitment and how multidisciplinary staffing models are evolving across NHS trusts.

What This Means for IMGs

For international medical graduates preparing for the UKMLA and planning a route into the NHS, GMC regulation of physician associates matters for a slightly different reason: it shapes the team environment you will join, and it is a useful reference point for understanding how the GMC's regulatory model works more broadly — since the same regulator that governs PA/AA registration also governs your own registration route.

Understanding the NHS Multidisciplinary Team Before You Arrive

Many IMGs come from healthcare systems with a different skill-mix model, where a role directly equivalent to the PA or AA either does not exist or works very differently. Arriving in the NHS without understanding that PAs and AAs are now GMC-regulated colleagues — with their own register, their own professional standards obligations, and an individualised, supervisor-confirmed scope of practice — can lead to avoidable confusion in your first few weeks: assuming a PA has broader authority than they have been signed off for, or conversely being unsure how to delegate or hand over safely to a PA whose competencies you have not yet had explained to you. This applies whether you join the NHS through a substantive post, a fellowship, or a fixed-term route such as the Medical Training Initiative scheme, since every one of these routes places you in a ward team that is likely to include PA or AA colleagues.

A practical first step in any new post is the same one recommended earlier in this guide: ask directly, check local documentation, and speak to the named supervisor rather than assuming. This is doubly useful for IMGs, since it also demonstrates exactly the kind of proactive, safety-conscious communication that UK clinical culture and GMC standards expect.

How This Connects to Your Own GMC Registration

As an IMG, your own path onto the GMC register — whether via the UKMLA route, sponsorship, or another eligibility pathway — runs through the same regulator now responsible for PA and AA registration. Understanding how the GMC structures registration timelines, transition periods, and enforcement (as seen in the PA/AA December 2026 offence deadline) gives useful insight into how seriously the GMC treats registration compliance generally, and reinforces why keeping your own registration, English language evidence, and any conditions attached to your licence to practise fully up to date matters just as much once you are working as it did during your initial application. For the practicalities of your own registration process, the GMC registration guide remains the definitive starting point.

Multidisciplinary Team Working as an Explicit Competency

Effective multidisciplinary team working — including understanding the roles, responsibilities, and scope of colleagues from different professional backgrounds — is itself an explicit theme in both UKMLA preparation and day-to-day NHS practice. IMGs who take time to understand the structural facts covered in this guide (registration timelines, the supervision model, and shared professional standards) will generally integrate into ward teams more smoothly than those who first encounter PAs and AAs without any prior context, since they will already understand why a PA's scope might differ from a colleague of the same title at a previous post, or from what they may have seen in training programmes overseas.

The Wider Context: GMC Order Reform and Protected Medical Titles

GMC regulation of physician associates does not exist in isolation. It is part of a broader modernisation of the legislation that governs the GMC itself — commonly referred to as GMC Order reform. This wider reform programme, which has been the subject of government consultation, covers issues including how the GMC's own governing legislation should be updated, and — closely related to the PA/AA regulation story — questions of title protection and clarity around who may describe themselves using specific medical or medically-adjacent titles.

The connection matters practically: as PAs and AAs have become GMC-regulated professionals working alongside doctors, questions have naturally followed about how their professional titles should be protected and distinguished from those of registered medical practitioners, so that patients are never left uncertain about whether the person treating them is a doctor. This title-clarity strand sits within the same overall GMC Order reform consultation on protected medical titles, rather than being a separate, unrelated initiative. Doctors who want the fuller picture of how GMC regulation is evolving structurally — beyond just the PA/AA registration story covered in this guide — should treat that title-protection reform as directly connected background reading.

Practical Steps for Doctors Working Alongside PAs and AAs

Bringing the threads of this guide together, here is a practical checklist for any doctor — UK-trained or IMG, junior or senior — working alongside physician associates or anaesthesia associates under the current regulatory framework.

If You Are Not Yet a Supervisor

  • Do not assume a PA or AA's scope of practice based on job title alone — ask, or check local documentation
  • Introduce yourself clearly by role, and expect the same clarity from PA/AA colleagues
  • Escalate concerns about a colleague's practice through the same professional channels you would use for any team member, regardless of their professional background
  • Use the GMC's public register to confirm registration status if it is ever genuinely unclear, particularly before December 2026

If You Are Becoming, or May Become, a Named Supervisor

  • Read your trust or department's local PA/AA supervision policy before you are asked to sign off a competency, not after
  • Treat scope-of-practice decisions as ongoing and reviewable, not fixed at the start of a placement
  • Document competency assessments clearly, in a way that would be defensible if a concern is later raised
  • Actively support a PA or AA who escalates a task as being outside their competence — this is the system working as intended, not a failure
  • Stay aware of relevant Royal College guidance for your specialty, since it will often be more specific than general GMC materials

If You Are an IMG New to the NHS

  • Treat your first encounters with PA/AA colleagues as an opportunity to ask direct, clarifying questions about scope and supervision
  • Do not import assumptions about equivalent roles from a different healthcare system without checking how the role actually functions locally
  • Keep your own GMC registration status and any associated requirements current, using the same regulator that oversees PA/AA registration as your reference point

Frequently Asked Questions

When did the GMC start regulating physician associates?

The GMC began regulating physician associates and anaesthesia associates on 13 December 2024, with registration opening shortly afterwards on 16 December 2024. Before this date, PAs and AAs worked in the NHS but had no statutory regulator or public register.

What happens in December 2026 for physician associates?

From December 2026, it becomes a criminal offence to practise as a physician associate or anaesthesia associate anywhere in the UK without being registered with the GMC. This follows a two-year transition period, introduced from December 2024, that allowed individuals already working in these roles to continue practising while they completed registration.

Does the GMC set a fixed scope of practice for physician associates?

No. The GMC deliberately does not set a single, defined national scope of practice for PAs or AAs after qualification. Instead, what tasks an individual can safely perform depends on their own skills and competence, built up over time and confirmed by their named supervisor, with additional clarity provided by guidance from individual Royal Colleges and the Academy of Medical Royal Colleges.

Who decides what a physician associate is allowed to do?

The named supervising doctor determines the range of activities a specific physician associate or anaesthesia associate can undertake, and at what level of supervision. This is an individualised, ongoing judgement based on demonstrated competence, not a fixed job description that applies identically to every PA or AA nationally.

Do physician associates have to follow Good Medical Practice?

Yes. Once registered with the GMC, PAs and AAs are required to follow the same core professional standards as doctors, as set out in Good Medical Practice. This includes working within the limits of their competence and clearly introducing themselves and their professional role to patients.

Can a physician associate refuse to carry out a task they previously agreed to do?

Yes. PAs and AAs have a professional duty to prioritise patient safety and to seek help if a task turns out to be outside their competence, training, or agreed scope of practice — even if they had previously agreed to attempt it. This mirrors the same professional obligation doctors have to recognise and work within the limits of their own competence.

Is there disagreement about how much physician associates should be allowed to do?

The appropriate scope of PA and AA practice remains an actively discussed topic among medical Royal Colleges and professional bodies, reflecting genuine, ongoing professional debate about supervision, training, and patient safety. This guide reports the GMC's structural regulatory framework factually and does not take a position in that wider debate.

Conclusion

GMC regulation of physician associates has fundamentally changed the accountability structure around PAs and AAs in the NHS, moving them from an unregulated role into a statutory framework with their own register, shared professional standards under Good Medical Practice, and a fitness-to-practise process broadly similar to that used for doctors. For any doctor — UK-trained or an IMG newly registered via the UKMLA route — the practical takeaway is not a fixed list of what a PA or AA can and cannot do, but an understanding that scope of practice is individual, supervisor-confirmed, and worth actively checking rather than assuming, especially before the December 2026 deadline makes registration a criminal-law matter rather than a professional one. Staying informed on this, alongside related developments such as GMC Order reform and title protection, is now a genuine part of working safely and professionally within a modern NHS multidisciplinary team.
