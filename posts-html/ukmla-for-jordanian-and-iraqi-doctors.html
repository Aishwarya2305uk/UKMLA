UKMLA for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors is a route that combines two of the Middle East's strongest medical education traditions with a single, standardised UK licensing exam. Jordan has long trained doctors for regional and international practice, with graduates from institutions across the country regularly moving into the Gulf, North America, and Europe, while Iraq's medical schools — despite a genuinely difficult recent history for the country's health infrastructure — continue to produce clinically capable graduates who are increasingly looking at the UK as a stable, structured place to build a long-term career. Because both countries sit within the same broad regulatory picture from the GMC's point of view, this guide covers them together, while being clear about where the practical detail — test centre access, document logistics, English testing — genuinely differs between the two.

The General Medical Council (GMC), working with Pearson VUE, runs the UK Medical Licensing Assessment (UKMLA) as the single gateway exam for international medical graduates (IMGs) seeking full UK registration. For Jordanian and Iraqi doctors specifically, the pathway does not depend on nationality or on any bilateral agreement between the two governments and the UK — it depends entirely on whether the doctor's primary medical qualification comes from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS), and on the doctor completing the same sequence of credential verification, English testing, and two structured assessments that every other IMG route follows. This guide walks through eligibility, the realistic costs in USD and GBP, where Jordanian and Iraqi candidates can currently access a Pearson VUE test centre for the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), the mandatory in-person Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) in the UK, and a step-by-step timeline from first application to a first NHS post.

Because verifiable detail on exact medical school accreditation status, local currency conversions, and test centre availability in Iraq specifically is more limited than for some other IMG source countries, this guide hedges deliberately wherever a specific number or claim cannot be independently confirmed, and points candidates to the GMC's own live resources — WDOMS, GMC Online, and the GMC's fee pages — as the definitive source at the point a decision or payment is actually made.

UKMLA for Jordanian and Iraqi Doctors: A Quick Overview

Before working through each stage in detail, it helps to see the whole pathway at a glance. The general shape of UKMLA for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors runs as follows, regardless of which of the two countries a candidate originally qualified in:

  1. Confirm your medical school's listing on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) for your graduation year, since this is the GMC's baseline test of whether a primary medical qualification is even eligible for assessment.
  2. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your primary medical qualification, historically run through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials); candidates should check current GMC guidance, since the verification platform used for this step has been subject to change and the GMC's own website is the authoritative source on which system currently applies.
  3. Sit and pass an approved English language test — IELTS Academic or OET Medicine — unless a specific, GMC-confirmed exemption applies to your situation.
  4. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT at a Pearson VUE test centre, checking realistic regional options for Jordan and Iraq specifically, since availability differs meaningfully between the two countries.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA, which can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.
  6. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise, once both assessments are passed and every supporting document is verified.
  7. Apply for NHS posts — typically trust-grade, clinical fellow, or Foundation Year 2-equivalent roles — and begin the practical process of relocating to the UK.

Each of these stages carries its own processing time, cost, and common pitfall, all covered in detail below. Understanding the full sequence early matters particularly for candidates from Jordan and Iraq, because the administrative steps — medical school verification, Certificate of Good Standing requests, and English testing logistics — often take considerably longer than exam preparation itself. Treating them as a project that starts on day one, rather than something to arrange after the AKT is passed, is the single biggest factor separating candidates who move through the pathway smoothly from those who face avoidable delays.

Medical School Recognition and UKMLA Eligibility for Jordan and Iraq

The starting point for any international medical graduate, including doctors from Jordan and Iraq, is the same baseline test the GMC applies to every applicant regardless of country: does the primary medical qualification come from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant year of graduation, and did the programme meet minimum duration and curriculum requirements? Jordan has an established, internationally engaged medical education sector, and Iraq's medical schools — many with a long institutional history predating the last several decades of instability — also maintain listings that international graduates from the country continue to rely on for postgraduate mobility into the Gulf, Europe, and North America.

Rather than naming specific universities in either country and risking stating an accreditation status that may have changed, the single most reliable step any candidate can take is to search their own medical school directly on the World Directory of Medical Schools before investing further time or money in the pathway. WDOMS listings are maintained centrally and can change over time — a school's listing status is the authoritative fact that determines eligibility, not general reputation or the fact that earlier graduates registered with the GMC. The site's broader guide to which primary medical qualifications the GMC recognises covers this check in more depth and applies equally to Jordanian and Iraqi graduates. Because it is a five-minute check with a definitive answer, it should be the first action taken by a candidate considering this route.

Beyond the WDOMS listing itself, the GMC also expects evidence of full, unrestricted registration with the relevant national medical regulator — the Jordan Medical Council for Jordanian-trained doctors, or the Iraqi Ministry of Health's medical licensing authority for Iraqi-trained doctors — rather than a provisional, student, or conditional registration status. Most Jordanian and Iraqi medical degree programmes include a structured internship or pre-registration year immediately after the core academic years, broadly comparable in shape to the UK's own foundation-year concept, in that both represent a supervised period of practice before full independent registration. This is a general orientation only, not a guarantee of formal equivalence — the GMC assesses each application's documentation individually, so confirm how your own internship year will be treated as part of your specific application rather than assuming automatic parity with the UK system. For the baseline eligibility framework that applies across every IMG route, see the site's guide to UKMLA eligibility requirements.

Primary Source Verification Is the Same Process Regardless of Country

Once basic WDOMS eligibility is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of the primary medical qualification — meaning the degree is confirmed directly with the issuing medical school rather than accepted on the strength of a certificate alone. This has historically run through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials); candidates should check current GMC guidance for the exact platform in use, since infrastructure of this kind can change and the GMC's own website is the definitive source. What does not change is the underlying principle: verification is identical regardless of whether the applicant is Jordanian, Iraqi, Indian, Egyptian, or any other nationality, because the system verifies the issuing institution rather than adjusting by nationality.

Response times for this step vary primarily by how quickly a given medical school's registrar responds, not by the doctor's nationality or current country of residence. Well-established medical schools with dedicated international relations or alumni offices tend to respond within weeks, whereas others — particularly institutions that have experienced periods of administrative disruption — can take considerably longer. Candidates from Iraq in particular should build in extra contingency time given the country's documented history of institutional disruption over recent decades, without assuming this applies to every school or applicant. Full detail on how primary source verification works, what documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting evidence is available directly from the regulator: see the GMC's primary source verification requirements for international medical graduates. Reading this guidance in full, before initiating any verification request, is the best way to avoid an incomplete submission that then requires a second round of correspondence.

Why This Guide Does Not Name Specific Medical Schools

Both Jordan and Iraq have multiple recognised medical schools whose graduates appear on the World Directory of Medical Schools, and doctors from institutions across both countries have successfully completed the UKMLA pathway. However, because WDOMS listings and accreditation periods can change over time, candidates are encouraged to check their own specific medical school directly on WDOMS rather than relying on any general claim about which universities "count." A school being well-regarded regionally is not the same as a current, confirmed WDOMS listing for the specific graduation year in question.

Documents Jordanian and Iraqi Candidates Should Gather Early

Because both Jordanian and Iraqi doctors are often coordinating documentation across more than one institution and, in some cases, more than one period of study or practice, gathering the full document set in parallel rather than sequentially is the single most effective way to shorten the overall timeline:

  • Primary medical degree certificate and complete academic transcripts, year by year, from the issuing medical school.
  • Internship or pre-registration year completion certificate, confirming the mandatory supervised period immediately after graduation has been completed in full.
  • Certificate of Good Standing from the Jordan Medical Council or the relevant Iraqi medical licensing authority, confirming registration status and disciplinary record.
  • Valid international passport, ideally valid well beyond the expected date of GMC registration and any subsequent visa application.
  • Evidence of any name variations across documents, which is common where transliteration from Arabic to English has varied between different issuing bodies or over time.
  • Certified translations of any Arabic-language document not already issued with an official English translation, produced by a recognised professional translation service — relevant for the great majority of primary documents from both countries.
  • Evidence of continuous practice or further training, particularly for Iraqi doctors whose career may include periods working in the Gulf after initial qualification, useful supporting context even though the underlying eligibility test rests on the original degree.

Requesting the Certificate of Good Standing early is important for both countries, since this document follows the national regulator's own administrative timeline, and delays here are a common cause of an otherwise well-prepared application stalling.

How to Apply for UKMLA From Jordan or Iraq: Step by Step

With eligibility confirmed against WDOMS and the relevant national regulator, the practical application process for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors follows six sequential steps, identical in structure to the process followed by IMGs from any other country.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

The first practical step is creating a GMC Online account through the regulator's official website. This account becomes the central hub for the entire process, used to track credential verification status, book both the AKT and CPSA, submit English language evidence, and eventually complete a full registration application. Candidates should use a personal email address they intend to retain long-term, since GMC Online correspondence commonly spans well over a year, and university or hospital-issued addresses are frequently deactivated once a candidate moves on from a given institution or employer.

Step 2: Begin Credential Verification Early

Primary source verification of the original medical degree is usually the single longest step in the entire pathway for both Jordanian and Iraqi candidates, and it should begin as early as possible — ideally before English test dates or exam bookings are finalised. Because this step runs through the issuing medical school and its national regulator rather than through any UK body, it can proceed largely independently of exam preparation. Keeping a written record of every piece of correspondence with the medical school's registrar is worthwhile, since this becomes useful evidence if a delayed response ever needs escalating.

Step 3: Gather and Submit English Language Evidence

In parallel with credential verification, sit an approved English language test — IELTS Academic or OET Medicine, covered in full detail below — and upload the resulting certificate to GMC Online once results are issued. This requirement is universal and does not vary by nationality, meaning Jordanian and Iraqi candidates follow exactly the same testing rules, score thresholds, and evidence submission process as IMGs from any other country. Booking a test slot as early as realistically possible is worthwhile, since candidates who wait to discover whether an exemption might apply sometimes lose valuable weeks only to find that formal evidence is required after all.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once a GMC Online account is active, the AKT can be booked through the linked Pearson VUE booking system. Regional test centre access for Jordan and Iraq specifically is covered in detail in the next section, since availability differs meaningfully between the two countries and candidates should always check the live, current list before committing to travel or accommodation plans. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines exactly what conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas are examinable, alongside a structured, well-reviewed question bank — see the site's guidance on AKT test centre locations and booking logistics for a starting point on working through the practical side of this step.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once the AKT has been passed. Because the CPSA is available only in Manchester, both Jordanian and Iraqi candidates need to plan a UK trip around this stage, factoring in visa arrangements, flights, and several days of accommodation for final preparation immediately before the assessment. Visa requirements and processing times can vary depending on a candidate's specific circumstances and current country of residence, so this should be confirmed directly against current UK Home Office guidance well in advance of a planned CPSA date, rather than assumed from general reputation or another candidate's experience.

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and credential verification, English language evidence, and every outstanding supporting document are confirmed, the full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise can be submitted. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage, including document checklists and typical processing times, is set out in the site's GMC registration process guide.

UKMLA AKT Test Centres for Jordanian and Iraqi Candidates

Test centre access is one of the areas where the practical experience of Jordanian and Iraqi candidates genuinely diverges, and this guide deliberately avoids assuming a specific city works for both countries without qualification. The AKT is delivered globally through the Pearson VUE test centre network, and Jordan has a confirmed, growing local testing footprint: Pearson has expanded its professional assessment presence in Amman, and additional Pearson VUE-authorised centres operate elsewhere in the country, giving Jordanian candidates realistic prospects of sitting professional and academic exams via the Pearson VUE platform without leaving the country. Whether the AKT specifically is offered at a given centre and sitting window must always be confirmed directly through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE booking portal — general Pearson VUE presence in a country is not the same as confirmed AKT availability on a specific date.

Iraq's position is less straightforward to summarise with confidence. Authorised Pearson VUE testing centres do operate within Iraq, including in Baghdad, delivering a range of professional and IT certification exams, but the specific, current availability of GMC/UKMLA sittings at any given Iraqi centre is not something this guide can verify with certainty, and candidates should not assume a centre listed for other exam programmes automatically offers AKT sittings. The most reliable approach for an Iraqi candidate is to check the live list of AKT-approved centres through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE portal at the time of booking, and — if no suitable Iraqi centre is confirmed — to realistically plan for travel to a nearby regional centre, such as one in Jordan, a Gulf city, or Turkey, all of which have more consistently documented Pearson VUE testing infrastructure. The dedicated guide to AKT test centre locations across the global network is worth reading before committing to a specific booking.

This regional test centre picture is a significant contrast with the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in Jordan or Iraq, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — it is held exclusively at the GMC's purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. This means every candidate from either country must, at minimum, make one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway, regardless of how much of the rest of the process can be completed regionally. Budgeting for this mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visa if required, Manchester accommodation, and living costs for the preparation and exam period — should be built into financial planning from the very start rather than left until after the AKT is passed.

English Language Requirements for Jordanian and Iraqi Doctors

English language testing is a universal requirement for the great majority of international medical graduates, and this does not change based on nationality or on how much of a candidate's medical training was conducted in English. Medical education in both Jordan and Iraq typically involves substantial English-language content at the textbook and clinical documentation level, which sometimes leads candidates to assume formal testing will not apply to them. In practice, the GMC requires documentary evidence of English proficiency from almost every IMG applicant regardless of this background, and candidates from both countries should plan for formal testing as a standard part of the pathway rather than hoping for an automatic waiver.

Two tests are accepted: IELTS Academic, where the GMC requires an overall score of 7.0, with no individual component (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the GMC requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. OET is often more intuitive for practising clinicians because its scenarios are built around healthcare communication rather than general academic subject matter, whereas IELTS Academic draws on broader topics and tends to have wider test centre and preparation material availability across the region. Both tests are generally accessible from major cities in Jordan, and IELTS in particular has a long-established test centre presence there; Iraqi candidates should check current test centre availability directly with the relevant test provider, since options can be more limited and some candidates may need to travel regionally to sit either test, similar to the AKT test centre consideration discussed above. The site's detailed walk-through of English language testing requirements, while written with a South Asian example, describes exactly the same accepted tests, score thresholds, and evidence submission process that applies equally to Jordanian and Iraqi candidates.

Sequencing matters just as much as scores. Both certificates carry a defined validity period from the test date, so sitting the test too early relative to the overall pathway risks the certificate expiring before a registration application is submitted, which then forces a costly and time-consuming re-sit. Full detail on accepted scores, accepted test providers, and how evidence is submitted is available directly from the regulator at the GMC's English language evidence requirements. Life and Career for Jordanian and Iraqi Doctors in the NHS After Registration

Once GMC registration is secured, Jordanian and Iraqi doctors join a wider community of Middle Eastern IMGs within the NHS, many of whom followed a broadly similar journey through credential verification, English testing, the AKT and CPSA, and eventual registration. Career progression generally follows one of several established routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post, the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs, offering Foundation Year 2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place; a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK experience and portfolio evidence are in place; or locum work through NHS staff banks and agencies for doctors who want flexibility while building a UK clinical track record. Many Iraqi doctors who spent time working in the Gulf region before pursuing UK registration find that this intermediate experience — described in more detail in the site's guide to returning to practice after a period working in Gulf countries — provides useful continuity of clinical practice and documentation that strengthens a subsequent UK application, even though it does not itself substitute for GMC-specific verification steps.

Clinically, the biggest early adjustments for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors moving into the NHS tend to involve NHS-specific documentation standards, such as structured handover formats, a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary team working, and a more codified approach to consent and capacity under UK law than either country's system typically requires in the same form. Socially, doctors from both countries are joining an established and growing Arabic-speaking professional community within several NHS regions, particularly London, Manchester, and the Midlands, with informal peer networks increasingly available to new arrivals navigating the early adjustment period. Salary progression follows the standard NHS pay scale for the relevant grade, and while it can take time to reach specialty training, many doctors who complete this pathway cite the structured, transparent nature of UK postgraduate training — clear competency frameworks, protected training time, and defined progression points — as a significant factor in their decision, alongside the practical benefit of long-term professional and family stability that UK registration provides.

It is worth noting that the broad shape of this journey — degree recognition, credential verification, English testing, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam, then registration — mirrors the equivalent routes followed by doctors from other major IMG source regions. Some Jordanian and Iraqi doctors weighing up their options also look at the pathway followed by Egyptian-trained doctors, given a degree of regional and professional overlap in training culture, or compare notes with doctors following the Gulf and Saudi route, particularly relevant for the many Jordanian and Iraqi doctors who have spent part of their career working in Gulf healthcare systems before considering a further move to the UK. Doctors researching this pathway alongside colleagues from further afield, including Chinese-trained doctors exploring the same UKMLA route, will find the underlying GMC process is identical in structure even though the specific national regulators, document names, and regional test centre options differ.

Common Mistakes Jordanian and Iraqi Doctors Make on This Route

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among candidates from both countries pursuing the UKMLA pathway, and recognising them early is one of the most cost-effective steps in the entire process. Most of these mistakes are administrative and planning errors rather than anything to do with clinical knowledge, yet they are responsible for a disproportionate share of the delays and extra costs candidates experience.

  • Assuming a specific test centre offers the AKT without checking the live listing. General Pearson VUE presence in a city — for other professional or IT certification exams — is not the same as confirmed AKT availability. Always check the current, live list through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE portal before committing to a booking or travel plan.
  • Starting credential verification too late. Because verification processing time depends heavily on the medical school's own administrative speed rather than the candidate's effort, this step should be initiated well before AKT preparation begins in earnest, not treated as something to arrange once exam study is already underway.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Flights, several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, food, and often a short UK preparation course before the exam can together add well over $1,000 on top of the fee. Candidates who budget only for the headline fee frequently scramble for additional funds close to their exam date.
  • Not building a currency buffer into the budget. Because all GMC and exam fees are charged in pounds sterling while savings are typically held in Jordanian or Iraqi dinars, a candidate who calculates a local-currency budget many months before actually paying can find the real cost has shifted by the time payment is due. Building in a buffer of at least 15–20% above an initial estimate, and rechecking live rates close to each payment, is a simple but frequently skipped precaution.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. Both IELTS and OET certificates are valid for a defined period from the test date. Candidates who sit their English test early in the process — before credential verification or AKT preparation is complete — sometimes find their certificate has expired by the time they submit a full registration application, forcing a costly re-sit.
  • Treating document translation and certification casually. Any Arabic-language document not already issued with an official English translation requires a certified professional translation, not an informal translation from a bilingual colleague. Underestimating the time this takes, or submitting translations that do not meet the GMC's certification standard, is a common cause of application delay.
  • Not naming a specific medical school as automatically eligible without checking WDOMS directly. Because accreditation listings can change and are not something this guide — or any third-party summary — can guarantee indefinitely, candidates should check their own institution's current WDOMS status directly rather than relying on the fact that earlier graduates from the same school successfully registered with the GMC in the past.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Jordanian and Iraqi medical degrees accepted for UKMLA registration?

Degrees from medical schools in both Jordan and Iraq can be accepted, provided the specific medical school is listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant graduation year and the doctor holds full, unrestricted registration with the relevant national regulator. Because WDOMS listings and accreditation status can change over time, candidates should check their own medical school's current listing directly rather than assuming eligibility based on general reputation or the fact that earlier graduates were successfully registered.

Where can Jordanian and Iraqi doctors sit the UKMLA AKT?

Jordan has a growing Pearson VUE testing presence, including an expanded professional testing centre in Amman, giving Jordanian candidates realistic prospects of sitting the AKT without leaving the country, subject to confirmed availability for the specific exam and sitting window. Iraq's position is less certain — authorised Pearson VUE centres exist, including in Baghdad, but confirmed current AKT availability should always be checked directly through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE portal, and candidates unable to confirm a local sitting should realistically plan for travel to a nearby regional centre.

Do Jordanian and Iraqi doctors have to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, this cannot be avoided regardless of country of origin. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. There is no option to sit the CPSA remotely or anywhere within Jordan or Iraq, so every candidate from either country must budget for at least one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway.

How much does the whole UKMLA process cost in USD?

The core GMC and exam fees — AKT, CPSA, and initial registration — total approximately £1,800, which converts to roughly $2,290 at broadly indicative exchange rates, though the actual figure moves with the market. Once English testing, credential verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget rises to approximately £3,300 to £4,800, or roughly $4,200 to $6,100. Candidates should always verify current GMC fees and live exchange rates before making any payment rather than relying on an estimate calculated months in advance.

Do Jordanian and Iraqi doctors need an English language test if their medical training was largely in English?

In almost all cases, yes. A medical education conducted substantially in English does not automatically exempt a candidate from the GMC's formal English language evidence requirement. Most candidates from both countries need to submit a valid IELTS Academic score of 7.0 overall with no component below 7.0, or an OET Medicine result of Grade B minimum across all four components, unless a specific, GMC-confirmed exemption applies.

Does a doctor's time working in the Gulf count for anything in a UKMLA application?

Time spent working in the Gulf does not itself substitute for any GMC eligibility step, since the GMC's assessment is built around the original primary medical qualification rather than subsequent licensure or employment history. However, Gulf employment references, continuing professional development records, and evidence of ongoing clinical practice can serve as useful supporting context for an application, particularly for doctors whose original degree dates back many years.

What is the realistic timeline from graduating in Jordan or Iraq to working in the NHS?

Most candidates should plan for a realistic total timeline of 18 to 30 months from starting credential verification of their original degree to beginning a first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. This accounts for national regulator document processing, credential verification delays, English test preparation, exam preparation for both assessments, the GMC registration application itself, and typical NHS recruitment cycles.

How does the process differ between Jordanian and Iraqi candidates specifically?

The underlying GMC process — WDOMS eligibility, credential verification, English testing, the AKT, the CPSA, then registration — is identical for candidates from both countries. The practical differences lie mainly in test centre access, since Jordan currently has a more established local Pearson VUE testing footprint than Iraq, and in document processing timelines, where some Iraqi applicants may face longer waits given the country's documented history of institutional disruption. Both factors are logistics to plan around rather than differences in eligibility itself.

Conclusion

The route through UKMLA for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors follows exactly the same underlying GMC framework as every other international medical graduate route, but candidates from both countries benefit from planning deliberately around the areas where practical detail genuinely differs: confirming a specific medical school's current WDOMS listing rather than assuming it, checking live AKT test centre availability in Jordan and Iraq rather than assuming a specific city works, and budgeting in USD or GBP with a realistic currency buffer rather than relying on outdated local-currency conversions. Doctors who start credential verification early, treat English testing and exam preparation as universal requirements unaffected by nationality, and plan realistically for both the mandatory Manchester trip and typical processing delays consistently move through the pathway with fewer costly surprises.
