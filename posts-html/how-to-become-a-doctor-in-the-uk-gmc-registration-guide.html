Working out how to become a doctor in UK practice can feel overwhelming at first, because the process looks different depending on where you trained, whether you are already a qualified doctor or still a student, and which specialty you eventually want to pursue. In simple terms, the route to becoming a doctor in the UK for the vast majority of international medical graduates (IMGs) runs through six stages: confirming eligibility, completing primary source verification of your medical degree through EPIC, providing evidence of English language proficiency, passing the UK Medical Licensing Assessment (UKMLA), applying for General Medical Council (GMC) registration with a licence to practise, and then securing the right visa before starting an NHS post.

This guide is the site's complete, country-agnostic overview of that pathway. It exists because most people searching for how to become a doctor in the UK have not yet worked out which of the site's more specific guides applies to them — whether that is a country-specific walkthrough, a deep dive into one exam component, or the visa process itself. Rather than duplicating that detail, this page synthesises the whole journey at a sensible depth and links out to dedicated posts wherever a topic deserves fuller treatment.

Three genuinely different routes lead to UK medical registration, and conflating them is the single most common source of confusion for readers researching this topic. The first, and by far the most common route for readers of this site, is the international medical graduate (IMG) pathway described above, built around the UKMLA. The second is qualifying via a UK medical school as an undergraduate, which is a longer route entirely separate from the IMG process, since it leads to provisional GMC registration automatically on graduation rather than requiring separate exams. The third is a narrow set of specialist and GP registrar routes, reserved for doctors who already hold recognised postgraduate qualifications and meet stringent criteria, which are covered in detail in the dedicated post on exemptions available to specialist and GP doctors. This guide focuses primarily on the first route, while explaining clearly how the other two differ.

The Quick Answer: How to Become a Doctor in the UK as an International Medical Graduate

For an international medical graduate, the complete route to becoming a doctor in the UK follows a defined, sequential pathway. Understanding the shape of this journey before diving into the detail of any single stage prevents the most common planning mistake: treating the UKMLA exams as the only hard part, when in practice the surrounding administrative and immigration steps often take just as long.

  1. Confirm your eligibility — verify that your primary medical qualification comes from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) and that your programme met minimum GMC standards.
  2. Begin primary source verification of your medical degree through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials), the platform the GMC uses to confirm your qualification directly with your medical school.
  3. Provide evidence of English language proficiency, usually through IELTS Academic or OET Medicine, unless an accepted exemption applies.
  4. Pass the UKMLA, which consists of two components: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA).
  5. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise once both assessments are passed and all supporting evidence is verified.
  6. Secure the correct visa to work in the UK, most commonly the Skilled Worker visa sponsored by an NHS employer.
  7. Find an NHS post and begin practising medicine in the United Kingdom.

This sequence represents the standard route to practising medicine in the UK for the overwhelming majority of internationally trained doctors. However, it is worth restating that two other, quite different routes exist. A UK medical student completing an undergraduate MBBS or MBChB at a GMC-approved UK medical school does not need to sit the UKMLA as an IMG-style exam at all in the same sense — they sit it as an integrated part of their degree and move automatically to provisional registration on graduation, a genuinely different and considerably longer route that begins years earlier, at the point of university admission. Meanwhile, a small number of doctors with specific specialist or GP postgraduate qualifications recognised as equivalent to UK training can apply through narrower registration routes that bypass parts of the standard IMG process, detailed further in the post on UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors. Because these three routes are so often confused in general search queries, the comparison table further down this page sets out their differences at a glance.

Eligibility: Who Can Pursue the IMG Route to UK Medical Registration

Eligibility for the IMG pathway is defined primarily by where and how you qualified as a doctor, rather than by your nationality. In short, the GMC requires that your primary medical qualification was awarded by a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) for the year you graduated, and that the programme met certain minimum standards around duration, curriculum, and supervised clinical training.

In practice, this means graduates of the great majority of established medical schools across South Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and elsewhere satisfy this first hurdle without difficulty, provided their institution held valid accreditation at the time of graduation. However, eligibility is not simply a matter of holding a medical degree — the GMC also expects evidence of full, unrestricted registration with your home country's medical regulator (following completion of any mandatory internship or housemanship year), since provisional or temporary registration status is not sufficient on its own.

Because eligibility rules include specific provisions for unusual qualification types, part-time programmes, and degrees awarded through non-standard routes, every prospective candidate should check their own circumstances against the site's dedicated UKMLA eligibility requirements guide before committing time and money to the process. Confirming your medical school's current WDOMS listing takes only a few minutes and is one of the most valuable early steps in becoming a doctor in the UK, since a lapsed listing can cause confusion much later in the process if left unchecked.

Eligibility also depends on the minimum programme standards the GMC expects any recognised medical degree to meet. In practice, this means a full undergraduate medical programme of appropriate length, incorporating both a theoretical curriculum and a substantial period of supervised clinical placement, examined through a formal assessment process recognised by the relevant national regulator. Degrees earned through accelerated, part-time, or otherwise non-standard programme structures are not automatically excluded, but they are more likely to trigger additional scrutiny during primary source verification, so candidates in this position should read the eligibility guidance particularly carefully before assuming their situation is straightforward. Consequently, candidates whose programme structure differs meaningfully from the conventional model should budget extra time for this stage rather than assuming eligibility will be confirmed as quickly as it is for graduates of more conventional programmes.

It is also worth noting that eligibility is assessed at the level of the individual applicant, not simply the medical school as an institution. Two graduates of the same medical school, one who graduated while the school held valid WDOMS accreditation and one who graduated during a lapse in that accreditation, can face entirely different outcomes at this stage. For this reason, checking your own graduation year specifically against the WDOMS record, rather than assuming your medical school's current listing status applies retroactively, is a small but genuinely important piece of due diligence before beginning the wider process of becoming a doctor in the UK.

Primary Source Verification: How EPIC Fits Into the Process

Primary source verification is the GMC's mechanism for confirming that your medical qualification is genuine, verified directly with the issuing institution rather than accepted on the strength of a certificate alone. This is handled through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials), a global verification platform used by medical regulators, including the GMC, around the world.

In broad terms, the EPIC process involves creating an account, submitting your degree and internship documents, and then waiting while EPIC contacts your medical school directly to confirm the qualification's authenticity. Processing time varies enormously by institution — well-resourced medical schools with dedicated international relations offices often respond within weeks, whereas others can take several months, particularly around examination periods. Because this step depends on your medical school's administrative speed rather than your own effort, most successful candidates begin the EPIC process as early as possible, running it in parallel with exam preparation rather than treating it as a final formality.

This overview deliberately keeps things at a summary level, since EPIC's document requirements, submission mechanics, and common delay points are covered in full in the dedicated post on GMC registration requirements for international doctors. That guide is the right place to look for detail on exactly which documents EPIC requires and how to avoid the most common submission errors.

What matters most at this overview level is understanding why EPIC verification so often becomes the pacing item for the entire route to becoming a doctor in the UK. Unlike exam preparation, which is largely within a candidate's own control, EPIC's timeline depends on a third party — your medical school's registrar or international office — responding to a verification request in a timely manner. Some institutions have streamlined, dedicated processes for handling these requests and respond within a few weeks; others, particularly smaller or under-resourced institutions, may take several months, especially if the request arrives during an examination period or an academic holiday. Therefore, treating EPIC verification and exam preparation as two separate, parallel projects, rather than a strict sequence where one must finish before the other begins, is consistently the single most effective piece of planning advice for candidates starting this journey.

In addition, candidates should keep a written record of every communication exchanged with their medical school during this process. If a verification request stalls for an unusually long period, having a clear paper trail makes it considerably easier to escalate the matter, whether directly with the institution or, in some cases, through EPIC's own support channels. This single habit — treated as routine administrative housekeeping rather than an afterthought — resolves a meaningful share of the delays candidates report during this stage.

English Language Requirements for Becoming a Doctor in the UK

Almost every IMG pursuing UK medical registration must submit formal evidence of English language proficiency, regardless of whether their medical education was conducted in English. The GMC accepts two tests for this purpose: IELTS Academic, where the required overall score is 7.0, with no individual component below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the requirement is a minimum of Grade B across all four sub-tests.

Both certificates carry a defined validity period from the test date, so timing matters considerably. Candidates who sit their English test too early in their overall journey risk their certificate expiring before they are ready to submit a full registration application, which then forces an unwelcome and costly re-sit. For this reason, sequencing the English test against your expected registration date deserves real thought rather than being treated as an early box-ticking exercise.

Because accepted score thresholds, exemption criteria, and submission mechanics are subject to periodic review, this overview intentionally avoids restating every detail. Readers preparing to book a test should consult the GMC's own current guidance directly, cited in full below, and can also find broader country-specific context in some of the site's dedicated guides on this exact requirement.

Choosing between IELTS and OET is a genuinely practical decision worth thinking through rather than defaulting to whichever test is more widely available locally. OET's scenarios are built entirely around healthcare communication — referral letters, clinical case notes, and doctor-patient consultations — which many practising clinicians find more intuitive than the broader academic subject matter covered by IELTS Academic. Meanwhile, IELTS preparation material tends to be more widely available in some regions, and its test centre network is often larger, which can matter for candidates in areas with fewer local testing options. Neither test is inherently easier, so the right choice generally comes down to which format suits an individual candidate's strengths and which is more logistically convenient to sit.

What the UKMLA Actually Tests: AKT and CPSA Explained

The UK Medical Licensing Assessment is the single national examination that both UK medical students and IMGs must pass to demonstrate the knowledge and skills required for safe practice in the UK. It consists of two distinct components, each testing something different, and both must be passed to gain GMC registration with a licence to practise.

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT)

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) is a computer-based examination testing clinical knowledge, decision-making, and the professional judgement expected of a newly qualified doctor. It is delivered through Pearson VUE test centres, which means IMG candidates can often sit it in their home country or region rather than travelling to the UK — a genuinely useful practical advantage over the CPSA. The AKT draws on the official MLA content map, a defined syllabus of clinical presentations, conditions, and professional knowledge areas, so preparation is most effective when built directly around that published content rather than around general revision material.

The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA)

The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) is the practical, scenario-based component, examining clinical examination technique, patient communication, data interpretation, and professionalism through a structured circuit of stations. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre, which for the great majority of candidates means a mandatory trip to the UK regardless of how much of the earlier process was completed remotely. Candidates should therefore build travel, accommodation, and visa considerations into their planning for this stage well in advance, rather than leaving it until after the AKT result arrives.

Passing both components in sequence, AKT before CPSA, is a strict requirement of the pathway, and candidates cannot book the CPSA until they hold a passing AKT result. Because becoming a doctor in the UK ultimately depends on clearing both assessments, most successful candidates treat AKT and CPSA preparation as two related but distinct projects, each requiring its own study plan, timeline, and — for the CPSA in particular — its own logistical planning.

It is worth being clear about what each assessment is actually trying to measure, since candidates sometimes prepare for the wrong thing. The AKT is fundamentally a test of applied clinical reasoning under exam conditions — it rewards candidates who can quickly identify the most appropriate next step in a clinical scenario, drawing on UK-specific guidelines such as those published by NICE, rather than simply recalling textbook facts. The CPSA, in contrast, rewards candidates who can demonstrate safe, structured clinical behaviour in real time — taking a focused history, performing an examination correctly, and communicating clearly with a patient or colleague under some time pressure. Consequently, revising AKT content in isolation does not automatically prepare a candidate for the CPSA, and vice versa; each requires a distinct form of practice, ideally including realistic mock scenarios and, where possible, structured feedback from someone familiar with the assessment format.

GMC Registration With a Licence to Practise

Once both UKMLA components are passed and all supporting evidence is verified, the final step before working as a doctor in the UK is applying for GMC registration with a licence to practise. This dual status is what actually grants the legal right to practise medicine in the UK — registration alone confirms your name is on the medical register, while the licence to practise is the specific authorisation that allows you to prescribe, sign death certificates, and hold a substantive NHS post. In other words, both halves of this status are necessary; one without the other does not permit clinical practice.

The application itself draws together every strand of the process covered so far: your verified EPIC evidence, your English language test certificate, your passing UKMLA results, and a set of identity and good-standing documents from your home country's medical regulator. The GMC reviews the full application before granting registration, and processing typically takes a matter of weeks once every document is in order, though incomplete submissions are a common and avoidable source of delay.

In practice, the strongest applications are the ones where every prior stage was completed with the final registration application already in mind, rather than treated as an isolated hurdle to clear and forget. For example, a candidate who requests a Certificate of Good Standing from their home regulator only after passing the CPSA often faces an unwelcome extra wait at exactly the point they expected to be closest to registration, whereas a candidate who requested it in parallel with CPSA preparation avoids this bottleneck entirely. Similarly, keeping certified copies of every document — degree certificate, transcripts, internship completion evidence, identity documents — organised and readily accessible throughout the process, rather than searching for them after both exams are passed, meaningfully shortens the final stretch of becoming a doctor in the UK.

Because this stage involves detailed document checklists, common query points, and specific guidance on what to do if an application is queried or delayed, readers should consult the site's dedicated GMC registration process guide and the deeper post on GMC registration requirements for international doctors for full step-by-step detail. For the GMC's own authoritative description of what happens before you apply, see the GMC's official guide for doctors before you apply, which should always take precedence over any third-party summary, including this one, whenever a detail appears to have changed.

Visa and Immigration Considerations

Passing the UKMLA and gaining GMC registration does not, on its own, grant the right to live and work in the UK — a separate immigration process runs alongside, and in practice often overlaps with, the registration pathway. Most IMGs enter the UK workforce on a Skilled Worker visa, sponsored by an NHS trust or other approved healthcare employer, though a small number of candidates use other routes depending on their personal circumstances, such as a Health and Care Worker visa variant or family-based immigration status.

Because visa rules, sponsorship requirements, and application costs change relatively often and depend heavily on individual circumstances, this overview deliberately stays at a high level. Readers should consult the dedicated UK visa guide for international doctors for a full breakdown of visa categories, sponsorship mechanics, dependants' rights, and the immigration health surcharge, since getting the immigration side wrong can delay a start date every bit as much as a failed exam attempt. In addition, candidates already inside the UK on another visa category — for example, a spouse or graduate visa — should check whether their existing status already permits NHS employment before assuming a fresh sponsored visa application is required.

Timing is the aspect of immigration planning most commonly underestimated. Sponsorship cannot begin until a specific NHS employer has actually made a job offer, which in turn usually cannot happen until GMC registration is secured, meaning the visa stage sits firmly at the end of the pathway rather than something that can be meaningfully accelerated in parallel with the earlier exam stages. However, candidates can still do useful preparatory work well in advance — gathering the supporting documents a visa application will eventually require, understanding the costs involved (including the visa fee itself and the immigration health surcharge), and, where dependants are involved, researching how family members are affected by a change in immigration status. Doing this groundwork early means the final visa stage, once it becomes actionable, moves as quickly as possible rather than introducing yet another unplanned delay right at the point of starting an NHS post.

Realistic Timeline and Typical Total Cost

One of the most persistent misconceptions among candidates researching how to become a doctor in the UK is underestimating how long the full journey actually takes. Exam preparation time is relatively easy to plan for, whereas administrative processing time — EPIC verification, document requests from a home regulator, GMC application processing — depends on external parties and is far less predictable.

Combining every stage from beginning EPIC verification through to starting a first NHS post, most IMG candidates should realistically plan for a total timeline of 18 to 30 months, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. Candidates who need to re-sit either assessment, or who experience delays at the credential verification stage, should expect this range to extend further. This estimate is necessarily a synthesis across many different starting countries and personal circumstances, so treating it as a planning guideline rather than a guarantee is the sensible approach.

Cost follows a similarly wide but estimable range. Core GMC and Pearson VUE fees — covering the AKT, the CPSA, and initial GMC registration — typically total in the region of £1,800, before accounting for English test fees, EPIC verification charges, mandatory UK travel for the CPSA, visa costs, and study materials. Once every additional cost is included, most candidates should budget a realistic total in the region of £3,000 to £5,000 across the full pathway, though this varies by home country and by how many additional costs, such as flights, are involved. For exact, currently applicable figures broken down stage by stage, and for guidance on budgeting in a range of local currencies, see the dedicated full IMG fee breakdown, since fee schedules are reviewed periodically and this overview should not be treated as a substitute for checking current figures before making a payment.

Both the timeline and the cost figures above are, by necessity, averages drawn across many different starting countries, personal circumstances, and exam outcomes, so they should be read as planning benchmarks rather than fixed guarantees. A candidate whose medical school responds to EPIC within a few weeks, who passes both the AKT and the CPSA on the first attempt, and who secures visa sponsorship promptly after registration will likely land toward the shorter and cheaper end of these ranges. Meanwhile, a candidate who faces a slow EPIC response, needs to re-sit one assessment, or experiences delays gathering documents from their home regulator should expect both the timeline and the total cost to extend meaningfully beyond these figures. Building a financial and psychological buffer into your own planning, rather than assuming the shortest possible version of the pathway, is consistently the more realistic approach.

Life After Registration: Finding an NHS Post and Career Progression

Gaining GMC registration with a licence to practise is a genuine milestone, but it is the beginning of a UK medical career rather than its conclusion. Most newly registered IMGs enter the NHS workforce through a trust-grade or clinical fellow post, offering FY2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place, since the Foundation Programme after the UKMLA is primarily the route taken by UK medical school graduates moving directly from provisional to full registration.

From this entry point, career progression typically follows one of several paths: applying for Specialty Training once sufficient UK clinical experience and portfolio evidence are in place, taking on locum work through NHS staff banks for those who want flexibility while building a UK track record, or pursuing General Practice training through the standard GP registrar pathway. The practicalities of this transition — how to search for suitable posts, what NHS recruiters look for from IMG applicants, and how trust-grade experience translates into a stronger training application — are covered in detail in the dedicated post on the pathway from UKMLA registration to an NHS job.

Newly registered doctors should also expect a genuine adjustment period covering both clinical and cultural dimensions of NHS practice, including structured handover formats, a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary working, and a more codified approach to consent and capacity than many candidates are accustomed to from their home health systems. This adjustment is a normal, well-documented part of settling into UK practice rather than a sign that something has gone wrong, and most doctors who remain committed to the pathway describe the structured nature of UK training — clear competency frameworks and defined progression points — as a genuine long-term benefit once the initial adjustment period has passed.

It is also worth acknowledging, honestly, that this entire journey is a long one, and the administrative uncertainty involved can weigh on candidates just as much as the exams themselves. Readers who find the process stressful, particularly during long verification waits or after a difficult exam attempt, may find it useful to read the site's guide on managing UKMLA exam anxiety, which addresses the psychological side of this pathway directly rather than treating it as a purely administrative exercise.

Career progression beyond the first NHS post also tends to reward doctors who plan deliberately rather than simply staying in a trust-grade role indefinitely. Building a structured portfolio from the outset — audits, teaching experience, courses relevant to a target specialty — strengthens a future Specialty Training application considerably, and doctors who start this portfolio work from their first day in an NHS post generally progress faster than those who wait until they feel fully settled before beginning. Similarly, actively seeking feedback from consultants and educational supervisors during the trust-grade period, rather than treating it purely as a stepping stone, helps build the reference base that a competitive Specialty Training application requires. In this sense, life after registration is very much a continuation of the same deliberate planning that got a candidate through the earlier stages of becoming a doctor in the UK, rather than a point at which planning can stop.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to become a doctor in the UK from abroad?

For an international medical graduate, most candidates should plan for a realistic total timeline of 18 to 30 months from beginning EPIC verification to starting a first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. Candidates who need to re-sit either assessment, or who experience delays at the credential verification stage, should expect this timeline to extend. This estimate synthesises typical processing times across many source countries, so individual circumstances can shift it in either direction.

Do I need to retake exams to become a doctor in the UK?

Yes, in almost every case. Regardless of how many postgraduate exams you may have already passed in your home country, or how many years of clinical experience you hold, international medical graduates must pass both components of the UKMLA — the AKT and the CPSA — to gain GMC registration with a licence to practise. The only exceptions are the narrow specialist and GP registrar routes described in the post on UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors, which apply to a small number of doctors with specific recognised postgraduate qualifications.

Can I become a doctor in the UK without sitting the UKMLA?

Only in two circumstances. UK medical students who qualify through a GMC-approved undergraduate medical school move to provisional registration automatically rather than sitting the UKMLA in the same way as an IMG, since the assessment is integrated into their degree. Separately, a small number of doctors holding recognised specialist or GP postgraduate qualifications may qualify for one of the narrower exemption routes, though these apply to a limited population and require individual assessment.

What qualifications do I need to become a doctor in the UK as an international graduate?

You need a primary medical qualification from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year, full and unrestricted registration with your home country's medical regulator, evidence of English language proficiency, and passing results in both components of the UKMLA. Full detail on specific edge cases, including non-standard qualification routes, is available in the site's dedicated UKMLA eligibility requirements guide.

How much does it cost to become a doctor in the UK from another country?

Core GMC and exam fees for the AKT, CPSA, and initial registration typically total around £1,800, though a realistic total budget once English testing, EPIC verification, mandatory UK travel, visa costs, and study materials are included usually falls between £3,000 and £5,000. Because fee schedules are reviewed periodically and vary somewhat by home country, candidates should consult the dedicated full IMG fee breakdown for current, stage-by-stage figures rather than relying on a single estimate.

Is the UK a good place to practise medicine compared with other countries?

This is ultimately a personal decision that depends on career goals, family circumstances, and which licensing pathway feels most achievable, rather than one this guide can answer definitively. However, candidates weighing the UK against other major destinations such as Australia or Canada may find it useful to compare exam structures and typical timelines directly in the guide on how the UKMLA compares with the AMC and MCCQE pathways before committing to a particular country's licensing process.

Do I need a visa to become a doctor in the UK?

Yes, unless you already hold the right to live and work in the UK through another route, such as an existing family or settled status. Most international medical graduates enter the NHS workforce on a Skilled Worker visa sponsored by an NHS employer, and immigration considerations should be planned alongside, not after, the UKMLA and GMC registration process. Full detail is available in the dedicated UK visa guide for international doctors.

What is the first practical step to becoming a doctor in the UK?

The first practical step is confirming that your medical school appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year and that you hold full, unrestricted registration with your home country's medical regulator. Once this is confirmed, the next action is creating a GMC Online account and beginning primary source verification of your degree through EPIC, since this stage usually takes the longest and should start well before exam preparation is finished. Reviewing the site's complete guide to what the UKMLA is is a useful next step for candidates who want to understand the exam itself before committing to a preparation timeline.
