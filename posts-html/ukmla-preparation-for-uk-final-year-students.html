If you're a UK final-year medical student, the short answer is this: your medical school will embed the two national UKMLA components — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — inside your own finals timetable, so your job is to plan backwards from your school's confirmed sitting dates, protect dedicated revision blocks around your dissertation, SSC and elective commitments, and lean on the OSCE technique you've already built over several years. This guide is written specifically for students sitting the UKMLA as part of a UK medical degree. If you're an international medical graduate (IMG) preparing for the AKT and CPSA independently outside a UK medical school, the IMG-focused counterpart to this guide covers that route in detail — the two pathways share a content map but differ substantially in how the exams are booked, delivered and resat.

Everything below assumes you are already enrolled at a UK medical school and progressing toward graduation. We'll walk through how the AKT and CPSA sit inside your finals, a realistic term-by-term timeline, how to protect time for your dissertation or elective, how the CPSA maps onto OSCE skills you already have, which resources tend to work best for UK students specifically, how to manage the anxiety that final year understandably brings, and what happens in the weeks immediately after you pass — including your Foundation Programme application and the transition into F1.

Key takeaways

  • UK final-year students sit the AKT and CPSA as an integrated part of their own medical school's finals, on dates the school sets — this is procedurally different from the independent route IMGs take.
  • Local exam boards can and do add extra assessments on top of the national UKMLA components, so check your own school's exam regulations rather than assuming the national blueprint is the whole picture.
  • A realistic final-year timeline front-loads dissertation, SSC and elective work into autumn and winter, leaving a protected 8-12 week revision block before the AKT and CPSA sitting window.
  • The CPSA uses the same circuit-of-stations format as the OSCEs you've already sat, so your existing clinical exam technique transfers directly rather than requiring a new skill set.
  • Foundation Programme applications typically run in parallel with final-year UKMLA revision, not after it, so both need to be planned into the same academic year.
  • If a resit is needed, medical schools have their own GMC-quality-assured remediation policies — check your school's exam regulations early rather than assuming a single national process applies.

Understanding the UKMLA: what UK final-year students are actually sitting

The Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA), usually shortened to UKMLA in UK contexts, is the exam every doctor must pass before joining the UK medical register, regardless of where they trained. According to the General Medical Council (GMC), the MLA is taken by two distinct candidate groups: UK medical students graduating from a UK university, who must pass it as part of their degree before joining the register, and international doctors, who continue to take PLAB in a form that is compliant with MLA requirements. Both groups are assessed against the same underlying MLA content map and the same delivery standards, even though the practical experience of sitting the exam is very different.

For a UK final-year student, this means the UKMLA is not a separate, bolt-on national exam you book yourself. It is two components — the AKT and the CPSA — that your own medical school incorporates into its finals. Your school chooses the dates, runs the logistics, marks (or coordinates marking of) your CPSA stations, and reports your results to the GMC once you've met the required standard. The GMC's role is to set the shared content map and requirements and to quality-assure each school's delivery, not to run the exam sitting itself for home students.

The two UKMLA components, in brief

The AKT is a written, computer-based test of applied clinical knowledge and decision-making, delivered as single-best-answer questions covering the content map's themes of readiness for safe practice, managing uncertainty, and delivering person-centred care. Full detail on format and timing is on the AKT exam pattern page. The CPSA is the practical, clinical-skills component, run as a circuit of stations testing history-taking, examination, communication, data interpretation and practical procedures — see the CPSA exam pattern page for the full station-by-station breakdown.

Both the AKT and CPSA are set to the same national content map whether you're a UK final-year student or an IMG, but the route to sitting them, the resit process, and what happens if you don't pass first time are handled very differently. Know which route applies to you before you plan your revision.

How UK medical schools embed the AKT and CPSA in finals

UK medical schools deliver the UKMLA to their own students by folding the AKT and CPSA into the assessment structure they already run for finals, rather than sending students to an external test centre. This is the single biggest procedural difference from the IMG route, where the AKT is booked individually through a Pearson VUE test centre and the CPSA is sat via PLAB 2 at a dedicated GMC clinical assessment centre, entirely separate from any university.

The Medical Schools Council (MSC), which represents the heads of all UK medical schools, coordinates national delivery of the Medical School AKT (MS AKT) through an MS AKT Board with representation from every school. Public reporting from the Medical Schools Council on the first full national delivery cycle noted that more than 10,000 medical students across the UK — including students at UK medical schools' overseas campuses — sat the AKT between September 2024 and August 2025, with both the AKT and CPSA delivered at each school on dates the school itself selects. This is a genuinely national, coordinated exercise, not 40-plus schools independently reinventing the exam from scratch, but the local school still owns the timetable your revision has to work around.

What "embedded in finals" means day-to-day

In practice, embedding means:

  • Your AKT sitting is scheduled by your school, usually alongside or close to other written finals papers, not as a standalone national sitting day shared identically across every university.
  • Your CPSA stations are set and run by your own school's clinical skills or OSCE team, following the GMC's CPSA requirements for station number, content spread and standard-setting.
  • Local exam boards frequently add extra assessment on top of the national UKMLA components — additional OSCE stations, written papers, portfolio sign-off, or professionalism assessments specific to that school's curriculum. The exact mix varies significantly between schools, so this guide deliberately avoids naming specific universities; check your own exam regulations handbook for what applies to you.
  • The GMC quality-assures each school's AKT and CPSA delivery against shared requirements documents, publishing compliance reports by school, but does not set your personal exam timetable.

This is different enough from the IMG pathway that revision advice genuinely diverges. An IMG preparing independently has to build their own study schedule around a test centre booking window they control entirely themselves. A UK final-year student has far less control over exact dates but far more built-in structure — teaching, mock exams, and formative assessments your school already runs are, in effect, UKMLA preparation whether they're labelled that way or not.

A term-by-term final-year UKMLA timeline

There is no single national calendar that fits every UK medical school, because each school sets its own AKT and CPSA dates within the academic year. What follows is a realistic, generalised structure most UK final-year students can adapt once they know their own school's confirmed dates — check key dates for the current UKMLA cycle and cross-reference against your own exam board's calendar the moment it's published.

Autumn term: foundations, not cramming

Early autumn is usually when final-year placements begin in earnest and dissertation or SSC topics are confirmed. This is the term to:

  • Get a firm, written confirmation of your own school's AKT and CPSA dates, plus any local assessment components layered on top.
  • Start light, spaced question-bank practice mapped to the MLA content map, rather than saving all applied-knowledge revision for the last few weeks.
  • Block out dissertation and SSC deadlines on the same calendar as your revision plan, so you can see clashes months in advance rather than discovering them in week 2 of a revision block.
  • If your school runs an elective in this window, use it for light maintenance revision (spaced flashcards, occasional question sets) rather than intensive new-topic study — you cannot out-revise a busy clinical attachment abroad, and trying to will just produce guilt without much learning.

Winter term: building volume and closing the dissertation loop

Winter is typically when dissertation drafts are due and CPSA-style teaching intensifies on clinical placements. Priorities:

  • Finish first drafts of dissertation or SSC write-ups before ramping up AKT question volume — trying to write a dissertation chapter and grind past-paper AKT blocks in the same fortnight is the most commonly cited cause of final-year burnout.
  • Start deliberate CPSA station practice with peers, since most schools run formative OSCE-style mocks in this window that map directly onto CPSA station types — treat every one of these as a dress rehearsal.
  • Decide early whether you're a group-study or solo-revision person for AKT content, and build your weekly schedule around that preference rather than switching methods repeatedly under pressure.

Spring term: the protected revision block

For most UK medical schools, spring term is when the AKT and/or CPSA sitting window falls, or immediately precedes it. This is the term to treat as a protected revision block:

  • Submit dissertation and SSC work before this term starts wherever your school's regulations allow — spring should have as few competing academic deadlines as you can arrange.
  • Run a structured countdown-style revision plan in the 8-12 weeks before your AKT, working through the full content map systematically rather than only your weakest topics.
  • Increase full-length timed mocks to build exam stamina, and debrief every mock in detail rather than just noting a score.
  • For CPSA, rehearse full station circuits under time pressure, ideally with peers or clinical skills tutors giving structured feedback, not just informal practice.
  • Actively manage stress in this window — see the section on exam anxiety below and the dedicated anxiety-management guide for practical techniques.

Summer term: results, graduation and the Foundation Programme handover

Summer term is typically when results are confirmed, resits (if needed) are scheduled, and graduation requirements are finalised. This is also when Foundation Programme preparation intensifies — many students will already have submitted their Foundation Programme application earlier in the year, since the application and educational performance measure process runs on its own separate national timetable that overlaps with, rather than follows, your final-year UKMLA revision. Use this term to confirm provisional GMC registration requirements, complete any outstanding portfolio sign-off, and begin early F1 preparation reading.

Balancing UKMLA revision with dissertation, SSCs and electives

The defining challenge of UK final year is not the difficulty of the UKMLA content itself — most of it has been taught across the course — it's the sheer number of competing deadlines landing in the same twelve months. Dissertations, student-selected components (SSCs), electives, Foundation Programme applications and the AKT/CPSA all compete for the same finite revision hours.

Sequencing beats simultaneous effort

The single most effective strategy reported by successful final-year students is sequencing rather than parallel-processing: front-load dissertation and SSC deadlines into autumn and winter, and protect spring as a low-academic-deadline revision block. If your school allows any flexibility in when you schedule an elective, placing it in autumn rather than immediately before your AKT sitting reduces the risk of losing revision momentum right before the exam.

Practical tactics for the dissertation/SSC overlap

  • Write in short, scheduled blocks rather than open-ended sessions — a dissertation with no deadline structure will expand to fill all your available time, crowding out AKT practice.
  • Choose SSC topics that overlap with clinical content where your school allows choice — an SSC in a specialty you're weak on for the AKT does double duty.
  • Negotiate submission dates early if your school allows any flexibility, rather than assuming the default deadline is fixed — most exam offices will discuss workload clustering if you flag it months in advance rather than during the week itself.
  • Use elective time for maintenance, not acquisition — spaced-repetition flashcard review and occasional question blocks maintain what you already know; starting new topics from scratch on elective rarely sticks under the fatigue of travel and unfamiliar clinical environments.

Protecting revision time without falling behind on placements

Final-year placements are themselves a form of UKMLA preparation — ward-based reasoning, examination practice and professionalism assessment on placement map directly onto both AKT content and CPSA station skills. Rather than treating placement and revision as competing, use placement time actively: verbalise your clinical reasoning to registrars, ask to practise examination routines you're less confident with, and treat every ward round as low-stakes CPSA rehearsal.

How the CPSA relates to the OSCEs you already know

If you've completed earlier years of a UK medical degree, you have almost certainly already sat OSCEs — objective structured clinical examinations built around a circuit of timed stations. The CPSA is, structurally, the same format: a circuit of stations, each testing a specific skill against a marked checklist, run under exam conditions.

What transfers directly

  • Station discipline — reading the station instructions carefully, managing your time within a station, and resetting mentally between stations are skills you've already built.
  • Communication technique — structured history-taking, explanation and consent conversations, and breaking bad news frameworks you've practised for OSCEs apply directly to CPSA communication stations.
  • Examination routines — systematic examination sequences (cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, neurological, and so on) transfer without modification.
  • Data interpretation under pressure — interpreting an ECG, blood gas or set of blood results within a timed station is a skill you've rehearsed repeatedly by final year.

What's different about the CPSA specifically

The CPSA is set to the national MLA content map and quality-assured by the GMC against shared requirements for station design, standard-setting and fairness, as described on the GMC's CPSA quality assurance page. This means the underlying content spread is nationally consistent even though your own school designs and delivers the actual stations. In practice, expect the CPSA to feel like a higher-stakes, nationally benchmarked version of the OSCEs you've already sat, rather than an unfamiliar new exam format. Full detail on the station types and scoring approach is on the CPSA exam pattern page.

Because the format is familiar, the marginal revision gain for UK final-year students tends to come less from learning a new exam technique and more from volume and variety of station practice — rehearsing enough different scenarios that no single station type catches you off guard, and getting structured feedback on the small habits (eye contact, closing a consultation, hand hygiene at examination stations) that quietly cost marks.

Question bank and resource strategy for UK final-year students

Resource choice matters less than consistency of use, but UK final-year students have some specific considerations that differ from the IMG route.

What to prioritise

  • A question bank explicitly mapped to the MLA content map, so your practice reflects the same blueprint your AKT is drawn from — a full comparison of the main options is in the question-bank comparison guide.
  • Your own medical school's teaching material — lecture content, placement-based teaching and formative assessments are built by people who know exactly what your local exam board expects on top of the national components, so don't discount them in favour of generic resources.
  • Past-paper-style mocks set by your own exam board, where available — these are the closest available proxy for your actual sitting's style and difficulty.
  • Spaced-repetition flashcards for high-yield facts, particularly useful for maintaining content during placements, electives and dissertation-heavy weeks when full question blocks aren't practical.

Volume versus quality

A common final-year mistake is treating total questions answered as the main metric of progress. Reviewing every incorrect answer in detail — understanding why the correct option was right and why your chosen option was wrong — produces more learning per hour than rushing through a larger volume of questions without proper review. Build review time into your schedule as a fixed block, not an optional extra squeezed in if time allows.

Group study versus solo revision in final year

Final-year students often default to whichever revision style they used in earlier years without reassessing whether it still fits. Both group and solo approaches have genuine value for different parts of the UKMLA:

  • Group study works particularly well for CPSA communication-station rehearsal (you need a partner to role-play the patient), for talking through applied-knowledge reasoning out loud, and for maintaining motivation during the long spring revision block.
  • Solo revision is usually more effective for timed AKT question blocks and full-length mocks, where the goal is building individual pacing and exam stamina without the pauses that group discussion naturally introduces.

Most successful UK final-year students blend both rather than picking one exclusively — see the group-study-versus-solo comparison for a fuller breakdown of when each approach earns its place in your weekly schedule.

Managing exam anxiety in UKMLA final year

Final year is widely regarded as one of the most demanding periods of UK medical training, precisely because UKMLA revision overlaps with dissertation deadlines, Foundation Programme applications, placements and the emotional weight of an imminent transition to working life. It is normal, not a sign of inadequate preparation, to feel anxious in this window.

What tends to help

  • A written revision timetable that makes the workload visible and finite, rather than an open-ended, anxiety-inducing sense that there's always more you could be doing.
  • Realistic mock exposure under timed conditions, so the actual exam day feels rehearsed rather than unknown.
  • Peer support structures — study groups, year-group WhatsApp groups sharing resources, and informal debriefs after mocks all reduce the isolation that drives anxiety.
  • Your university's wellbeing and student support services, which are used far more often by final-year students than most people realise, and which are specifically resourced for exam-period pressure.
  • Protected downtime scheduled into the revision plan itself, not left to chance — sustainable revision produces better retention than back-to-back study with no recovery built in.

For a fuller set of practical techniques, including how to structure the final week before your sitting, see the dedicated guide to managing UKMLA exam anxiety.

What happens if you need a resit in final year

Not passing the AKT or CPSA on a first attempt is more common than final-year students often assume, and it does not automatically mean a lost year. UK medical schools have their own resit and remediation policies, which the GMC quality-assures against shared requirements for fairness and consistency, but the specific number of permitted attempts, the timeframe for a resit, and the impact on your graduation date vary by school.

What to do if you're worried about a resit

  • Read your own school's exam regulations handbook on resits before results day, so you know exactly what the process involves rather than finding out under stress.
  • Speak to your personal tutor, student support office, or the exam board directly as soon as you're concerned — most schools have structured remediation support (targeted teaching, additional mock exposure, extended time before a resit sitting) that students under-use because they don't ask early enough.
  • Understand that a resit affecting your Foundation Programme start date is a possibility worth planning for, but not the default outcome — many resit students still graduate and start F1 on the standard timetable, depending on how early in the year the resit falls.

This is another point of genuine divergence from the IMG route: IMGs resitting the AKT or PLAB 2 (the CPSA-equivalent independent assessment) rebook independently through GMC/Pearson VUE processes with their own separate fee and waiting-time structure, whereas a UK student's resit sits inside their own school's academic regulations and support structures.

What happens immediately after passing the UKMLA

Passing both the AKT and CPSA, alongside meeting your school's other graduation requirements, clears you to graduate and to apply for provisional registration with the GMC. But the practical transition into working life starts well before your final results are confirmed.

The Foundation Programme application overlap

The Foundation Programme application process — including the Educational Performance Measure and, for some students, Situational Judgement Test-derived scoring depending on the current cycle's methodology — runs on a national timetable set independently of your own school's AKT/CPSA dates. In practice, most UK final-year students submit their Foundation Programme application in the same academic year as their UKMLA sitting, not after receiving their final results, because the application windows simply don't wait. Build this into your term-by-term plan explicitly — it is a real deadline competing for the same hours as AKT/CPSA revision, not an afterthought for the summer.

Academic Foundation Programme applicants

If you're applying to the Academic Foundation Programme (AFP), which combines clinical F1/F2 training with dedicated academic time, the application and interview process typically falls even earlier in final year than the standard Foundation Programme timeline, adding another layer to your scheduling. See how the UKMLA interacts with Academic Foundation Programme applications for the specific timing considerations AFP applicants need to plan around.

Provisional registration and F1 preparation

Once your results and graduation are confirmed, the practical next steps are: applying for provisional GMC registration with a licence to practise, completing any outstanding trust induction requirements for your allocated F1 post, and beginning F1-specific preparation — shadowing, prescribing safety awareness, and familiarising yourself with the electronic systems used at your allocated trust. Many students find it useful to compare how this transition differs for IMGs entering the Foundation Programme or specialty training via different routes; see Foundation Year versus specialty training for IMGs for that comparison, which is also useful context if you're weighing your own long-term training pathway.

A short F1-readiness checklist

  • Confirm provisional GMC registration status and licence to practise as soon as results allow.
  • Complete your allocated trust's mandatory induction and IT system training ahead of your start date.
  • Refresh prescribing safety and common on-call scenario knowledge — content you already covered for the AKT, but worth revisiting in a practical, ward-facing format.
  • Arrange shadowing of the outgoing F1 in your allocated post where your trust offers it, since this is consistently rated by new F1s as the single most useful preparation activity.
  • Sort practical logistics (accommodation, commute, on-call arrangements) well before your start date so your first week of F1 isn't complicated by avoidable admin.

Building a weekly revision timetable that actually survives contact with final year

Most final-year students don't fail because they lack a plan — they fail to stick to a plan because it was never realistic in the first place. A timetable built around eight hours of AKT questions a day, seven days a week, alongside full-time placements and dissertation writing, is not a plan; it's a wish. A workable weekly structure accounts for placement hours, travel, sleep and at least one genuinely protected rest day, and still leaves consistent, smaller blocks for revision rather than relying on rare marathon sessions.

A sample structure for a placement week

  • Weekday mornings/afternoons: placement as scheduled — treat ward-based reasoning and examination practice as active CPSA and AKT rehearsal rather than time separate from revision.
  • Weekday evenings: one to two hours of focused question-bank practice with full review of incorrect answers, rather than passive re-reading of notes.
  • One weekend day: a longer session combining a timed mock block with CPSA station rehearsal, ideally with at least one peer.
  • One full rest day per week: genuinely protected, with no revision guilt attached — this is what makes the rest of the week sustainable over months rather than weeks.

Adjusting the plan as your sitting date approaches

In the final 8-12 weeks before your school's confirmed AKT and CPSA dates, most students shift the balance toward full-length timed mocks and away from untimed topic review, since exam stamina and pacing become the limiting factor as much as raw knowledge. It's normal, and often useful, to feel that your question-bank percentage plateaus or even dips slightly in this window as you attempt harder, more representative mixed papers rather than topic-sorted easy questions — a plateau on a harder paper is not the same as a decline in your actual readiness.

Working with your medical school rather than around it

Because UK final-year students sit the UKMLA components through their own school rather than an independent booking, your school's exam office, personal tutors and clinical skills leads are a resource most students under-use. Unlike an IMG preparing entirely independently, you have direct access to people who know precisely what your local exam board expects on top of the national AKT and CPSA requirements.

Questions worth asking your medical school directly

  • What are the confirmed AKT and CPSA dates for my year, and how far in advance are they normally finalised?
  • Does our school add any local assessment components on top of the national UKMLA components, and how are they weighted toward final progression?
  • What formative mock opportunities are built into the curriculum before the real sitting, and are past papers or station lists made available for practice?
  • What is the resit policy if I don't pass first time, including timeframes and any impact on my graduation or Foundation Programme start date?
  • Who do I contact if I need adjustments for a disability, long-term health condition, or other extenuating circumstances affecting my sitting?

Asking these questions in autumn term, well before your revision block begins, means you're planning around confirmed facts rather than assumptions carried over from older year groups or generic online advice that may not reflect your own school's current arrangements.

Putting it all together: a realistic mindset for UK final year

The UKMLA is, for UK final-year students, less a standalone hurdle to fear and more a formal, nationally benchmarked version of assessments you've been building toward across your entire degree. The AKT draws on applied knowledge you've accumulated through years of teaching and placements; the CPSA draws on OSCE technique you've rehearsed repeatedly. What makes final year genuinely hard is not the unfamiliarity of the content, but the compressed timeline in which dissertation deadlines, SSCs, electives, Foundation Programme applications and the UKMLA itself all land in the same twelve months.

A term-by-term plan that front-loads academic writing, protects a genuine revision block in spring, and treats placement time as active rehearsal rather than a distraction from revision gives you the best realistic chance of moving through final year, the UKMLA, graduation and into F1 without unnecessary crisis points along the way. Where your plan needs adjusting is something only your own school's confirmed dates and your own working patterns can tell you — use the general structure here as a starting scaffold, not a substitute for checking your local exam regulations directly.

Frequently asked questions

How is the UKMLA different for UK final-year students compared with IMGs?

UK final-year students sit the AKT and CPSA as an integrated part of their own medical school's finals, on dates the school sets, alongside any local assessments the school adds. IMGs sit the AKT independently through a Pearson VUE test centre and the CPSA (via PLAB 2) as separate, standalone bookings outside any university course.

Do UK medical schools set their own exams on top of the national UKMLA components?

Yes. The AKT and CPSA are national components set to a common content map and quality-assured by the GMC, but individual medical schools can and do add extra local assessments, extended matching questions, OSCE stations or portfolio requirements on top, so your finals timetable is rarely just the two UKMLA components in isolation.

When in final year do UK students typically sit the AKT and CPSA?

Most UK medical schools schedule the AKT and CPSA within the final year or in the penultimate year for some five- and six-year courses, often clustering the CPSA stations in autumn or winter and the AKT written paper in winter or spring, but exact timing is set locally by each school, so always check your own exam board's calendar.

How does the CPSA relate to the OSCEs I've already sat in earlier years?

The CPSA uses the same circuit-of-stations format as the OSCEs UK students sit throughout medical school, testing history-taking, examination, data interpretation, communication and practical procedures against a shared national blueprint, so most of your existing OSCE technique and station discipline transfers directly.

Can I take my elective or intercalated year and still be ready for the UKMLA?

Yes, but you need to plan backwards from your school's confirmed AKT and CPSA dates. Most students protect a dedicated revision block after returning from elective rather than trying to revise heavily during it, and use elective time itself for lighter, spaced recall rather than starting fresh topics.

How should I balance dissertation or SSC deadlines with UKMLA revision?

Front-load dissertation and student-selected component work into terms before your school's AKT/CPSA sitting window, and treat the final 8-12 weeks before the written and clinical assessments as a protected revision block with minimal new academic writing due.

What happens if I fail the AKT or CPSA in final year?

Medical schools have their own resit and remediation policies, quality-assured by the GMC, which typically allow a defined number of resit attempts within set timeframes before graduation is affected; policies vary by school, so check your own exam regulations and speak to your student support team early if you're worried.

How does passing the UKMLA fit around my Foundation Programme application?

Foundation Programme applications and educational performance measure scoring run on a separate timetable to your UKMLA sitting, but you must pass both AKT and CPSA and graduate to take up a Foundation Programme place, so most students apply to the Foundation Programme in parallel with their final-year UKMLA revision rather than waiting for results.

What resources should UK final-year students use for AKT revision?

Most UK students combine a UKMLA-aligned question bank mapped to the GMC content map, their medical school's own lecture and placement material, past-paper-style mocks set by their exam board, and spaced-repetition flashcards, prioritising quality practice with review over sheer question volume.

Is group study or solo revision better for final-year UKMLA preparation?

Both have a place: group sessions work well for CPSA communication-station practice and talking through applied-knowledge reasoning, while solo timed question blocks build the exam stamina and pattern recognition the AKT rewards, so most successful students blend the two rather than choosing exclusively one.

How common is exam anxiety in UKMLA final year, and what helps?

Final year is widely reported as one of the most stressful periods of UK medical training because UKMLA components overlap with dissertations, job applications and placements; structured revision timetables, peer support, university wellbeing services and realistic mock exposure are the most commonly recommended countermeasures.

What should I do in the weeks immediately after passing the UKMLA?

After confirmed results, most students finalise Foundation Programme preparation, complete any outstanding portfolio or graduation requirements, apply for provisional GMC registration, and begin F1-specific induction reading and shadowing before starting Foundation Year 1 in August.
