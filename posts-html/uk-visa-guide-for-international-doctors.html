The UK visa for international doctors taking up an NHS or other eligible healthcare post is, in almost every case, the Health and Care Worker visa — a dedicated variant of the Skilled Worker visa created specifically for doctors, nurses, and a defined list of health and social care professionals. It carries a reduced application fee compared with the general Skilled Worker route and, most significantly, exempts the applicant from the Immigration Health Surcharge that otherwise applies to most other UK visa categories. This guide sets out, in detail, everything a doctor moving to the UK needs to understand about this specific visa route: who qualifies, how the Certificate of Sponsorship process works, what the application actually involves, how dependants are included, how the visa is extended or switched from another status, and the long-term route to settlement and citizenship that follows.

Unlike the site's broader guide to the job-search process that leads from GMC registration to an NHS post, which covers the whole pathway from exam results to a first shift, this article focuses solely and in depth on the immigration mechanics of the doctor visa for the UK itself. It assumes a reader who is either actively job-hunting, has just received a conditional offer, or is trying to understand the visa landscape before committing to the UKMLA route at all. Consequently, it goes considerably further into eligibility detail, fee structure, dependant costs, and settlement timelines than a general job-pathway guide reasonably can.

Before going further, an important caveat applies throughout this entire guide. Quick Answer: What Visa Do International Doctors Need for the UK?

The Health and Care Worker visa is the primary immigration route for internationally trained doctors taking up NHS or other eligible healthcare posts in the UK. It is a variant of the Skilled Worker visa, built specifically for health and social care professionals, and it carries two concrete financial advantages over the general route: a reduced application fee and a full exemption from the Immigration Health Surcharge for the applicant. In short, a doctor visa for the UK obtained through this route is both cheaper to apply for and cheaper to hold than the equivalent general work visa.

However, the visa is not something a doctor can simply apply for independently. It depends entirely on first securing a genuine job offer from a UK healthcare employer that holds a Home Office sponsor licence, and that employer then issuing a Certificate of Sponsorship tied to the specific role. Without that certificate, there is no valid application to submit. The sections below walk through eligibility, the sponsorship mechanism, the application process itself, the Immigration Health Surcharge exemption, dependant arrangements, visa duration and switching rules, and the long-term settlement pathway, in that order, so a doctor at any stage of the journey can find the relevant detail quickly.

Health and Care Worker Visa Eligibility for Doctors

Eligibility for the Health and Care Worker visa rests on four pillars, and a doctor genuinely needs to satisfy all four before an application can succeed. Missing any single one — even temporarily — means the application cannot proceed, which is why understanding each requirement in advance is worth far more time than most candidates initially give it.

A Confirmed Job Offer From a Licensed Sponsor

The first and most fundamental requirement is a confirmed job offer from an approved UK healthcare employer holding a valid Home Office sponsor licence. Almost every NHS trust already holds such a licence, since sponsoring international staff is now routine across the health service. Nevertheless, smaller independent-sector providers, some private hospitals, and certain smaller community health organisations do not always hold one, so a doctor should always confirm sponsor licence status directly with a prospective employer's recruitment or HR team before investing significant time in an application. An employer without a sponsor licence simply cannot issue the certificate that makes a Health and Care Worker visa application possible, regardless of how genuine or attractive the job offer itself might be.

The Role Must Appear on the Eligible Occupation List

Secondly, the specific role on offer must appear on the list of eligible occupations for the Health and Care Worker visa, which covers doctors, nurses, paramedics, and a defined range of allied health and social care professions. Medical practitioner roles are consistently included on this list, so this requirement rarely blocks a genuine doctor's application in practice. However, because the exact list of eligible occupation codes is reviewed and can be updated by the Home Office, a doctor should always check the current list on the official Health and Care Worker visa guidance rather than assuming a role is automatically covered simply because it is medical in nature.

Meeting the Minimum Salary Threshold

Thirdly, the role must meet a minimum salary threshold set for the visa route, which can vary depending on the specific occupation code, the applicant's experience level, and whether the role falls under a designated shortage occupation. Because these thresholds are reviewed periodically and have changed more than once in recent years, this guide deliberately does not quote a fixed figure as a permanent fact. Instead, doctors should treat the salary threshold as something to verify directly on gov.uk's Health and Care Worker visa page at the time of application, and should also confirm the specific figure that applies to their exact role and grade with their sponsoring trust's HR team, since NHS pay scales for entry-level Trust Grade posts are generally structured to satisfy the threshold in any case.

English Language Requirements — Distinct From the GMC's Clinical English Test

Fourthly, and a point that genuinely confuses many candidates, the visa itself carries its own English language requirement, separate and distinct from the GMC's clinical English language requirement used for registration purposes. In other words, satisfying the GMC's IELTS or OET threshold for registration does not automatically satisfy the Home Office's visa requirement, and vice versa — these are two different tests administered by two different bodies for two different purposes, even though a candidate's underlying English ability is, of course, the same in both cases. In practice, many doctors are able to use the same qualifying test result to satisfy both requirements simultaneously, since the accepted test types and score bands overlap considerably. Nevertheless, a doctor should check the current Home Office guidance on acceptable English language evidence for the visa specifically, rather than assuming that a test taken for GMC purposes will be accepted for immigration purposes without separate verification. Doctors who have not yet begun the registration process may find it useful to first review the full GMC registration requirements for international doctors, since registration and visa eligibility are closely linked but governed by entirely separate rules.

These four pillars sit on top of, rather than replace, the underlying clinical and professional eligibility criteria that determine whether a doctor can practise in the UK at all — the site's full UKMLA eligibility guidance covers the exam, qualification recognition, and registration prerequisites that must already be satisfied, or nearing completion, before visa sponsorship becomes a realistic prospect. In other words, visa eligibility and professional/registration eligibility are two separate checklists that happen to converge at roughly the same stage of a doctor's journey, and a strong application addresses both simultaneously rather than assuming one guarantees the other. A prospective employer's international recruitment team will typically confirm all four at once when making a formal offer, which is one further reason a genuine, written job offer matters so much before any visa planning begins in earnest.

The Certificate of Sponsorship: Why You Cannot Apply Without One

A Certificate of Sponsorship (CoS) is a digital record, not a physical document, that a licensed employer generates and assigns to a specific job offer once that offer has been formally confirmed. It functions as proof that a genuine vacancy exists, that the employer has verified the role meets Health and Care Worker visa requirements, and that the specific applicant has been matched to that vacancy. Each certificate carries a unique reference number, and it is this reference number — not the job offer letter itself — that a doctor actually needs in hand before a visa application can be submitted.

An NHS trust issues a Certificate of Sponsorship only after a job offer has been made and accepted, and typically only once pre-offer checks such as right-to-work verification and basic eligibility screening have been completed internally. In practice, this means there is always a gap — commonly one to three weeks, though it can be longer depending on a trust's internal HR processing speed — between "I have accepted a job offer" and "I have a CoS reference number in hand." A doctor genuinely cannot submit a Health and Care Worker visa application during this gap, no matter how confident the job offer feels, because the application form itself requires the CoS reference number as a mandatory field.

This is worth stating plainly because it is one of the most common points of confusion for doctors moving to the UK for the first time: a job offer alone is not equivalent to visa eligibility. A conditional offer is precisely that — conditional — until the certificate exists. Doctors who understand this distinction early tend to plan their finances and travel far more realistically than those who assume the visa process can begin the moment an offer email arrives. For context on how a job offer itself is typically secured in the first place, the site's guide to the job-search process that produces the sponsorship-backed offer covers the practical mechanics of finding and winning an NHS post, which is the necessary step before any Certificate of Sponsorship can exist at all.

How to Apply: Visa Application Mechanics

Once a Certificate of Sponsorship reference number has been issued, the visa application itself follows a defined online process. Doctors should treat the steps below as the general shape of the process, while always confirming the exact current requirements on the official Health and Care Worker visa application page, since document requirements and online systems are updated from time to time.

Where and How to Apply

The application is submitted online through the official UK government visa application service, and the exact form and required supporting evidence depend on whether the applicant is applying from outside the UK or switching from within the UK. Applicants typically need a valid passport, the Certificate of Sponsorship reference number, evidence of the qualifying English language test, proof of the job offer and salary, and — for some applicants — a criminal record certificate covering recent years of residence overseas. Because document requirements can vary by nationality and individual circumstances, doctors should complete the guided eligibility questions on the official application portal rather than relying solely on a general checklist.

Biometric Requirements

Almost all applicants are required to provide biometric information — a photograph and, in most cases, fingerprints — as part of the application. Applicants outside the UK usually attend a visa application centre in their home country or a nearby country to provide biometrics, while applicants switching from within the UK are often able to use a smartphone-based identity verification app instead, depending on their nationality and current immigration status. Booking a biometric appointment promptly after submitting the online application, rather than treating it as an afterthought, is one of the more effective ways to avoid unnecessary delay.

Typical Processing Times

Processing times vary depending on whether the application is made from outside or inside the UK, and a priority service is usually available at an additional cost for applicants who need a faster decision. As a general guide, applications from outside the UK have historically taken several weeks under standard processing, though this figure moves depending on application volumes and the applicant's location, and should not be treated as a guarantee. Doctors with a confirmed start date should build a realistic buffer into their planning rather than assuming standard processing will comfortably finish well before the date a trust has proposed.

Visa Fees

The Health and Care Worker visa carries a reduced application fee compared with the standard Skilled Worker route, and the exact fee depends on the length of the visa being applied for and whether the application is made from outside or inside the UK. Because fees are reviewed periodically and have changed more than once in recent years, this guide deliberately avoids quoting a fixed number as a permanent fact. The single most reliable source for the current fee is gov.uk's own fee table for the Health and Care Worker visa, and doctors should check it immediately before submitting payment rather than relying on a figure seen in an older article, forum post, or social media group. Preparing Supporting Documents in Advance

Doctors who assemble their supporting documentation well before the Certificate of Sponsorship is issued generally move through the application stage considerably faster than those who begin gathering paperwork only once the reference number arrives. Passports should have enough validity remaining to comfortably outlast the intended visa period, and any document not originally issued in English — degree certificates, police clearance certificates, marriage or birth certificates for dependants — typically needs a certified translation rather than an informal one. Because certified translation services can themselves take days or weeks to complete, particularly for less commonly requested language pairs, starting this process early is one of the simplest ways a doctor can shorten their own overall timeline. Similarly, criminal record certificates from countries of prior residence sometimes take considerably longer to obtain than the standard UK equivalent, so doctors who have lived or worked in multiple countries should request these certificates as soon as a genuine job search begins, rather than waiting until a sponsor has been confirmed.

It is also worth planning for the possibility that supporting evidence goes out of date before the application is actually submitted. A police certificate or a bank statement used as proof of funds is sometimes only considered valid for a limited window before the application date, so doctors who assemble every document too far in advance can occasionally find themselves needing to refresh one or two items just before submission. Keeping a simple checklist of each document's issue date, and revisiting it shortly before the Certificate of Sponsorship is expected, avoids this becoming a last-minute scramble.

The Immigration Health Surcharge Exemption

The Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) is a charge that most UK visa applicants must pay, upfront, to access NHS services during their stay, and for a multi-year visa this charge can add a very substantial sum to the overall cost of relocating. The Health and Care Worker visa, however, carries a specific and genuinely valuable exemption: eligible applicants working in defined health and social care roles, including doctors, do not have to pay the Immigration Health Surcharge at all.

This exemption is one of the clearest, most concrete financial advantages of moving to the UK specifically through a health or care role rather than the general Skilled Worker route, and it is worth understanding precisely because the saving compounds over the length of the visa — the longer the visa, the larger the surcharge would otherwise have been. Furthermore, as covered in the dependants section below, this exemption is not limited to the main applicant alone. Doctors should nevertheless confirm their own eligibility for the exemption at the point of application, since it depends on the specific occupation code and sponsor arrangement being correctly recorded, and should treat gov.uk's own explanation of the surcharge exemption as the definitive source rather than assuming eligibility automatically.

Bringing Dependants: Spouse, Partner, and Children

Many doctors relocate to the UK as part of a family unit, and the Health and Care Worker visa route was deliberately designed with this in mind. A spouse or partner, and dependent children, can generally be included either as part of the same application or as a subsequent application once the main applicant's visa is in place, provided the relevant relationship and financial requirements are satisfied.

Dependants' Right to Work and Study

Dependant partners on the Health and Care Worker visa route are generally permitted to work in most occupations, which is a meaningful practical advantage for families relocating together, since it means a spouse or partner is not confined to a narrow list of permitted jobs. Dependent children are generally entitled to attend UK state schools in the same way as any other resident child, which removes one significant source of uncertainty for families planning a move with school-age children.

Additional Costs and Documentation for Dependants

However, bringing dependants meaningfully increases both the cost and the documentation burden of the overall application, and this is precisely the area where doctors most commonly underestimate the total expense of relocating. Each dependant requires their own visa application, their own supporting documents — proof of relationship, such as a marriage certificate or birth certificate, and evidence of the applicant's ability to support them financially — and, in many cases, their own biometric appointment. The visa application fee itself is charged per dependant, not as a single family rate, so a doctor relocating with a spouse and two children should expect to plan for multiple full applications rather than one combined fee.

On the positive side, dependants of a Health and Care Worker visa holder also benefit from the same Immigration Health Surcharge exemption as the main applicant, which meaningfully offsets what would otherwise be one of the largest additional costs of a family relocation. Even so, doctors planning a family move should budget for visa application fees for each family member, translation or certification costs for supporting documents not originally issued in English, and the practical costs of biometric appointments for each dependant, rather than assuming the family's total visa cost will resemble the individual applicant's fee multiplied by a simple family discount. Because dependant eligibility criteria and documentation requirements are reviewed periodically, the current detailed requirements should always be checked on gov.uk's dependant guidance for the Health and Care Worker visa before an application is prepared.

Visa Duration, Switching, and Extension

The Health and Care Worker visa is typically granted for a period tied to the length of the employment contract and Certificate of Sponsorship, often up to five years at a time, though the exact maximum duration and any conditions attached to it should always be confirmed against current Home Office guidance rather than assumed from a previous year's rules.

Switching From Another Visa Type

Many doctors are not applying from overseas for the first time, but are instead already in the UK on a different visa type and wish to switch into the Health and Care Worker route once they have secured a qualifying job offer. Switching from a Student visa is a common and generally workable route for doctors who completed a UK medical degree and subsequently secured an eligible job offer, since the Student visa route is specifically designed to allow a transition into skilled work once study has finished and a genuine sponsored role has been found.

However, one misconception recurs often enough to warrant explicit correction: a Visitor visa is not switchable into the Health and Care Worker visa, or indeed into most other work visa categories, from within the UK. A doctor who enters the UK on a visit visa — for example, to attend an interview or sit an exam — cannot simply accept a job offer and switch into sponsored work status while remaining in the country on that visit visa. In practice, this means a doctor who travels to the UK as a visitor and is then offered a sponsored role must normally leave the UK and apply for the Health and Care Worker visa from outside the country, or from their home country, rather than switching status locally. Understanding this distinction before travelling on a visit visa avoids a genuinely disruptive and entirely avoidable situation, and doctors uncertain about their own current visa status should verify their specific switching eligibility directly on gov.uk or with a regulated immigration adviser before making any travel or job-acceptance decisions.

Extending the Visa

Extension applications follow broadly the same mechanics as the original application — a continuing or new Certificate of Sponsorship, continued eligibility against the salary and occupation requirements, and payment of the relevant fee — and should generally be submitted before the current visa expires, since working beyond an expired visa without a valid extension in progress carries serious immigration consequences. Doctors approaching the end of their visa period should begin extension planning well in advance, in the same way they would plan any other major administrative process, rather than leaving it until the final weeks before expiry.

The Long-Term Route: Settlement and British Citizenship

For many doctors, the Health and Care Worker visa is not simply a temporary work permit but the first step on a longer path toward permanent settlement in the UK. After a qualifying period of continuous residence and employment on an eligible visa route, doctors generally become eligible to apply for indefinite leave to remain (ILR) — the UK's term for permanent settlement status, which removes the need for further employer sponsorship and time-limited visa renewals.

Beyond indefinite leave to remain, doctors who go on to hold settled status for a further qualifying period generally become eligible to apply for British citizenship, subject to additional requirements such as residence history and, in most cases, a citizenship test and English language evidence. However, because the exact qualifying periods for both indefinite leave to remain and British citizenship are set by immigration rules that are reviewed and have been amended by government from time to time, this guide deliberately does not state a fixed number of years as a permanent fact. Doctors planning their long-term future in the UK should treat the current qualifying periods published on gov.uk and the wider gov.uk settlement and citizenship guidance as the only reliable reference point, and should revisit that guidance periodically throughout their visa period rather than relying on a figure they read when they first arrived, since rules affecting doctors already mid-way through a qualifying period have occasionally changed.

In practice, most doctors treat the settlement pathway as a long-term background consideration rather than something requiring active management year to year, since the day-to-day requirement is simply to remain in continuous, lawful sponsored employment and to keep visa extensions current without a gap. However, a small number of practical habits make the eventual settlement application considerably smoother when the qualifying period finally arrives. Keeping a personal record of every trip abroad, since excessive time spent outside the UK during the qualifying period can affect eligibility, is one such habit. Retaining copies of every Certificate of Sponsorship, payslip, and visa grant letter throughout the qualifying period is another, since an indefinite leave to remain application typically requires evidence of continuous lawful residence and employment across the entire period, not just the most recent year. Doctors who keep this evidence organised as they go, rather than trying to reconstruct years of employment history at the point of application, generally find the eventual settlement process considerably less stressful than those who leave it until the qualifying period is almost complete.

It is also worth being clear that the standard Health and Care Worker visa route described throughout this guide is entirely separate from immigration status obtained through asylum or refugee protection claims. Doctors who arrive in the UK via the refugee and asylum route into medical practice follow a genuinely different immigration process, with different rights, timelines, and rules governing work and settlement, and should not assume the guidance in this article applies directly to their situation. The two routes exist for different purposes and are governed by different parts of UK immigration law, even though both can ultimately lead to a doctor practising within the NHS.

Common Visa Mistakes International Doctors Make

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among doctors navigating the Health and Care Worker visa for the first time, and nearly all of them are planning mistakes rather than anything related to genuine ineligibility.

  • Applying, or attempting to apply, before a Certificate of Sponsorship has been issued. As explained above, the CoS reference number is a mandatory part of the application, and no amount of enthusiasm about a verbal or emailed job offer changes this. Doctors should treat a job offer as provisional until the certificate genuinely exists.
  • Underestimating the total cost of bringing dependants. Because the visa application fee is charged per dependant rather than as a family package, doctors relocating with a spouse and children routinely underestimate the combined cost of the move if they only budget for their own individual application fee.
  • Misunderstanding switching rules from a visit visa. As covered above, a doctor who travels to the UK on a visitor visa cannot simply switch into the Health and Care Worker visa from inside the country if offered a job during that visit — a mistake that can force an unplanned return home and a fresh overseas application.
  • Assuming the GMC's English test automatically satisfies the visa's English requirement. Although the same test result often does satisfy both, doctors should verify this explicitly rather than assuming it, since the two requirements are set by different bodies for different purposes.
  • Treating quoted fees and salary thresholds as permanent. Because these figures are reviewed periodically, doctors who rely on an outdated figure from a forum post or an older article risk budgeting incorrectly or being surprised by a shortfall against the current salary threshold.
  • Leaving extension applications until the last minute. Doctors nearing the end of their visa period who delay gathering a new Certificate of Sponsorship and supporting evidence risk a gap in valid status, which carries serious consequences for both employment and future settlement eligibility.

Avoiding these mistakes will not remove every source of delay, since processing times and document verification depend on external bodies beyond any individual doctor's control. Nevertheless, sidestepping these specific, well-documented errors removes a substantial share of the friction that doctors otherwise experience entirely unnecessarily.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I bring my family on a Health and Care Worker visa?

Yes. A spouse or partner and dependent children can generally be included either alongside the main application or as a subsequent application, provided relationship and financial requirements are met. Dependants also benefit from the same Immigration Health Surcharge exemption as the main applicant, although each dependant requires their own separate visa application and associated fee.

How much does the doctor visa for the UK cost?

The Health and Care Worker visa carries a reduced application fee compared with the standard Skilled Worker route, and the applicant is exempt from the Immigration Health Surcharge, but exact fees change periodically and depend on visa length and where the application is made from. The only reliable current figures are published directly on gov.uk's Health and Care Worker visa fee page, which should always be checked before applying.

Do I need a job offer before I can apply for the Health and Care Worker visa?

Yes. A doctor must first secure a confirmed job offer from a UK healthcare employer holding a valid sponsor licence, and that employer must then issue a Certificate of Sponsorship tied to the specific role. There is no route to apply for this visa independently of a genuine, sponsored job offer.

Is the GMC's English language requirement the same as the visa's English requirement?

No. The GMC's clinical English requirement, used for medical registration, and the Home Office's English requirement for the Health and Care Worker visa are genuinely separate requirements set by different bodies. Many doctors are able to use the same qualifying test result to satisfy both, but this should be verified rather than assumed.

Can I switch to the Health and Care Worker visa from a visit visa?

No. A Visitor visa is not switchable into the Health and Care Worker visa from within the UK, which means a doctor offered a job while visiting the UK would generally need to leave and apply from outside the country. This is one of the most common and most disruptive misconceptions among doctors new to the UK visa system.

Can I switch to the Health and Care Worker visa from a Student visa?

Yes, in most cases. Doctors who complete a UK medical degree on a Student visa and subsequently secure an eligible sponsored job offer can generally switch into the Health and Care Worker visa from within the UK, since the Student visa route is designed to allow this kind of transition into skilled sponsored work.

How long does it take to get indefinite leave to remain as a doctor?

Doctors generally become eligible for indefinite leave to remain after a qualifying period of continuous residence and employment on an eligible visa route, but the exact number of years is set by immigration rules that are reviewed periodically and should not be treated as fixed. Always check the current qualifying period on gov.uk rather than relying on a figure from an earlier year.

Does the Health and Care Worker visa cost less than a standard work visa?

Yes, in two concrete ways. The application fee itself is reduced compared with the general Skilled Worker route, and the applicant is exempt from paying the Immigration Health Surcharge altogether, which together represent a meaningful saving over the life of the visa compared with most other UK work visa categories.

Conclusion

The Health and Care Worker visa remains, for the overwhelming majority of internationally trained doctors, the most direct and cost-effective doctor visa for the UK available, provided the underlying job offer, Certificate of Sponsorship, and eligibility requirements are genuinely in place before an application is submitted. Doctors who understand the distinction between a provisional job offer and a certificate-backed sponsorship case, who budget realistically for dependant costs, and who avoid the well-documented switching mistakes covered above consistently move through this process with far less stress and far fewer delays than those who treat it informally. Because this visa stage sits downstream of GMC registration itself, doctors still working through the registration process should first review the site's guidance on the broader journey from qualification to GMC registration, and country-specific readers may also find it useful to compare notes with doctors following the equivalent pathway from India or the route followed by Nigerian doctors, since the visa mechanics described here apply consistently regardless of country of training. This is not legal advice, and individual circumstances vary considerably, so always confirm current fees, thresholds, and eligibility on gov.uk's official Health and Care Worker visa page and consult a regulated immigration adviser or your sponsoring trust's HR team for anything specific to your own case.
