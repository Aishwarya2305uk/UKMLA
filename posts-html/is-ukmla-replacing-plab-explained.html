Is UKMLA replacing PLAB? No — not in the sense of PLAB disappearing. PLAB still exists as the route International Medical Graduates (IMGs) use to reach UK registration, but since August 2024 it has been rebuilt to test exactly the same content, at exactly the same standard, as the Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA) that UK medical students sit through their university as the "UKMLA." The two names now describe different delivery routes into a single shared standard, not two competing exams.

This is one of the most persistent sources of confusion among candidates researching UK medical registration, and it is easy to see why. Search results, forum threads, and even some coaching providers still talk about "PLAB" and "UKMLA" as if they were rival examinations with different content, different difficulty, and a looming date when one will replace the other. None of that is accurate any more. The General Medical Council (GMC) has been explicit that PLAB is "compliant" with the MLA requirements — meaning PLAB has converged onto the same content map, not been scheduled for retirement.

This guide exists to answer the exact question in its title, directly and completely, then unpack every layer of nuance an IMG actually needs before deciding what to study, what to book, and what to expect. It deliberately does not repeat the step-by-step mechanics of registering for PLAB 1 or preparing for PLAB 2 — those are covered in dedicated guides linked throughout — and it does not re-run the full side-by-side UKMLA vs PLAB comparison, which also has its own dedicated page. Instead, this article isolates one specific, high-volume question — is UKMLA replacing PLAB — and answers it as completely and precisely as the GMC's own published position allows, drawing directly on the GMC's official "PLAB and the MLA" guidance page.

Quick Answer: Is UKMLA Replacing PLAB?

The direct answer to is UKMLA replacing PLAB is no — PLAB is not being abolished, phased out, or scheduled for replacement. What has happened instead is a structural convergence: PLAB, the assessment route IMGs sit, has been rebuilt so that it tests candidates against the identical MLA content map and delivery standard that UK medical schools use to deliver the UKMLA to their own students. The GMC's own guidance states this plainly: "international doctors who want to join the UK medical register will continue to take PLAB which is compliant with our MLA requirements. Both groups take assessments based on the same set of topics, as set out in the MLA content map, and which meet the same delivery requirements."

In practical terms, this means three things at once. First, the brand name "PLAB" is not going away — IMGs will keep booking, sitting, and referring to "PLAB 1" and "PLAB 2" for the foreseeable future, and the GMC has given no announced timeline for retiring that name. Second, PLAB's content is no longer a separate syllabus maintained independently of UK medical school finals — it draws on the same MLA content map. Third, from September 2026 this convergence becomes total: every PLAB sitting from that date is assessed against the same updated content map that UK-graduate UKMLA assessments use, closing the last remaining gap between the two routes (see the full breakdown of what changes in the September 2026 content map update for the detail behind that specific transition).

The confusion behind "is UKMLA replacing PLAB" usually stems from a reasonable but mistaken assumption: that because the GMC introduced a single national standard called the MLA, it must also have introduced a single national exam that everyone sits, and that PLAB is the older system being phased out in favour of it. That is not what happened. The MLA is a standard, not a single sitting. UK medical students meet that standard through their university's UKMLA assessment; IMGs meet the identical standard through PLAB. Neither route is being deprecated in favour of the other — they are two delivery mechanisms converging on one shared bar.

What the MLA Actually Is — And Why That Matters Here

The Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA) is the framework the GMC introduced to test the core knowledge, skills, and professional behaviours expected of any doctor practising in the UK — regardless of where they trained. Its purpose, as the GMC describes it, is to give patients and employers a consistent assurance that every doctor joining the medical register, whether educated at a UK university or overseas, has demonstrated the same baseline of safe practice before they start treating patients independently.

Crucially, the MLA is not itself a single exam booked through one shared portal. It is a standard made up of two components: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), a written assessment of clinical knowledge and decision-making, and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), a station-based assessment of practical clinical and communication skills. Every candidate seeking full GMC registration — whichever route they take — must demonstrate they meet both components.

Two Delivery Routes, One Standard

Where candidates differ is not in what they are tested on, but in how they access that test. UK medical students meet the MLA standard through an assessment embedded directly into their medical school's final year — commonly referred to as "sitting the UKMLA." Their university runs the assessment, in partnership with the GMC and the Medical Schools Council, as part of the standard graduation requirements. IMGs, by contrast, are not enrolled at a UK medical school, so they have no institutional route into that same assessment. Instead, they meet the identical MLA standard by sitting PLAB — a GMC-administered, independently bookable examination route that has been rebuilt specifically to test the same content map at the same pass standard.

This is the single most important distinction to internalise before reading anything else about "is UKMLA replacing PLAB": UKMLA and PLAB are not two exams with different content that compete for the same candidates. They are two different delivery mechanisms for reaching the same MLA standard, aimed at two different candidate populations who arrive at UK registration by different routes. A UK final-year student cannot "choose" to sit PLAB instead, and an IMG cannot "choose" to sit the university-embedded UKMLA instead — but both are, in substance, being tested against the same underlying content.

Why the GMC Built It This Way

Before this framework existed, the GMC's own analysis found a real problem: UK medical school finals and the pre-2024 PLAB test were not formally aligned to the same content standard. Two different assessment systems, built independently, were both being used as the gateway to full UK registration, without a shared guarantee that they tested the same underlying competencies at the same level of difficulty. That inconsistency was flagged repeatedly by patient safety researchers and medical education bodies, and it is precisely the gap the MLA framework was designed to close — not by abolishing either existing route, but by rebuilding both to point at the same content map and the same pass standard.

Who Takes "UKMLA" vs Who Takes "PLAB"

Understanding exactly which candidates take which route resolves most of the remaining confusion behind "is UKMLA replacing PLAB." The split is not about difficulty, nationality, or exam quality — it is purely about which institutional pathway a candidate is entering UK registration through.

UK Medical Students: The University Route

If you are enrolled at a GMC-approved UK medical school, you sit the MLA standard as "the UKMLA," delivered by your university. Since the 2024–25 academic year, every UK medical school has been required to incorporate the AKT and CPSA into its final assessments as a graduation requirement. You do not independently book this assessment through a GMC portal in the way an IMG books PLAB — your institution coordinates the sitting, the fee (if any) is generally absorbed into your existing tuition and institutional costs rather than paid directly by you per attempt, and the assessment happens on your own campus or an assigned centre arranged by your school.

On successful completion, UK graduates typically move into provisional GMC registration and the Foundation Programme, progressing to full registration on satisfactory completion of Foundation Year 1. For the detail of what happens if a UK student does not pass first time, see the full breakdown of what a failed attempt means for your Foundation Year progression — that is a distinct question from the one this article addresses, but a common follow-on concern for students working through this pathway.

International Medical Graduates: The PLAB Route

If you trained at a medical school outside the UK and are not eligible for one of the GMC's specialist registration routes, you meet the MLA standard by sitting PLAB. PLAB 1, the written component, has been aligned to meet the requirements of the MLA AKT since August 2024. PLAB 2, the clinical component, has been aligned to meet the requirements of the MLA CPSA over the same period. This means an IMG who passes PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 today has, in substance, met the same UKMLA standard that a UK graduate meets through their university — via a different, independently bookable route rather than an institutionally embedded one.

Unlike the UK-student route, PLAB is self-funded and self-booked. Candidates create a GMC Online account, complete credential verification, meet English language requirements, and book each component individually through the GMC's own systems, paying the exam fee directly for each attempt. This guide does not repeat that process in detail — the full step-by-step PLAB 1 registration walkthrough and the dedicated PLAB 2 preparation guide cover the mechanics comprehensively, including credential verification timelines, English language thresholds, booking strategy, and test centre logistics.

The Common Ground: Same Standard, Different Doors

Both groups, despite entering through different doors, are held to the same underlying MLA content map and pass standard. Neither route is "easier" by design — any observed difference in pass rates between UK graduates and IMGs reflects differences in preparation context (institutional support, familiarity with the UK clinical system, structured revision time) rather than a different, less demanding syllabus. This is precisely why the question "is UKMLA replacing PLAB" has a nuanced answer rather than a flat yes or no: nothing is being replaced, but the two routes have been engineered to converge on identical substance.

Timeline: How PLAB and the UKMLA Converged

Understanding the timeline behind this convergence makes it far easier to answer follow-up questions confidently, rather than relying on secondhand forum summaries that are often out of date.

Before August 2024: Two Independently Built Systems

For most of its history, PLAB — administered by the GMC since the 1970s under the Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board — was built and maintained as an entirely separate assessment system from UK medical school finals. PLAB 1 was a written multiple-choice test; PLAB 2 was an OSCE-style clinical assessment held at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester. There was no formal mechanism ensuring that PLAB tested the same content, at the same standard, as any given UK medical school's own final assessments. This is the root of the historical inconsistency the MLA framework was ultimately built to fix.

August 2024: The MLA Alignment Takes Effect

From August 2024, PLAB 1 was formally aligned to meet the requirements of the MLA AKT, and PLAB 2 was formally aligned to meet the requirements of the MLA CPSA. From the same period, UK medical schools were required to embed the AKT and CPSA into their own final assessments as the UKMLA. This is the date most candidates should think of as "when PLAB and the UKMLA became the same standard" — not a date when either exam disappeared, but the date the convergence became binding rather than aspirational.

Candidates who sat PLAB before this alignment do not need to worry about their existing results — pre-alignment PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 results retain their standard five-year validity and are recognised within the current framework. There is no requirement to retrospectively re-sit a component you passed before the alignment took effect, provided your result remains within its validity window.

September 2026: Full Content Map Convergence

The most recent and most complete stage of this convergence is scheduled for September 2026. The GMC published an updated MLA content map in October 2025, expanding the list of named examinable conditions substantially and removing the previous grid that restricted which conditions could be tested within which specialty context. From September 2026, every MLA-linked assessment — the UKMLA sat by UK medical students and PLAB sat by IMGs — is assessed against this same updated content map. The dedicated guide to the 2026 syllabus changes covers exactly what has been added, what has been restructured, and what candidates on both routes need to check in their revision materials — this article deliberately does not repeat that level of syllabus-specific detail, since it belongs to a companion piece built entirely around that transition.

Put simply: as of September 2026, there is no remaining content gap between what a UK graduate is tested on via the UKMLA and what an IMG is tested on via PLAB. The convergence process that began in August 2024 reaches its logical endpoint.

Why the "Replacement" Misconception Persists

Given how clearly the GMC has stated its position, it is worth examining why "is UKMLA replacing PLAB" remains such a commonly searched question. Several factors reasonably contribute to the confusion.

The Naming Itself Invites the Assumption

When a national body introduces a new, prominently branded standard — the "Medical Licensing Assessment" — alongside an existing, decades-old exam name, it is a natural (if incorrect) assumption that the new name is meant to supersede the old one. This is compounded by the fact that "UKMLA" and "MLA" are sometimes used loosely and interchangeably in casual discussion, which makes it easy to assume PLAB sits outside that new system altogether, when in fact PLAB is one of the two ways of accessing it.

Outdated Preparation Materials and Forum Threads

A significant amount of online discussion — question banks, coaching provider marketing, older forum threads — predates the August 2024 alignment or has simply not been updated since. Materials that still describe "the PLAB syllabus" as something distinct from "the MLA content map" are using terminology that stopped being accurate once the alignment took effect. IMGs researching their pathway should treat any resource that frames PLAB and the UKMLA as separate, competing syllabuses as a signal that the material may be out of date, regardless of how comprehensive it otherwise appears.

No Single Combined Booking System

Because UK students and IMGs still book and sit their respective assessments through entirely separate institutional systems — a university-run process for UK students, GMC Online for IMGs — there is no single shared "MLA booking portal" that would visually reinforce, to a casual observer, that both groups are being tested on the same thing. The absence of a unified front-end system makes the underlying content unification easy to miss.

Genuine, Ongoing Change Creates Uncertainty

It is also fair to say that because the convergence has happened in stages — August 2024's alignment, then the further completion scheduled for September 2026 — candidates researching the topic at different points over the past two years have encountered genuinely different snapshots of "how aligned" PLAB and the UKMLA currently are. That staged rollout, while sensible from an implementation standpoint, has made it harder for any single explainer to feel permanently authoritative, which is exactly why checking the GMC's own current guidance remains important however comprehensive a third-party guide (including this one) tries to be.

What This Means Practically for IMGs

Beyond the historical and structural explanation, IMGs researching "is UKMLA replacing PLAB" usually have a handful of practical questions sitting just underneath it. This section addresses the most common ones directly.

You Do Not Need to Sit Two Separate Exams

Because PLAB is the IMG route into the MLA standard, there is no separate "UKMLA exam" that IMGs must additionally sit alongside PLAB. Passing PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 fully satisfies the MLA requirement for IMGs — there is no parallel booking system, no additional UKMLA sitting available to or required of international candidates, and no scenario in which an IMG is asked to sit "the UKMLA" as something distinct from PLAB.

Your Preparation Should Target the MLA Content Map Directly

Since PLAB now tests the MLA content map rather than an independently maintained PLAB syllabus, the most efficient preparation strategy is to study directly against the current GMC content map rather than relying solely on legacy "PLAB-specific" materials that may not reflect the current scope. The tailored UKMLA preparation guide for IMG candidates covers exactly how to build a study plan around this reality, including how to adapt UK clinical guidelines, NICE-aligned prescribing knowledge, and communication-skills expectations that UK-trained candidates absorb implicitly through their degree but that IMGs often need to build more deliberately.

Booking Logistics Have Not Changed Because of This Convergence

The content alignment between PLAB and the UKMLA does not change how or where you book your sittings — the GMC Online booking process, test centre network, and fee structure for PLAB continue to operate as their own system, separate from how UK universities coordinate their students' UKMLA sittings. For the practicalities of how the GMC Online booking process itself works, including timing windows and how to avoid common booking mistakes, see the dedicated guide to the UKMLA exam booking process on GMC Online.

The Convergence Is a Reassurance, Not a Threat

It is worth stating plainly: this convergence is good news for IMGs, not a source of additional risk. It means that an IMG who studies rigorously against the MLA content map is not preparing for a "lesser" or "different" exam than UK graduates — they are demonstrably meeting the exact same standard that every other doctor on the UK register has met, through a legitimate, GMC-recognised route. Employers and patients are, by design, given the same assurance about an IMG who passed PLAB as they are about a UK graduate who passed their university's UKMLA assessment.

Could PLAB Ever Actually Be Replaced? A Fair Look at the Alternative

It is worth engaging seriously with the underlying question rather than dismissing it outright, because "is UKMLA replacing PLAB" is, at its core, a reasonable question about the future direction of UK medical licensing. So what would it actually take for PLAB, as a named route, to be replaced — and how likely is that, based on what the GMC has said and done so far?

What "Replacement" Would Actually Require

A genuine replacement of PLAB would mean the GMC retiring the PLAB name and booking system entirely, and routing all IMGs through some new, differently branded assessment system — not merely aligning PLAB's content to the MLA standard, which has already happened. Nothing published by the GMC to date describes this kind of structural retirement. Every official communication frames PLAB as the continuing, compliant delivery mechanism for IMGs, not as a legacy system being wound down in favour of something else.

Why a Full Merger Is Unlikely in the Near Term

There are practical reasons a full merger of the two systems into one shared booking process is unlikely soon, independent of any GMC announcement. IMGs are not enrolled at a UK medical school, so they have no institutional mechanism to deliver an assessment the way a university does for its own students — someone still needs to independently verify credentials, confirm English language proficiency, and administer a bookable sitting for candidates scattered across dozens of countries. PLAB, through GMC Online and its international test centre network, is precisely the infrastructure that already does this. Dismantling it would mean building an entirely new IMG-specific delivery system from scratch, for no obvious added value, since the content standard is already unified. This is why the far more plausible ongoing direction — and the one actually reflected in GMC guidance — is exactly what has been happening: keep PLAB as the IMG delivery mechanism, and continue tightening the content alignment between it and the UKMLA, as the September 2026 update does.

What Would Actually Change the Answer to This Question

If the GMC ever does formally announce a timeline for retiring the PLAB name or restructuring how IMGs access the MLA standard, that would be a genuine, newsworthy change to the picture described in this article — and it is exactly the kind of update this guide would need to reflect promptly. Until such an announcement exists, the accurate, current answer remains what the GMC's own guidance says today: PLAB continues, aligned to the MLA standard, with no scheduled replacement.

Where PLAB Fits Alongside Other Registration Routes

Understanding PLAB's continuing role is easier with the wider registration picture in view. PLAB is not the only pathway to GMC registration for IMGs, and knowing where it sits among the alternatives reinforces why it is highly unlikely to be discontinued rather than refined.

The Specialist and GP Registration Routes

Doctors who already hold a recognised postgraduate specialty qualification — MRCP(UK), MRCS, MRCGP, or an equivalent recognised by the GMC — may be eligible for specialist or GP registration without sitting PLAB at all, often via a Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) or an equivalent route. This has always existed alongside PLAB, not as a replacement for it, and continues entirely unaffected by the PLAB/MLA content alignment discussed throughout this guide. For most earlier-career IMGs who do not yet hold a recognised specialty qualification, PLAB remains the standard, most direct pathway.

Eligibility Still Comes First

Whichever route applies, the starting point for any IMG is confirming the GMC's core eligibility requirements — an acceptable primary medical qualification, English language proficiency, and (for the PLAB route specifically) a completed internship period. None of these prerequisites have been altered by the MLA content alignment; they sit logically prior to the question of which assessment content you will be tested on, and they are unaffected by whether PLAB and the UKMLA share a content map.

Registration and Costs Remain Separate Questions

It is worth being clear that the PLAB/UKMLA content convergence discussed in this article is entirely separate from the GMC registration process itself and from the fee schedule that applies to PLAB sittings and GMC registration. Passing PLAB is a necessary step toward registration, not the registration itself, and the fees involved — for PLAB 1, PLAB 2, and GMC registration — continue to apply exactly as they did before the content alignment, since none of this changes what candidates are charged or how registration is finally granted.

What This Means Practically for UK Medical Students

Although this article is written primarily for IMGs confused about the PLAB/UKMLA relationship, UK medical students researching the same question benefit from the same clarity.

Your Assessment Has Not Changed Because of PLAB's Alignment

UK students should not read any of this convergence as implying a change to their own assessment. The UKMLA, as delivered through your medical school, continues to operate exactly as it did before PLAB's alignment — the AKT and CPSA structure, your institution's coordination of the sitting, and your progression into the Foundation Programme are unaffected by the fact that PLAB now shares the same content map. What has changed is that IMGs are now held to your same standard, not that your standard has shifted to accommodate anything about PLAB.

The Comparison Point Worth Understanding

UK students sometimes ask a related but distinct question — whether IMGs sitting PLAB are being tested to an easier standard. Given everything above, the answer is clearly no: the content map is identical. Any genuine difference in outcomes reflects preparation conditions (structured five-year curricula with institutional mock exams and OSCE practice facilities, versus self-directed international preparation), not a different or reduced content standard being applied to PLAB candidates.

Common Misconceptions, Addressed Directly

Several specific misunderstandings recur often enough to address individually and plainly.

  • "PLAB is being phased out and everyone will eventually sit the same UKMLA exam." Inaccurate. There is no GMC-announced plan or timeline to retire the PLAB name or merge IMG and UK-student assessments into a single shared booking system. PLAB remains the IMG delivery route indefinitely, as far as current published guidance confirms.
  • "PLAB tests different, easier content than the UKMLA." Inaccurate since August 2024. Both routes are assessed against the identical MLA content map, and from September 2026 that alignment covers the fully updated map with no remaining gap.
  • "If I already passed PLAB before the alignment, I need to re-sit under the new standard." Inaccurate. Results obtained before the alignment retain their standard five-year validity and are recognised within the current framework — there is no retrospective re-sit requirement.
  • "IMGs can choose to sit the university-style UKMLA instead of PLAB." Inaccurate. IMGs are not enrolled at a UK medical school and therefore have no access to the institutionally delivered UKMLA sitting — PLAB is their only route into the MLA standard.
  • "The September 2026 changes mean PLAB is being replaced by a new exam." Inaccurate. September 2026 completes a content map convergence that has been underway since 2024 — it does not introduce a new or different exam, and PLAB continues under its existing name and booking process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is UKMLA replacing PLAB in 2026?

No. From September 2026, PLAB sittings will be assessed against the same updated MLA content map used for UK-graduate UKMLA assessments, which completes a convergence process rather than a replacement. PLAB continues to exist as the IMG route into UK registration, under its existing name and GMC Online booking process, with no announced date for retiring it.

If I pass PLAB, have I actually met the UKMLA standard?

Yes. Passing PLAB 1 meets the requirements of the MLA Applied Knowledge Test, and passing PLAB 2 meets the requirements of the MLA Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment — together, these are the same two components that make up the UKMLA standard for UK medical students. There is no additional, separate UKMLA exam an IMG needs to sit after passing PLAB.

Do I need to sit both PLAB and a separate UKMLA exam as an IMG?

No. IMGs only sit PLAB 1 and PLAB 2. There is no parallel or additional UKMLA sitting available to IMGs outside the PLAB route, because PLAB is itself the recognised delivery mechanism through which IMGs meet the MLA standard.

Will the GMC eventually stop using the name PLAB?

Possibly in the long term, but there is no confirmed timeline. As things currently stand, PLAB branding remains active across GMC Online, booking communications, and official GMC guidance pages. The GMC's position is that PLAB is compliant with MLA requirements, not that the PLAB name itself is being retired on any published schedule.

Does the PLAB and UKMLA convergence affect doctors already registered with the GMC?

No. This convergence only affects candidates seeking initial GMC registration. Doctors already fully registered are not required to retrospectively demonstrate compliance with the updated content map — their registration continues through the standard annual retention and continuing professional development requirements, not through re-sitting a licensing assessment.

Is PLAB now harder because it matches the UKMLA standard?

Not inherently harder — it is aligned to the same standard, not a more demanding one. The MLA content map defines the same Foundation Year 1-level standard for both routes. Any perceived difficulty difference reflects preparation resources and context rather than the underlying content being more challenging for PLAB candidates specifically.

What should I actually study if PLAB now follows the MLA content map?

Study directly against the GMC's current MLA content map rather than relying only on older PLAB-specific materials that may predate the alignment. Resources built or substantially updated after the relevant alignment dates — particularly anything reflecting the September 2026 content map update — will be the most accurate reflection of what PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 currently test.

Is there a separate "UKMLA for IMGs" exam I should be booking instead of PLAB?

No such separate exam exists. IMGs occasionally encounter marketing from preparation providers using phrases like "UKMLA course for IMGs," which understandably raises the question of whether there is a distinct exam to book. There is not — any legitimate preparation resource aimed at IMGs is preparing you for PLAB 1 and PLAB 2, which are themselves the MLA-compliant route for international candidates, not a different, additional exam sitting alongside them.

Does PLAB no longer exist as a name, and has it been renamed "UKMLA"?

PLAB has not been renamed. Both names remain in active, simultaneous use: "PLAB" refers to the specific IMG-facing route (PLAB 1 and PLAB 2, booked through GMC Online), while "UKMLA" is most commonly used to refer to the university-delivered assessment UK medical students sit, or sometimes loosely to the overall MLA standard as a whole. Neither name has replaced the other in official GMC terminology.

Conclusion

The honest, complete answer to is UKMLA replacing PLAB is no — PLAB is not disappearing, and there is no GMC timeline scheduling its retirement. What has genuinely changed is that PLAB has been rebuilt, in stages since August 2024 and completing in September 2026, to test IMGs against exactly the same MLA content map and standard that UK medical students meet through their university's UKMLA assessment. The two names refer to two different delivery routes into one shared standard, not two competing exams on a collision course.

For IMGs, this is reassuring rather than threatening: a pass on PLAB carries the same demonstrated standard as a UK graduate's UKMLA pass, through a legitimate, GMC-recognised route built specifically for international candidates. The practical task is not to worry about PLAB being phased out, but to make sure your preparation targets the current MLA content map directly, using materials that reflect the alignment rather than outdated pre-2024 assumptions.
