This NHS e-Portfolio guide is written for doctors who are about to encounter one of the most underestimated tools of their early career: the digital, ongoing record of training and practice that follows them from their first Foundation placement through specialty training and beyond. An e-portfolio is not an exam and it is not a one-off form — it is a continuously updated body of evidence that a doctor builds, entry by entry, across every rotation, and that later gets drawn on for two very different purposes: the annual training review known as ARCP, and the longer-cycle GMC process of revalidation. Handled well, from the first week of a new post, an e-portfolio is barely noticeable admin. Handled badly — left untouched for months and then attacked in a panic — it becomes one of the most stressful parts of a doctor's year.

This guide is deliberately practical rather than regulatory. It is not about the appraisal cycle itself or the five-yearly revalidation timeline — those are covered in detail in the companion article on how the GMC revalidation process actually works. This guide is about the tool doctors actually sit down and use, week to week, during training: what an e-portfolio contains, what kinds of entries need to go into it, how to build sustainable habits around it rather than a last-minute scramble, and the common mistakes that turn a useful professional record into a source of dread. It is written equally for UK medical graduates starting Foundation Year 1 and for international medical graduates (IMGs) beginning their first UK training post or clinical role, since both groups encounter broadly the same portfolio expectations, often for the first time, in their first few months of UK practice.

Quick Answer: What Is an NHS E-Portfolio?

An NHS e-portfolio is a secure, online system in which a doctor records and organises the evidence of their clinical training and professional development as it happens — assessments completed with supervisors, reflections on cases and events, feedback gathered from colleagues and patients, and records of courses and learning activity. It functions as a running, cumulative account of a doctor's competence and professional growth, built up continuously rather than compiled retrospectively, and it is the primary evidence base used at each formal training review.

Two things distinguish an e-portfolio from a simple diary or CV. First, it is structured — most platforms organise entries against a defined curriculum or set of competencies, so an assessment or reflection is filed under the specific skill or outcome it demonstrates, not just dumped into a generic timeline. Second, it is used by other people, not just the doctor who writes it: an educational or clinical supervisor reviews entries, a training programme director draws on the whole portfolio at ARCP, and — over a much longer timeframe — the pattern of engagement it shows can be relevant to a Responsible Officer's revalidation recommendation. An e-portfolio is therefore a working professional record with real downstream consequences, not a private notebook.

Different training pathways use different named e-portfolio platforms — Foundation training in England, for instance, uses a system provided centrally by NHS England, while individual specialties typically run their own portfolio through their aligned royal college, and the devolved nations of the UK organise things slightly differently again. Because the exact platform varies by programme and by nation, this guide deliberately avoids treating any single named system as universal — the underlying expectations described here (what to log, how often, and why) apply broadly across whichever specific platform a doctor is issued with at induction.

What Goes Into an NHS E-Portfolio

An e-portfolio is not a single document but a collection of distinct evidence types, each serving a slightly different purpose. Understanding what each category is actually for makes it far easier to recognise, in real time, which everyday moments in a working week are worth capturing.

Workplace-Based Assessments (WBAs)

Workplace-based assessments are structured, standardised tools used to assess a specific skill or encounter as it happens, rather than in an artificial exam setting. Common formats include direct observation of a clinical or procedural skill, structured case-based discussions where a supervisor talks through a doctor's clinical reasoning on a specific patient, and mini-assessments of a single consultation or handover. Each WBA is typically completed jointly — the doctor identifies a suitable case or moment, a supervisor or senior colleague observes or discusses it, and both contribute to a structured form that is then logged in the portfolio against the relevant curriculum outcome.

WBAs matter because they are the closest thing an e-portfolio has to objective, third-party evidence. A reflective entry is the doctor's own account of their practice; a WBA is a supervisor's direct assessment of it. Training programmes typically specify a minimum number of WBAs of each type expected per placement or per year, and falling behind on this number is one of the most common and most avoidable causes of a difficult ARCP outcome, covered in more detail later in this guide.

Reflective Practice Entries

A reflective entry is a doctor's own written account of a case, event, or piece of learning — structured around what happened, what was learned, and what (if anything) will change in future practice as a result. Reflection is deliberately not the same as simply describing a case: a strong reflective entry demonstrates genuine thinking about the doctor's own role, decision-making, and development, not just a factual summary of what occurred clinically.

Good reflective practice tends to follow a simple, honest structure: a brief description of the situation, an account of the doctor's own thoughts and actions at the time, an honest evaluation of what went well and what did not, and a concluding note on what specifically will be done differently next time, or what has already changed. This does not need to be long — a short, specific, genuine reflection consistently reads as more credible to a supervisor than a long but generic one, a distinction explored further in the pitfalls section below.

Case-Based Discussions

Case-based discussions (CBDs) deserve their own mention alongside general WBAs because they are one of the most consistently useful entries a doctor can generate, and one of the easiest to schedule proactively. A CBD involves selecting a case the doctor has personally managed, then talking a supervisor through the clinical reasoning behind the decisions made — not just what was done, but why. Because a CBD is a genuine two-way conversation rather than a passive observation, it tends to generate some of the richest, most specific feedback in the whole portfolio, and doctors who actively choose interesting or challenging cases for their CBDs — rather than whichever case happens to be convenient — generally get more out of the exercise.

Multi-Source Feedback

Multi-source feedback (MSF), sometimes called 360-degree feedback, is a structured exercise in which a range of colleagues — doctors, nurses, allied health professionals, and sometimes administrative staff who work closely with the doctor — complete a standardised questionnaire about their working relationship and professional behaviour. Unlike a WBA, which assesses a single clinical encounter, MSF assesses the doctor's overall professional conduct, teamwork, and communication as observed by the people who work alongside them day to day.

Because MSF depends on colleagues actually completing the questionnaires, it has a longer lead time than most other portfolio entries — a doctor should identify their MSF raters and send out requests well ahead of the point in the year the feedback is actually needed, rather than trying to organise it in the final fortnight before a review. Selecting a genuinely varied group of raters, spanning different roles and levels of seniority rather than only close friends, also produces more credible, useful feedback.

Courses, CPD and Learning Logs

Continuing professional development (CPD) entries record structured learning activity completed outside direct clinical work — courses attended, conferences, e-learning modules, journal clubs, teaching sessions delivered, and any other formal or semi-formal learning. Most e-portfolio platforms allow a doctor to upload a certificate or record of completion alongside a brief note on what was learned and how it applies to their practice, turning a passive certificate into an active piece of evidence rather than just proof of attendance.

Significant Event Reflections

A significant event reflection is a specific, structured account of a notable incident — which might be a near-miss, an unexpected clinical outcome, a system failure, or occasionally something that went unusually well and is worth understanding and replicating. Significant event entries differ from routine reflective entries in that they are typically expected to follow a more formal structure, often involving a description of the event, a timeline, a root-cause-style analysis of contributing factors, and explicit learning points and actions arising from it.

Significant event reflections are one of the categories of supporting information the GMC's own guidance on supporting information for appraisal and revalidation identifies as valuable evidence over a doctor's whole career, not just during training — which is one reason the habit of writing these promptly and honestly, rather than defensively, pays off well beyond any single training year.

Sustainable Portfolio Habits: A Little and Often

The single biggest determinant of whether portfolio-keeping feels manageable or miserable is not how much work is ultimately required — it is how that work is distributed across the year. Doctors who spread portfolio entries evenly across each placement report the process as mildly tedious at worst; doctors who leave it all until the weeks before a review report it as one of the most stressful parts of their training year. The content required is broadly the same either way — it is the timing that changes the experience entirely.

Write Reflections Promptly, While Detail Is Fresh

The most practical single habit a doctor can build is writing a reflective entry within a day or two of the case or event it concerns, while the specific details — what was actually said, what the doctor was actually thinking, what specifically surprised them — are still genuinely fresh. A reflection written a day later contains details a reflection written six weeks later has already lost. This is not simply a matter of memory decay; it also changes the quality of the reflection itself. A prompt reflection can capture a specific, honest moment of uncertainty or a precise clinical reasoning step; a delayed reflection, reconstructed from a vague memory of "a difficult patient," tends to default to generic, safe language because the specific texture of the event is gone.

A simple, low-effort trick many doctors use is keeping a very brief private note — a single sentence — at the point a potentially reflection-worthy moment happens during a shift, even if the full, polished portfolio entry is written later that evening or the next day. The private note preserves the specific detail; the fuller entry can then be written slightly after the fact without losing anything important.

Seek Assessments Proactively, Don't Wait to Be Offered Them

A second high-leverage habit is treating workplace-based assessments as something the doctor actively arranges, rather than something that happens to be offered occasionally by a supervisor. In a busy clinical environment, it is genuinely easy for WBAs to simply not happen organically across a placement — not through anyone's deliberate neglect, but because a busy ward or clinic rarely pauses naturally for a structured assessment unless someone specifically asks for one. Doctors who identify a suitable case themselves, approach the relevant supervisor directly, and ask for a specific assessment type there and then, consistently accumulate WBAs faster and with less last-minute pressure than doctors who wait to be offered one.

This proactive approach also tends to produce better assessments, not just more of them. A doctor who chooses their own case for a case-based discussion can pick one that genuinely challenged their reasoning, producing a richer discussion and a more useful entry than a discussion built around whichever case happened to be convenient on a quiet afternoon.

Spread the Work Across the Whole Placement, Not the Final Weeks

A useful practical target is to treat each placement as having a rough, self-imposed monthly quota of portfolio activity — a small number of WBAs, at least one genuine reflective entry, and any CPD logging that is due — rather than treating the placement's final review date as the actual deadline for all of it. Doctors who adopt something close to a "little and often" rhythm generally find that, by the time a formal review approaches, most of the required evidence already exists, and the remaining task is simply organising and reviewing it rather than generating it from scratch under time pressure.

The following checklist summarises the habits that make portfolio-keeping sustainable rather than a recurring source of stress:

  • Write reflective entries within a day or two of the case or event, while specific detail is still fresh
  • Keep a brief private note in the moment for anything that might be reflection-worthy, even before writing the full entry
  • Proactively approach supervisors to request specific workplace-based assessments rather than waiting to be offered one
  • Choose genuinely challenging or interesting cases for case-based discussions, not just convenient ones
  • Send multi-source feedback requests to a varied group of colleagues well ahead of any deadline, not in the final fortnight
  • Log CPD, courses and e-learning with a short note on relevance immediately after completing them, alongside the certificate
  • Set a rough personal monthly quota of portfolio activity for each placement, rather than relying on a single pre-review push
  • Review portfolio progress briefly at each meeting with a supervisor, not only at the end of a placement
  • Keep language in every entry specific and genuine, describing an actual moment rather than a generic statement
  • Back up or export key entries periodically, particularly around any change of post, rotation, or training programme

Common E-Portfolio Pitfalls

A handful of recurring mistakes account for most of the portfolio-related stress and difficulty doctors encounter, and nearly all of them are considerably easier to avoid from the outset than to fix retrospectively once a review date is close.

Generic, Low-Effort Reflective Entries

The most common pitfall is writing reflective entries that are technically complete but genuinely low-value — vague, generic statements that could have been written about almost any case, with no specific detail and no real evidence of learning. A reflection that reads "I saw a patient with chest pain and learned the importance of good communication" demonstrates nothing a supervisor or ARCP panel can meaningfully assess, because it contains no specific clinical or personal detail at all. Reflections like this are usually a symptom of the entry being written too long after the event, under time pressure, rather than a genuine lack of things worth reflecting on.

A stronger reflective entry names the specific clinical dilemma or moment of uncertainty, describes what the doctor actually thought and did at the time — including anything they got wrong or would do differently — and states a concrete, specific change in practice or understanding that resulted. Supervisors and training programme directors read a very large number of reflective entries over a career, and the difference between a genuine, specific reflection and a generic, templated one is usually obvious on a first read.

Leaving Everything Until the Week Before ARCP

The second major pitfall — closely related to the first — is treating the annual review as the actual deadline for producing the whole year's evidence, rather than as a checkpoint that reviews evidence that should already largely exist. Doctors who leave portfolio-building until the final week before their review face two compounding problems: there is rarely enough time left to arrange all the outstanding workplace-based assessments a busy clinical rota can accommodate, and the reflective entries produced under that time pressure are, almost without exception, weaker than ones written promptly throughout the year.

This pattern is also visible to others. A portfolio in which every entry across an entire placement is dated within the final ten days before a review is a recognisable pattern to supervisors and panels, and it does not read the same way as a portfolio showing steady, consistent engagement spread across the whole period. Beyond the immediate stress, a rushed, incomplete portfolio is one of the more common reasons a trainee receives an outcome at ARCP other than straightforward, unqualified progression — a topic covered in full in the companion article on the annual review of competence progression process.

Losing Track of Login Details or Access Across Rotations

A more mundane but genuinely common pitfall is losing continuous access to the portfolio platform itself — particularly around a change of rotation, a change of trust, or a change of specialty, when login credentials, employer identifiers, or supervisor assignments can all shift at once. Doctors who confirm their portfolio access is working correctly at the very start of a new placement, rather than assuming it will simply carry over, avoid a scenario where weeks of a placement pass before an access issue is even noticed. This is a particularly common trap for doctors moving between a series of short-term placements rather than one continuous rotation, so it is worth understanding, in general terms, how locum and substantive NHS posts differ before assuming portfolio access and supervisor assignment will behave identically in both settings.

Treating Multi-Source Feedback as an Afterthought

Because multi-source feedback depends on other people completing a questionnaire, it is particularly vulnerable to being left too late. A doctor who only requests MSF in the final weeks before it is due often finds that colleagues — reasonably busy with their own workload — do not complete the questionnaire in time, leaving a genuine gap in the portfolio that is difficult to fill retrospectively. Requesting MSF early, and following up politely partway through the window rather than only at the deadline, avoids this almost entirely.

Not Reading Supervisor Feedback Properly

A final, easily overlooked pitfall is treating supervisor comments on WBAs and end-of-placement reports as a formality to be skimmed rather than genuine, specific feedback to act on. A supervisor's written comment often contains a precise, actionable point — a particular documentation habit to improve, a specific communication pattern to work on — and doctors who visibly act on this kind of feedback across subsequent entries tend to build a portfolio that shows real, demonstrable development over time, which is exactly the pattern both ARCP panels and, much later, revalidation recommendations are designed to recognise.

How the E-Portfolio Links to ARCP and Revalidation

An e-portfolio is not an end in itself — it exists because two separate, formal processes rely on it as their primary evidence base, and understanding how each one draws on the portfolio clarifies why the habits described above matter well beyond simply satisfying a supervisor.

The E-Portfolio and ARCP

The annual review of competence progression is the yearly assessment of whether a trainee has met the requirements of their curriculum and should progress to the next stage of training. An ARCP panel does not typically re-interview a trainee about their whole year from scratch — it reviews the trainee's completed e-portfolio directly, alongside the educational supervisor's structured end-of-year report, which is itself substantially informed by the portfolio's contents across the year. A portfolio that shows a complete, evenly-distributed set of workplace-based assessments, genuine reflective entries, and up-to-date CPD logging gives a panel a straightforward, positive picture to review. A portfolio that is incomplete, thin, or backdated in a rush shortly before the review date is one of the most common reasons a panel cannot straightforwardly confirm satisfactory progress, sometimes resulting in an outcome that requires the trainee to address specific gaps before progression is confirmed. The full detail of how ARCP panels reach their decisions, and what the different possible outcomes mean, is covered in the dedicated guide to the ARCP process.

The E-Portfolio and Revalidation

Revalidation operates on a longer cycle than ARCP — generally five-yearly rather than annual — and applies to every licensed doctor throughout their career, not only those still in formal training. For a doctor still in training, engagement with the training e-portfolio effectively substitutes for a standalone employer appraisal, since the training programme's own annual review already provides the structured check that appraisal is designed to give. The reflective habits built during training — writing prompt, genuine reflections, engaging honestly with feedback, keeping a clear record of learning and significant events — carry forward directly into the kind of supporting information a Responsible Officer reviews when making a revalidation recommendation later in a doctor's career, once they have moved into a substantive post outside formal training. The mechanics of that longer cycle, including the role of the Responsible Officer and what happens without one, are explained fully in the companion guide to the GMC revalidation process.

Put simply: the e-portfolio is the tool used continuously, week to week, throughout training; ARCP is the annual formal review that draws on it; and revalidation is the much longer-cycle process, running for the rest of a doctor's career, that the same underlying habits of honest, evidenced reflective practice ultimately feed into. A doctor who builds strong e-portfolio habits early is, without necessarily realising it at the time, also building the foundation of a straightforward revalidation record many years later.

Why This Matters Especially for IMGs

For international medical graduates starting their first UK training post or clinical role, the e-portfolio is often one of the more unfamiliar administrative systems encountered in the first few months, simply because equivalent structured, continuous portfolio systems are not universal in every country's training system. IMGs benefit in particular from asking, during induction, exactly which platform their training programme or trust uses, how to request access, and which specific workplace-based assessments and reflective entries their curriculum expects in the first few months — rather than assuming the system works the same way it did in their previous training environment. Because much of the wider pathway from the UKMLA into an NHS role and into UK training involves adjusting to systems that are procedurally different, even when the underlying clinical standards are similar, treating the e-portfolio as one of the first things to actively understand — rather than something to figure out passively over time — pays off quickly.

E-Portfolio Habits for Locum and Non-Standard Placements

Most of the guidance so far assumes a doctor working through a continuous training rotation with a single, stable educational supervisor for each placement. In practice, a meaningful number of doctors — including many IMGs in the period before securing a substantive training number, and some doctors deliberately choosing locum work between training stages — build their portfolio across a more fragmented series of short-term posts instead. This arrangement is not incompatible with good portfolio habits, but it does require a slightly different, more deliberate approach.

Why Fragmented Placements Make Proactive Habits More Important, Not Less

A doctor moving between several short locum placements, rather than one continuous six- or twelve-month rotation, has less time in any single post to build a working relationship with a supervisor, and correspondingly less time to accumulate the assessments and reflections that relationship would otherwise generate organically. This makes the proactive habits described earlier in this guide — actively requesting assessments rather than waiting to be offered them, writing reflections promptly rather than deferring them — considerably more important, not less, since there is less institutional momentum carrying the portfolio forward automatically. A doctor who understands upfront how locum and substantive NHS posts differ in terms of supervision structure and continuity is better placed to compensate deliberately for the parts a locum arrangement does not provide automatically.

Confirming Supervisory Responsibility in Short Placements

In a short locum placement, it is worth confirming, explicitly and early, who is actually responsible for signing off any workplace-based assessments completed during that specific post, since this can be less obvious than in a substantive role with a named, continuous educational supervisor. Raising this directly with the department or rota coordinator on the first day of a short placement — rather than assuming it will become clear organically — avoids a situation where a placement ends with completed clinical work but no one clearly positioned to formally assess and log it.

Keeping the Portfolio Continuous Across Multiple Employers

A doctor whose training or working history spans several different employing organisations should also be deliberate about keeping their portfolio itself continuous, even where the people supervising each placement change frequently. This generally means exporting or backing up completed entries periodically, confirming portfolio access is transferred correctly whenever a new post begins, and keeping a simple personal record — outside the portfolio system itself — of which supervisor covered which period, in case any entry later needs a query resolved. None of this is complicated, but it is easy to overlook precisely because it does not arise naturally the way it does in a single continuous post with one supervisor throughout.

Setting Up an E-Portfolio: Practical First Steps

The first few weeks in a new training post are the right time to establish good e-portfolio habits, before a busy rota makes it easy to defer.

Confirm Access and the Curriculum Structure Early

At induction, confirm exactly which e-portfolio platform applies to the specific training programme, how to log in, and — critically — how the platform is structured against the relevant curriculum, since most platforms file evidence under specific competency domains rather than a single flat timeline. Understanding this structure early makes it far easier to recognise, in the moment, which curriculum outcome a given case or piece of feedback is actually evidence for.

Identify the Placement's Assessment Targets

Most training programmes specify a rough minimum number of each assessment type expected per placement or per training year. Establishing this number early — by asking the educational supervisor directly, rather than guessing — turns an abstract expectation into a concrete personal target that can be tracked, informally, across the placement.

Schedule a Mid-Placement Portfolio Check-In

Rather than waiting until the end of a placement to review portfolio progress, a brief, informal mid-placement check — either self-directed or as part of a routine supervisor meeting — makes it much easier to spot and correct a gap (too few WBAs of a particular type, no significant event reflections yet, MSF not yet requested) while there is still time to address it comfortably, rather than discovering the gap only when a formal review is imminent.

Treat Portfolio Admin Alongside Other Ongoing GMC Obligations

An e-portfolio is one of several ongoing administrative responsibilities a licensed doctor keeps on top of throughout their career, alongside separate obligations such as paying the annual GMC retention fee that keeps a licence to practise active in the first place. None of these obligations are individually onerous, but they share a common thread with the pitfalls described earlier in this guide: they are all considerably easier to manage with a small, steady, recurring effort than with an occasional large push once a deadline is already close. A doctor who builds a habit of periodically checking their GMC Online account, their portfolio progress, and any outstanding fees together, rather than treating each as a separate, unrelated task, tends to find none of them become a source of last-minute stress.

Building Portfolio Habits Alongside Exam Preparation

For IMGs who are still working through UKMLA preparation while also settling into clinical placements or observerships, it is worth recognising that the discipline behind good portfolio habits and good exam preparation is essentially the same discipline applied to different material: steady, distributed effort rather than concentrated last-minute effort. Doctors who have already built a consistent, spaced approach to their own UKMLA revision notes and study strategy often find the transition into e-portfolio habits during training relatively natural, since both reward exactly the same "little and often" approach rather than a last-minute scramble.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an NHS e-portfolio used for?

An NHS e-portfolio is used to record and organise the ongoing evidence of a doctor's training and clinical practice — including workplace-based assessments, reflective entries, feedback, and CPD — as it happens throughout each placement. It serves as the primary evidence base reviewed at the annual training assessment and, over a much longer timeframe, contributes to the kind of supporting information used in GMC revalidation.

Do all UK doctors use the same e-portfolio platform?

No. Different training pathways use different named e-portfolio platforms — Foundation training typically uses a system provided centrally, while individual medical specialties generally run their own portfolio through their aligned royal college, and arrangements can vary further across the UK's devolved nations. A doctor should confirm the specific platform used by their own training programme at induction rather than assuming it matches a previous post.

What kinds of entries need to go into an e-portfolio?

A typical e-portfolio contains workplace-based assessments such as case-based discussions and direct observations, reflective practice entries on cases and learning, multi-source feedback from colleagues, records of courses and CPD, and structured reflections on any significant events encountered during a placement. Training programmes generally specify a minimum expected number of each type per placement or per training year.

How often should reflective entries be written?

Reflective entries are best written within a day or two of the case or event they concern, while the specific detail is still genuinely fresh, rather than reconstructed from memory weeks later. A steady, "little and often" approach across a whole placement consistently produces stronger, more specific entries than a small number written under time pressure just before a review.

What is the biggest mistake doctors make with their e-portfolio?

The two most common mistakes are writing generic, low-effort reflective entries that do not demonstrate genuine learning, and leaving the bulk of portfolio-building until the final weeks before a formal review. Both problems tend to compound each other, since entries written hastily under time pressure are almost always weaker than ones written promptly throughout the year.

How does the e-portfolio relate to ARCP?

The annual review of competence progression panel reviews a trainee's completed e-portfolio directly, alongside their educational supervisor's end-of-year report, to decide whether they have met the requirements to progress. A complete, evenly-distributed portfolio gives a panel a straightforward picture to assess, while a thin or last-minute portfolio is one of the more common reasons a panel cannot immediately confirm satisfactory progress.

Does the e-portfolio matter for GMC revalidation as well as training?

Yes. While a doctor is still in training, engagement with their training e-portfolio effectively stands in for a standalone employer appraisal, since the training programme's annual review provides an equivalent structured check. The reflective habits built during this period — prompt, honest reflection and genuine engagement with feedback — carry forward into the kind of supporting information reviewed under the GMC's longer, generally five-yearly revalidation cycle later in a doctor's career.

Should IMGs approach the e-portfolio differently to UK graduates?

The underlying expectations are the same for UK graduates and IMGs, but IMGs starting their first UK training post often find the structured, continuous portfolio system less familiar than doctors who trained through the UK undergraduate system. IMGs benefit in particular from confirming the specific platform, access process, and expected assessment numbers directly with their training programme at induction, rather than assuming the system mirrors their previous training environment.

Conclusion

An e-portfolio is, at its core, a simple idea applied consistently: capture genuine evidence of training and practice as it happens, rather than trying to reconstruct it later from memory. The doctors who find portfolio-keeping straightforward are rarely the ones with the least to record — they are the ones who write reflections promptly, request assessments proactively, and spread the work evenly across each placement rather than compressing it into a stressful pre-review scramble. Built this way, the e-portfolio stops being an administrative burden and becomes what it was always meant to be: a genuine, cumulative record of a doctor's development, useful at ARCP, useful at revalidation, and useful to the doctor themselves in seeing how far their practice has come.
