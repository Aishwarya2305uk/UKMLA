ECG interpretation for the UKMLA is one of the most consistently tested skills across both the AKT and the CPSA, because the ability to read an ECG and identify life-threatening arrhythmias, acute ischaemia, and conduction abnormalities is an essential clinical competence that every day-one doctor must possess. Many candidates find ECG interpretation daunting, but it becomes straightforward and reliable once a systematic reading approach is internalised and practised consistently across a wide range of tracings.

The Systematic ECG Reading Approach

Every ECG should be read in the same order every time. A systematic approach prevents the common error of jumping straight to an obvious abnormality and missing a second diagnosis. The ten-step approach covers: rate, rhythm, axis, P waves, PR interval, QRS complex, ST segment, T waves, QT interval, and overall impression.

Step 1 — Rate

Normal heart rate is 60 to 100 bpm. Bradycardia is below 60 bpm; tachycardia is above 100 bpm. Two methods for rate calculation: the 300 method (for regular rhythms) — count the number of large squares between two consecutive R waves, then divide 300 by that number (300 divided by 1 large square = 300 bpm; 300 divided by 2 = 150 bpm; 300 divided by 3 = 100 bpm; 300 divided by 4 = 75 bpm; 300 divided by 5 = 60 bpm; 300 divided by 6 = 50 bpm). The 1500 method — count the number of small squares between consecutive R waves and divide 1500 by that number (more accurate for slower rates). For irregular rhythms, count the number of QRS complexes in a ten-second rhythm strip and multiply by six.

Step 2 — Rhythm

Is the rhythm regular or irregular? Use the "march out" method: place a piece of paper against the ECG strip, mark three consecutive R wave peaks, then move the paper along to check if subsequent R waves align with the marks. Regular rhythms include sinus rhythm, SVT, VT, atrial flutter with a fixed block, and complete heart block (regular P waves and regular QRS complexes at separate rates). Irregularly irregular rhythms: atrial fibrillation (the hallmark). Regularly irregular rhythms: Wenckebach (Mobitz type 1) — progressive lengthening of PR then dropped beat, creating a grouped pattern.

Is the complex narrow or broad? Narrow complex (QRS below 120 ms or below 3 small squares): supraventricular in origin — impulse travels normally through the His-Purkinje system. Broad complex (QRS 120 ms or above): either aberrant conduction (bundle branch block), accessory pathway conduction, or ventricular origin (VT or VF).

Step 3 — Axis

Normal axis is minus 30 to plus 90 degrees. Left axis deviation (LAD) is minus 30 to minus 90 degrees. Right axis deviation (RAD) is plus 90 to plus 180 degrees. Rapid axis assessment using leads I and aVF: if both are positive (upward deflection), the axis is normal. If lead I is positive but aVF is negative, the axis is in the left upper quadrant — check lead II: if lead II is also negative, LAD is confirmed (beyond minus 30 degrees). If lead I is negative and aVF is positive, RAD is present. If both are negative, the axis is in the "no man's land" (extreme axis deviation) — this is seen in VT. Causes of LAD: LBBB, left anterior fascicular block, inferior MI (loss of inferior forces), and hyperkalaemia. Causes of RAD: RBBB, right ventricular hypertrophy, PE (acute cor pulmonale), left posterior fascicular block, Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome with posteroseptal pathway.

Step 4 — P Waves

Normal P waves: upright (positive) in leads I, II, aVF, and V4 to V6; inverted in aVR. Duration below 0.12 seconds (below 3 small squares) and amplitude below 2.5 mm. P mitrale: bifid (notched) P wave in lead II, broad P wave above 120 ms, suggests left atrial enlargement (mitral valve disease). P pulmonale: tall, peaked P waves above 2.5 mm in lead II, suggests right atrial enlargement (pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale). Absent P waves: AF. Retrograde P waves (inverted P in II, III, aVF with short PR or P after QRS): junctional rhythm or AVNRT. Flutter waves (F waves) at 300 bpm with a sawtooth baseline: atrial flutter.

Step 5 — PR Interval

Normal PR interval is 0.12 to 0.20 seconds (3 to 5 small squares). Short PR (below 0.12 seconds): Wolff-Parkinson-White (accessory pathway bypassing the AV node, associated with delta wave and broad QRS), Lown-Ganong-Levine syndrome, junctional rhythm. Prolonged PR (above 0.20 seconds): first-degree heart block (fixed prolonged PR). Progressively lengthening PR with a dropped beat: Mobitz type 1 (Wenckebach) — second-degree heart block. Fixed PR with occasional dropped P waves: Mobitz type 2 — second-degree heart block, higher risk of progressing to complete heart block, requires pacing. Variable PR with no relationship between P waves and QRS complexes: complete (third-degree) heart block.

Step 6 — QRS Complex

Normal QRS duration is below 0.12 seconds. Prolonged QRS indicates LBBB, RBBB, ventricular ectopy, or ventricular tachycardia. LBBB: QRS above 120 ms, dominant S wave in V1 (W pattern in V1), monophasic broad R in V5/V6 (M pattern in V5/V6) — use the "WiLLiaM" mnemonic (W in V1, M in V5 for LBBB). RBBB: QRS above 120 ms, RSR' pattern in V1 (M or rabbit ears in V1), wide S wave in V6 and I — use the "MaRRoW" mnemonic (M in V1, W in V6 for RBBB). Delta wave: a slurred upstroke at the beginning of the QRS, seen in Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome due to pre-excitation via the accessory pathway. Pathological Q waves: Q waves above 25% of the amplitude of the subsequent R wave, or deeper than 2 mm, or wider than 1 mm — suggest previous myocardial infarction (scar tissue creates an electrically silent zone, causing the remaining myocardium to generate Q waves in the overlying leads). Septal Q waves (small Q waves in V5/V6 and I) are normal and represent septal depolarisation.

Step 7 — ST Segment

The ST segment should be isoelectric (at the same level as the TP segment baseline). ST elevation of 1 mm or more in at least two contiguous limb leads, or 2 mm or more in at least two contiguous precordial leads, represents STEMI until proved otherwise in the right clinical context. ST depression: downsloping or horizontal ST depression of 1 mm or more in two or more contiguous leads suggests myocardial ischaemia or NSTEMI. Upsloping ST depression is less specific and may be a normal variant. "Reciprocal" ST depression: seen in the leads opposite to the area of MI — for example, inferior STEMI (elevation in II, III, aVF) causes reciprocal ST depression in the high lateral leads (I, aVL). Saddle-shaped ST elevation in multiple leads without reciprocal changes: pericarditis. Concave ST elevation in V1 to V3 with right bundle branch block pattern: Brugada syndrome (type 1 — coved morphology).

Step 8 — T Waves

Normal T waves are upright in leads I, II, and V4 to V6; inverted in aVR; variable in III, V1 to V3. T wave changes: tall, peaked, narrow (tented) T waves — earliest ECG sign of hyperkalaemia (also seen in hyperacute STEMI — very tall T waves in the ischaemic territory preceding ST elevation). T wave inversion: NSTEMI or deep T wave inversion (Wellens waves — critical LAD stenosis with deeply inverted T waves in V1 to V4), right heart strain (T wave inversion V1 to V4 — PE, pulmonary hypertension), LVH strain pattern (T wave inversion in lateral leads I, aVL, V5, V6), RBBB (appropriate T wave inversion in V1 to V3). Flattened T waves: digoxin effect (combined with the characteristic reverse tick ST depression), hypokalaemia, and non-specific changes.

Step 9 — QT Interval

The QT interval is measured from the start of the QRS to the end of the T wave. It must be corrected for heart rate using the Bazett formula: QTc = QT / √(RR interval in seconds). Normal QTc is below 440 ms in men and below 460 ms in women. Prolonged QTc (above 450 ms in men, above 470 ms in women) predisposes to Torsades de Pointes (polymorphic VT). Causes of long QT: congenital (Romano-Ward syndrome, Jervell-Lange-Nielsen syndrome), electrolyte disturbance (hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypocalcaemia), drugs (amiodarone, sotalol, haloperidol, methadone, clarithromycin, fluconazole, ondansetron, domperidone — check the CREDIBLEMEDS database). Short QT (below 340 ms): hypercalcaemia, digoxin toxicity, short QT syndrome (channelopathy predisposing to VF).

Step 10 — Overall Impression

After completing steps one to nine, synthesise all findings into an overall impression. State the rate and rhythm, any axis deviation, conduction abnormalities, ischaemic changes, and any other significant findings. A complete ECG interpretation should address all of these in a structured manner — this is expected in the CPSA when a candidate is asked to "interpret this ECG" at a data interpretation station.

Common Arrhythmias

Sinus Tachycardia

Rate above 100 bpm with normal P waves before every QRS, normal PR interval, and narrow complex. It is not a primary arrhythmia but a physiological response to pain, fever, dehydration, anaemia, hyperthyroidism, pulmonary embolism, heart failure, or stimulant drug use. Treatment is of the underlying cause.

Atrial Fibrillation

AF is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia. ECG features: irregularly irregular RR intervals (no two consecutive RR intervals are the same), absence of discrete P waves (replaced by an irregular, low-amplitude, fibrillatory baseline — particularly visible in V1 as irregular F waves), and narrow QRS complexes (unless there is aberrant conduction or pre-existing bundle branch block — then broad complex AF). Rate in untreated AF is typically 100 to 180 bpm. The management of AF includes rate control and, in appropriate cases, rhythm control and anticoagulation — see the NICE guidelines section of this blog for full detail.

Atrial Flutter

Atrial flutter is a macro re-entrant circuit around the tricuspid valve ring (isthmus-dependent flutter). The atrial rate is approximately 300 bpm (one flutter wave per large square), producing the characteristic sawtooth baseline — best seen in leads II, III, aVF, and V1 as regular, negative flutter waves without an isoelectric baseline between them. The AV node blocks some impulses, producing a regular ventricular rate that is a fraction of the atrial rate: 2:1 block gives ventricular rate of approximately 150 bpm; 3:1 block gives approximately 100 bpm; 4:1 block gives approximately 75 bpm. The ventricular response in atrial flutter is usually regular. Management: rate control (as for AF), anticoagulation (same CHA2DS2-VASc criteria as AF), and radiofrequency catheter ablation of the cavo-tricuspid isthmus is the definitive curative treatment for typical flutter.

SVT — AVNRT and AVRT

Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) refers to tachycardias originating above the bundle of His. The two commonest are AVNRT (AV nodal re-entrant tachycardia) and AVRT (AV re-entrant tachycardia using an accessory pathway). Both present as a narrow complex tachycardia (unless aberrantly conducted) at a rate of 150 to 250 bpm with an abrupt onset and offset. ECG features: AVNRT — P waves are buried in or immediately after the QRS (the retrograde P wave is simultaneous with or just after the QRS, producing a pseudo-R' in V1 or pseudo-S in the inferior leads); RP interval is very short (below 70 ms). AVRT — if the accessory pathway conducts retrogradely (orthodromic AVRT), P waves appear after the QRS at a longer RP interval (above 70 ms but below PR). If the pathway conducts anterogradely (antidromic AVRT), the result is a broad complex tachycardia. Acute management of SVT: vagal manoeuvres first (Valsalva manoeuvre — modified supine, legs raised position, bear down for fifteen seconds then drop legs to supine; carotid sinus massage — only in the absence of carotid bruits or TIA history). If vagal manoeuvres fail: IV adenosine 6 mg rapid IV bolus (in a large antecubital vein, flush immediately with fast saline) — if no response, repeat with 12 mg, then 18 mg. Warn the patient of transient chest tightness, flushing, and feeling of doom. Adenosine is contraindicated in asthma (causes severe bronchospasm), in Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome with pre-excited AF (increases AV block but not pathway conduction, accelerating ventricular rate and potentially precipitating VF), and in heart transplant patients (causes prolonged heart block). If adenosine fails or is contraindicated: IV verapamil or beta-blocker.

Ventricular Tachycardia

VT is defined as three or more consecutive ventricular complexes at a rate above 100 bpm. It is a life-threatening arrhythmia and must be distinguished from SVT with aberrant conduction (which may look identical on ECG). Diagnostic criteria for VT using the Brugada criteria (apply in sequence, and VT is diagnosed at the first positive criterion): 1. Is there an absence of RS complex in all precordial leads (V1 to V6)? If yes, diagnose VT. 2. Is the RS interval (onset of R to nadir of S) greater than 100 ms in any precordial lead? If yes, diagnose VT. 3. Is there AV dissociation (P waves marching through independent of QRS complexes)? If yes, diagnose VT. 4. Do the morphology criteria for VT apply in V1 and V6? (LBBB morphology VT: in V1 — broad R wave above 30 ms, slurred or notched S wave descent; in V6 — QS or rS pattern. RBBB morphology VT: in V1 — R monophasic or qR or RS; in V6 — RS ratio less than 1). If any criterion is positive, diagnose VT; if all four are negative, diagnose SVT with aberrant conduction. Other features supporting VT: very wide QRS (above 160 ms), positive or negative concordance across precordial leads (all positive or all negative), fusion beats (between a sinus beat and a ventricular beat — intermediate morphology), and capture beats (occasional narrow complex sinus beats that break through — pathognomonic of VT).

VT management: if the patient is haemodynamically compromised (hypotension, chest pain, loss of consciousness) — immediate synchronised DC cardioversion (150 to 200 J biphasic). If haemodynamically stable — IV amiodarone 300 mg over twenty to sixty minutes, then 900 mg over twenty-four hours. IV lidocaine is an alternative for VT associated with acute MI. If VT is recurrent or incessant, consider electrophysiological study and catheter ablation. Long-term: investigate for structural heart disease (echo, MRI), consider ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator) if sustained VT with impaired LV function.

Ventricular Fibrillation

VF is characterised by a chaotic, irregular, high-amplitude, rapid oscillation with no discernible P waves or QRS complexes. It is immediately fatal without treatment. Management: call for help and start CPR immediately, attach defibrillator, deliver unsynchronised shock (150 to 360 J biphasic) as soon as defibrillator is available. Follow current Resuscitation Council UK guidelines: two minutes of CPR between shocks, adrenaline 1 mg IV every three to five minutes after the third shock, amiodarone 300 mg IV after three shocks, identify and treat reversible causes (the 4Hs and 4Ts: Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hyper-/hypokalaemia and metabolic disorders, Hypothermia; Thrombosis — coronary or pulmonary, Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins).

Heart Blocks

Complete Heart Block (Third-Degree AV Block)

Complete heart block means there is no conduction from the atria to the ventricles through the AV node. ECG: regular P waves at a normal rate (70 to 80 bpm) and regular QRS complexes at a slower escape rate (junctional escape rhythm at 40 to 60 bpm with narrow QRS, or ventricular escape rhythm at 20 to 40 bpm with broad QRS) that are completely independent of each other — AV dissociation. The PR interval varies randomly because P waves and QRS complexes have no relationship. Management: transcutaneous pacing as a bridge, then permanent pacemaker implantation. Causes: inferior MI (transient — usually resolves with reperfusion), anterior MI (permanent — requires pacing), Lyme disease, hyperkalaemia, digoxin toxicity, and post-cardiac surgery.

Wenckebach (Mobitz Type 1)

Progressive lengthening of the PR interval with each beat, until a P wave is not conducted (dropped QRS), after which the sequence restarts. The RR interval shortens progressively before the dropped beat (a subtle feature). Wenckebach is usually a benign finding, often associated with inferior MI (right coronary artery supplies the AV node) or increased vagal tone. It does not routinely require pacing unless symptomatic.

Mobitz Type 2 (Second-Degree Heart Block Type 2)

Fixed PR interval with occasional non-conducted P waves (dropped QRS complexes) without preceding PR lengthening. The PR interval is constant for every conducted beat, then a P wave fails to conduct. This is more dangerous than Wenckebach as it can progress unpredictably to complete heart block. It requires pacemaker implantation. Causes: anterior MI (damage to the His-Purkinje system), fibrocalcific conduction system disease.

Posterior STEMI is the most commonly missed STEMI pattern. The standard twelve-lead ECG shows dominant R waves in V1 to V3 (because the posterior forces are directed away from V1 to V3 leads, giving a tall R wave — the mirror image of what would be a Q wave in posterior leads), with ST depression in V1 to V3. Right-sided leads and posterior leads (V7 to V9) show the true ST elevation. Right ventricular MI commonly accompanies inferior STEMI — look for ST elevation in lead V4R (right-sided ECG). These patients are preload-dependent — avoid diuretics and nitrates, and give IV fluids to maintain cardiac output.

Pulmonary Embolism on ECG

The most common ECG finding in PE is sinus tachycardia — present in approximately 50 to 60% of cases. The classic S1Q3T3 pattern (deep S wave in lead I, Q wave in lead III, inverted T wave in lead III) is seen in approximately 20% of PE cases and represents right heart strain. Other PE-related ECG changes: new RBBB (acute right ventricular dilatation causing right bundle branch conduction delay), right axis deviation, and T wave inversion in V1 to V4 (right ventricular strain pattern). Atrial flutter or AF can be precipitated by acute right heart strain from massive PE. A normal ECG does not exclude PE.

Hyperkalaemia ECG Changes

Hyperkalaemia produces a progressive sequence of ECG changes that correlate broadly with the serum potassium level, though individual patients vary considerably. Mild hyperkalaemia (K+ 5.5 to 6.5 mmol/L): peaked (tall, narrow, tented) T waves — this is the earliest and most sensitive sign, best seen in V3 to V5. Moderate hyperkalaemia (K+ 6.5 to 7.5 mmol/L): PR interval prolongation, P wave flattening and eventual disappearance (the atria become inexcitable), widening of the QRS complex (slowing of ventricular conduction). Severe hyperkalaemia (K+ above 7.5 mmol/L): further QRS widening, the QRS and T wave merge to produce a sine wave pattern. Above 8 to 9 mmol/L: ventricular fibrillation or asystole. The progression from peaked T waves to VF can occur rapidly and without warning, particularly in patients who develop acute rises in potassium (e.g., massive haemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, AKI).

Drug Effects on the ECG

Digoxin: the characteristic digoxin effect (not toxicity) produces a down-sloping ST depression that curves gently upwards like a reverse tick or Salvador Dali moustache — best seen in lateral leads V4 to V6. It is also associated with T wave flattening or inversion, shortened QT, and occasionally PR interval prolongation. Digoxin toxicity produces a wide range of arrhythmias — classically, any arrhythmia with a slow ventricular rate (toxicity causes AV block) or a regularly irregular rhythm. Specific arrhythmias of digoxin toxicity include: complete heart block with junctional or ventricular escape, accelerated junctional rhythm with AV dissociation, ventricular bigeminy (alternating sinus beats with ventricular ectopics), and bidirectional VT (alternating QRS axis — pathognomonic of digoxin toxicity). QT-prolonging drugs (causing long QT and predisposing to Torsades de Pointes): amiodarone, sotalol, haloperidol and other antipsychotics (quetiapine, ziprasidone), erythromycin and clarithromycin, fluconazole, methadone, quinolones (ciprofloxacin), ondansetron, and domperidone. Amiodarone: the most potent QT-prolonging drug in clinical use. It also causes significant bradycardia and AV block, and produces a characteristic ECG of slow rate with prolonged QT and broad QRS.

Brugada Syndrome and Long QT Syndrome

Brugada syndrome is an inherited channelopathy (sodium channel gene SCN5A mutation in 25 to 30% of cases) that predisposes to ventricular fibrillation, particularly during fever, vagal stimulation, or at rest. The characteristic ECG pattern is a coved-type ST elevation of at least 2 mm in V1 (and sometimes V2 to V3) with a right bundle branch block morphology, spontaneously or following sodium channel blocker challenge (ajmaline or flecainide provocation test). Type 1 (coved type) is diagnostic; types 2 and 3 (saddleback patterns) are not diagnostic but may be unmasked to type 1 with provocation. Management: ICD for symptomatic patients; avoid triggering drugs (sodium channel blockers, some antiarrhythmics); advise aggressive fever management (antipyretics); quinidine is used in some patients. Congenital Long QT syndrome: Romano-Ward (autosomal dominant — LQTS 1 to 16) and Jervell-Lange-Nielsen (autosomal recessive, associated with congenital deafness — LQTS 1 and 2). QTc above 480 ms with symptoms (syncope, family history of sudden cardiac death) warrants cardiology referral. Management: avoid QT-prolonging drugs, beta-blockers (particularly for LQTS 1 and 2), left cardiac sympathetic denervation, ICD for high-risk patients.

Left and Right Ventricular Hypertrophy

Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) voltage criteria: the Sokolow-Lyon criteria are most commonly taught — S wave in V1 plus R wave in V5 or V6 greater than 35 mm. Or Cornell criteria — R in aVL plus S in V3 greater than 28 mm in men or greater than 20 mm in women. LVH strain pattern: T wave inversion in the lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6) in addition to high voltages. LVH is caused by pressure overload (hypertension, aortic stenosis). Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH): dominant R wave in V1 (R/S ratio greater than 1 in V1), right axis deviation, T wave inversion in V1 to V4 (right heart strain pattern). Causes: pulmonary hypertension, mitral stenosis, pulmonary stenosis, chronic PE.

Frequently Asked Questions About ECG Interpretation for the UKMLA

How do I distinguish VT from SVT with aberrant conduction?

Apply the Brugada criteria systematically. Features favouring VT include: QRS above 160 ms, extreme axis deviation (positive I and negative aVF, or negative in both — "no man's land"), AV dissociation, fusion or capture beats, positive or negative concordance in V1 to V6, and RBBB morphology VT (R in V1 is monophasic or has a taller left rabbit ear, R/S ratio in V6 below 1). In clinical practice, if in doubt, treat a broad complex tachycardia with haemodynamic compromise as VT and cardiovert. Adenosine can also be used diagnostically — it terminates SVT with aberrancy but does not terminate VT (and is safe to use in broad complex tachycardia if haemodynamically stable, as it may reveal flutter waves or terminate AVRT).

What is the WILLIAM MARROW mnemonic?

This mnemonic helps distinguish LBBB from RBBB. In LBBB: in V1, the QRS pattern is a W shape (the complex goes down then briefly up then down — a QS or rS pattern); in V5 or V6, the QRS pattern is an M shape (a broad, tall monophasic R, or an RR' pattern) — "WiLLiaM" (W in V1, M in V5 for Left Bundle Branch Block). In RBBB: in V1, the QRS pattern is an M shape or rabbit ears (RSR' pattern — R, then S dipping below the baseline, then a second R' or R prime); in V5 or V6, the QRS has a W shape (broad S wave gives the WS or RS morphology) — "MaRRoW" (M in V1, W in V6 for Right Bundle Branch Block).

What does AV dissociation prove?

AV dissociation — where P waves and QRS complexes are completely independent of each other — proves that the atria and ventricles are being driven by separate pacemakers. In the context of a broad complex tachycardia, AV dissociation is pathognomonic of ventricular tachycardia. It is best seen by carefully scanning the rhythm strip at a rate where P waves would be expected, looking for P waves that appear at a regular, slower rate than the ventricular rate, marching through the QRS complexes at their own independent rate.

What is the most important ECG finding to recognise immediately?

For patient safety, the two most critical ECG patterns to recognise immediately are VF (treat with immediate defibrillation) and STEMI (escalate immediately for primary PCI or thrombolysis). After these, recognising complete heart block (requires urgent pacing if symptomatic or haemodynamically compromised), Torsades de Pointes (IV magnesium, stop offending drugs), and hyperkalaemia with ECG changes (give calcium gluconate immediately) are life-saving. Do not attempt to read the ECG fully before acting on these patterns when the patient is compromised.

When is a new LBBB treated as a STEMI equivalent?

Per current ESC and NICE guidance, new LBBB in the context of typical ischaemic chest pain should be treated as a STEMI equivalent — the patient should be taken for primary PCI as rapidly as possible. This is because LBBB makes it impossible to reliably assess ST changes in the standard leads. The Sgarbossa criteria can be applied to identify STEMI in the context of LBBB: concordant ST elevation of 1 mm or more (ST elevation in the same direction as the QRS) in any lead is highly specific for MI; concordant ST depression of 1 mm or more in V1 to V3; and excessively discordant ST elevation of 5 mm or more (ST elevation opposite in direction to the QRS, which is the expected finding in LBBB — but if it is excessively large, it suggests superimposed MI).

Conclusion

ECG interpretation is a core clinical competency that every doctor needs from day one of their career, and it is tested extensively throughout the UKMLA. The key to competence is consistent use of a systematic approach — rate, rhythm, axis, P waves, PR, QRS, ST, T waves, QT, and overall impression — applied to every ECG you encounter during revision, not just those that look abnormal.
