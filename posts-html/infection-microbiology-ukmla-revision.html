Infection, Microbiology and Antimicrobials for the UKMLA: A Complete Revision Guide

Infection and microbiology permeate every specialty. The UKMLA AKT expects candidates to recognise serious infections early, apply antimicrobial stewardship principles, use appropriate investigations, and select guideline-concordant treatments. This guide covers the full infection syllabus including sepsis management, common bacterial infections, antimicrobial choices, sexually transmitted infections, tuberculosis, HIV, and the major global infections a UK doctor must recognise.

Sepsis — NICE NG51 (2016) and NICE NG253 (2024 Update)

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. It is responsible for over 44,000 deaths per year in the UK. The Sepsis-3 definition (2016) defines sepsis as suspected or confirmed infection with acute organ dysfunction, measured by a SOFA (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment) score increase of ≥2 points.

Recognition Tools

qSOFA (quick SOFA): Bedside screening tool — score 1 point each for: respiratory rate ≥22 breaths/min, altered mentation (GCS <15 or any acute confusion), systolic BP ≤100 mmHg. Score ≥2 suggests high risk of sepsis — escalate immediately. Suitable for non-ICU settings. Note: qSOFA is a screening tool, not diagnostic — it should prompt full assessment, not be used in isolation.

Septic shock (Sepsis-3 definition): Sepsis with vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg AND lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Hospital mortality >40%.

Sepsis 6 — The "Hour-1 Bundle"

The NICE NG253 (2024) update and the UK Sepsis Trust maintain the Sepsis 6 bundle — six interventions within 1 hour of recognition, divided as "take 3, give 3":

Take 3 (investigate):

  1. Blood cultures (before antibiotics where possible — minimum 2 sets peripheral, plus additional cultures if indwelling lines)
  2. Serum lactate (venous or arterial)
  3. Urinalysis (urgent — start MSU culture if indicated)

Give 3 (treat):

  1. IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (within 1 hour of recognition)
  2. IV fluid bolus 500ml 0.9% sodium chloride or Hartmann's over 15 minutes if systolic BP <90 or evidence of hypoperfusion — reassess response, further fluid guided by clinical response. NICE NG253 emphasises avoiding fluid overload — reassess after each bolus.
  3. Supplemental oxygen — target SpO₂ ≥94% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia)

Antimicrobial stewardship in sepsis: NICE 2024 emphasises that antibiotics should be reviewed at 24–48 hours with microbiology guidance and de-escalated or stopped as soon as appropriate. The mantra is "start smart, then focus." Duration should be the minimum effective course. Blood cultures must be taken before antibiotics but should not delay antibiotic administration by more than 45 minutes.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

CAP is one of the most common causes of emergency hospital admission in the UK. Caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common, ~40%), Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis, atypical organisms (Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Legionella pneumophila, Chlamydophila pneumoniae — no cell wall, not covered by beta-lactams), viruses.

Severity Assessment — CURB-65

Score 1 point each for: Confusion (new, AMTS ≤8), Urea >7 mmol/L, Respiratory rate ≥30 breaths/min, Blood pressure (systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60 mmHg), age ≥65.

  • Score 0–1: Low severity, community treatment appropriate
  • Score 2: Moderate severity, consider hospital admission
  • Score 3–5: High severity, hospital admission required; score ≥4 consider ICU

Antibiotic Choice — NICE NG138

  • Low severity (CURB-65 0–1): Amoxicillin 500mg–1g three times daily for 5 days. If allergy: doxycycline 200mg loading then 100mg daily, or clarithromycin 500mg twice daily.
  • Moderate severity (CURB-65 2): Amoxicillin 500mg–1g TDS + clarithromycin 500mg BD (to cover atypicals), or doxycycline 200mg/100mg BD as monotherapy. Oral if able to swallow.
  • High severity (CURB-65 3–5): IV co-amoxiclav 1.2g TDS + IV clarithromycin 500mg BD. If penicillin allergy: IV levofloxacin 500mg BD or IV co-trimoxazole. Legionella and pneumococcal urinary antigens.

Chest X-ray — shadowing consistent with pneumonia (consolidation, air bronchograms). Repeat CXR at 4–6 weeks for those aged >50 or smokers (exclude underlying lung malignancy).

Urinary Tract Infections

UTIs are the most common bacterial infection in adults. Escherichia coli causes 70–80% of uncomplicated UTIs.

Treatment Choices

  • Uncomplicated lower UTI (women): Nitrofurantoin 100mg modified release BD for 3 days (avoid if eGFR <30 — risk of drug accumulation and pulmonary toxicity), or trimethoprim 200mg BD for 3 days (avoid if high resistance rates or pregnant in first trimester — antifolate). Pivmecillinam as alternative.
  • Uncomplicated lower UTI (men): Treat for 7 days. Trimethoprim or nitrofurantoin. If recurrent, consider prostate pathology.
  • Uncomplicated upper UTI (pyelonephritis): Cefalexin 500mg QDS or co-amoxiclav 625mg TDS for 7–14 days. Hospitalise if severe: IV cefuroxime or co-amoxiclav. If ESBL-producing organism: ertapenem or meropenem.
  • Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI): Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria (common in catheterised patients — not clinically significant unless immunocompromised, pregnant, or pre-urological procedure). Treat only if symptomatic (fever, rigors, loin pain, new confusion, worsening renal function). Change catheter if been in place >7 days. Antibiotic choice guided by local sensitivities.

Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

Cellulitis

Infection of dermis and subcutaneous tissue, usually caused by Group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes) or Staphylococcus aureus. Classifies as non-purulent (streptococcal, most common) or purulent (more likely S. aureus). Treatment: flucloxacillin 500mg–1g QDS for 5–7 days (covers both organisms). Oral for mild/moderate; IV flucloxacillin for severe (Eron class III–IV), systemic features, or failure of oral treatment. Add MRSA cover (vancomycin, daptomycin) if: purulent, failed prior antibiotic treatment, recent hospitalisation, or known MRSA colonisation. Limb elevation and mark extent of erythema to monitor progress.

Necrotising Fasciitis

Rapidly spreading life-threatening infection of deep fascia and subcutaneous tissue. Two types: Type 1 (polymicrobial — mixed aerobic/anaerobic, in diabetics, elderly, immunocompromised, post-abdominal surgery) and Type 2 (mononicrobial — Group A Streptococcus — commonest in previously healthy individuals). Features suggesting NF: pain disproportionate to appearance, skin blistering, crepitus (gas in tissue), dark/purple discolouration, numbness over erythema (anaesthesia — nerve necrosis), rapid spread. LRINEC score (Laboratory Risk Indicator for Necrotising Fasciitis) uses CRP, WBC, Hb, sodium, glucose, creatinine. Diagnosis confirmed intraoperatively — friable grey necrotic fascia, failure of normal tissue planes to dissect, dishwater fluid.

Management: Surgical emergency — immediate and aggressive surgical debridement is the only life-saving treatment. Delay increases mortality significantly. Simultaneously: broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin + IV vancomycin for MRSA risk), ITU admission, repeat surgery every 24–48 hours until clean margins achieved. Mortality 25–35% even with optimal treatment.

Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) — NICE NG199 (2021)

C. difficile is the most common cause of healthcare-associated diarrhoea in the UK. Spore-forming gram-positive anaerobic rod. Produces toxins A and B which damage the colonic mucosa causing pseudomembranous colitis. Classic presentation: profuse watery diarrhoea ≥3 loose stools/day after antibiotic use.

Risk Factors

  • Recent broad-spectrum antibiotic use (highest risk: clindamycin, cephalosporins, quinolones, co-amoxiclav)
  • PPIs (alter gut pH and microbiome)
  • Age >65
  • Hospitalisation
  • Immunocompromise
  • Recent gastrointestinal surgery

Diagnosis

Stool toxin PCR (glutamate dehydrogenase antigen + toxin immunoassay ± NAAT for clarification). Do NOT test formed stool. Do NOT test during or immediately after treatment (persistence of positive test does not indicate ongoing infection).

Treatment — NICE NG199

  • First episode: Oral vancomycin 125mg QDS for 10 days. (Metronidazole is no longer first-line.) Stop precipitating antibiotics where possible.
  • First recurrence: Fidaxomicin 200mg BD for 10 days (preferred over repeat vancomycin — reduces recurrence rate). Bezlotoxumab (monoclonal antibody against toxin B) can be given alongside antibiotics to reduce recurrence risk in high-risk patients.
  • Multiple recurrences or severe refractory disease: Faecal microbiota transplant (FMT) — recommended by NICE, highly effective (cure rate ~80–90%).
  • Severe C. diff (WBC >15, creatinine rising, colitis features): Oral vancomycin 500mg QDS ± IV metronidazole 500mg TDS. Surgical input if megacolon, perforation, or failure to improve.

Infection Control

Spores are NOT killed by alcohol gel — soap and water handwashing is essential. Gloves and aprons for contact. Dedicated toilet. Thorough environmental cleaning with chlorine-based disinfectants. Avoid antidiarrhoeal drugs (mask severity, may worsen toxic megacolon).

MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus)

MRSA carries the mecA gene encoding a modified penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a) that is not inhibited by beta-lactam antibiotics. Spread by contact — hand hygiene is the primary prevention measure.

Screening: NHS policy — all elective admissions screened (nose, throat, groin swabs). Emergency admissions screened at admission. Positive carriers decolonised before elective surgery.

Decolonisation: Mupirocin 2% nasal ointment TDS for 5 days, chlorhexidine 4% body wash daily for 5 days, chlorhexidine mouthwash.

Treatment of MRSA infection: IV vancomycin (monitoring trough levels/AUC-guided dosing, nephrotoxic) or IV teicoplanin (glycopeptides). Daptomycin (lipopeptide) — good for skin/soft tissue and bacteraemia (not effective for pneumonia — inactivated by pulmonary surfactant). Linezolid (oxazolidinone) — oral bioavailability excellent, good for MRSA pneumonia and bone/joint infections; risk of thrombocytopaenia, serotonin syndrome with SSRIs, optic neuropathy with prolonged use. Ceftaroline — fifth-generation cephalosporin active against MRSA.

PrEP and PEP

PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis): Tenofovir/emtricitabine (Truvada or Descovy). Daily or event-based regimen. Highly effective (>99%) at preventing HIV acquisition when taken correctly. Available on NHS for eligible individuals at substantial HIV risk.

PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis): Must be started within 72 hours of exposure (earlier = more effective). Standard regimen: tenofovir/emtricitabine + raltegravir or dolutegravir, for 28 days. Available from sexual health clinics, A&E, GUM clinics. Efficacy declines rapidly — ineffective if >72 hours post-exposure.

Tuberculosis — NICE NG33 (2024)

TB remains the world's leading cause of death from a single infectious agent. In the UK, incidence is higher in urban areas and in migrants from high-burden countries.

Active TB — RIPE Regimen

Standard four-drug initial phase for 2 months followed by two-drug continuation phase for 4 months (total 6 months for pulmonary TB):

  • Rifampicin (RIF): Most potent anti-TB drug. Induces hepatic enzymes — many drug interactions (reduces efficacy of oral contraceptive pill, anticoagulants, antiretrovirals). Turns body fluids (urine, tears, sweat) orange-red. Hepatotoxic.
  • Isoniazid (INH): Bactericidal. Peripheral neuropathy (prevents by prescribing pyridoxine/vitamin B6 with it). Hepatotoxic. SIADH. Check for slow vs fast acetylator status.
  • Pyrazinamide (PZA): Active in acidic environment of macrophages. Hepatotoxic. Hyperuricaemia → gout flares. Arthralgia.
  • Ethambutol (EMB): Optic neuritis — colour vision loss, reduced visual acuity. Check baseline visual acuity and colour vision. Dose reduce in renal impairment.

Continuation phase (months 3–6): rifampicin + isoniazid. Total 6 months. Extend to 9 months for CNS TB. Monitor LFTs monthly for first 2 months.

Latent TB Infection (LTBI)

NICE NG33 recommends screening all new entrants from high-incidence countries. Interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) is preferred in the UK (QuantiFERON-TB Gold or T-SPOT.TB). Mantoux test used in children under 5. Treatment: 3 months isoniazid + rifampicin, or 6 months isoniazid alone, or 3 months rifampicin alone. Screen before biologic therapy.

Contact Tracing

Identify close contacts of smear-positive (infectious) pulmonary TB. Public Health England (now UKHSA) responsibilities. Household contacts screened with IGRA and CXR.

BCG Vaccination

BCG (Bacillus Calmette-Guérin) is offered in the UK to: all neonates in areas of high TB incidence (London boroughs), neonates whose parents/grandparents were born in high-incidence countries (>40/100,000), previously unvaccinated individuals under 35 who are new entrants from high-incidence countries, and healthcare workers. Not offered routinely to all UK children (unlike some countries).

Neutropenic Sepsis — NICE CG151

Defined as neutrophil count <0.5 ×10⁹/L (or <1.0 and expected to fall) with temperature >38°C or clinical signs of sepsis in a patient receiving anticancer treatment. Medical emergency — high mortality without rapid treatment.

  • Start IV piperacillin-tazobactam (Tazocin) within 1 hour of recognition. If penicillin allergic: meropenem or ceftazidime + gentamicin.
  • Blood cultures (2 sets peripheral + central line), FBC, U&E, LFTs, LDH, CRP, CXR.
  • Add MRSA cover (teicoplanin) if indwelling central catheter infection suspected.
  • Antifungal (posaconazole/fluconazole/micafungin) if fever persistent >4–5 days on antibiotics or known fungal colonisation.
  • G-CSF (granulocyte colony-stimulating factor — filgrastim) — consider if severe or prolonged neutropaenia. Reduces duration but not mortality in most standard-risk patients.

HIV — Antiretroviral Therapy and Opportunistic Infections

WHO Staging and ART

All patients diagnosed with HIV should be offered ART regardless of CD4 count (U=U — Undetectable = Untransmittable — preventing onward transmission). Standard first-line ART: 2 NRTIs + 1 INSTI (integrase strand transfer inhibitor). Preferred: tenofovir alafenamide (TAF)/emtricitabine + bictegravir (or dolutegravir). Efavirenz is no longer first-line (neuropsychiatric side effects).

CD4 Count Thresholds for Opportunistic Infection (OI) Prophylaxis

  • CD4 <200 cells/µL: Start PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia) prophylaxis — co-trimoxazole (trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole) one double-strength tablet daily. Alternative: dapsone + pyrimethamine, or nebulised pentamidine monthly.
  • CD4 <100 cells/µL: Toxoplasma prophylaxis — co-trimoxazole (same as PCP). MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex) prophylaxis — azithromycin weekly.
  • CD4 <50 cells/µL: CMV retinitis prophylaxis (valganciclovir) in high-risk. Cryptococcal meningitis prophylaxis in high-prevalence settings.

PCP Pneumonia

Subacute onset in HIV with CD4 <200: progressive dyspnoea on exertion, dry cough, low-grade fever, desaturation with exercise. CXR — bilateral perihilar infiltrates (or normal in early disease). LDH elevated. BAL with Gomori methenamine silver stain or immunofluorescence. High-resolution CT: ground-glass opacification. Treatment: high-dose co-trimoxazole 120mg/kg/day in divided doses for 21 days. Add prednisolone if PaO₂ <9.3 kPa (reduces inflammatory response as P. jirovecii dies).

Malaria

Must be considered in any febrile patient returning from a malaria-endemic area. ABCDE for returning traveller: A — Aware of risk, B — Bitten less (insect repellent, treated nets, clothing), C — Chemoprophylaxis, D — Diagnose promptly, E — Emergency treatment.

Plasmodium falciparum: most dangerous species, causes cerebral malaria, severe anaemia, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), AKI, hypoglycaemia, coagulopathy. Definitive diagnosis: thick and thin blood films ×3 (need three negative films 12–24 hours apart to exclude). Rapid antigen test (RDT). PCR for species identification and resistance. Treatment of uncomplicated falciparum malaria: artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) — artemether/lumefantrine (Riamet) or artesunate/amodiaquine. Severe/complicated falciparum: IV artesunate (preferred over IV quinine).

Non-falciparum (vivax, ovale): chloroquine sensitive (mostly). P. vivax and P. ovale have liver hypnozoites causing relapse — primaquine after chloroquine (test G6PD before prescribing). P. malariae: chloroquine. P. knowlesi: chloroquine or ACT.

FAQ: Infection and Microbiology for the UKMLA

What are the Sepsis 6 interventions and within what timeframe?

The Sepsis 6 bundle should be completed within 1 hour of recognition. Take 3: blood cultures (2 sets), serum lactate, urinalysis. Give 3: IV broad-spectrum antibiotics, IV fluid bolus 500ml crystalloid (if hypotensive or evidence of hypoperfusion), supplemental oxygen targeting SpO₂ ≥94%. NICE NG253 (2024) emphasises reassessment after each fluid bolus to avoid fluid overload, and de-escalation of antibiotics at 24–48 hours based on culture results.

What is the first-line treatment for C. difficile according to NICE NG199 2021?

Oral vancomycin 125mg four times daily for 10 days is first-line for a first episode of C. difficile infection — metronidazole is no longer recommended as first-line. For a first recurrence, fidaxomicin 200mg twice daily for 10 days is preferred as it significantly reduces the risk of further recurrence compared to repeat vancomycin. Faecal microbiota transplant (FMT) is recommended for multiple recurrences.

Why is alcohol gel ineffective against C. difficile?

Clostridioides difficile forms spores that are highly resistant to alcohol-based hand sanitisers. Alcohol gels denature proteins but cannot penetrate the spore coat. Physical removal with soap and water — mechanical action of scrubbing — is required to reduce spore transmission from hands. This is why soap and water handwashing (not gel) is mandatory for HCWs before and after contact with C. difficile patients or their environment.

What is the management of necrotising fasciitis?

Necrotising fasciitis is a surgical emergency. Management requires immediate surgical debridement of all necrotic tissue — this is the primary life-saving intervention and must not be delayed for investigation or CT scanning if clinical diagnosis is clear. Simultaneously: broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin ± vancomycin), ITU admission, and repeated surgical review every 24–48 hours until clean wound margins are achieved. Hyperbaric oxygen is used as adjunct in some centres but evidence is limited.

What is the RIPE regimen for TB and what are the main side effects of each drug?

RIPE: Rifampicin (hepatotoxicity, enzyme induction — reduces OCP and anticoagulant efficacy, orange body fluids), Isoniazid (peripheral neuropathy — prevent with pyridoxine, hepatotoxicity, SIADH), Pyrazinamide (hepatotoxicity, hyperuricaemia, arthralgia), Ethambutol (optic neuritis — monitor visual acuity and colour vision, dose reduce in renal impairment). Initial phase: all 4 drugs for 2 months. Continuation phase: rifampicin + isoniazid for 4 months (6 months total for pulmonary TB; 9 months for CNS TB).

When should PEP be started and what does it consist of?

PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis) for HIV must be started within 72 hours of exposure — the earlier the better, ideally within 24 hours. It consists of tenofovir/emtricitabine (2-in-1 tablet) plus raltegravir or dolutegravir, taken for 28 days. PEP is available from emergency departments, sexual health clinics, and GUM clinics. It is highly effective if started promptly but reduces in efficacy rapidly after 24 hours and is not effective after 72 hours.

Conclusion

Infection and microbiology in the UKMLA requires both breadth and clinical precision. The key skills are: recognising sepsis and initiating the Sepsis 6 bundle within 1 hour; selecting the correct antibiotic for the commonest infections (using current NICE guidance); knowing when NOT to treat (asymptomatic bacteriuria, C. diff during treatment); and understanding antimicrobial stewardship principles that underpin all prescribing decisions.

Pay particular attention to the recently updated NICE NG253 (2024) sepsis guidance, NICE NG199 (2021) C. difficile management, and BASHH 2024/2025 STI guidelines — these are high-yield given their recency and clinical relevance across all settings.
