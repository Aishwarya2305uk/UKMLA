UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors is fast becoming one of the more closely watched international routes to UK medical registration, as a growing number of Kenya Medical Practitioners and Dentists Council (KMPDC)-registered doctors, alongside colleagues trained in Uganda and Tanzania, look at the NHS as a realistic next step in their careers. Kenyan medical education has a strong international reputation, English is the language of instruction and examination throughout Kenyan medical school, and the country's diaspora presence in the UK is already well established outside medicine — all of which makes the pathway genuinely achievable, provided the practical sequence of credential verification, exams, and registration is planned properly from the outset.

This guide walks through the entire route specifically as it applies to doctors trained in Kenya, with additional context throughout for colleagues qualified in Uganda and Tanzania, since the three East African Community countries share broadly similar medical training structures and face similar practical questions about test centre access, document verification, and cost. It covers how a Kenyan MBChB is assessed for GMC eligibility, why Kenyan candidates may be well placed for the GMC's English language exemption route, where the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) can realistically be sat within the East Africa region, why the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) still requires a trip to the UK, typical costs in US dollars and pounds sterling with a hedge on Kenyan shilling conversion, and a realistic month-by-month timeline from graduation to an NHS post.

Kenya's relationship with UK medicine is not new. Kenyan-trained doctors have moved into NHS posts for decades, Kenyan postgraduate medical training has long referenced UK curricula and Royal College examinations, and professional and alumni networks connecting Kenyan doctors already working in the UK with those still at home are well developed. What has changed more recently is the exam structure itself — the shift from the old PLAB system to the UKMLA — and this guide sets out exactly how that updated pathway works for Kenyan and wider East African candidates today.

The UKMLA Route From Kenya and East Africa: A Quick Overview

Before working through each stage in detail, it is worth seeing the whole pathway at a glance. Understanding UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors as a single, sequential process — rather than a loose collection of separate exams and forms — is the single most useful mental model for planning realistically. For a Kenyan-trained doctor — and, with minor variations in the verifying body, for doctors trained in Uganda or Tanzania — the route generally runs as follows:

  1. Confirm eligibility: verify that your MBChB from a KMPDC-recognised Kenyan medical school (or the equivalent body in Uganda or Tanzania) is accepted by the GMC, and that you hold full, unrestricted registration following your internship year.
  2. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your medical qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials).
  3. Establish whether you qualify for the GMC's English language exemption or alternative-evidence route, given that Kenyan medical education is conducted and examined almost entirely in English — or, if not, sit IELTS Academic or OET Medicine.
  4. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT (the PLAB 1 equivalent for international medical graduates), sat at a Pearson VUE test centre, with realistic options now available within the East Africa region.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA (the PLAB 2 equivalent), which can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.
  6. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise once both assessments are passed and every supporting document is verified.
  7. Apply for NHS posts — typically trust-grade or clinical fellow roles — and begin practising in the UK.

Each stage carries its own processing time, cost, and common source of delay, covered in detail below. The single biggest planning error Kenyan and East African candidates make is treating this as a purely academic challenge — assuming that passing two demanding exams is the hard part, when in practice credential verification, document logistics, and the mandatory UK trip for the CPSA are what actually determine how long the whole journey takes. Running the administrative track and the exam preparation track in parallel, rather than one after the other, consistently produces the fastest realistic route to registration.

KMPDC Recognition and UKMLA Eligibility for Kenyan Doctors

The starting point for any Kenyan doctor considering UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors as a realistic pathway is confirming eligibility against the GMC's core requirements. The GMC requires that your primary medical qualification comes from a school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS), and that the qualifying degree is recognised by your national regulator — in Kenya's case, the Kenya Medical Practitioners and Dentists Council (KMPDC). Graduates of well-established Kenyan medical schools such as the University of Nairobi, Moi University, Kenyatta University, Egerton University, and other KMPDC-recognised institutions generally satisfy this first eligibility hurdle without difficulty, provided the school held valid accreditation at the time of graduation.

Beyond the medical school itself, the GMC also expects evidence of your completed internship year and full, unrestricted KMPDC registration rather than provisional or temporary licensing status. A Certificate of Good Standing confirming your registration status and disciplinary record is typically required, and this must be requested directly from KMPDC — a step that candidates in Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania alike commonly leave too late, since national regulatory bodies across the region can take several weeks to process such requests during busy periods.

Full detail on who is permitted to sit the exam, including rules for degrees awarded through non-standard timeframes or routes, is set out in the GMC's guidance on confirming who can sit the exam. It is worth reading this in full before committing time and money, since eligibility for some qualification types is assessed case by case. For the broader requirements applying across all international medical graduates, see the site's guide to UKMLA eligibility requirements.

Primary Source Verification via EPIC

Once basic eligibility is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of your Kenyan medical degree through EPIC — meaning your qualification is confirmed directly with your medical school rather than accepted on a certificate alone. Kenyan medical schools vary in how quickly they respond to EPIC requests; the larger, longer-established faculties with dedicated registrar or academic affairs offices tend to respond within four to ten weeks, while smaller or newer institutions can take three months or more, particularly around examination periods.

Full detail on how primary source verification works, what documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting evidence is available directly from the regulator at the GMC's guide to full registration for international medical graduates. Candidates are strongly encouraged to read this guidance in full before initiating an EPIC application, since incomplete submissions remain one of the most common causes of avoidable delay.

Uganda and Tanzania: How the Regional Picture Differs

Doctors trained in Uganda work through the Uganda Medical and Dental Practitioners Council, while Tanzanian graduates are verified through the Medical Council of Tanganyika (operating within Tanzania's wider Medical, Dental and Allied Health Professionals framework). The underlying GMC assessment — WDOMS listing, primary source verification through EPIC, evidence of internship completion, and a Certificate of Good Standing from the relevant national council — is structurally identical across all three East African Community countries, even though the verifying body's name and typical response time differ. Makerere University's medical school in Kampala and Tanzania's Muhimbili University of Health and Allied Sciences are both well-established, WDOMS-listed institutions whose graduates commonly pursue the same UKMLA route as their Kenyan counterparts, and much of the guidance in this article — on English language evidence, fees, test centre access, and timeline — applies to them in essentially the same way.

What KMPDC Recognition Actually Confirms for GMC Purposes

It is worth being precise about what "KMPDC-recognised" means in GMC terms, since the two regulators are assessing different things. KMPDC licensing confirms your entitlement to practise within the Kenyan health system; it does not, by itself, transfer any recognition or rights in the UK. The GMC instead looks at three separate elements: whether your medical school appears on WDOMS for your specific graduation year, whether the programme met minimum duration and curriculum standards, and whether primary evidence — degree certificate, full transcripts, and internship completion documentation — stands up to independent verification through EPIC. A doctor can hold entirely valid KMPDC registration and still face delays at the GMC stage if, for instance, a medical school's WDOMS listing lapsed for a period, or academic transcripts are incomplete. Checking your medical school's current WDOMS status before starting the process is a five-minute task that can prevent weeks of confusion later.

Documents Kenyan and East African Candidates Should Gather Early

Beyond the primary degree certificate, most candidates from Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania need a broadly consistent set of supporting documents, and gathering them in parallel rather than sequentially saves considerable time:

  • MBChB degree certificate and full academic transcripts, year by year, from your medical school.
  • Internship completion certificate confirming you completed the mandatory supervised internship year required for full registration.
  • Certificate of Good Standing from KMPDC (or the Uganda or Tanzania equivalent), confirming registration status and disciplinary history.
  • International passport, valid well beyond your expected registration date.
  • Evidence of any name changes — marriage certificates or statutory declarations — where degree and passport names differ.
  • Certified translations of any document not already issued in English, from a recognised professional translation service — a step that applies to relatively few Kenyan applicants given the English-medium nature of Kenyan medical education, but which still applies to some supporting civil documents.

Requesting the Certificate of Good Standing early is particularly important, since it is issued by a separate national body from your medical school and follows its own, often slower, administrative timeline than EPIC verification itself.

English Language Requirements: Why Kenyan Candidates Are Often Well Placed

English is the official language of instruction and assessment throughout Kenyan medical education, from pre-clinical lectures through clinical rotations and final qualifying examinations. This is a genuinely significant advantage for anyone researching UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors, because it may make many candidates eligible for the GMC's English language exemption or an alternative-evidence route, rather than requiring a fresh IELTS or OET sitting purely to prove a proficiency they already use every working day.

The GMC's exemption framework recognises, among other grounds, doctors whose primary medical qualification was taught and examined entirely in English at a recognised institution within a defined recent period before the registration application, provided a sufficient proportion of clinical interaction during training was also conducted in English. Kenyan medical schools generally satisfy the language-of-instruction element clearly, since Kenyan clinical practice itself is conducted in English in hospital settings, even where Kiswahili or a local language is used in some patient-facing community contexts. However, this route is evidentially demanding rather than automatic: it typically requires a formal, purpose-specific letter from the medical school confirming both the language of instruction and the proportion of clinical interaction conducted in English, and it is time-limited to qualifications obtained within a set recent window before the application. The full detail of which grounds apply, what documentary evidence each requires, and how the GMC's case-by-case assessment actually works is set out in the dedicated guide to the GMC English language exemption for IMGs, which every Kenyan and East African candidate should read carefully before assuming an exemption will apply rather than confirming it against their own specific circumstances.

Candidates who do not clearly satisfy an exemption ground — for example, those who graduated some years ago, or whose medical school cannot readily produce the specific confirmatory letter required — should plan for standard English testing rather than delaying preparation while an exemption claim is assessed. Two tests are accepted: IELTS Academic, where the GMC requires an overall score of 7.0, with no individual component below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the GMC requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. Both tests are available at test centres in Nairobi, and OET in particular tends to suit practising doctors well, since its scenarios are built entirely around healthcare communication rather than general academic topics.

Both test certificates carry a defined validity period from the date of sitting, so sequencing matters: sitting a test very early in the process risks the certificate expiring before the full registration application is submitted, which would force a costly and time-consuming re-sit. Full detail on accepted scores, accepted providers, and how evidence is submitted is set out directly by the regulator at the GMC's English language evidence requirements, which should always be checked directly rather than relied upon from a general summary, since specific evidentiary rules are reviewed periodically.

How to Apply for the UKMLA From Kenya: Step by Step

With eligibility confirmed, the practical application process follows six sequential steps, described below in the order they should typically be actioned.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

Registration begins with creating a GMC Online account through the GMC's official website. This becomes the central hub for the entire process — tracking EPIC verification status, booking both the AKT and CPSA, submitting English language evidence (or an exemption claim), and eventually completing the registration application itself. Use a personal email address you will retain long-term rather than a university or hospital account, since GMC Online correspondence for Kenyan and East African candidates commonly spans well over a year.

Step 2: Begin EPIC Verification Early

As covered above, EPIC verification of your MBChB is usually the single longest step in the entire pathway. Start this as early as possible — ideally before finalising an English language exemption claim or booking exam dates — because it runs in parallel with other preparation and requires little active involvement once submitted, beyond following up with your medical school if there are delays. Keep a written record of every communication with your medical school's registrar, since this becomes useful evidence if a delayed response needs escalating.

Step 3: Establish Your English Language Position

In parallel with EPIC verification, work out whether you have a realistic case for the GMC's English language exemption or alternative-evidence route, given Kenyan medical education's English-medium structure, and gather the specific documentary evidence — typically a formal letter from your medical school — that this route requires. If an exemption claim looks uncertain or the required letter cannot be produced promptly, book an IELTS Academic or OET Medicine sitting in parallel rather than waiting for a decision, since this avoids losing months to an exemption claim that does not ultimately succeed.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once your GMC Online account is active, book the AKT through the Pearson VUE booking system linked from your account. Booking windows typically open several months ahead of each testing period, and available regional dates can fill quickly, so book as soon as a suitable slot appears. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines exactly what conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas are examinable, alongside a structured question bank — see the guidance on choosing a good AKT question bank for a comparison of the main options available to East African candidates.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once the AKT has been passed. Because the CPSA is only available in Manchester, Kenyan, Ugandan, and Tanzanian candidates alike need to plan a UK trip around this stage, factoring in visa arrangements, flights, and several days of accommodation for final preparation immediately before the assessment. Many candidates combine the trip with a short period of UK clinical observation or shadowing beforehand, which helps with the specific communication and consultation style the CPSA examines.

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and EPIC verification, English language evidence (or an approved exemption), and any outstanding documents are confirmed, submit the full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage is available in the site's GMC registration process guide, covering document checklists, processing times, and what to do if an application is queried.

UKMLA AKT Test Centre Access in East Africa

A recurring practical question about UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors is where the AKT can actually be sat, and one of the most practically important developments for Kenyan candidates in recent years has been the expansion of Pearson VUE's own testing footprint within the region. Pearson VUE — the company that delivers the AKT worldwide on the GMC's behalf — opened a dedicated, company-owned Pearson Professional Center in Nairobi, based in Westlands, built specifically to handle high-stakes professional and licensing examinations at scale. This sits alongside a number of long-standing authorised Pearson VUE testing partners already operating in Nairobi. Together, this means Kenyan candidates now have realistic access to sit the AKT without leaving the country, in contrast to the situation some years ago when regional test centre access for high-stakes medical licensing exams was far more limited.

For Ugandan and Tanzanian candidates, Pearson VUE-authorised testing centres also operate in Kampala and Dar es Salaam respectively, though as with any Pearson VUE location, candidates should always confirm current availability for the specific AKT testing window directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal, since which centres are actively open for which exam programme, and on which dates, can change between sitting periods. Candidates in Uganda or Tanzania who find no suitable local slot for a given window should also check availability in Nairobi as a realistic regional alternative, since it is generally a shorter and less expensive trip than travelling to the UK or elsewhere.

This regional AKT access stands in sharp contrast to the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in Kenya, Uganda, or Tanzania, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — it is held exclusively at the GMC's purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. This means every East African candidate must, at minimum, make one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway, even if the AKT is passed entirely from Nairobi, Kampala, or Dar es Salaam without international travel. Budgeting for this single mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visitor visa, accommodation, and living costs for the preparation and exam period — should be built into financial planning from the very start of the process, not left until after the AKT is passed.

UKMLA Exam Cost for Kenyan and East African Candidates

Cost is one of the most frequently searched practical questions around UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors, and it is consistently one of the most practical concerns for candidates across the region, both because the fees themselves are substantial in absolute terms and because East African currencies — the Kenyan shilling in particular — have shown meaningful volatility against the pound in recent years. Because so much of this budget is denominated in pounds while income and savings across the region are typically held in local currency, many candidates choose to build their fund gradually over several months rather than attempting a single large currency purchase close to a payment deadline. Spreading currency purchases over time, using a specialist international transfer provider rather than a standard bank counter rate, and tracking the mid-market rate rather than relying on a single bureau de change quotation are all practical ways to reduce the effective cost of converting shillings to pounds across the pathway.

Life and Career for Kenyan and East African Doctors in the NHS After Registration

Once GMC registration is secured, Kenyan doctors join a well-established East African diaspora community within the NHS. Kenyan-trained doctors have practised in the UK for decades, and Kenya, alongside Uganda and Tanzania, remains a consistent source of internationally trained doctors on the UK medical register. This means new arrivals typically find peer networks, informal mentorship, and professional associations already in place to help with the practical realities of settling into UK practice.

Career progression after registration generally follows one of several routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post (the most common entry point for newly registered international medical graduates, offering Foundation Year 2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place), a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK experience and portfolio evidence are in place, or locum work through NHS staff banks and agencies for those wanting flexibility while building a UK clinical track record. Many Kenyan and East African doctors who enter through clinical fellow posts subsequently progress into core or specialty training within two to three years, particularly in specialties experiencing workforce shortages such as general internal medicine, psychiatry, and emergency medicine, where competition ratios for international medical graduate applicants tend to be more favourable.

It is worth noting that the broad shape of this journey — degree recognition, credential verification, an English language decision, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam, then registration — mirrors the equivalent route for doctors trained in Nigeria and the routes covered for other African and international candidates in the site's round-up guide to country-specific IMG routes, even though the specific verification bodies, English language position, and typical processing times differ between countries.

Day-to-day life for newly registered Kenyan and East African doctors in the NHS typically involves an adjustment period covering both clinical and cultural dimensions. Clinically, the biggest early adjustments tend to be around NHS documentation standards (structured handover formats such as SBAR), a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary team working, and a more codified approach to consent and capacity than many candidates are accustomed to from East African clinical practice. Socially, Kenyan doctors moving to the UK are joining an established and growing East African professional community in the NHS, with active alumni networks from Kenyan, Ugandan, and Tanzanian medical schools, church and community networks, and informal mentorship arrangements in most major NHS regions, particularly London, Manchester, Birmingham, and other cities with substantial East African populations. Salary progression follows the standard NHS pay scale for the relevant grade, and while it can take time to reach specialty training, most East African doctors who remain committed to the pathway report that the structured, competency-based nature of UK training compares favourably with the less standardised progression routes available in some parts of the region.

Common Mistakes Kenyan and East African Doctors Make on This Route

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among candidates from Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania pursuing the UKMLA pathway. Most of these mistakes are not clinical — they are planning and administrative errors that have nothing to do with medical knowledge, yet they are responsible for a disproportionate share of the delays and extra costs candidates experience. Being aware of them in advance, before committing money to a specific exam date or travel booking, can save months of delay and hundreds of pounds in wasted fees.

  • Assuming the English language exemption applies automatically. Kenyan medical education being conducted in English is a genuine advantage, but it is not, on its own, an automatic exemption. Candidates who assume this and delay booking an English test, only to find their medical school cannot produce the specific confirmatory letter the exemption route requires, can lose valuable months. Establish your actual position early, and book a test as a parallel safety net if there is any doubt.
  • Starting EPIC verification too late. Because EPIC processing time depends heavily on your medical school's administrative speed rather than your own effort, this step should be initiated before, not after, beginning AKT preparation. Candidates who wait until they are ready to book the AKT before starting EPIC often lose two to three months they could have used productively.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Flights from Nairobi, Kampala, or Dar es Salaam, several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, food, and — for many candidates — a short UK preparation course before the exam, can together add well over £1,000 on top of the fee. Candidates who budget only for the headline fee frequently find themselves scrambling for additional funds close to their exam date.
  • Not accounting for regional currency volatility. Because all UKMLA and GMC fees are charged in pounds sterling, a candidate who calculates their local-currency budget many months before actually paying can find the real cost has risen substantially by the time payment is due. Build a buffer of at least 15–20% above your initial budget calculation, and check live exchange rates in the weeks leading up to each payment rather than assuming an old estimate still holds.
  • Assuming regional AKT test centre access is guaranteed for every sitting window. While Nairobi, Kampala, and Dar es Salaam all have Pearson VUE presence, availability for the specific AKT programme, on specific dates, is not fixed indefinitely. Always confirm current centre and date availability through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE booking portal well ahead of a target sitting window, rather than assuming past availability guarantees a future slot.
  • Treating document translation and certification casually. Any document not already in English requires a certified professional translation, not an informal translation from a bilingual colleague. This applies to relatively few core documents for Kenyan applicants given the English-medium system, but underestimating certification standards for the documents that do require it is still a common cause of delay.
  • Assuming AKT and CPSA preparation can be compressed into the final weeks. Both assessments draw on a specific, defined content base — the official MLA content map for the AKT and a structured OSCE circuit for the CPSA — and candidates who begin serious preparation only a few weeks before their booked date typically underperform relative to their actual clinical knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Kenyan doctors sit the UKMLA AKT without travelling outside East Africa?

Yes, in most cases. Pearson VUE, which delivers the AKT worldwide, operates a dedicated Pearson Professional Center in Nairobi as well as long-standing authorised testing partners in the city, and further Pearson VUE-authorised centres exist in Kampala, Uganda, and Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. Candidates should always confirm current centre and date availability for the specific AKT sitting window directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal, since availability can change between testing periods.

Do Kenyan doctors need to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, this step cannot be avoided. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. There is no option to sit the CPSA remotely or anywhere within Kenya, Uganda, or Tanzania, so every candidate from the region must budget for at least one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway.

Is a Kenyan MBChB automatically accepted by the GMC?

Degrees from KMPDC-recognised Kenyan medical schools generally satisfy the GMC's basic eligibility requirement, provided the school was properly accredited at the time of graduation and the qualification is listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools. Acceptance also depends on completing primary source verification through EPIC and holding full, unrestricted KMPDC registration following the internship year. Always confirm your specific situation against the current guidance on confirming who can sit the exam rather than assuming eligibility automatically.

Do Kenyan doctors qualify for the GMC English language exemption?

Some do, but not automatically. Because Kenyan medical education is taught and examined almost entirely in English, many Kenyan candidates have a realistic case for the GMC's English language exemption or an alternative-evidence route, but this still requires specific documentary evidence — typically a formal letter from the medical school confirming the language of instruction and clinical interaction — and is time-limited to qualifications obtained within a defined recent window. Candidates whose case is uncertain should book an IELTS Academic or OET Medicine sitting in parallel rather than delay, and should read the full GMC English language exemption for IMGs guide before assuming an exemption will apply.

How much does the whole UKMLA process cost for Kenyan candidates?

The core GMC and exam fees — AKT, CPSA, and initial registration — total approximately £1,800, which converts to roughly $2,285 at broadly representative exchange rates, though this figure moves with the market. Once English testing (where applicable), EPIC verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget is closer to £3,300–£4,800 (roughly $4,200–$6,100). Candidates should always convert to Kenyan shillings using the live mid-market rate close to the time of each payment rather than an estimate made months in advance.

What is the realistic timeline from graduating in Kenya to working in the NHS?

Most candidates should plan for 16 to 28 months from starting EPIC verification to beginning their first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA and a reasonably swift resolution of the English language question. This accounts for KMPDC document processing, EPIC verification delays, exam preparation for both assessments, and the GMC registration application itself. Candidates who need to re-sit either exam, or who face slower-than-average verification, should expect this timeline to extend.

Is the process the same for UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors regardless of which East African country someone trained in?

Broadly, yes. UKMLA for Kenyan and East African doctors follows the same core sequence — degree recognition, EPIC verification, an English language decision, the AKT, the CPSA, and GMC registration — whether the underlying qualification is Kenyan, Ugandan, or Tanzanian. The main differences are which national regulator issues the Certificate of Good Standing and how quickly each medical school responds to EPIC verification requests, rather than any difference in the exams themselves or the fees charged.

Does the same pathway apply to doctors trained in Uganda and Tanzania?

Yes, in broad structure. Doctors trained in Uganda are verified through the Uganda Medical and Dental Practitioners Council, and Tanzanian graduates through the Medical Council of Tanganyika, rather than KMPDC, but the underlying GMC assessment — WDOMS listing, EPIC verification, English language position, the AKT, the CPSA, and registration — is essentially the same across all three East African Community countries. Regional AKT test centre access, exam fees, and the mandatory Manchester CPSA trip apply equally to candidates from all three countries.

How does the Kenyan and East African route compare to other African IMG routes?

The overall structure — degree recognition, primary source verification, an English language decision, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam in the UK, then registration — is broadly the same across African IMG source countries, including the equivalent route for doctors trained in Nigeria. What differs between countries is the specific verification body, typical processing times, local AKT test centre availability, and how straightforward the English language exemption case is likely to be.
