Gastroenterology UKMLA AKT questions span the full breadth of abdominal medicine — from the acutely unwell patient with upper GI bleeding to the outpatient presenting with altered bowel habit and weight loss. This system is consistently high-yield, and mastering it requires fluency in clinical scoring tools, investigation algorithms, and the major guidelines from NICE and the British Society of Gastroenterology. Whether you are revising inflammatory bowel disease, acute pancreatitis, jaundice workup, or colorectal cancer screening, this guide covers the data tables and clinical frameworks that appear most frequently in gastroenterology UKMLA AKT questions.

Acute Abdominal Pain: Differential Diagnosis by Location

Localising abdominal pain is one of the most tested gastroenterology UKMLA AKT skills. The differential shifts significantly with the quadrant involved, and exam questions frequently present a clinical vignette requiring you to identify the most likely diagnosis based on location, character, and associated features.

GI Bleeding: Upper vs Lower, Scoring, and 2WW Referral

Upper GI Bleeding: Glasgow-Blatchford Score

Upper GI bleeding presents with haematemesis (fresh blood or coffee-ground vomiting) or melaena (black, tarry, malodorous stool). The Glasgow-Blatchford score (GBS) is used pre-endoscopy to risk-stratify patients and identify those who can be managed as outpatients (GBS 0 = low risk, can be discharged for outpatient endoscopy within 24 hours).

GBS 0 = low risk (outpatient); GBS ≥1 = hospital admission. Upper GI endoscopy (OGD) should be performed within 24 hours in non-variceal bleed, and immediately (within 1 hour) in suspected variceal bleed with haemodynamic instability. Terlipressin and prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone) are given before endoscopy in variceal bleeding; band ligation is the endoscopic treatment of choice for oesophageal varices.

Lower GI Bleeding

Bright red rectal bleeding (haematochezia) is usually lower GI in origin. Differentials vary by age: young patients — haemorrhoids, fissure, IBD, infective colitis; middle-aged — polyps, IBD, diverticular disease; older — diverticular disease, colorectal cancer, ischaemic colitis, angiodysplasia. Two-week-wait (2WW) colorectal cancer referral: rectal bleeding with no anal symptoms in age ≥40; change in bowel habit to looser/more frequent stools in age ≥60; iron deficiency anaemia in men or post-menopausal women; palpable right-sided mass; rectal mass on PR examination; raised CEA. Bowel cancer screening programme (BCSP): faecal immunochemical testing (FIT) offered every two years from age 50–74 in England (now extended toward 45); FIT ≥10 micrograms haemoglobin/g faeces triggers colonoscopy referral.

Acute Pancreatitis: Severity Scoring and Management

Acute pancreatitis most commonly presents with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea and vomiting. Diagnosis requires two of three: characteristic pain, serum lipase/amylase >3Ã— upper limit of normal, and characteristic imaging (CT). The commonest causes are gallstones (~45%) and alcohol (~35%) — remembered by GET SMASHED (Gallstones, Ethanol, Trauma, Steroids, Mumps, Autoimmune, Scorpion venom, Hyperlipidaemia/Hypercalcaemia, ERCP, Drugs).

Severity is assessed using the Glasgow (Imrie) score — 8 criteria, score ≥3 indicates severe pancreatitis requiring HDU/ITU: PaO2 <8 kPa, albumin <32 g/L, neutrophil count >15 Ã—10â¹/L, calcium <2 mmol/L, raised urea >16 mmol/L, AST/ALT >200 U/L, blood glucose >10 mmol/L, LDH >600 U/L. CT severity index (CTSI) is used 48–72 hours into admission to identify necrosis. CRP >150 at 48 hours also predicts severe disease. Management: aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (crystalloid), analgesia (opioids), nil by mouth initially transitioning to early enteral nutrition (nasojejunal tube preferred to TPN), thromboprophylaxis, monitoring in HDU if severe. ERCP and sphincterotomy within 72 hours if biliary pancreatitis with obstructive jaundice or cholangitis. Infected pancreatic necrosis: IV antibiotics, then necrosectomy (endoscopic or surgical).

H. pylori: Testing and Eradication

Helicobacter pylori causes peptic ulcer disease, chronic gastritis, MALT lymphoma, and is a risk factor for gastric cancer. Testing: urea breath test (gold standard non-invasive; must stop PPIs 2 weeks before), stool antigen test (less commonly used), CLO test (rapid urease test at endoscopy — invasive). Serology is not recommended for diagnosis of active infection. First-line eradication (NICE): 7-day triple therapy — full-dose PPI (e.g. omeprazole 20 mg BD) + amoxicillin 1 g BD + clarithromycin 500 mg BD. Second-line (if clarithromycin resistance suspected): PPI + amoxicillin + metronidazole 400 mg BD; or PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole (quadruple therapy). Confirm eradication with urea breath test ≥4 weeks after completing eradication therapy. PPIs must be stopped 2 weeks before testing.

IBS, Coeliac Disease, and GORD

Irritable Bowel Syndrome

IBS is diagnosed using Rome IV criteria: recurrent abdominal pain at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with two or more of: related to defecation; associated with change in stool frequency; associated with change in stool form. Red flags that exclude IBS: age >60, rectal bleeding, unintentional weight loss, family history CRC/IBD, anaemia, raised CRP/faecal calprotectin, palpable mass. NICE management: lifestyle advice (regular meals, adequate fluid, reduce insoluble fibre); antispasmodics (mebeverine, hyoscine) for cramps; loperamide for diarrhoea-predominant; laxatives for constipation-predominant; low FODMAP diet (refer to dietitian); TCAs (amitriptyline 5–10 mg at night) or SSRIs if refractory; CBT/hypnotherapy for psychological component. Routine colonoscopy not required if Rome IV met and no red flags — test with FBC, CRP, coeliac serology, and faecal calprotectin to exclude organic disease.

Coeliac Disease

Coeliac disease is an autoimmune enteropathy triggered by gluten (wheat, rye, barley) causing villous atrophy in the small bowel, leading to malabsorption. Presentations: diarrhoea, weight loss, iron deficiency anaemia (most common presentation in adults), folate deficiency, osteoporosis, fatigue, dermatitis herpetiformis (itchy blistering rash on extensor surfaces). Investigation: tissue transglutaminase antibody (TTG IgA) is first-line serology — check total IgA to exclude IgA deficiency (use IgG-based tests if deficient). Positive serology: refer for upper GI endoscopy and duodenal biopsy (gold standard: Marsh classification III — subtotal/total villous atrophy). Management: lifelong strict gluten-free diet. Annual monitoring: FBC, B12, folate, iron, calcium, vitamin D. Screen for complications: DEXA scan, risk of T-cell lymphoma (refractory coeliac).

GORD Management (NICE Stepwise)

Step 1: lifestyle advice (lose weight, elevate head of bed, avoid trigger foods, small meals, avoid lying down after eating). Step 2: antacids or alginate for mild intermittent symptoms. Step 3: full-dose PPI (omeprazole 20 mg, lansoprazole 30 mg) for 4–8 weeks. Step 4: if persisting, H2RA (ranitidine/famotidine) can be added or PPI dose doubled. Investigate with OGD if red flags (dysphagia, odynophagia, unexplained weight loss, persistent vomiting, age >55 with new onset dyspepsia, iron deficiency anaemia). Barrett's oesophagus (metaplasia of squamous to columnar epithelium) is a premalignant complication of chronic GORD — surveillance OGD is recommended. Oesophageal adenocarcinoma risk is increased in Barrett's.

Liver Disease: Hepatitis B, C, Alcohol, and NAFLD

Hepatitis B Serology

HBsAg = surface antigen (indicates current infection — acute or chronic). Anti-HBs = surface antibody (indicates immunity — from vaccination or resolved infection). HBeAg = envelope antigen (high replication, high infectivity). Anti-HBc IgM = acute infection. Anti-HBc IgG = past exposure or chronic carrier. HBV DNA quantification guides treatment. Treatment: entecavir or tenofovir (antivirals) in chronic HBV with active replication or significant fibrosis. Screen contacts and offer vaccination.

Hepatitis C

HCV antibody testing (anti-HCV) is first-line; if positive, HCV RNA confirms viraemia (active infection). Genotyping guides treatment duration. Treatment: direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — sofosbuvir-based regimens; cure rates >95%. All diagnosed patients with chronic HCV should be offered treatment. Blood-borne virus; test PWID, MSM, healthcare workers with needlestick, prison population.

Alcohol and Liver Disease

AUDIT-C: three-item screening (how often do you drink; how many units per session; how often do you have 6+ units). Score ≥5 in men or ≥4 in women suggests hazardous drinking. Safe limits: up to 14 units/week (both sexes), spread over 3+ days, 2 alcohol-free days/week. Alcoholic liver disease spectrum: fatty liver (reversible with abstinence) → alcoholic hepatitis (jaundice, AST:ALT ratio >2:1, raised GGT; Maddrey's discriminant function ≥32 indicates severe disease — consider prednisolone) → cirrhosis. Complications of cirrhosis: ascites (spironolactone Â± furosemide; large-volume paracentesis + albumin replacement), spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP — prophylaxis with norfloxacin; treat with ceftriaxone), hepatic encephalopathy (lactulose, rifaximin), variceal bleeding, hepatorenal syndrome, hepatocellular carcinoma (6-monthly USS + AFP screening).

NAFLD/NASH

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is the most common liver disease in the UK, closely linked to obesity, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidaemia, and metabolic syndrome. NASH (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis) is the progressive inflammatory subtype that can lead to cirrhosis and HCC. Diagnosis: raised LFTs (ALT predominant), fatty liver on USS, exclude other causes, FIB-4 score or ELF test to stage fibrosis non-invasively. FibroScan (transient elastography) measures liver stiffness. Liver biopsy is gold standard for staging. Management: lifestyle (weight loss 5–10% reduces steatosis), manage cardiovascular risk factors, vitamin E (for NASH without diabetes), semaglutide showing benefit in trials; resmetirom licensed in USA 2024.

Nutritional Assessment and Colorectal Cancer Screening

MUST Score and Nutritional Support

The Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST) assesses BMI (0–2 points), unintentional weight loss (0–2 points), and acute disease effect (0 or 2 points). Score 0 = low risk (routine care); score 1 = medium risk (observe, dietary advice); score ≥2 = high risk (refer to dietitian, nutritional support). Refeeding syndrome occurs when malnourished patients receive rapid nutritional support after starvation — phosphate, potassium, and magnesium drop dramatically as anabolic processes resume. Risk factors: BMI <16, minimal intake >5 days, low electrolytes before feeding. Prevention: correct electrolytes before starting, start nutritional support slowly (50% of target on day 1), thiamine supplementation (IV Pabrinex before glucose-containing feeds), monitor electrolytes daily.

Gastrointestinal Motility Disorders and Functional GI Disease

Functional gastrointestinal disorders are diagnosed when symptoms are present without structural, biochemical, or microbiological abnormality. Beyond IBS, the UKMLA tests knowledge of gastroparesis (delayed gastric emptying, common in diabetes — nausea, vomiting, early satiety; managed with metoclopramide, domperidone, or erythromycin), functional dyspepsia (Rome IV criteria: epigastric pain or postprandial distress syndrome; managed with lifestyle, PPI, H2RA, low-dose TCA), and achalasia (failure of lower oesophageal sphincter relaxation due to loss of Auerbach's plexus — dysphagia to both solids and liquids, regurgitation, weight loss; barium swallow shows "bird's beak" appearance; manometry is gold standard; treatment: endoscopic pneumatic dilatation or Heller myotomy).

Oesophageal motility disorders include diffuse oesophageal spasm (intermittent dysphagia and chest pain, "corkscrew" oesophagus on barium swallow) and nutcracker oesophagus (high-amplitude peristaltic contractions causing chest pain). Both are diagnosed on high-resolution manometry. Management: calcium channel blockers, long-acting nitrates, PPI if GORD component.

Constipation assessment and management: chronic constipation is defined as fewer than three bowel movements per week, straining, hard stools, or sensation of incomplete evacuation. Secondary causes to exclude: hypothyroidism, hypercalcaemia, opioids, TCAs, calcium channel blockers, Parkinson's, spinal cord disease, colon cancer. Investigations: thyroid function, calcium, FBC, colonoscopy if red flags. Management: dietary fibre (20–30 g/day), adequate fluid intake (2 L/day), bulk-forming laxatives (ispaghula husk) first-line, osmotic laxatives (lactulose, macrogol), stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) for resistant constipation, suppositories or enemas for distal constipation, prucalopride (5HT4 agonist) for women with chronic constipation not responding to laxatives. Avoid phosphate enemas in renal failure (risk of hyperphosphataemia).

Upper GI Cancer: Oesophageal and Gastric Cancer

Oesophageal cancer: two main types — squamous cell carcinoma (middle and upper third, associated with smoking, alcohol, achalasia, hot drinks) and adenocarcinoma (lower third and gastro-oesophageal junction, associated with GORD, Barrett's oesophagus, obesity). Presentation: progressive dysphagia (first to solids, then liquids), weight loss, odynophagia, hoarseness (RLN involvement). Investigation: OGD with biopsy (diagnostic); CT thorax/abdomen/pelvis (staging); endoscopic USS (local staging); PET-CT (distant metastases). Treatment: early stage — surgery (Ivor-Lewis oesophagectomy or transhiatal); locally advanced — neoadjuvant chemotherapy (FLOT or CROSS protocol — chemoradiotherapy) then surgery; metastatic — palliative chemotherapy (FOLFOX, ECF), immunotherapy (nivolumab/pembrolizumab for PD-L1 positive). Palliative: oesophageal stent for dysphagia; PEG tube if unable to swallow.

Gastric cancer: presentation is often late — epigastric pain, weight loss, early satiety, anaemia, dysphagia, vomiting. Risk factors: H. pylori infection (most important modifiable risk), smoking, salt-preserved foods, previous gastric surgery, blood group A, family history, Lynch syndrome, hereditary diffuse gastric cancer (CDH1 mutation). Virchow's node (left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy), Sister Mary Joseph nodule (periumbilical metastasis), Krukenberg tumour (ovarian metastasis), and Irish node (left axillary) are paraneoplastic signs of advanced disease. Two-week-wait: age 55 or over with weight loss AND dysphagia, nausea/vomiting, reflux, dyspepsia, or upper abdominal pain. Also 2WW for haematemesis at any age. Treatment: endoscopic mucosal resection for T1a; surgery (gastrectomy with D2 lymphadenectomy); perioperative chemotherapy (FLOT); HER2-positive: add trastuzumab.

Gastrointestinal Investigations: Endoscopy, Colonoscopy, and ERCP

Upper GI endoscopy (OGD — oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy): visualises oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum to D2. Indications: dysphagia, haematemesis/melaena, dyspepsia with red flags, suspected coeliac (duodenal biopsy), Barrett's surveillance, PUD follow-up. Complications: perforation (<0.01%), bleeding (rare), aspiration. Capsule endoscopy: small bowel imaging using a swallowed camera; used when OGD and colonoscopy are negative but small bowel pathology suspected (e.g. obscure GI bleeding, Crohn's of small bowel); contraindicated in suspected stricture (risk of capsule retention). Colonoscopy: visualises entire colon and terminal ileum; gold standard for colorectal cancer diagnosis, polyp removal, IBD assessment. Complications: perforation (~1:1000), bleeding, missed lesions (adenoma miss rate 6–12%). Bowel preparation is required — patients fast from midnight and take sodium picosulphate or polyethylene glycol solution. ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography): therapeutic procedure to access biliary and pancreatic ducts via a duodenoscope; used for choledocholithiasis (stone removal), biliary stricture (stenting), cholangitis (drainage), sphincterotomy. Complications: post-ERCP pancreatitis (3–5%), cholangitis, perforation, bleeding. MRCP (magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography) is a non-invasive diagnostic alternative — no radiation, no risk of pancreatitis; used when therapeutic intervention not needed or as a pre-ERCP roadmap.

Frequently Asked Questions About Gastroenterology in the UKMLA AKT

What is the difference between Crohn's and UC on histology?

Crohn's disease shows transmural inflammation affecting all layers of the bowel wall, with non-caseating granulomas, cobblestone mucosal appearance, and skip lesions (areas of normal bowel between affected segments). Ulcerative colitis shows mucosal and submucosal inflammation only, with crypt abscesses, goblet cell depletion, and pseudopolyps. These are continuous changes starting from the rectum and extending proximally without skip areas. Biopsy findings guide treatment decisions in the AKT — if granulomas are described, think Crohn's.

How do I remember the Glasgow severity score for pancreatitis?

Use the mnemonic PANCREAS: PaO2 <8 kPa, Age >55, Neutrophils >15, Calcium <2 mmol/L, Raised urea >16, Enzymes (AST >200, LDH >600), Albumin <32, Sugar (glucose) >10 mmol/L. A score of 3 or more within 48 hours of admission indicates severe acute pancreatitis requiring HDU-level monitoring. Note that the Glasgow score uses different variables from the Ranson criteria (which includes WBC, not used in Glasgow) — the AKT typically tests Glasgow in UK clinical contexts.

When should I refer for 2WW colorectal cancer?

NICE NG12 2WW criteria for colorectal cancer: age ≥40 with unexplained weight loss + abdominal pain; age ≥50 with unexplained rectal bleeding; age ≥60 with IDA or change in bowel habit; positive FIT test on BCSP; palpable rectal or right-sided abdominal mass at any age; PR examination suggesting rectal cancer. Also consider 2WW if age ≥50 with rectal bleeding AND any of: abdominal pain, weight loss, change in bowel habit, or IDA. The FIT threshold of 10 micrograms/g used in BCSP is different from the lower thresholds being evaluated for symptomatic patients in some pathways.

What are the NICE criteria for H. pylori testing in dyspepsia?

NICE recommends a test-and-treat strategy for uninvestigated dyspepsia in patients under 55 without alarm features: test for H. pylori (urea breath test or stool antigen test) and treat if positive before considering PPI therapy. If symptoms persist despite eradication, consider OGD. In patients ≥55 with new-onset dyspepsia, or those with red flag symptoms (dysphagia, weight loss, iron deficiency anaemia, persistent vomiting, epigastric mass), direct OGD referral is recommended. PPIs must be stopped at least 2 weeks before urea breath testing to avoid false negative results.

What is the significance of faecal calprotectin in UKMLA questions?

Faecal calprotectin is a non-invasive inflammatory marker released by neutrophils in the bowel mucosa. Elevated calprotectin (>50–200 micrograms/g depending on the local threshold) indicates intestinal inflammation and favours IBD over IBS. It is used in primary care to differentiate between IBS (low calprotectin) and IBD or bowel cancer (elevated calprotectin), guiding referral for colonoscopy. Normal calprotectin in a patient with IBS-type symptoms supports a diagnosis of functional bowel disorder and avoids unnecessary endoscopy. In UKMLA questions, calprotectin is often the discriminating investigation that determines whether to refer for colonoscopy or manage as IBS.

Conclusion

Gastroenterology UKMLA AKT questions reward candidates who know their scoring tools (Glasgow-Blatchford, CURB-65 equivalents for pancreatitis, MUST), can interpret LFT patterns, and know the management algorithms for high-frequency presentations like IBD, GI bleeding, jaundice, and pancreatitis. Ensure you know the 2WW referral thresholds for GI cancers, the H. pylori test-and-treat pathway, and the colorectal cancer screening programme.
