UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors is a pathway that is only beginning to attract serious attention, even though both countries produce large numbers of medical graduates every year and both already send significant numbers of nurses, allied health professionals, and increasingly doctors into international healthcare markets. For physicians trained in Vietnam or Indonesia, the route to sitting the UKMLA and eventually securing full GMC registration follows the same defined sequence used by every other international medical graduate (IMG): credential verification, an English language test, two structured examinations, and a formal registration application. None of these stages is individually difficult, but together they require a level of forward planning, budgeting discipline, and patience with administrative processes that catches many first-time applicants from both countries off guard.

This guide walks through the entire journey specifically as it applies to doctors trained in Vietnam and Indonesia — from confirming that a medical degree from a recognised Vietnamese or Indonesian medical school satisfies GMC eligibility rules, through primary source verification, English language testing, booking exams, and budgeting in US dollars and pounds sterling with a clear hedge to check current Vietnamese dong (VND) and Indonesian rupiah (IDR) conversions. It also covers where candidates from the wider Southeast Asia region can realistically sit the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), why the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) must be taken in Manchester, and a realistic step-by-step timeline from graduation to NHS registration.

The UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors makes most sense as a single combined guide, since Vietnam and Indonesia are grouped together here because their doctors face a broadly similar set of practical questions when evaluating the route: neither country delivers medical education primarily in English, both have well-established national medical regulators whose recognition needs to be checked against the GMC's own requirements, and both sit within a Southeast Asian region where Pearson VUE test centre coverage is real but less dense than in South Asia, the Gulf, or West Africa. None of this makes the pathway unreachable — it simply means Vietnamese and Indonesian candidates benefit from a slightly different practical playbook than doctors from countries where English-medium medical education or a dense local test-centre network is already the norm. This guide sets out that playbook in full.

The UKMLA Route From Vietnam and Indonesia: A Quick Overview

Before examining each stage in detail, it helps to see the whole pathway at a glance. The route for a Vietnamese- or Indonesian-trained doctor generally runs as follows:

  1. Confirm eligibility: Verify that your primary medical qualification — from a Vietnamese medical university or an Indonesian faculty of medicine — appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year, and that you hold a valid, unrestricted licence to practise from your national medical regulator.
  2. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your medical qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials).
  3. Sit and pass an approved English language test — IELTS Academic or OET Medicine — since neither Vietnam nor Indonesia delivers medical education in English as the primary teaching language, meaning almost every candidate from either country needs to plan for formal testing as a standard part of the pathway.
  4. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT, the knowledge-based examination sat at a Pearson VUE test centre, with realistic regional options for Southeast Asia-based candidates covered later in this guide and in the site's dedicated test-centre guide.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA, the practical clinical skills assessment, which can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.
  6. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise once both assessments are passed and all supporting documents are verified.
  7. Apply for NHS posts — typically trust-grade or clinical fellow roles, or Foundation Year 2-equivalent positions — and begin practising in the UK.

Each of these stages carries its own processing time, cost, and set of common delays, covered in the sections below. Understanding the full sequence early prevents the single most common planning mistake among Vietnamese and Indonesian candidates: assuming that passing the exams is the hard part, when in practice credential verification, English testing, and travel logistics for a comparatively less test-centre-dense region often take longer than exam preparation itself. Candidates who treat the administrative track and the exam-preparation track as two parallel projects, rather than one strictly sequential process, consistently reach registration faster than those who wait for one stage to fully finish before starting the next.

Medical School Recognition: Eligibility for UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian Doctors

The starting point for any Vietnamese or Indonesian doctor considering the UKMLA pathway is confirming basic eligibility. The GMC requires that your primary medical qualification comes from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the year you graduated, and that your qualification is recognised by your country's relevant national regulator. Vietnam's medical education and licensing framework sits under the Ministry of Health, while Indonesia's sits under the Indonesian Medical Council (Konsil Kedokteran Indonesia, KKI) working alongside the Indonesian Medical Association. Rather than naming any specific university as automatically acceptable, the reliable approach for both countries is the same: check your own medical school's current listing status on the World Directory of Medical Schools directly, since recognised institutions in both Vietnam and Indonesia generally appear there, but individual listing status can vary by graduation year and by whether accreditation lapsed or changed at any point.

Beyond the medical school listing itself, the GMC also expects evidence that you passed any required national licensing or competency assessment in your home country and that you hold a valid, unrestricted licence to practise rather than a provisional, conditional, or lapsed one. A certificate of good standing confirming your registration status and disciplinary history is typically required as part of your application, and this must be requested directly from your national regulator — a step many candidates in both countries leave too late, given that processing times for official certificates from government health bodies can run into several weeks or longer, particularly outside major administrative centres such as Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, Jakarta, or Surabaya.

This eligibility check is the true starting gate for the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors, and it is worth completing before any exam booking or English test is planned. Full detail of who is permitted to sit the exam, including specific rules for degrees awarded outside standard timeframes or through non-standard routes, is set out in the site's general guide to GMC-recognised primary medical qualifications, which explains how the World Directory of Medical Schools works and what happens when a qualification needs individual assessment. It is worth reading this in full before committing time and money to the process, since eligibility for less commonly reviewed qualification routes is sometimes assessed on a case-by-case basis. For a broader view of the requirements applying across every international medical graduate route, see the site's guide to UKMLA eligibility requirements.

Primary Source Verification via EPIC

Once basic eligibility is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of your Vietnamese or Indonesian medical degree — meaning your qualification is confirmed directly with your medical school rather than accepted on the strength of a certificate alone. The GMC handles this through EPIC, the same platform used globally for this purpose regardless of home country. Medical schools in both Vietnam and Indonesia vary considerably in how quickly they respond to EPIC verification requests; larger, longer-established universities with dedicated international-affairs or alumni offices tend to respond within a reasonably predictable window, whereas smaller institutions, or those without a regular history of handling international verification requests, can take considerably longer, particularly around graduation season or national holiday periods such as Tet in Vietnam or the extended Lebaran period in Indonesia.

The GMC publishes full detail on how primary source verification works, what documents candidates need, and how caseworkers use the resulting evidence, directly on its website. Candidates from Vietnam and Indonesia are strongly advised to start this step as early as possible — ideally before finalising an English test date or exam bookings — since it runs in parallel with other preparation and does not require active candidate involvement once submitted, beyond following up with the medical school if there are delays. Keeping a written record of every communication with your medical school's registrar is a simple habit that becomes valuable evidence if a delayed response later needs escalating.

Documents Vietnamese and Indonesian Candidates Should Gather Early

Beyond the primary degree certificate itself, most applicants from both countries need a broadly consistent set of supporting documents, and gathering them in parallel rather than sequentially saves considerable time:

  • Primary medical degree certificate and full academic transcript of records, obtained from your medical school's registrar or international office.
  • Certificate confirming your national medical licence — issued by the Ministry of Health in Vietnam or the Indonesian Medical Council (KKI) in Indonesia — confirming that the licence is currently valid and unrestricted.
  • Certificate of good standing, confirming your registration status and disciplinary record, requested directly from your national regulator.
  • Certificate of internship or mandatory clinical training completion from your training hospital, where this forms part of your national licensing requirement.
  • Valid passport, with an expiry date extending well beyond your expected registration date.
  • Police clearance certificate from the relevant national authority, sometimes requested to support the good-standing evidence.
  • Evidence of any name changes — marriage certificates or equivalent legal documents — where your degree name and passport name differ.

Requesting the certificate of good standing early is particularly important, since this document is issued by a separate national body from your medical school and follows its own, often slower, administrative timeline. Candidates who have previously registered with regulators elsewhere in Asia or the Gulf will already have a sense of how long this kind of government document request can take; even so, it is worth budgeting extra time, particularly if your national regulator's office is based in a different city from where you currently live or work.

How to Apply for the UKMLA From Vietnam or Indonesia: Step by Step

With eligibility confirmed, the practical application process follows six sequential steps, described below in the order they should typically be actioned.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

Registration begins with creating a GMC Online account at the GMC's official website. This account becomes your central hub for the entire process; you will use it to track your EPIC verification status, book both the AKT and CPSA, submit your English language evidence, and eventually complete your registration application. Use a personal email address you will retain long-term, not a hospital or agency email that may be deactivated after you change employer, since GMC Online correspondence can span well over a year for most candidates from Vietnam and Indonesia.

Step 2: Begin EPIC Verification Early

As covered above, EPIC verification of your primary medical qualification is usually the single longest step in the entire pathway. Start this as early as possible — ideally before you have finalised your English test date or exam bookings — because it runs in parallel with other preparation and does not require your active involvement once submitted, beyond following up with your medical school if there are delays.

Step 3: Gather and Submit English Language Evidence

In parallel with EPIC verification, sit your English language test and upload your certificate to GMC Online once results are issued. Because neither Vietnam nor Indonesia teaches medicine primarily in English, IELTS or OET is the standard route for almost every candidate from either country — full detail is set out in the dedicated section below. Booking a test slot early, rather than waiting to see whether an exemption might apply, is the more reliable approach, since formal exemptions are narrow and case-specific rather than nationality-based.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once your GMC Online account is active, you can book the AKT through the Pearson VUE booking system linked from your account. Booking windows typically open several months ahead of each testing period, and popular test-centre dates fill quickly, so it is best to book as soon as a suitable slot appears rather than waiting until the last moment. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines exactly what conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas are examinable, alongside a structured question bank — see the guidance on choosing a good AKT question bank for a comparison of the main preparation resources available.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once you have passed the AKT. Because the CPSA is only available in Manchester, candidates from Vietnam and Indonesia need to plan a UK trip around this stage, factoring in visa arrangements, long-haul flights, and several days of accommodation for preparation immediately before the assessment. Many candidates combine the trip with a short period of UK clinical observation or shadowing beforehand, which helps with the specific communication and consultation style the CPSA examines, particularly given the differences between UK consultation norms — around consent, shared decision-making, and structured history-taking — and clinical communication styles more commonly taught in Vietnamese and Indonesian medical curricula.

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and your EPIC verification, English language evidence, and any outstanding documents are all confirmed, you can submit your full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage is available in the site's GMC registration process guide, which covers document checklists, processing times, and what to do if your application is queried.

English Language Requirements on the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian Doctors

The English language stage is often the part of the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors that requires the most lead time. Unlike some IMG source countries where medical education is delivered largely in English, medical training in both Vietnam and Indonesia is conducted primarily in the national language — Vietnamese and Bahasa Indonesia respectively — with English typically taught as a secondary academic or professional-development subject rather than the medium of clinical instruction. This is an important practical difference from routes such as the Philippines or parts of the Caribbean, and it means formal English language testing is, in practice, the standard and expected route for essentially every Vietnamese and Indonesian applicant, not an occasional formality.

The GMC accepts two tests: IELTS Academic, which requires an overall score of 7.0, with no individual component (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, which requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. Many practising doctors find OET more intuitive because its scenarios are built entirely around healthcare communication — referral letters, doctor-patient consultations, and clinical case notes — rather than general academic topics, while IELTS Academic draws on a broader range of subject matter. Both tests have established preparation and testing centres in Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, Jakarta, Surabaya, and other major cities in both countries, so access to a test venue is rarely the limiting factor; the more common challenge is reaching the required score band on a first attempt, particularly for candidates whose clinical training and daily practice were conducted almost entirely in their national language. For a detailed side-by-side comparison of the two tests, including which candidates tend to perform better on each, see the site's guide to OET versus IELTS for GMC registration.

Both test certificates have a defined validity period from the date of the test, so timing matters: sitting the test too early relative to your overall pathway risks the certificate expiring before you submit your registration application, which would require a costly and time-consuming re-sit. Full detail on accepted scores, accepted test providers, and how evidence is submitted is available directly from the regulator on the GMC's own guidance on routes to registration for international medical graduates. It is worth reading this page in full, since the specific evidentiary requirements are updated from time to time and always take precedence over general summaries.

UKMLA AKT Test Centre Access for Vietnamese and Indonesian Candidates

One of the most practical questions shaping the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors is where candidates can actually sit the AKT without an expensive international flight. The AKT is delivered through the global Pearson VUE network, and both Vietnam and Indonesia have at least some existing Pearson VUE-authorised testing infrastructure already operating in-country, since Pearson VUE serves multiple other professional and IT certification programmes across Southeast Asia in addition to the AKT. Vietnam has authorised Pearson VUE testing capacity in cities including Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, and Indonesia has authorised Pearson VUE testing capacity in cities including Jakarta and Surabaya. However, whether a specific centre is currently configured and open to deliver the UKMLA AKT for a given sitting window is a separate question from whether Pearson VUE has a general presence in that city, since not every Pearson VUE-authorised venue supports every examination programme in every testing period.

Because of this, the single most reliable approach for candidates in Vietnam and Indonesia is to check the live, current list of open AKT test centres directly through GMC Online or the Pearson VUE booking portal at the time you are ready to book, rather than assuming a specific city will definitely have an open slot. The site's dedicated UKMLA AKT test centres guide explains exactly how to search live availability and what to do if no centre is currently listed for your city in a given sitting period. In that situation, candidates in this region typically look to the nearest available regional Pearson VUE centre elsewhere in Southeast Asia — commonly a short regional flight away — rather than travelling all the way to the UK for the AKT alone, since the AKT itself does not require a UK visit.

This is a significant contrast with the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in Vietnam or Indonesia, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — the GMC holds it exclusively at its purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. This means every candidate from either country must, at minimum, make one long-haul trip to the UK to complete their UKMLA pathway, even in scenarios where the AKT is sat at a regional Southeast Asian Pearson VUE centre without ever leaving the wider region. Budgeting for this single mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visa if required, accommodation, and living costs for the preparation and exam period — should be built into financial planning from the very start of the process, not left until after the AKT is passed.

Spreading currency purchases over time, using a specialist international money transfer provider rather than a standard bank counter rate, and tracking the mid-market rate rather than relying on a single remittance-counter quotation are all practical ways to reduce the effective cost of converting dong or rupiah into pounds across the pathway.

Life and Career After the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian Doctors: NHS Registration Onward

Once a doctor completes the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors and secures GMC registration, they join what is currently a much smaller but steadily growing community of Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors within the NHS compared with more established IMG source countries such as India, Nigeria, or Pakistan. This means new arrivals from either country may find fewer existing compatriot medical colleagues in a given trust than doctors from larger source countries would, although both nationalities benefit from wider Southeast Asian and international professional networks already present across many NHS trusts, along with the general support structures the NHS provides to all new international recruits.

Career progression after registration generally follows one of several routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post — the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs, offering FY2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place — a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK experience and portfolio evidence are in place, or locum work through NHS staff banks and agencies for those who want flexibility while building their UK clinical track record. Doctors who enter through clinical fellow posts often progress into core or specialty training within two to three years, particularly in specialties experiencing significant workforce shortages such as general internal medicine, geriatric medicine, and emergency medicine, where competition ratios for IMG applicants tend to be more favourable than in the most oversubscribed specialties.

It is worth noting that the broad shape of this journey — degree recognition, credential verification, English testing, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam, then registration — mirrors the equivalent route followed by doctors from other major IMG source countries, even where the specific verification body, typical processing times, and local test-centre landscape differ considerably. The route for doctors coming from the Philippines follows the same overall structure, as does the pathway increasingly used by doctors trained in China and by doctors trained in Jamaica and Trinidad and Tobago — different regions, different regulators, but the same underlying GMC framework applied consistently. Candidates from Vietnam and Indonesia who compare notes with doctors from these other routes often find the shared administrative experience — waiting on EPIC, sequencing an English test, planning a single Manchester trip — more similar than the differences in home-country regulatory systems might initially suggest.

Day-to-day life for newly registered Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors in the NHS typically involves an adjustment period covering both clinical and cultural dimensions. Clinically, the biggest early adjustments tend to be around NHS documentation standards, a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary team working, and a more codified approach to patient consent and shared decision-making than many candidates are accustomed to from clinical training in Vietnam or Indonesia. Socially, doctors from both countries typically build their support networks through a combination of hospital induction programmes, wider Southeast Asian community groups in larger UK cities, and online communities of doctors who have taken the same route before them. Salary progression follows the standard NHS pay scale for the relevant grade, and while it can take time to reach specialty training, most doctors who remain committed to the pathway report that the structured nature of UK training — clear competency frameworks, protected training time, and defined progression points — compares favourably with the training structures they experienced at home.

Common Mistakes on the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian Doctors

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among candidates pursuing the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors. Most of these mistakes are not clinical — they are planning and administrative errors that have nothing to do with medical knowledge, yet they are responsible for a disproportionate share of the delays and extra costs candidates experience. Being aware of them in advance, before committing money to a specific exam date or travel booking, can save months of delay and hundreds of pounds in wasted fees.

  • Underestimating English test preparation time. Because neither country delivers medical education in English, candidates who assume they can prepare for IELTS or OET in a few weeks alongside full-time clinical work frequently fall short of the required score band on their first attempt, forcing a costly and time-consuming re-sit that delays the rest of the pathway.
  • Assuming a home-city AKT test centre is guaranteed. Pearson VUE's presence in Southeast Asia is real but less dense than in South Asia or the Gulf, and centre configuration for the specific UKMLA AKT programme can change between sitting windows. Candidates should always confirm live availability rather than assuming their nearest city will definitely have an open slot for their target sitting period.
  • Starting EPIC verification too late. Because EPIC processing time depends heavily on your medical school's administrative speed rather than your own effort, this step should be initiated before — not after — you begin AKT preparation.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Long-haul flights from Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, Jakarta, or Surabaya, several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, food, and — for many candidates — a short UK preparation course before the exam, can together add well over £1,000 on top of the fee.
  • Not accounting for dong or rupiah exchange rate movement. Because all UKMLA and GMC fees are charged in pounds sterling, a candidate who calculates a local-currency budget many months before actually paying can find the real cost has risen by the time payment is due. Build a buffer above your initial budget calculation, and check current rates in the weeks leading up to each payment rather than assuming an old estimate still holds.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. Both IELTS and OET certificates are valid for a defined period from the test date. Candidates who sit their English test early in the process — before EPIC verification or AKT preparation is complete — sometimes find their certificate has expired by the time they are ready to submit their full registration application.
  • Compressing AKT and CPSA preparation into the final weeks. Both assessments draw on a specific, defined content base, and candidates who begin serious preparation only a few weeks before their booked date typically underperform relative to their actual clinical knowledge, particularly on CPSA communication stations that reward familiarity with UK consultation structure rather than raw clinical knowledge alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can candidates on the UKMLA for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors sit the AKT without travelling to the UK?

In many testing windows, yes. Both Vietnam and Indonesia have existing Pearson VUE testing infrastructure in major cities, but candidates should always check current centre listings for the AKT specifically through the UKMLA AKT test centres guide, since availability for this particular examination changes between sitting periods and is not guaranteed to match Pearson VUE's general presence in a given city. When no local centre is open, the nearest available regional Pearson VUE centre elsewhere in Southeast Asia is generally a shorter, cheaper trip than travelling to the UK solely for the AKT.

Do Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors have to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, this step cannot be avoided. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. There is no option to sit the CPSA remotely or anywhere within Vietnam or Indonesia, so every candidate from either country must budget for at least one trip to the UK to complete their UKMLA pathway.

Are Vietnamese and Indonesian medical degrees automatically accepted by the GMC?

Degrees from recognised medical schools in Vietnam and Indonesia generally satisfy the GMC's basic eligibility requirement, provided the school appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant graduation year. However, acceptance also depends on completing primary source verification through EPIC and holding a valid, unrestricted licence from your national medical regulator. Always confirm your specific school's current listing status and check the site's guide to GMC-recognised primary medical qualifications rather than assuming eligibility automatically.

Do Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors need to take an English language test?

Yes, in almost all cases. Because medical education in both countries is delivered primarily in Vietnamese or Bahasa Indonesia rather than English, formal English language evidence is the standard route for candidates from either country, not an occasional formality. Most candidates need to submit a valid IELTS Academic score (7.0 overall, no component below 7.0) or OET Medicine result (Grade B minimum in all four components) as part of their application — see the site's comparison of OET versus IELTS for GMC registration for guidance on which test tends to suit different candidates.

How much does the whole UKMLA process cost for candidates from Vietnam and Indonesia?

The core GMC and exam fees — AKT, CPSA, and initial registration — total approximately £1,800, or roughly $2,285 at broadly representative exchange rates, though this figure moves with the market. Once English testing, EPIC verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget is closer to £3,300–£4,800 (roughly $4,200–$6,100). Because both the dong and the rupiah fluctuate against the pound and the dollar over time, candidates should recheck current conversion rates and current GMC fee levels close to the time of each payment rather than relying on an estimate made months in advance.

What is the realistic timeline from graduating in Vietnam or Indonesia to working in the NHS?

Most candidates should plan for 20 to 32 months from starting EPIC verification to beginning their first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. This accounts for national regulator document processing, EPIC verification delays, a realistic English test preparation period given that English is not the medium of medical instruction in either country, exam preparation for both assessments, and the GMC registration application itself. Candidates who need to re-sit either exam, or who face slower-than-average verification, should expect this timeline to extend.

How does the route for Vietnamese and Indonesian doctors compare to routes from other IMG source countries?

The overall structure — degree recognition, primary source verification, English testing, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam in the UK, then registration — is broadly the same across major IMG source countries, including the equivalent route for doctors coming from the Philippines, the route for doctors trained in China, and the route for doctors trained in Jamaica and Trinidad and Tobago. What differs between countries is the specific verification body, typical processing times, local AKT test centre density, and whether formal English testing is treated as a near-universal requirement — as it is for Vietnam and Indonesia — or as something only some candidates need.
