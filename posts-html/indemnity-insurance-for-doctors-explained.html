Indemnity insurance for doctors is one of the most misunderstood parts of practising medicine in the UK, largely because two genuinely different systems get bundled together in most people's minds under a single phrase. Most doctors employed by the NHS in England already have automatic, state-backed cover for clinical negligence claims arising from their NHS duties — they do not need to buy or arrange this themselves. What they usually do still need, separately, is membership of a medical defence organisation, which covers a distinct and equally important set of situations that state indemnity does not touch at all: General Medical Council (GMC) investigations, disciplinary hearings, Good Samaritan acts, private practice, and day-to-day professional and legal advice. Confusing the two, or assuming one makes the other unnecessary, is the single most common and costly misunderstanding new doctors have about their own professional protection.

This guide is written for both audiences most likely to be confused by this distinction at the point they need clarity the most: newly qualified UK medical graduates starting Foundation Year 1, and international medical graduates (IMGs) who have just passed the UKMLA and are joining the NHS workforce for the first time. Both groups are handed an employment contract, an NHS email address, and a GMC licence to practise within the same few weeks — and almost none are given a clear explanation of what is and is not automatically covered from that point onward. This article closes that gap and explains how indemnity insurance for doctors actually works once you are on the register and working in the NHS.

It covers, in order: what state-backed clinical negligence indemnity actually is and what it covers; what a medical defence organisation typically provides on top of that; a side-by-side comparison table of the two; a neutral framework for evaluating defence organisation membership options without recommending or ranking any specific provider, in keeping with this site's general policy of not endorsing named commercial products or services; and a detailed FAQ addressing the exact questions new doctors search for when they first encounter the words "indemnity insurance." Throughout, the goal is not to sell you anything, but to make sure you understand, before your first on-call shift, exactly what protection you already have and what gap — if any — you still need to fill yourself. The Quick Answer: State Indemnity and Defence Organisation Cover Are Not the Same Thing

In short: indemnity insurance for doctors in the UK NHS context comes from two separate sources that cover two separate things. Clinical negligence arising from your NHS employment is covered automatically by a state-backed scheme — the Clinical Negligence Scheme for Trusts (CNST) for hospital and Trust-based work, and the Clinical Negligence Scheme for General Practice (CNSGP) for general practice — both administered by NHS Resolution. Separately, most doctors also take out membership with a medical defence organisation (MDO) for everything CNST and CNSGP explicitly do not cover: GMC Fitness to Practise investigations, disciplinary proceedings, coroners' inquests, Good Samaritan acts outside your employment, private practice, and general medico-legal advice.

Framed as a single sentence: doctor indemnity cover in the UK is really two products working together, not one. State indemnity handles the financial risk of a negligence claim relating to NHS care; defence organisation membership handles almost everything else that can go wrong professionally. Anyone searching for indemnity insurance for doctors as though it were one simple purchase, the way car or home insurance is, is likely to end up either under-covered for the professional-support side or unnecessarily paying twice for clinical negligence protection they already have for free.

This means a newly employed NHS doctor is not, in practice, "uninsured" the moment they start work — their NHS clinical duties already carry occurrence-based negligence cover through their employing Trust or GP practice from day one. What they are missing, unless they arrange it separately, is the professional and legal support layer that a defence organisation membership typically provides. The GMC's own guidance on insurance, indemnity and medico-legal support confirms this directly: doctors working for an NHS body are indemnified by that body's clinical negligence scheme, but they should still consider whether they need additional cover for anything falling outside that scope.

The practical takeaway for a new doctor, whether a UK graduate starting Foundation Year 1 or an IMG beginning their first UK post after completing GMC registration, is this: check what your employer's state scheme covers, understand precisely where that cover stops, and make a deliberate, informed decision about defence organisation membership to fill the remaining gap — rather than assuming either that the NHS covers everything, or that you must personally insure against clinical negligence claims that the state scheme already handles. Getting this decision right early is part of the same broader planning that goes into confirming your eligibility to practise in the UK in the first place, since indemnity, registration, and employment paperwork all land in the same narrow window before a first shift begins.

What Is State-Backed NHS Clinical Negligence Indemnity?

State-backed clinical negligence indemnity is the mechanism through which the NHS itself, rather than the individual doctor, stands behind clinical negligence claims arising from NHS treatment. It exists because, historically, individual doctors purchasing commercial negligence cover on the open market faced rapidly rising premiums as claim values grew, particularly in high-risk specialties such as obstetrics — a cost that was ultimately passed on to the NHS through higher pay demands or recruitment difficulties. Moving the underlying financial risk to state-run schemes broke that link and made clinical negligence indemnity a background feature of NHS employment rather than a personal expense most doctors have to budget for.

This is the part of indemnity insurance for doctors that most new doctors never have to think about, precisely because it works quietly in the background of their employment contract. There is no form to complete, no premium to pay, and no policy document to keep — the cover simply exists because your employer is a member of the relevant scheme. That silence is exactly what causes confusion later, when a doctor is asked by a defence organisation, an employer, or their own curiosity whether they have "indemnity cover" and are not sure how to answer, having never consciously arranged anything.

The Clinical Negligence Scheme for Trusts (CNST)

CNST is the state indemnity scheme covering clinical negligence claims against NHS Trusts and Foundation Trusts in England, including the hospital-based clinical negligence liability of the doctors, nurses, and other staff those Trusts employ. Although formal membership of CNST is technically voluntary, every NHS Trust and Foundation Trust in England currently belongs to it, meaning that in practice almost every hospital-employed doctor in England is automatically covered for NHS clinical negligence claims through their employer's CNST membership, without needing to arrange anything themselves. Cover under CNST is unlimited in value, and contributions are paid by the Trust itself, calculated each year by actuaries based on projected claim costs, workforce numbers, and specialty risk profile — the individual doctor pays nothing directly into the scheme. For the hospital-based half of indemnity insurance for doctors in England, CNST is therefore the whole story: nothing further needs to be bought, checked, or renewed by the doctor themselves.

Because CNST cover follows the employing Trust rather than the individual doctor, a doctor who works across several different Trusts during Foundation training, or who rotates through several hospital placements as part of specialty training, remains covered throughout, since each employing Trust carries its own CNST membership for the period the doctor works there. This is one of the more reassuring features of the scheme for junior doctors and IMGs alike: cover is continuous and automatic across NHS moves, rather than something that has to be re-arranged with each new post.

The Clinical Negligence Scheme for General Practice (CNSGP)

General practice sits outside CNST and has its own dedicated state indemnity scheme, the Clinical Negligence Scheme for General Practice, introduced by NHS Resolution from 1 April 2019 specifically in response to rising commercial indemnity costs that were affecting GP recruitment and retention. CNSGP automatically covers clinical negligence liabilities arising from incidents occurring on or after that date, for the full range of people delivering NHS primary medical services: contractor and salaried GPs, GP locums, GPs still in training, retainer scheme GPs, and other staff involved in delivering NHS primary care, including out-of-hours provision. As with CNST, membership is automatic for all NHS GP service providers in England and carries no subscription cost to the individual practitioner.

It is worth being precise about scope here, because this is where the most common misunderstanding arises. NHS Resolution's own guidance on CNSGP states explicitly that the scheme indemnifies clinical negligence liabilities only — it does not extend to non-clinical matters, general professional advice, or regulatory and disciplinary proceedings. Anything falling into those categories remains the responsibility of the individual practitioner to arrange separately, typically through membership of a medical defence organisation.

What CNST and CNSGP Do Not Cover

Both schemes share the same fundamental limitation: they indemnify clinical negligence claims relating to NHS duties and nothing more. Neither scheme provides any of the following, all of which fall outside the definition of clinical negligence even though a doctor might reasonably assume that broad "indemnity insurance for doctors" cover automatically extends to them by default:

  • GMC Fitness to Practise investigations — the regulatory process the GMC runs when a doctor's fitness to practise is questioned, entirely separate from any civil negligence claim
  • Disciplinary proceedings brought by an employer — internal Trust or practice disciplinary processes, which are an employment matter rather than a clinical negligence matter
  • Coroners' inquests — representation and preparation support for doctors called to give evidence, which state schemes do not fund for the individual
  • Good Samaritan acts — care given in an emergency outside the scope of paid NHS employment, such as responding to a medical emergency on a flight or in the street
  • Private practice — any clinical work carried out outside NHS employment, even if it takes place on NHS premises
  • General professional and legal advice — day-to-day queries on consent, confidentiality, social media conduct, or contractual disputes

Every one of these gaps is exactly what medical defence organisation membership is designed to fill, which is why the two systems are best understood as complementary layers rather than competing alternatives.

What Does Medical Defence Organisation Membership Typically Cover?

A medical defence organisation (MDO) is a membership body that provides professional indemnity, medico-legal advice, and representation to doctors for the situations described above — the ones state schemes like CNST and CNSGP were never designed to reach. Three well-established medical defence organisations operate in the UK, each with decades of history supporting doctors through GMC investigations, disciplinary hearings, and medico-legal queries. This guide deliberately does not name, rank, or recommend one over another, in keeping with the same evaluation-framework approach this site takes to other categories of commercial product — see, for comparison, how this site handles the same challenge in its buyer's-guide to revision-resource evaluation without endorsing a specific platform. The reasoning is the same here: which defence organisation suits you best depends on your specialty, your career stage, your appetite for cost versus scope of cover, and factors specific to your individual circumstances that a generic recommendation cannot account for.

It helps to think of defence organisation membership as buying access to expertise and representation rather than buying a payout, which is a subtly different product from a conventional insurance policy. Whereas motor or home insurance pays out against a defined loss, defence organisation membership for indemnity insurance for doctors typically functions more like a retained legal and advisory service that activates the moment something goes wrong professionally, whether or not money ultimately changes hands at the end of the process.

Fitness to Practise and GMC Investigation Support

Perhaps the single most valuable service a defence organisation membership provides is support through a GMC Fitness to Practise investigation, a process that can be triggered by a patient complaint, an employer referral, or a self-declared concern, and that can run for many months even where the underlying complaint is ultimately dismissed. Defence organisation membership typically provides access to specialist medico-legal advisers from the moment a doctor is notified of a concern, support in preparing a written response, representation at any hearing before the Medical Practitioners Tribunal Service, and ongoing advice throughout what is often a lengthy and stressful process. Understanding how this process works before you ever need it is worthwhile reading in its own right — see this site's separate guide to how GMC Fitness to Practise investigations work for new doctors for a fuller walkthrough of the stages involved.

Disciplinary and Employment-Related Support

Internal Trust or practice disciplinary proceedings sit outside clinical negligence entirely, since they concern an employer's own conduct process rather than a patient claim. Defence organisation membership typically provides advice and, in more serious cases, representation for doctors facing a disciplinary hearing, helping distinguish a proportionate response from an overreaching one and ensuring a doctor's rights within the process are properly protected. This is a further reminder that indemnity insurance for doctors, in its fullest sense, is really about professional protection across an entire career, not simply about clinical negligence claims.

Coroners' Inquests

Being called to give evidence at a coroner's inquest following a patient death is a distinct and often unfamiliar experience for junior doctors in particular. Defence organisation membership typically includes preparatory advice and, where the case warrants it, representation at the inquest itself — support that falls well outside what CNST or CNSGP were designed to fund, since an inquest is a fact-finding process rather than a negligence claim. This is one of several situations, alongside the wider disciplinary landscape covered in this site's guide to Good Medical Practice and professional standards, where the difference between clinical negligence indemnity and defence organisation support becomes immediately practical rather than theoretical.

Good Samaritan Acts

A doctor who stops to help at a road traffic accident, responds to a call for medical assistance on an aircraft, or otherwise provides emergency care entirely outside the scope of their paid employment is acting as a Good Samaritan, not as an NHS employee — meaning CNST and CNSGP, which only cover NHS-employed duties, do not apply. Defence organisation membership typically extends cover to these situations as part of a rounded indemnity insurance for doctors package, which is one reason many doctors regard membership as valuable protection even while their day-to-day NHS clinical negligence risk is already covered by the state scheme.

Private Practice

Any clinical work carried out privately — whether occasional private patients seen alongside an NHS role, or a doctor working substantially or entirely in private practice — falls outside CNST and CNSGP entirely, even where that work physically takes place inside an NHS hospital. Doctors doing any private work at all need indemnity arranged separately for that portion of their practice, and this is one of the most consequential gaps to get right, since assuming NHS cover extends to private lists is a genuine and serious risk. Anyone planning even occasional private work should treat arranging appropriate indemnity insurance for that private-practice element as a precondition of seeing a first private patient, not a task to get around to afterwards.

General Medico-Legal Advice

Beyond formal investigations and hearings, defence organisation membership typically includes access to a telephone advice line staffed by medico-legal advisers for day-to-day questions — consent disputes, confidentiality dilemmas, social media conduct queries, requests for medical records from third parties, and similar situations that arise regularly in clinical practice but rarely rise to the level of a formal complaint. Many doctors describe this advice line as the single most-used benefit of their membership, precisely because it resolves uncertainty before it escalates into something more serious, and it is often the part of indemnity insurance for doctors that delivers value the most frequently, long before any formal claim or investigation ever arises.

Why Many Doctors Choose to Have Both

Since 2019, the GMC's own professional standards have made the underlying logic of indemnity insurance for doctors explicit rather than leaving it as an industry norm. Good Medical Practice, updated with effect from 30 January 2024, requires every doctor to ensure they have insurance or indemnity arrangements that cover the full scope of their practice, and to keep the adequacy of that cover under regular review as their practice changes. Where a doctor works exclusively as an NHS employee with no private work, CNST or CNSGP may cover the clinical negligence element of "the full scope of their practice" — but the GMC's guidance is clear that doctors should also actively consider whether they need additional cover for anything not covered by their employer's scheme, which for almost every doctor includes at minimum the possibility of a GMC investigation.

Three practical scenarios illustrate why relying on state indemnity alone is rarely sufficient in practice, even for a doctor working entirely within the NHS:

A Patient Complaint Escalates to a GMC Referral

A clinical incident that also generates a patient complaint can trigger two entirely separate processes: a civil negligence claim, handled through CNST or CNSGP, and a GMC referral questioning the doctor's fitness to practise, which neither state scheme touches. A doctor with only state indemnity in this situation has support for the financial claim but is navigating a Fitness to Practise investigation entirely alone — precisely the scenario defence organisation membership, as the second half of a complete indemnity insurance for doctors arrangement, exists to prevent.

A Newly Qualified Doctor Takes On Occasional Private Work

It is increasingly common for doctors, including those relatively early in their careers, to take on some private practice alongside NHS duties — locum shifts for a private provider, cosmetic procedures, or medico-legal report writing. The moment any of this work begins, CNST and CNSGP cover stops applying to it entirely, even if the doctor's main job remains full-time NHS employment. Defence organisation membership, or a specific commercial indemnity product designed for private work, needs to be arranged before that first private patient is seen, not after.

An IMG Assumes NHS Cover Extends Further Than It Does

IMGs arriving in the UK for the first time sometimes carry assumptions from their home country's indemnity system, where personal malpractice insurance purchased by the individual doctor may be the norm rather than an employer-provided state scheme. This can cut both ways: some IMGs under-insure, assuming (correctly) that NHS clinical negligence cover is automatic but wrongly extending that assumption to cover GMC investigations or private work too; others over-insure, purchasing a broad commercial policy that duplicates protection they already have automatically through their NHS employer's CNST or CNSGP membership. Understanding the actual UK split between state and defence-organisation cover, set out in this guide, helps avoid both mistakes, and is worth settling alongside the other logistics covered in this site's Health and Care Worker visa guide for international doctors, since visa conditions, GMC registration, and indemnity arrangements are usually finalised within the same short window before relocation.

A Neutral Framework for Evaluating Defence Organisation Membership

Rather than recommending a specific medical defence organisation, this section sets out the criteria worth applying to any option you are considering — the same approach this site takes to other buyer's-guide topics where naming or ranking a commercial provider would not serve readers well. Comparing options against these criteria, rather than against marketing claims or word-of-mouth alone, produces a more reliable basis for choosing membership that actually fits your circumstances, and turns the abstract idea of indemnity insurance for doctors into a concrete, personal decision rather than a box-ticking exercise completed on autopilot in your first week of work.

Scope of Cover for Your Specialty and Practice Type

Not all defence organisation products cover the same range of activities to the same depth, and the right scope depends heavily on your specialty and career stage. A doctor working entirely within NHS Foundation training has different needs from a GP partner with a private clinic on the side, or a consultant doing regular expert witness work. Before comparing cost, check precisely what each option covers for your actual practice — Fitness to Practise support, inquest representation, Good Samaritan cover, and any private practice you currently do or plan to do — rather than assuming a standard membership automatically covers every activity you might undertake. Scope of cover is arguably the single most important variable in the entire indemnity insurance for doctors comparison, since a cheap membership that does not cover your actual practice pattern provides no real protection at all.

Cost and How Premiums Are Structured

Membership subscriptions vary by specialty risk profile, career stage, and the scope of cover selected, and can change from year to year as claim trends shift. Junior doctors and trainees typically pay considerably less than established consultants in higher-risk specialties, reflecting the lower claims exposure at that career stage. When comparing cost between options, check what is actually included at each price point rather than comparing headline premiums alone, since a cheaper option with narrower scope of cover may not represent better value once your actual practice needs are accounted for. Because cost is the one figure most marketing pages foreground, it is worth deliberately looking past it and weighing indemnity insurance for doctors options against the full set of criteria in this framework rather than price in isolation.

24/7 Advice Line Availability

Medico-legal problems do not confine themselves to office hours — a difficult consent conversation, a confidentiality dilemma, or a request from police for patient records can arise during a night shift as easily as during the day. Whether a defence organisation option provides genuine 24/7 telephone access to a medico-legal adviser, rather than office-hours-only support with an out-of-hours message service, is a meaningful practical difference worth confirming directly with each provider rather than assuming from marketing material. For junior doctors working frequent night shifts in their first year, out-of-hours advice-line access is often the single most-used feature of their entire indemnity insurance arrangement, well ahead of anything formal like a Fitness to Practise case.

Claims-Made vs Occurrence-Based Cover

This is one of the more technical but genuinely important distinctions to understand before choosing between defence organisation products, since it affects what happens if a claim or complaint arises years after the underlying incident — a real possibility given how long clinical negligence and GMC processes can take to surface. Occurrence-based cover protects you against any incident that happened during your period of membership, regardless of when a claim relating to that incident is later made — even if you have since left that organisation's membership entirely. Claims-made cover, by contrast, generally only protects you if both the incident and the resulting claim or complaint occur while you are still an active member; if you have left by the time a claim is made, you may not be protected unless you have separately purchased "run-off" or "tail" cover extending that protection forward in time. State schemes such as CNST and CNSGP are occurrence-based by design, which is one reason they provide reassuring, permanent protection for the NHS clinical negligence element of your work. When evaluating defence organisation options, confirm directly which basis each product uses, and specifically ask about run-off cover terms for the point at which you might eventually retire, emigrate, or change your membership arrangements.

Reputation, History, and Financial Stability

Because defence organisation cover is frequently discretionary rather than a strict contractual insurance guarantee — a structure that differs meaningfully from a standard insurance policy and is worth understanding directly with any provider you are considering — the long-term financial stability and claims-handling track record of the organisation matters more than it might for a conventional insurance product. Established organisations with decades of operating history and a large, diversified membership base are generally better positioned to continue supporting members through long-running cases than newer entrants with a shorter track record, though this should always be weighed against the specific scope and cost factors above rather than treated as the only consideration when choosing indemnity insurance for doctors.

Ease of Notifying and Managing a Claim

How straightforward the notification and case-management process is when something goes wrong is difficult to judge until you need it, but it is worth asking directly, before joining, how quickly a dedicated adviser is typically assigned once a concern is raised, what documentation is expected at each stage, and how progress on a long-running case is communicated. A defence organisation that is excellent on paper but slow or opaque in practice when a real Fitness to Practise investigation begins provides far less genuine reassurance than one that is transparent and responsive from the first phone call.

How and When to Arrange Cover as a New Doctor

For UK Medical Graduates Starting Foundation Training

UK graduates moving from student status into Foundation Year 1 acquire automatic CNST cover for their NHS clinical duties the moment their employment with an NHS Trust begins — there is no application or paperwork required for this element. What does require action is arranging defence organisation membership, which most UK graduates do in the months before starting F1, often taking advantage of reduced or free introductory membership rates commonly offered to students and very early-career doctors by defence organisations as they transition into paid clinical practice. It is worth doing this before your first shift, not after, since GMC investigations and disciplinary processes can in principle arise from very early incidents in a career. This is a natural companion decision to the practical planning covered in this site's overview of what Foundation Year 1 and 2 actually involve, since indemnity arrangements are one of the administrative tasks that sits alongside rota, pay, and pension paperwork in the run-up to starting work.

For IMGs Joining the NHS for the First Time

IMGs typically arrange defence organisation membership around the same time they finalise their GMC registration and accept an NHS job offer, since most defence organisations require confirmation of GMC registration status before membership begins. Because IMGs may be less familiar with the specific UK split between state and defence-organisation cover than UK graduates who encounter it gradually through medical school teaching, it is worth explicitly confirming with your chosen defence organisation exactly what your specific post — Trust-employed hospital doctor, salaried GP, locum, or other arrangement — is and is not covered for, rather than assuming a standard membership automatically matches your particular employment structure. This sits alongside the other practical steps covered in this site's guide to the pathway from UKMLA success to a confirmed NHS post, since indemnity, visa, and induction paperwork typically all land in the same few weeks before a first shift.

Doctors relocating from countries where personal malpractice insurance is the norm sometimes over-plan for the state-indemnity side while under-planning for the defence organisation side, simply because the UK split between the two is unusual by international standards. Confirming both halves of your indemnity insurance for doctors arrangement before your start date removes one significant source of first-week anxiety that has nothing to do with clinical knowledge or exam preparation.

Reviewing Cover as Your Practice Changes

The GMC's 2024 update to Good Medical Practice specifically requires doctors to keep their level of cover under regular review, not simply arrange it once at the start of a career and forget about it. Moving from a purely NHS-employed role into any private work, taking on expert witness or medico-legal report writing, changing specialty into a higher-risk area, or reducing your defence organisation membership tier to save money as a cost-cutting measure are all changes that should prompt a fresh check against the framework set out above, rather than assuming your existing arrangement automatically continues to match your evolving scope of practice. Treat this review as an annual habit rather than a one-off task: a five-minute check of your indemnity insurance for doctors arrangement each year, timed alongside your GMC annual retention fee payment, is enough to catch most mismatches before they become a real problem.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do NHS doctors need to buy their own indemnity insurance?

No, not for clinical negligence arising from their NHS employment duties — that element is covered automatically through their employing Trust's CNST membership, or through CNSGP for general practice, at no cost to the individual doctor. What NHS doctors typically do still need to arrange themselves is medical defence organisation membership, which covers GMC investigations, disciplinary proceedings, inquests, Good Samaritan acts, and any private practice — none of which state indemnity schemes cover. This is the central point anyone new to the topic of indemnity insurance for doctors should take away first, before worrying about which specific defence organisation product to choose.

Is medical defence organisation membership a legal requirement?

The GMC requires every doctor to have insurance or indemnity arrangements appropriate to the full scope of their practice, and can act against a doctor's registration if adequate cover is not in place, but it does not mandate membership of one specific type of organisation. In practice, since state schemes like CNST and CNSGP only cover NHS clinical negligence, most doctors need defence organisation membership or an equivalent product to meet this requirement for the rest of their practice, making a complete indemnity insurance for doctors arrangement a practical necessity even without a single named-provider mandate.

What is the difference between CNST and CNSGP?

CNST is the state indemnity scheme covering clinical negligence claims against NHS Trusts and Foundation Trusts, applying to hospital-based NHS work, while CNSGP is the equivalent scheme specifically for general practice, introduced in 2019 and covering GPs, GP locums, GP trainees, and other primary care staff delivering NHS services. Both are administered by NHS Resolution, both provide automatic cover at no cost to the individual practitioner, and both are limited strictly to clinical negligence rather than covering regulatory, disciplinary, or non-clinical matters.

Does state indemnity cover GMC Fitness to Practise investigations?

No. Neither CNST nor CNSGP provides any support for GMC Fitness to Practise investigations, since these are regulatory processes rather than clinical negligence claims. Support through a Fitness to Practise investigation — including specialist medico-legal advice, help preparing a response, and representation at any tribunal hearing — is typically provided through medical defence organisation membership instead.

Do doctors doing private practice need separate indemnity?

Yes. Any clinical work carried out privately falls entirely outside CNST and CNSGP, even where that private work takes place inside an NHS hospital building, because the state schemes only cover NHS employment duties, not the individual episodes of care within them. Doctors doing any private work at all, whether occasional or substantial, need indemnity arranged separately for that portion of their practice before seeing a private patient.

What is the difference between claims-made and occurrence-based indemnity cover?

Occurrence-based cover protects you against incidents that happened during your period of cover, regardless of when a claim relating to that incident is later made, even after you have left that cover arrangement. Claims-made cover generally only protects you if the claim or complaint is also made while you remain an active member, meaning cover can lapse for old incidents once membership ends unless separate "run-off" or "tail" cover has been arranged. State schemes such as CNST and CNSGP are occurrence-based, while defence organisation products vary, so this is worth confirming directly with any option you are comparing.

How much does medical defence organisation membership cost for a new doctor?

Cost varies by specialty, career stage, and scope of cover selected, and defence organisations periodically adjust their fee structures, so this guide deliberately does not quote a single figure that could quickly become outdated or misleading if quoted out of context. Junior doctors and trainees typically pay considerably less than established consultants in higher-risk specialties, and many defence organisations offer reduced introductory rates for doctors in their first year or two of practice. Because this is the one part of indemnity insurance for doctors that genuinely does cost money out of pocket, budgeting for it alongside other early-career costs such as GMC retention fees is worth doing deliberately rather than treating it as an afterthought — the most reliable approach remains requesting a current quote directly from each option you are comparing against the evaluation criteria set out in this guide.

Can an IMG arrange defence organisation membership before arriving in the UK?

Most defence organisations allow IMGs to begin the membership application process before physically arriving in the UK, though full membership typically activates once GMC registration is confirmed and, in some cases, once an NHS employment start date is confirmed. It is worth starting this process as soon as your GMC registration and job offer are confirmed, rather than leaving it until your first week of NHS employment, since some administrative lead time is usually involved.

Conclusion

Understanding indemnity insurance for doctors comes down to recognising that two separate systems are doing two separate jobs: state-backed schemes like CNST and CNSGP automatically cover NHS clinical negligence claims at no cost to the individual doctor, while medical defence organisation membership fills the remaining gap for GMC investigations, disciplinary proceedings, inquests, Good Samaritan acts, and private practice. Most doctors, UK graduates and IMGs alike, ultimately choose to maintain both forms of protection precisely because neither one substitutes for the other, and the GMC's own professional standards now explicitly require doctors to keep the adequacy of their overall cover under regular review rather than treating it as a one-off decision made at the start of a career.
