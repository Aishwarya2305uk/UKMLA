Receiving your UKMLA results is a significant moment — but the result itself is only the beginning of a process. Whether you receive a pass or a fail, the feedback report that accompanies your UKMLA sitting is a structured document designed to inform your understanding of your own performance, guide professional development, and — if needed — form the basis of a resit strategy. This guide explains exactly what to expect from your UKMLA results and feedback, how to interpret every element of the information you receive, and what to do next regardless of the outcome.

How UKMLA Results Are Released

The AKT and CPSA are marked and reported separately. Results are typically released to candidates through their medical school, not directly from the GMC or the examination providers. The timeline from sitting to receiving your result varies by institution and examination window — your medical school will communicate expected result dates in advance of the sitting.

For the MS-AKT (Medical School Applied Knowledge Test — the version sat by UK medical students), results are processed centrally by the assessment provider working with the Medical Schools Council (MSC) and communicated to medical schools, which then release them to individual students according to their own protocols: some via student portals, some via email, some through formal results ceremonies.

For PLAB 1 (the equivalent AKT pathway for international medical graduates), results are released by the GMC directly, typically within a few weeks of sitting.

You should not attempt to infer your result from the examination room experience. The UKMLA uses standard setting (Angoff method for the AKT, borderline regression for the CPSA) to establish the pass mark after each sitting — meaning the pass mark you needed to meet was not fixed in advance, and what felt like a difficult or easy paper does not reliably predict whether you passed.

Understanding the AKT Feedback Report: Structure and Content

The AKT feedback report is structured around the content domains defined in the MLA Content Map. Rather than giving you a raw percentage score or a number of correct answers, the report typically describes your performance relative to the standard — specifically, in which content areas you performed at, above, or below the expected level for a minimally competent foundation doctor.

Overall pass/fail outcome: The first and most important piece of information. A pass means you met or exceeded the pass standard for this sitting. A fail means your overall performance fell below the pass standard.

Domain-level performance breakdown: The AKT is structured around several broad competency domains drawn from the MLA Content Map. Your feedback indicates your relative performance across these domains — for example, whether you performed particularly well or poorly in pharmacology, applied knowledge of clinical presentations, data interpretation, or professional and legal knowledge.

Comparison to the pass standard: Feedback is usually expressed relative to the pass standard, not as a raw percentage. A statement that you "scored at the 45th percentile" or "performed below the borderline standard in clinical knowledge" is more informative for resit planning than knowing you answered 67% of questions correctly. This is because the pass standard varies between sittings.

Sub-domain detail: Some institutions provide more granular feedback, breaking down performance within domains — for example, distinguishing between cardiovascular pharmacology and respiratory pharmacology, or between communication skills and clinical examination within the CPSA. The level of detail varies by sitting and by your medical school's reporting arrangements with the assessment provider.

Percentile rank: Some feedback reports include your performance percentile within the cohort. This is a norm-referenced descriptor — it tells you where you sat relative to other candidates in the same sitting, which is informative for self-calibration but should not be confused with the criterion-referenced pass standard.

Understanding the CPSA Feedback Report

The CPSA is an OSCE-format examination, and its feedback differs structurally from the AKT because each station assesses a specific competency in a specific clinical context.

Station-level pass/fail or score: Each OSCE station is marked individually. You will typically receive information about which stations you passed and which you did not reach the required standard in. In some formats, you receive a score out of the maximum for each station; in others, a simplified pass/borderline/fail classification.

Domain-based OSCE marking: Within each station, marks are typically allocated across several domains: clinical examination technique, clinical reasoning and interpretation, communication skills, and professionalism. Your feedback may indicate where within a station your performance was stronger or weaker.

Global rating: Most CPSA marking schemes include a global rating by the examiner — a holistic assessment of whether your performance was appropriate for a foundation doctor at that station. This rating is weighted heavily in the overall mark. Candidates who score well on individual checklist items but receive a low global rating have typically performed the right actions but without the appropriate clinical demeanour, patient-centredness, or safety awareness that the examiner expected to see.

Comparison to pass standard: Like the AKT, the CPSA pass standard is set after each sitting using borderline regression. In most CPSA models, compensatory passing is possible — strong performance in some stations can compensate for weaker performance in others, provided no single station falls below a specified minimum threshold (the so-called "bare fail" or "fail" category that cannot be compensated).

What a Pass Result Means — and What to Do With It

A pass in the AKT and CPSA means you have met the standard of a minimally competent foundation doctor. It is a significant achievement — the standard is set to reflect what is genuinely required at the point of independent practice, not a lowered bar.

However, a pass is not the end of the feedback report's usefulness. Even candidates who pass often have identifiable areas of weaker performance. The feedback report serves several ongoing purposes:

Identifying foundation year learning priorities: If AKT feedback shows relatively weaker performance in, for example, clinical pharmacology or data interpretation, this is a signal that these areas warrant continued attention in your portfolio of learning as a foundation doctor. The AiF (Assessment in Foundation) programme expects you to address learning needs that emerge from performance feedback.

Informing specialty training applications: Specialty training applications (core medical training, core surgical training, general practice, anaesthetics, psychiatry, etc.) require structured reflection on strengths and development areas. A specific analysis of your UKMLA feedback — including examples of how you have addressed identified gaps — provides concrete evidence for portfolio entries and application statements.

Calibrating self-assessment: Many doctors overestimate competence in areas where they feel confident and underestimate it in areas they find uncomfortable. External feedback from a standardised assessment provides an objective counterweight to self-perception. This calibration effect persists through training and influences how doctors seek learning opportunities.

CPD portfolio entry: Your UKMLA results can function as a formal reflective entry in your foundation portfolio. Documenting your performance, your interpretation, and your planned response to the feedback demonstrates the reflective practice that professional development frameworks expect.

What a Fail Result Means — and How to Use the Feedback

A fail in either the AKT or the CPSA is deeply disappointing, and it is important to acknowledge that emotional reality before attempting to use the feedback analytically. However, the feedback report after a failed sitting is the most important document you will receive in planning a resit.

Step 1 — Understand what you failed: Is your fail a global fail (overall performance below the pass standard, with weaknesses across multiple domains) or a targeted fail (strong overall performance but significant weakness in one or two specific areas)? The answer determines whether you need a broad revision strategy or a targeted one.

Step 2 — Quantify the gap: If you can determine how far below the pass standard you were, this helps calibrate the scale of preparation needed. A candidate who narrowly failed needs a different resit strategy from a candidate who failed significantly across most domains.

Step 3 — Map feedback to content: Take each area of relative weakness identified in the feedback and map it to specific content in the MLA Content Map. Use this mapping to construct a revision plan that allocates time proportionally — more time to areas of significant weakness, maintenance time to areas of strength.

Step 4 — Change your approach, not just your effort: Many candidates who fail a UKMLA sitting respond by revising more of the same material using the same method. But if the original approach did not produce a pass, the resit strategy should examine whether the revision method itself needs to change. If your preparation was reading-heavy and your performance was weak on applied reasoning questions, switching to active question practice addresses the root cause.

Step 5 — Seek support: Contact your educational supervisor and student support team promptly. A fail in the UKMLA requires a structured institutional response as well as a personal one. Your educational supervisor can provide pastoral support, arrange additional clinical supervision if CPSA skills are the concern, and help access specialist resources.

The Role of Standard Setting in Interpreting Your Result

The UKMLA does not use a fixed percentage pass mark. The pass standard is set after each sitting using the Angoff method (for the AKT) or borderline regression (for the CPSA). This means the pass mark varies slightly between sittings depending on the difficulty profile of that sitting's questions or stations.

A practical implication: if your feedback tells you that you fell below the standard, it does not tell you by exactly how many percentage points of absolute correctness. What it tells you is that your performance fell below what a panel of medical education experts determined was the minimum acceptable for safe independent practice on that sitting. The comparison between your performance and the pass standard is what matters — not a comparison between your score and a friend's score.

Two candidates with identical raw scores could have different outcomes if they sat in different windows where the standard-setting process produced different pass marks. This is an inherent and well-justified feature of criterion-referenced assessment, reflecting genuine differences in paper difficulty rather than inconsistency in the standard applied.

Using UKMLA Feedback for CPD and Portfolio

Good Medical Practice (2024) requires doctors to engage in continuing professional development and to reflect on their performance to identify learning needs. Your UKMLA feedback report is a formal, externally validated assessment output — exactly the kind of evidence that a professional portfolio entry should be built on.

A high-quality portfolio entry based on your UKMLA feedback would include:

  • A factual summary of your performance: which domains were strong, which were weaker
  • A reflective analysis: what you believe caused the performance profile (gaps in clinical exposure, revision method, time management, examination technique)
  • A learning plan: specific actions you have taken or plan to take in response to identified gaps (targeted reading, clinical supervision, skill practice)
  • Evidence of implementation: a record of what you did (log of question bank sessions, clinical supervisor feedback, OSCE practice sessions)
  • Revised self-assessment: what you believe your current level is after the learning intervention, and how you tested this

This reflective cycle — observation, analysis, planning, action, reassessment — is the structure of the formal CPD requirement and is demonstrated explicitly in the feedback report response.

Supporting a Peer Through a Fail Result

If a colleague receives a fail result, your response matters. Medical culture has historically been poor at supporting colleagues through examination failure — a combination of competitive culture, stigma around academic difficulty, and a general reluctance to discuss vulnerability in professional settings.

What actually helps:

  • Acknowledge the result directly — don't avoid the topic or pretend everything is fine
  • Listen without problem-solving in the first instance — the emotional impact is primary
  • Offer practical support: study together for the resit, share resources, provide accountability
  • Encourage them to access support services (medical school wellbeing, BMA Doctors for Doctors) rather than treating this as a solo problem
  • Maintain normal social engagement — isolation during a difficult period compounds the difficulty

What does not help: minimising ("everyone finds it hard"), comparing ("I found it easy"), advising before listening, or withdrawing.

Appeals Against UKMLA Results

If you believe there were procedural irregularities in your examination — not simply that you disagree with the pass standard — an appeals pathway exists. For the MS-AKT, appeals are handled through your medical school in the first instance. For PLAB 1, the GMC manages appeals directly.

Valid grounds for appeal typically include:

  • Evidence that examination conditions were materially disrupted and not appropriately addressed at the time
  • Evidence that agreed reasonable adjustments were not implemented
  • Evidence of an administrative error in marking, recording, or reporting your result
  • Evidence of a procedural irregularity affecting the fairness of your assessment

Grounds that are generally not accepted: disagreement with the pass standard; believing you "should have passed" based on your revision; or believing specific questions were ambiguous (unless the question was subsequently identified as faulty and removed from marking). Contact your medical school or the GMC (for PLAB) promptly — appeal timeframes are typically within 10–20 working days of the result date.

Supporting Your Wellbeing After a Difficult Result

Receiving a fail result affects identity, confidence, and relationships — not just academic progress. Medical students who fail licensing examinations are at elevated risk of anxiety, depression, and burnout, particularly if they have not experienced major examination failure before.

Helpful responses: give yourself time before entering analytical mode; access pastoral support available to you; reframe the narrative (a setback, not a permanent verdict); and reconnect with your reasons for entering medicine. Many excellent doctors have needed more than one attempt at licensing examinations — this does not define professional trajectory.

Unhelpful responses: isolating yourself, immediate unstructured cramming without a changed strategy, or catastrophising the outcome.

Frequently Asked Questions: UKMLA Results and Feedback

When will I get my UKMLA result?
Typically four to eight weeks after your sitting date for the AKT. Your medical school will communicate exact timelines. PLAB 1 results are typically available within a few weeks of sitting.

Can I see my marked paper?
No. You will not receive a copy of your individual question paper or see which questions you answered correctly. This is standard practice in high-stakes medical licensing examinations where item security is essential.

How long do I have to wait to resit if I fail?
Resit timelines depend on the examination calendar, your medical school's regulations, and your institutional policies on resit eligibility. Contact your medical school's student affairs office promptly after receiving a fail — there are often strict enrolment windows for supplementary sittings.

Does failing the UKMLA affect my medical registration?
You must pass both the AKT and CPSA before the GMC will grant full registration and a licence to practice. You can graduate from medical school before passing the UKMLA, but you cannot begin foundation training or work as a licensed doctor until both components are passed.

If I pass the AKT but fail the CPSA, do I have to resit both?
In most formats, each component is assessed independently. If you pass the AKT and fail the CPSA, you resit only the CPSA — your AKT pass is retained subject to the validity period specified by your medical school or the GMC. Confirm the specific rules with your medical school, as these can differ by institution.

Can my resit result be worse than my first attempt?
In theory, yes — performance in any sitting is subject to variation. However, candidates who make specific, targeted changes to their revision strategy between sittings based on their feedback report typically improve their scaled score. The key is addressing the root cause of the failure, not simply repeating the same preparation approach.

Conclusion: Use Your Results as a Clinical Document

Your UKMLA results and feedback report are not administrative bureaucracy — they are clinical information about your readiness to practice safely. Treat them with the same analytical rigour you would apply to any clinical investigation result: understand what the result means, identify what caused this outcome, and develop a management plan.

Whether you are celebrating a pass or beginning the work of planning a resit, the feedback report provides the most specific and objective information available about your performance at this stage of training. Use it fully — not just as a one-time document, but as the starting point for ongoing professional development.

How Formative Assessments Prepare You for Summative UKMLA Performance

The UKMLA is the summative gate — but the formative assessments embedded in your medical programme are where your preparedness is built and tested incrementally. Treating formative assessments as meaningful is one of the highest-leverage habits you can develop in medical school.

Progress tests: Many UK medical schools use regular longitudinal progress tests — standardised multiple-choice papers sat by all years simultaneously to test accumulating clinical knowledge. Progress test performance in years three and four is one of the strongest predictors of UKMLA AKT performance. If your progress test domain breakdown shows a persistent weakness in, for example, pharmacology or professionalism questions, this signal arrives before the licensing examination and gives you time to address it. Do not disregard progress test feedback.

Clinical placement assessments: DOPS (Direct Observation of Procedural Skills), mini-CEX (mini Clinical Evaluation Exercise), CBD (Case-Based Discussion), and ACAT (Acute Care Assessment Tool) all generate supervisor feedback on specific clinical skills. This feedback is your closest available proxy to a CPSA mark before you sit the CPSA itself. Actively seek this feedback throughout your placements, and ask supervisors specifically which aspects of your clinical examination or communication you should develop — this question is almost universally welcomed by supervisors and produces the most specific developmental guidance.

OSPEs and formative OSCEs: Many institutions run formative OSCE circuits in the penultimate year. Performance in these is highly predictive of summative CPSA performance — both the level and the domain profile. Treat formative OSCE feedback with the same analytical rigour you would apply to summative feedback: identify which stations you fell below the borderline standard in, analyse the specific mark scheme elements you missed, and address these systematically.

Using the UKMLA Feedback Report for Specialty Training Applications

The Foundation Programme and subsequent specialty training applications use the Educational Performance Measure (EPM) and Situational Judgement Test (SJT) for initial allocation, but specialty training applications from foundation onwards increasingly require structured reflection on performance and learning. The UKMLA feedback report is a legitimate piece of evidence in these applications when used correctly.

For core medical training (CMT) and acute care specialties: These applications reward demonstrated clinical reasoning ability and patient safety focus. A UKMLA feedback report showing above-average performance in clinical knowledge of presentations and pharmacology, with specific reflection on how you have built on this in foundation year, provides concrete evidence for portfolio statements about clinical competence.

For general practice specialty training (GPST): GP registrar applications reward breadth across the clinical domains and demonstrated professionalism and communication skills. Your CPSA feedback — particularly on communication stations — is directly relevant portfolio evidence. A statement such as "in my CPSA, I identified a relative weakness in breaking bad news stations; I have addressed this by attending the palliative care communication skills training in FY1 and seeking feedback from my GP supervisor" demonstrates exactly the reflective, responsive learning that GP applications look for.

For psychiatry, paediatrics, obstetrics and gynaecology: These applications typically weight demonstrated commitment to the specialty and specific competency evidence. UKMLA feedback showing strength in the relevant clinical domain, supplemented by portfolio evidence of specific learning activities, strengthens these applications.

For surgical specialties: Core surgical training applications weight procedural and technical skill evidence. CPSA clinical examination station performance — particularly on examination technique and clinical reasoning stations — provides relevant evidence of early clinical technical competence, supplemented by clinical logbooks and DOPS from foundation year.

The Psychological Impact of UKMLA Results at Different Life Stages

The UKMLA is sat at a specific life stage — the transition from medical student to doctor — that carries its own psychological weight regardless of examination outcome. Understanding the emotional landscape around results helps you respond adaptively whether you pass or fail.

For younger students (traditional five-year pathway, early twenties): The UKMLA may be the first major high-stakes assessment failure experience for students who have previously succeeded through school and early university. Failure challenges a core identity narrative ("I am someone who succeeds academically") in ways that are uniquely disorienting. The adaptive response: separate performance from identity; recognise that a single examination outcome does not define professional capability or trajectory; access support promptly rather than isolating.

For GEM students and older candidates: Older students who have previous professional careers and significant life investments (financial, relational, time) carry additional weight around UKMLA outcomes. Failure may activate concerns about whether the career change was the right decision, about financial pressure, or about the timeline to practicing income. These concerns are real and legitimate — and they need to be addressed alongside the practical resit strategy, not instead of it. A good educational supervisor will be alert to these broader life context pressures and factor them into support planning.

For international medical graduates taking PLAB: PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 are high-stakes not only for licensing but often for visa status, employment timelines, and family situations. The pressure is qualitatively different from that on a UK final-year student, and the support resources available are also different. The GMC's website provides guidance specific to international medical graduates, and the BMA's international graduate support resources are also available.

Long-Term Use of Feedback: The CPD Cycle in Foundation Training

The skills of using performance feedback effectively — objective interpretation, honest self-assessment, targeted learning planning, verification of learning — are not just UKMLA skills. They are the skills of a reflective clinician, required throughout a medical career by professional regulatory frameworks.

The GMC's revalidation framework requires all licensed doctors to demonstrate engagement with CPD and to collect multi-source feedback at regular intervals. The portfolio tools used in Foundation Programme (AiF), core training, and specialty training all instantiate the same cycle: gather evidence of performance, reflect on what it shows, identify learning needs, plan and implement learning, reassess. The UKMLA feedback report is your first formal opportunity to practise this cycle at a professional level.

Candidates who leave medical school having practised this reflective cycle genuinely — not as a portfolio box-ticking exercise, but as a real cognitive habit — are better prepared for the continuous self-directed learning that professional medicine requires. The UKMLA is not the end of the assessment journey; it is the entry point to a career of ongoing performance review.

Understanding Your AKT Scaled Score: What the Numbers Mean

The AKT result is reported as a scaled score rather than a raw mark or percentage. Understanding what a scaled score is — and what it is not — helps you read your feedback accurately and make sensible comparisons between sittings if you resit.

A raw score is the number of questions you answered correctly. A scaled score is a transformation of the raw score that accounts for the statistical difficulty of that particular paper and enables comparison across different sittings. The specific scale used for the UKMLA AKT is set by the examination authority; common medical licensing examination scales range from 1–100 or 100–300 (as in the USMLE), but the specific scale used in your sitting will be communicated in the assessment framework guidance from your medical school.

Why scaling matters: If two sittings of the AKT contain questions of different overall difficulty, a raw score of 130/180 in a hard sitting is not equivalent to 130/180 in an easy sitting. The easier sitting's 130 required less clinical knowledge to achieve. Scaling corrects for this by anchoring both scores to the same competency scale, so that a scaled score of X in any sitting reflects the same underlying level of clinical knowledge.

Why your percentage is not the same as your scaled score: A percentage correct (e.g. 72%) is a raw metric — it does not account for sitting difficulty. Your scaled score is the appropriate basis for comparing your performance to the pass standard and to other sittings. When interpreting your feedback, focus on the scaled score and its relationship to the scaled pass mark (cut score), not on the raw percentage.

What to Expect in the Weeks Between Sitting and Results

The period between completing the AKT or CPSA and receiving your result can be one of the more anxious phases of medical school. Understanding what happens during this period can reduce uncertainty.

Marking and quality assurance: AKT items are machine-marked. The time between sitting and results is used for item analysis — checking for malfunctioning items, reviewing items flagged for DIF, running equating procedures if multiple examination forms were used, and finalising the standard-setting process. For the CPSA, examiner mark sheets are collated and standardised, and station performance data is processed through the borderline regression model.

Examination board review: An examination board composed of the assessment director, lead psychometrician, and senior clinical examiners formally reviews the performance data, confirms item removals and scoring adjustments, approves the final pass mark, and signs off the results. This process typically takes three to six weeks for the AKT. CPSA results may follow a slightly different timeline.

Result release: Your medical school will communicate results via their standard student communication channels — student portal, email, or a formal ceremony depending on institution. Check your student portal's guidance on expected result dates rather than trying to infer from the sitting date.

How Resit Performance Compares to First-Attempt Performance

Candidates who resit the AKT after a first-attempt failure show a range of outcomes. Research on resit performance in medical licensing examinations consistently shows that a significant proportion — typically 50–70% of resit candidates — pass on the second attempt. The factors most strongly associated with resit success are:

  • A systematic change in revision approach (not just more of the same)
  • Targeted revision addressing the specific domains identified as weak in the first-attempt feedback
  • Use of active recall methods (question banks, practice tests) rather than passive review
  • Adequate time between the first attempt and the resit to allow meaningful preparation (minimum six to eight weeks; ideally three to four months)
  • Access to pastoral and academic support during the resit preparation period

Candidates who resit with the same approach that produced the initial failure typically show less improvement than those who change their strategy. If you are preparing for a resit, the feedback report from your first attempt is your most important planning document — use it specifically to guide where your preparation time goes.
