GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors looks nothing like it did a decade ago, and the change catches a surprising number of applicants off guard. Doctors who trained in France, Germany, Poland, Spain, or any other European Economic Area (EEA) country used to enjoy one of the simplest routes onto the UK medical register — a route built entirely on automatic mutual recognition, with no verification queue and no licensing exam. Brexit ended that automatic arrangement for most new applicants, and the General Medical Council (GMC) now assesses the great majority of EU-qualified doctors under broadly the same evidence-based process used for every other international medical graduate (IMG). Ireland, however, is not a simple copy-paste of the wider EU story — its qualifications occupy a genuinely distinct position that deserves its own explanation rather than a footnote.

This guide sets out, in plain and precise terms, what actually changed, when it changed, who is still covered by transitional protection, what the current process looks like for a doctor qualifying in the EU or EEA today, and exactly how the Irish position differs. It is written for doctors who trained anywhere in the EU/EEA or in Ireland and are weighing up a move into UK practice, whether that means registering directly with full registration or preparing for the UK Medical Licensing Assessment (UKMLA) as part of the standard international route.

Because this is a regulatory change with real financial and timeline consequences — the wrong assumption can mean months of unnecessary exam preparation, or conversely, missing a genuine shortcut a doctor is actually entitled to use — every claim in this guide is grounded in the GMC's own published position, with the primary source linked throughout.

GMC Registration for EU and Irish Doctors: The Quick Answer

GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors today depends primarily on one factor: whether the specific qualification, from the specific awarding country, appears on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification (REQ) list, which was fixed as it stood under EU law on 31 December 2020. If it does, the holder can generally still apply for full registration without sitting the UKMLA or the older PLAB test, in much the same way EU-qualified doctors always could. If it does not — which now applies to the large majority of newly graduating EU/EEA doctors — the standard international registration route applies instead: primary medical qualification (PMQ) verification, accepted evidence of English language proficiency, and the UKMLA (the Applied Knowledge Test and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment), exactly as any other IMG would complete.

Ireland sits in a materially different position from the rest of the EU for one simple reason: Ireland remains an EU member state, so Irish primary medical qualifications, and Irish specialist and GP qualifications issued by the Medical Council of Ireland, generally remain on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list on an ongoing basis, rather than only for qualifications frozen at the December 2020 cut-off in the way most other EU countries' newer graduates now find themselves. In practice, this means a substantial share of Irish-trained doctors can still use the REQ pathway into full GMC registration without the UKMLA, provided their specific qualification and awarding body appear on the current list.

The Pre-Brexit Position: How Automatic Recognition Used to Work

Before the end of the Brexit transition period, GMC registration for doctors qualified anywhere in the EEA or Switzerland worked on a fundamentally different basis from registration for doctors qualified outside Europe. Under EU law — principally the mutual recognition provisions of Directive 2005/36/EC on the recognition of professional qualifications — a primary medical qualification awarded by a recognised institution in any EEA member state (or Switzerland, under a parallel bilateral agreement) had to be accepted by every other member state's medical regulator, the GMC included, without a separate assessment of its content or standard.

This meant that a doctor who qualified in, for example, Italy, Romania, or the Netherlands could apply directly for full GMC registration on the strength of their primary medical qualification and did not need to sit PLAB (the Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board test, the UKMLA's predecessor for IMGs) at all. Specialist and GP qualifications benefited from a similar mutual recognition mechanism, provided the specific national qualification appeared on an EU-wide reference list. The determining factor throughout this period was, broadly, where a doctor's qualification had been awarded within the EEA/Swiss area — nationality itself was not usually the operative test, though in practice the two frequently aligned.

Why This Route Existed

The underlying rationale was straightforward: EU member states had, over decades, harmonised minimum training standards for medical education through directive-level requirements on curriculum length, content, and clinical training hours. Because every member state's medical schools were held to broadly equivalent minimum standards under this shared legal framework, regulators across the EEA extended reciprocal trust to one another's primary and postgraduate qualifications, rather than re-verifying each one individually the way non-EEA qualifications were (and still are) verified.

This is also why, historically, EEA-qualified doctors formed one of the largest single blocks of new joiners to the GMC register each year — the absence of an exam requirement, combined with genuine EU-wide harmonisation of training standards, made the UK an unusually accessible destination for European medical graduates compared with most other developed healthcare systems.

What Changed After Brexit

The change did not happen gradually — it took effect at a specific point tied to the end of the UK's post-referendum transition arrangements. From 31 December 2020, the automatic recognition mechanism under EU mutual recognition law stopped applying to new GMC registration applications in the way it always had. The GMC's own published position states plainly that, from this date, doctors relying purely on the fact of holding an EEA primary qualification could no longer expect automatic recognition on that basis alone.

Procedurally, the most important shift was this: the basis for assessing an application moved from a doctor's nationality to where their qualification was actually obtained. Before the change, being an EEA national with an EEA qualification was, in effect, the qualifying combination. After the change, nationality became largely irrelevant to how a primary medical qualification is assessed — what matters is whether the specific qualification, from the specific awarding institution and country, is captured by the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list, which was fixed as it stood under the recognition directive on 31 December 2020.

The Relevant European Qualification (REQ) List Explained

Rather than abolishing recognition of European qualifications outright, the GMC effectively froze the pre-Brexit recognition framework into a standing reference document: the Relevant European Qualification list. This list sets out, country by country, which primary medical qualifications, and which specialist and GP qualifications, continue to be treated as automatically acceptable evidence of training — mirroring what EU mutual recognition law required as of the end of the transition period.

A doctor whose qualification appears on the current REQ list can still apply for full registration (or provisional or specialist/GP registration, depending on career stage) without the UKMLA, using the GMC's dedicated application guide for holders of a relevant European qualification. This is the single most important practical fact for any EU-qualified doctor to establish early: checking REQ status is not optional background reading, it is the fork in the road that determines whether the entire registration journey involves an exam at all.

Because the REQ list mirrors the pre-Brexit legal position as it stood on a fixed date, it functions differently from a simple "which EU countries are recognised" question. A qualification can be on the list because the awarding country's training standards were captured by the recognition directive at the cut-off point, while a newer qualification structure introduced by that same country afterwards may not automatically inherit REQ status without being separately added. This is precisely why doctors should not assume that "my country is in the EU" settles the question — the specific qualification and awarding body must be checked against the current list.

Doctors Who Are Not Covered by the REQ List

For the substantial majority of doctors completing EU/EEA medical training now, particularly newer graduates whose qualification structure or awarding institution is not captured by the frozen REQ list, GMC registration works through the same standard international route used by IMGs from outside Europe entirely. This is the single biggest practical consequence of the post-Brexit change, and it is worth stating without euphemism: for most newly qualifying EU doctors today, there is no shortcut around primary source verification, English language evidence, and the UKMLA. The route is entirely navigable and well-trodden by thousands of IMGs every year, but it is a materially different — and longer — journey than the one European doctors could rely on before 2021.

Transitional Provisions: Who Still Has Protection

Brexit-related regulatory changes rarely apply with a hard, retrospective cut-off, and GMC registration is no exception. Several categories of doctor retain some form of continuing protection or recognition beyond the general end of automatic recognition, and it is worth understanding each precisely rather than assuming either "nothing changed for me" or "everything changed for me" by default.

Applications Submitted Before the Transition Deadline

Doctors who had already submitted a GMC registration application before the end of the transition period generally had that application completed under the rules in force at the time of submission, rather than being retrospectively assessed against the post-transition framework. This transitional courtesy meant that a doctor mid-application when the rules changed was not disadvantaged purely by administrative timing.

Doctors With Pre-Existing Qualifying Rights

Doctors who had already established qualifying rights under the pre-Brexit framework — for example, EEA nationals who had already begun exercising treaty rights, or family members of EEA/UK nationals with rights that had crystallised before the cut-off — may retain a route to registration reflecting those pre-existing rights, assessed on the specific circumstances of the individual case rather than through the standard online application process. The GMC has been explicit that this category is handled individually, often requiring direct contact with a GMC registration specialist rather than a straightforward self-service application, since evidence requirements vary by circumstance.

Switzerland's Distinct Transitional Position

Switzerland sits slightly apart from the wider EEA picture because UK–Swiss mutual recognition was never governed by EU membership in the first place, but by a separate bilateral agreement. Under the UK–Switzerland Citizens' Rights Agreement, Swiss-qualified doctors retained continuing access to GMC registration recognition for a defined transitional period following the end of free movement, reflecting the different legal footing Switzerland occupies relative to full EU/EEA membership. Doctors relying on this route should check the GMC's current guidance directly, since transitional windows of this kind are, by definition, time-limited rather than indefinite.

The practical takeaway across all three transitional categories is the same: none of them can be assumed from general knowledge of "how Brexit worked." Each depends on specific, checkable facts about an individual doctor's qualification date, application date, and prior UK connection — precisely the kind of detail that is worth confirming directly with the GMC's registration enquiries service before committing to any exam preparation or relocation plans.

GMC Registration for EU-Qualified Doctors Without REQ Status: The Current Process

For the majority of EU/EEA-qualified doctors applying today — those whose qualification is not on the Relevant European Qualification list — the registration journey now mirrors the standard international medical graduate pathway closely enough that it is worth walking through each stage in order, exactly as it would be explained to a doctor trained in India, Nigeria, or anywhere else outside Europe.

Step One: Primary Medical Qualification Verification

The first substantive step is confirming that the primary medical qualification itself is acceptable and independently verified. This has two components. First, the awarding medical school must appear in the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS), the internationally maintained database that confirms an institution is a genuine, recognised medical school for the relevant period. Second, the individual doctor's specific degree certificate and transcripts must go through primary source verification — most commonly via the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC) service, now largely operated through the Intealth/MyIntealth platform, which contacts the awarding institution directly to confirm the documents match its own internal records. The site's dedicated guide to how GMC recognition of a primary medical qualification actually works covers this verification process in full detail, including what to do if a medical school's listing status is unclear or a verification request stalls.

This is, in practical terms, the single biggest procedural change most EU doctors experience post-Brexit: a step that simply did not previously exist for EEA-qualified applicants now applies in full, with the same document standards, the same reliance on institutional responsiveness, and the same timing unpredictability that non-EEA IMGs have long had to plan around.

Step Two: English Language Evidence

Every applicant without an accepted exemption must provide evidence of English language proficiency, typically through an accepted IELTS or OET score at the GMC's required thresholds, or through recognised prior medical practice or education conducted substantially in English. A small number of applicants qualify for a genuine exemption route based on very specific criteria — the site's detailed guide to the GMC English language exemption for IMGs sets out exactly who can realistically claim it and what evidence the GMC actually accepts, since the criteria are narrower than many applicants assume. For most EU doctors trained in a language other than English, this represents a genuinely new requirement compared with the pre-Brexit position, where fluency was generally assumed rather than formally tested as part of the registration application itself.

Step Three: Sitting the UKMLA

Where REQ status does not apply, EU-qualified doctors must pass both components of the UK Medical Licensing Assessment: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), a computer-based written examination of clinical knowledge and applied reasoning, and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), a practical, scenario-based assessment of clinical and communication skills. Both exams are identical in content and standard to those sat by every other IMG and by UK medical students in their final year, and both must be booked, prepared for, and passed before an applicant can proceed to full registration. There is no separate, easier version of the UKMLA for EU-qualified doctors — the assessment itself does not distinguish between an applicant's country of origin once REQ status is off the table.

Step Four: Submitting the Full Registration Application

Once qualification verification, English language evidence, and (where applicable) UKMLA success are all in place, the doctor submits a complete registration application with all supporting evidence for GMC assessment. Application processing itself, once every piece of evidence is genuinely complete, is typically the fastest single stage of the overall journey — the site's guide to how long GMC registration actually takes breaks down realistic timelines for each stage and explains why verification delays, not the GMC's own decision-making, are usually the true bottleneck for internationally trained applicants, EU-qualified doctors now included.

The Distinct Position of Irish-Qualified Doctors

Ireland deserves separate, careful treatment in any discussion of post-Brexit GMC registration, because conflating it with the general EU position risks either overstating or understating what Irish-qualified doctors are actually entitled to. The distinction rests on one structural fact: Ireland remained an EU member state after the UK left, so Ireland's position relative to the GMC's Relevant European Qualification framework did not experience the same kind of rupture that affected, say, a doctor qualifying in a country whose newer training structures fell outside the frozen REQ list.

Why Ireland Is Different From the Rest of the EU Story

Because Irish primary medical qualifications, and Irish specialist and GP qualifications issued by the Medical Council of Ireland, were captured by the EU recognition directive as it stood at the 31 December 2020 cut-off, they generally continue to appear on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list on an ongoing basis, in the way the list was designed to preserve. A doctor holding a primary medical qualification from an Irish medical school recognised by the Medical Council of Ireland — including the medical schools of Trinity College Dublin, University College Dublin, the Royal College of Surgeons in Ireland (RCSI), University of Galway, and University College Cork, among others — can generally still apply for full GMC registration through the REQ application route, without sitting the UKMLA, provided their specific qualification remains on the current list and every other registration condition (English language evidence, any required internship or supervised-practice evidence) is met.

This is a genuinely more favourable position than the one most other EU-qualified doctors now find themselves in, and it reflects Ireland's continued EU membership rather than any UK–Ireland-specific side agreement. It is worth being precise about this, since some informal discussion online attributes the Irish exception to the Common Travel Area (CTA) — the long-standing UK–Ireland arrangement allowing free movement, residence, and work rights between the two countries without a visa. The CTA is real, unaffected by Brexit, and relevant to an Irish citizen's general right to live and work in the UK — but the specific mechanism that keeps most Irish medical qualifications recognised for GMC purposes is Ireland's continuing EU membership and its consequent presence on the REQ list, not the CTA itself. Both facts point in the same favourable direction for Irish-qualified doctors, but they are legally distinct, and an applicant should not assume CTA rights alone settle a qualification-recognition question.

What Irish-Qualified Doctors Still Need to Confirm

REQ status is not automatic simply because a doctor trained in Ireland — it depends on the specific qualification and awarding body appearing on the GMC's current list, exactly as with any other EEA country. Doctors with newer Irish qualification structures, or qualifications from institutions not captured by the frozen list, should check their specific position directly against the GMC's published REQ list rather than assuming Irish training automatically clears the bar. Doctors who previously held provisional registration and completed an internship in Ireland typically also need a Certificate of Experience from the Medical Council of Ireland as supporting evidence, alongside their primary qualification documentation.

Irish-qualified doctors relying on REQ status still need to satisfy every other standard registration requirement — accepted English language evidence (though many Irish-trained doctors will have completed their entire medical education in English, this must still be evidenced in the form the GMC accepts) and a complete application with all supporting documentation. REQ status removes the UKMLA requirement specifically; it does not remove every other condition set out on the site's UKMLA eligibility requirements pillar page.

Irish Doctors Who Fall Outside REQ Status

Where an Irish qualification genuinely does not appear on the current REQ list — a less common scenario than for most other EU countries, but not impossible, particularly for newer or restructured qualification pathways — the doctor follows the same standard international route as any other IMG: PMQ verification through WDOMS and EPIC/Intealth, English language evidence, and the UKMLA. The underlying process does not differ from the general EU position covered earlier in this guide; what differs for Ireland is simply that a meaningfully larger proportion of Irish-qualified doctors are likely to find their qualification already covered by REQ status in the first place, given Ireland's continued EU membership.

How to Check Whether a Specific Qualification Has REQ Status

Given how much of this guide depends on a single fork — REQ-listed or not — it is worth setting out the practical steps for checking a specific qualification's status directly, rather than relying on general assumptions about "my country" or "my degree type."

  • Identify the exact qualification name and awarding body as they appear on the original degree certificate, since the REQ list is organised by specific qualification and issuing authority, not simply by country.
  • Check the GMC's current Relevant European Qualification list for the awarding country, confirming the specific primary medical, specialist, or GP qualification is listed, since primary and postgraduate qualifications are assessed separately.
  • Note the qualification date where relevant, since the REQ list reflects the recognition position as it stood on 31 December 2020 — a qualification structure introduced or substantially changed by the awarding country afterwards may not automatically carry the same status without separate confirmation.
  • Where status is unclear, raise a direct enquiry with the GMC's registration team before committing to either the REQ application route or a full course of UKMLA preparation, since an incorrect assumption in either direction carries a real cost in wasted time or unnecessary exam fees.
  • Keep dated records of any enquiry and its response, since this becomes useful supporting evidence if a qualification's status is later queried during the formal application itself.

GMC Registration for EU and Irish Doctors: UK Medical Students vs IMGs

Because this site serves both UK medical students and internationally trained doctors, it is worth being explicit about how this entire topic applies to each audience. UK medical students graduating from a UK medical school are unaffected by any of the changes described in this guide — a UK primary medical qualification has always been, and remains, directly recognised by the GMC without reference to EU mutual recognition law at all, since UK medical schools' curricula are directly overseen by the GMC itself.

For IMGs more broadly — including EU/EEA and Irish-qualified doctors specifically covered in this guide — the post-Brexit landscape is the one that matters. A doctor who trained in continental Europe or Ireland and is now researching UK registration should treat their situation as a specific sub-case of the wider IMG journey, checking REQ status first before assuming either that "EU training means no exam" (no longer generally true) or that "Brexit means I definitely need the UKMLA" (not true for REQ-listed qualifications, including most Irish ones).

How the EU/Irish Route Compares With Other International Routes

It is easy for a doctor trained in Europe to assume their situation is unique, but in practical terms the post-Brexit EU/EEA position now has more in common with other international routes than it does with the pre-2021 EEA arrangement it replaced. Understanding that parallel is genuinely useful, because the site's country-specific guides for other regions of the world describe, in granular detail, exactly the process an EU doctor without REQ status now has to follow.

The guide to UKMLA registration for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors, for example, walks through PMQ verification, English language evidence, and UKMLA preparation for doctors trained at English-medium Caribbean medical schools — structurally the same three pillars an EU doctor outside the REQ list now has to complete, even though the underlying qualification itself was earned in an entirely different part of the world. Similarly, the site's guide to UKMLA registration for Kenyan and East African doctors sets out how KMPDC-registered doctors approach WDOMS listing, primary source verification, and the exam itself — again, the same sequence a non-REQ EU applicant now works through. The point of the comparison is not that these are interchangeable applicant groups, but that the post-Brexit change effectively brought EU/EEA doctors (outside the REQ list) into the same well-established, well-documented process that IMGs from every other part of the world have long used — nothing about the standard route is untested or improvised, even though it may feel unfamiliar to a European doctor encountering it for the first time.

This also means EU doctors researching their own registration journey can usefully draw on the wider body of country-specific guidance already published for IMGs generally, rather than assuming their situation requires an entirely separate body of advice. Realistic timelines, common verification pitfalls, and English language evidence requirements are, in the vast majority of cases, identical regardless of which country outside the REQ list a doctor's qualification happens to come from.

Practical Planning: Costs, Timelines, and Sequencing

Whichever side of the REQ line an EU or Irish-qualified doctor falls on, practical planning for GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors matters as much as understanding the legal position. This section sets out how to approach the financial and scheduling side of the decision, since the two routes described throughout this guide differ substantially in both cost and duration.

Costs to Expect on the Standard International Route

A doctor following the standard route — because their qualification is not REQ-listed — should budget for several distinct cost items: the PMQ verification fee charged by the Intealth/EPIC service, an accepted English language test (IELTS or OET) if not already exempt, the GMC registration application fee itself, and the UKMLA fees for both the Applied Knowledge Test and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment. None of these fees are refundable simply because an applicant later discovers they were, in fact, REQ-eligible — which is precisely why establishing REQ status first, before booking any exam or paying any verification fee, is the financially sensible order of operations rather than an optional nicety.

By contrast, a doctor confirmed as REQ-eligible avoids the UKMLA fees and the PMQ verification fee entirely, since neither the exam nor the WDOMS/EPIC verification process forms part of the REQ application route. English language evidence costs still apply in most cases, and the GMC's own registration application fee is payable regardless of which route is used.

Realistic Timelines for Each Route

The REQ route, where it applies, is generally the faster of the two paths into full registration, since it removes the single longest and least predictable stage of the standard journey — UKMLA preparation and sitting both exam components. Doctors going through REQ still need to allow time for English language evidence gathering and standard application processing, but without the months of exam preparation that dominate the standard-route timeline for most applicants.

Doctors following the standard route should plan on a timeline shaped primarily by two variable factors: how quickly their medical school responds to a primary source verification request, and how much dedicated preparation time they realistically need for the UKMLA. Application processing itself, once every piece of evidence is complete, tends to be the shortest and most predictable stage of the entire journey — a pattern explored in full in the site's guide to how long GMC registration actually takes, which applies equally to EU-qualified doctors on the standard route as it does to any other IMG.

Sequencing Advice for GMC Registration for EU and Irish Doctors

  • Confirm REQ status first, before spending money on English tests, verification services, or UKMLA preparation courses — this single check determines which of two materially different cost and timeline profiles applies.
  • If REQ status does not apply, start PMQ verification immediately, in parallel with UKMLA preparation rather than sequentially after it, since verification response times are outside the applicant's control and frequently become the longest-running item on the critical path.
  • Book English language testing early if evidence is not already in place, since accepted test scores typically carry a validity window and sitting the test too early relative to the rest of the application can create its own timing problem.
  • Do not assume Irish or EU training automatically shortens the process — confirm the specific qualification's REQ status directly rather than working backwards from an assumption about the country of training.

Common Mistakes EU and Irish Doctors Make

Because this is a genuinely new landscape compared with the pre-Brexit position, certain avoidable mistakes recur often enough among doctors researching GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors that they are worth naming directly.

Assuming Pre-Brexit Rules Still Apply

The most common and costly mistake is assuming that EU/EEA training still guarantees automatic recognition, exactly as it did before 2021. Doctors who proceed on this assumption sometimes discover only at the point of application — after investing time in relocation planning — that their specific qualification requires the full standard route. Checking REQ status at the very start of the planning process avoids this entirely.

Assuming the Opposite: That Brexit Removed All EU Advantages

The reverse mistake is equally common and equally costly: assuming Brexit ended every form of European recognition, and consequently investing in a full UKMLA preparation course without first checking whether the specific qualification is, in fact, still REQ-listed. This is a particular risk for Irish-qualified doctors, given how frequently informal online discussion conflates the general EU position with the distinct, more favourable Irish one.

Confusing the Common Travel Area With Qualification Recognition

As covered earlier in this guide, the Common Travel Area governs the right to live and work in the UK or Ireland without a visa — it does not, on its own, determine whether a specific Irish medical qualification is recognised by the GMC. Doctors who assume CTA rights alone settle the registration question sometimes skip the REQ list check entirely, only to find the actual determining factor was something else.

Not Checking the Specific Qualification, Only the Country

Because the REQ list is organised by specific qualification and awarding body rather than by country as a whole, assuming that "my country is covered" without checking the specific degree and institution is a recurring source of confusion — this applies equally within the EU generally and within Ireland specifically, since even EU member states can have some qualification structures on the list and others not, depending on how they were captured by the recognition directive at the relevant date.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do EU-qualified doctors still need to take the UKMLA after Brexit?

Most EU-qualified doctors now need to take the UKMLA, because automatic recognition based on nationality ended on 31 December 2020. The exception is doctors whose specific qualification appears on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list, which preserves recognition for qualifications captured by EU mutual recognition law as it stood at that date — these doctors can still apply for full registration without the UKMLA.

Is GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors the same process?

Not entirely. Both groups are assessed against the same Relevant European Qualification list mechanism, but because Ireland remains an EU member state, a larger proportion of Irish primary medical qualifications and Medical Council of Ireland-issued specialist and GP qualifications continue to appear on that list, meaning more Irish-qualified doctors retain the no-UKMLA route than doctors from EU countries generally.

What is the Relevant European Qualification list and how do I check it?

The Relevant European Qualification (REQ) list is the GMC's record of which EEA and Swiss primary, specialist, and GP qualifications continue to be recognised for UK registration purposes, fixed as they stood under EU recognition law on 31 December 2020. Doctors should check their specific qualification and awarding country against the GMC's current published list, or raise a direct enquiry with the GMC's registration team if status is unclear.

Do Irish doctors need to sit PLAB or the UKMLA to register with the GMC?

Irish doctors holding a primary medical qualification that appears on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list generally do not need to sit the UKMLA (or, previously, PLAB), and can apply for full registration directly. Irish doctors whose specific qualification is not REQ-listed follow the standard international route, which does include the UKMLA.

Does the Common Travel Area mean Irish medical qualifications are automatically recognised by the GMC?

No. The Common Travel Area gives Irish and UK citizens broader rights to live and work in each other's country without a visa, but it is not the legal mechanism behind GMC qualification recognition. Irish qualifications remain on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list because Ireland continues to be an EU member state, which is a separate legal basis from the Common Travel Area.

What happens if my EU qualification was obtained after 31 December 2020?

A qualification obtained after that date is not automatically excluded from REQ status, but it must still be checked against the GMC's current list for the awarding country and qualification type, since the list is not a simple "before or after" cut-off — it reflects which specific qualifications and awarding bodies were captured by EU recognition law as it stood at that point. Where a newer qualification structure is not covered, the doctor follows the standard international registration route, including the UKMLA.

Can family members of EEA nationals still claim any transitional recognition rights?

Some doctors with pre-existing qualifying rights established before 31 December 2020 — including certain family members of EEA or UK nationals — may retain a route reflecting those pre-existing rights, assessed on individual circumstances rather than through the standard online application. This is handled case by case by the GMC's registration team, so anyone in this position should raise a direct enquiry rather than assuming either automatic protection or automatic loss of rights.

Conclusion

GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors no longer runs on the single, simple automatic-recognition track it did before 2021 — but it has not collapsed into an undifferentiated version of the standard IMG route either. The real, checkable question for any doctor in this position is whether their specific qualification appears on the GMC's Relevant European Qualification list: if it does, the pre-Brexit-style route into full registration, without the UKMLA, generally remains open, and Irish-qualified doctors are considerably more likely than most other EU-trained doctors to find themselves in that position, given Ireland's continued EU membership. If it does not, the standard international pathway — PMQ verification, English language evidence, and the UKMLA — applies in full, exactly as it would for any other internationally trained doctor. Establishing which side of that line a qualification falls on, early and directly with the GMC, is the single most useful step any EU or Irish-qualified doctor can take before committing time or money to either exam preparation or an application.
