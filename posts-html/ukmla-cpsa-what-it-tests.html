The UKMLA CPSA — the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment — is the practical, face-to-face component of the UK Medical Licensing Assessment, and for many candidates it is both the most daunting and the most rewarding part of the entire licensing journey. Unlike the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), which you can sit at a test centre anywhere in the world, the CPSA places you in a room with trained simulated patients and clinical examiners who observe, in real time, whether you can apply your medical knowledge as a safe, communicative, and professional Foundation Year 1 doctor. No amount of theoretical preparation fully substitutes for practising the physical and interpersonal demands of an OSCE circuit under exam conditions — and understanding exactly what is tested, how it is marked, and what examiners are looking for is the foundation of effective preparation.

For UK medical students, the CPSA is integrated into the final year of the degree programme at their medical school, typically delivered in a similar format to existing OSCEs, often on familiar ground with trusted faculty. For International Medical Graduates (IMGs), the CPSA is taken via the PLAB 2 route at the GMC's dedicated Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester — a purpose-built facility staffed by professional simulated patients and trained examiners. The experience can be unfamiliar and pressured, particularly for candidates who have never experienced a UK-style OSCE, which is why preparation for the CPSA demands both clinical competence and emotional readiness.

This guide provides a comprehensive breakdown of the CPSA format, station types, marking criteria, scoring, logistics, preparation strategies, and the specific communication behaviours that consistently earn marks. By the time you finish reading, you will have a clear picture of what the CPSA is testing and how to demonstrate it confidently.

CPSA Format: Stations, Timing, and Circuit Structure

The CPSA is structured as an OSCE (Objective Structured Clinical Examination) comprising 18 stations of 8 minutes each. Candidates rotate through the stations in a circuit, with brief transition times between stations to read the instructions for the next scenario posted outside the station door. The total assessed time in the CPSA is therefore 144 minutes (18 stations Ã— 8 minutes), with additional time for transitions, briefings, and administrative procedures.

Each station is fully self-contained: you enter, address the scenario presented, and exit when the bell sounds. There is no carryover between stations — a poor performance at one station does not affect the assessment at the next. This is an important psychological fact that experienced OSCE candidates use to their advantage: whatever happened in the previous station is irrelevant the moment you cross the threshold of the next one. The circuit is designed so that consecutive stations test different skill types, preventing fatigue in any single domain.

What Each CPSA Station Type May Assess

CPSA stations are designed to mirror the realistic clinical situations a Foundation Year 1 doctor would encounter. The GMC does not publish a definitive list of station types used in each sitting, but based on the MLA Content Map and the structure of PLAB 2, stations will typically assess one or more of the following skill categories.

History-Taking Stations

These stations present you with a patient (simulated) who has a clinical complaint. Your task is to take a focused, structured history within the time available. Good history-taking at CPSA level means: introducing yourself professionally, establishing rapport quickly, using open questions to elicit the presenting complaint, covering the key relevant history components (symptom onset, character, radiation, severity, timing, associated symptoms, relevant past history, medications, allergies, family history, social history where relevant), and demonstrating you are listening actively. Candidates who launch immediately into a rapid-fire checklist of closed questions without establishing rapport, or who run out of time before covering the essentials, score poorly.

Communication and Counselling Stations

These stations test your ability to communicate effectively in a clinical context. Scenarios may include: breaking bad news to a patient about a new diagnosis; explaining an investigation and obtaining consent; discussing a treatment plan and addressing concerns; navigating a patient who is anxious, angry, or in denial; or speaking to a carer or family member about a patient's condition. Communication stations are assessed heavily on both the process (how you communicate — tone, pace, empathy, checking understanding, non-verbal behaviour) and the content (whether you covered the medically necessary points accurately and completely).

Clinical Examination Stations

These stations ask you to examine a patient (simulated or standardised) or a clinical model. Systems typically include cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, neurological (upper limb, lower limb, cranial nerves), and musculoskeletal. The marking scheme for examination stations rewards: appropriate clinical setup (positioning, exposure, patient comfort, informed consent), a systematic and fluent examination technique, correct interpretation of findings, and professional communication throughout. Candidates who miss required examination steps, use incorrect technique, or fail to communicate findings to the examiner will lose marks.

Practical Procedures Stations

These stations assess competency in clinical procedures on task trainers or mannequins. Procedures tested at CPSA level may include: venepuncture, peripheral venous cannulation, basic life support (BLS) and advanced life support (ALS), urinary catheterisation, ECG electrode placement and interpretation, blood pressure measurement, peak flow measurement, and injection technique. As with examination stations, the marking scheme rewards correct technique, appropriate patient communication, and safety behaviours (hand hygiene, sharps safety, patient identification).

Clinical Reasoning and Decision-Making Stations

These stations present a clinical scenario and require you to demonstrate your reasoning process — for example, reviewing investigation results and formulating a management plan, or assessing a deteriorating patient and deciding on escalation. These stations test the integration of clinical knowledge with decision-making under time pressure, and they often reward candidates who think out loud, narrate their reasoning to the examiner, and demonstrate awareness of when to seek senior support.

Prescribing Stations

Some CPSA sittings include stations requiring the candidate to write or review a prescription, calculate a drug dose, or identify a prescribing error. These stations test safe prescribing practice including: correct drug name, dose, route, frequency, duration; appropriate monitoring; and awareness of contraindications and interactions. Prescribing stations are typically marked by a combination of checklist (correct entries) and global rating (did the candidate demonstrate safe prescribing behaviours overall).

What Examiners Are Looking For: The Marking Scheme

Each CPSA station is marked using a dual marking scheme consisting of a structured checklist and a global rating. Understanding both elements helps candidates understand what to prioritise during preparation.

The Structured Checklist

The checklist is a list of specific tasks, behaviours, or statements that the examiner records as present or absent during the station. For a history-taking station, checklist items might include things like: asked about onset of symptoms; asked about associated chest pain; asked about medication history; explained what you were doing next. For a procedure station, checklist items might include: washed hands before starting; verified patient identity; obtained verbal consent; used correct technique for step X. Checklist items are typically binary — the examiner marks them as done or not done. Candidates can significantly improve their checklist scores by practising with a structured approach to each station type, ensuring they systematically cover the expected components within the eight-minute window.

The Global Rating Scale

The global rating is the examiner's holistic judgment of the candidate's performance on a scale (typically 1–5 or similar). It reflects qualities that resist full capture in a checklist: the overall quality of communication, the degree of patient-centredness, clinical reasoning depth, professionalism under pressure, and the extent to which the candidate performed at the level expected of a competent Foundation Year 1 doctor. A candidate who ticks all the checklist items but does so in a cold, robotic, or rushed manner may score poorly on the global rating; a candidate who misses one checklist item but demonstrates excellent clinical reasoning and patient communication throughout may still pass the station comfortably.

How CPSA Scores Are Calculated and the Pass/Fail Decision

The pass/fail decision for the CPSA is based on overall circuit performance, not performance at individual stations. This means you can perform poorly at one or two stations without necessarily failing the overall circuit, as long as your cumulative performance across all 18 stations meets the standard. The pass mark for the CPSA is set using criterion-referenced standard setting, analogous to the AKT, and varies slightly between sittings.

Candidates who fail the CPSA receive a structured feedback report that shows their performance across station types and domains. This feedback is genuinely useful for re-preparation: it identifies whether weakness lies predominantly in clinical examination technique, communication skills, clinical reasoning, or procedural competence, allowing targeted practice in the areas that cost the most marks.

CPSA Preparation: Timeline and Methods

Effective CPSA preparation requires more lead time and a more active preparation style than the AKT. Most candidates benefit from a dedicated CPSA preparation period of eight to twelve weeks, ideally including a mix of solo practice, partner role-play, and structured feedback from experienced practitioners.

Solo Preparation

Begin by reviewing the MLA Content Map's practical skills list and ensuring you can describe the correct technique for each listed skill. Use anatomy and clinical skills textbooks (Oxford Handbook of Clinical Medicine, Macleod's Clinical Examination) to review examination technique. Watch structured clinical skills videos to see correct technique demonstrated. Write out frameworks for history-taking for each major presentation type, and practise reciting these frameworks aloud to build fluency.

Partner Role-Play Practice

Role-play with a preparation partner is the most valuable CPSA preparation activity. Set up mock stations with a partner taking the role of the simulated patient, using scenario prompts from OSCE preparation books or online resources. Use a timer set to eight minutes. After each station, exchange detailed feedback using the examiner's dual marking scheme. Rotate roles so both partners develop feedback skills as well as station skills. Candidates who practise this way weekly throughout their preparation period consistently report feeling dramatically more confident in the real circuit.

Video Review

Recording yourself performing clinical skills and reviewing the footage is an underused but highly effective preparation tool. Watching your own performance reveals habits you cannot detect in real time: poor eye contact, rushed examination technique, missed steps, unclear communication. Reviewing even two or three recordings of your history-taking or examination technique can yield insights that hours of solo study do not.

In-Person Preparation Courses

For IMGs preparing for PLAB 2 / CPSA in Manchester, structured in-person preparation courses are widely available in the UK and range from one-day introductory sessions to two-week intensive programmes. These courses typically provide professional simulated patients, mock circuits, examiner feedback, and technique workshops. The investment (£500–£1,500 for a quality course) is often justified by the significant reduction in re-sit risk it provides.

CPSA Logistics for IMGs: Booking and the Manchester Experience

The GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester is a purpose-built OSCE facility that runs PLAB 2 / CPSA sittings continuously throughout the year. IMGs must have passed PLAB 1 (AKT) before booking PLAB 2. Booking is done through GMC Online, and the £1,036 fee is payable at the time of booking. Availability at the Manchester centre is in high demand; popular dates can be booked out months in advance, so booking as early as possible after receiving your PLAB 1 pass result is strongly recommended.

On the day of the CPSA, candidates should arrive at the centre with their valid identification (passport for international candidates). Dress code is smart professional attire — typically smart trousers or skirt, shirt or blouse, and appropriate footwear. No clinical uniforms are required (the centre provides these if needed). Bring nothing unnecessary into the assessment area; a small bag can be stored in a designated area. Arrive at least 30 minutes before your start time; late arrivals may be unable to sit.

Top Communication Phrases That Score Marks

Specific phrases and communication behaviours that recur in high-scoring CPSA performances include:

  • Introduction: "Hello, my name is [name] and I'm one of the doctors here today. Would it be all right if I [took a history / examined you / discussed your results] with you?"
  • Signposting: "I'd like to start by asking you some questions about your symptoms, and then I'll examine you. Does that sound okay?"
  • Empathy: "That sounds very difficult for you. I'm sorry to hear you've been going through this."
  • Checking understanding: "I've covered quite a lot of information there — what questions do you have?" / "Can I check that I've explained that clearly enough?"
  • Escalation: "I'd like to get one of my senior colleagues involved because I want to make sure we give you the best care possible."
  • Safety-netting: "If things change or you have any concerns before your next appointment, please don't hesitate to come back or call us."
  • Consent: "Before I examine you, I'd like to explain what I'm going to do. Is that all right with you?"

Frequently Asked Questions About the UKMLA CPSA

Can I fail one CPSA station and still pass overall?

Yes. The CPSA pass/fail decision is based on cumulative circuit performance across all 18 stations, not on whether every individual station is passed. Poor performance at one or two stations can be compensated by strong performance across the remainder of the circuit. This design prevents the loss of a single station — perhaps due to nerves, an unfamiliar scenario, or a temporary lapse — from determining the overall outcome. However, consistently weak performance across multiple stations will result in a fail, and candidates should aim for confident, competent performance in every station rather than banking on compensation from strong stations.

How do I book the CPSA as an IMG in Manchester?

You must have passed PLAB 1 (AKT) before you can book PLAB 2 (CPSA). Log in to your GMC Online account, navigate to the PLAB 2 booking section, select your preferred sitting date and time at the Manchester Clinical Assessment Centre, and pay the £1,036 fee. Confirmation is sent by email with details of the centre address, arrival instructions, and what to bring on the day. GMC advises booking as early as possible as dates are in high demand.

What is the pass rate for the CPSA / PLAB 2?

Historical PLAB 2 pass rates have ranged approximately 65–78% for first-attempt IMG candidates. The pass rate for UK medical students in their CPSA is estimated at 85–92%, reflecting the advantage of completing a five-year UK curriculum that includes structured OSCE training throughout. The CPSA is designed so that a well-prepared candidate who has practised OSCE stations systematically should comfortably meet the Foundation-level standard.

Can I receive feedback on my CPSA performance after failing?

Yes. Candidates who fail the CPSA receive a structured performance feedback report that shows their scores across the different station types and domains assessed in the circuit. This feedback is sent with the result notification and provides a domain-level breakdown (e.g., communication, clinical examination, professional behaviour, practical skills) that identifies where the candidate's performance fell below the standard. This feedback should be treated as a precise re-preparation guide: invest the most study time in the domains where the feedback indicates the greatest shortfall.

What should I wear to the CPSA in Manchester?

Smart professional attire is expected for the CPSA. Think in terms of NHS clinical professional dress: smart trousers or skirt, a shirt or blouse, and appropriate closed-toe shoes. Clinical uniform or scrubs are not required — the GMC centre provides these if needed. Avoid jewellery that might interfere with clinical examination (rings that could catch on a stethoscope, long necklaces). Your clothing should be clean, professional, and practical enough to allow you to move comfortably during examination stations.

Conclusion

The UKMLA CPSA is unlike any written examination — it is a live performance assessment that tests clinical competence, communication, and professionalism in real time, under observation, across eighteen consecutive clinical scenarios. The candidates who perform best are not necessarily those with the deepest specialist knowledge, but those who have systematically practised the skills the CPSA tests, understand what examiners are looking for, and have the emotional readiness to perform consistently through a demanding circuit.

Preparation for the CPSA should be active, social, and realistic: role-play with partners using timed mock stations, seek structured feedback, record and review your performance, and attend a preparation course if your budget allows. Combine this active preparation with systematic revision of the MLA Content Map's practical skills list and the professional behaviour frameworks expected in communication stations, and you will arrive in Manchester ready to demonstrate the Foundation-level standard with confidence.
