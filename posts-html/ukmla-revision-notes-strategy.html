UKMLA Revision Notes Strategy: How to Study Smarter and Retain More for the MLA

An effective UKMLA revision notes strategy is the single biggest factor separating candidates who pass with confidence from those who spend hundreds of hours studying and still feel underprepared on exam day. The UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) covers an enormous breadth of clinical medicine, public health, ethics, and professional practice across the full MLA Content Map. The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) demands not just knowledge but the ability to apply it under pressure in a realistic clinical environment. In this comprehensive guide, we break down the science of effective revision, map the best resources available for UKMLA preparation, explain how to build an evidence-based revision system using notes, flashcards, and question banks, and show you how to track and learn from your errors so that every hour of study translates into genuine exam performance.

Why Passive Revision Fails: The Science of Learning

Most medical students arrive at UKMLA preparation having spent years using the same revision techniques they learned in secondary school: re-reading notes, highlighting textbooks, and transcribing lecture slides into tidy summaries. These methods feel productive because they create a sense of familiarity with the material — a phenomenon cognitive psychologists call the fluency illusion. When you re-read something and it flows easily, your brain interprets that ease as evidence that you have learned it. But familiarity is not memory, and recognition is not recall.

The Failure of Highlighting and Re-Reading

A landmark 2013 meta-analysis by Dunlosky and colleagues (published in Psychological Science in the Public Interest) evaluated the utility of ten common study techniques. Highlighting and underlining received a "low utility" rating — there was little to no benefit compared to reading without marking. Re-reading received the same low utility rating. Both techniques are passive: they do not force the brain to work hard, and information is not encoded deeply enough to be retrievable under exam conditions.

Transcribing notes — copying out lecture content into your own exercise books — is slightly better because it requires some active processing, but the benefit is largely from the selection and reframing involved, not from the act of writing itself. If you are simply copying text verbatim, you are spending time on a low-yield activity. And critically, a well-organised folder of typed notes is not revision — it is preparation for revision. The learning happens when you put the notes away and try to retrieve the information.

Cognitive Load and Overloading Working Memory

Another reason passive revision fails for the UKMLA is cognitive load. The AKT presents complex clinical scenarios that require you to hold multiple pieces of information in working memory simultaneously — patient demographics, presenting complaint, examination findings, investigation results, differential diagnoses, and management options — and to apply reasoning across all of them in a short time. If your knowledge is only superficially encoded (because you have re-read it rather than practised retrieving it), working memory becomes rapidly overloaded and performance deteriorates. Deep encoding through active retrieval practice frees up working memory by automating the retrieval of core knowledge, allowing higher-order reasoning to function effectively.

The Principles of Effective Revision

Retrieval Practice

Retrieval practice — the act of recalling information from memory, rather than passively reviewing it — is the single most evidence-supported learning technique available. Every time you retrieve a memory, you strengthen its neural trace and make it easier to retrieve in the future. This is the testing effect, robustly demonstrated across decades of cognitive psychology research. For UKMLA purposes, retrieval practice means doing questions, not reading. Every session with a question bank is a retrieval practice session. Closing your notes and trying to write out the management of a condition from memory is retrieval practice. Using flashcards to test yourself (covering the answer before reading it) is retrieval practice. Simply reading the question and then immediately checking the answer is not.

The practical implication for UKMLA revision is this: your primary activity in each study session should be answering questions or testing yourself, not reading. Notes and resources are reference material to consult after you have tested yourself and identified gaps. This is the opposite of how most students naturally organise their revision.

Spaced Repetition and the Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve

Hermann Ebbinghaus, a 19th-century German psychologist, described the forgetting curve — the exponential rate at which newly learned information is forgotten without reinforcement. Without review, the average person forgets approximately 40% of new material within 20 minutes, 56% within an hour, and 66% within a day. However, each time a memory is retrieved and reinforced, the forgetting curve resets and flattens — the next forgetting curve is shallower, and subsequent review intervals can be progressively lengthened.

Spaced repetition is the practice of scheduling reviews at increasing intervals — typically one day, three days, one week, two weeks, one month — to intercept the forgetting curve at the optimal moment. Reviewing material just as you are about to forget it produces far stronger encoding than reviewing it when it is still fresh. Spaced repetition is the algorithmic principle behind flashcard apps such as Anki, and it is why Anki-based revision is substantially more efficient than reading notes at a fixed schedule.

For UKMLA preparation, spaced repetition means revisiting question bank topics and flashcard decks at intervals, not just working through them once. A common mistake is to complete a question bank topic, score 85%, feel satisfied, and never return to it. Six weeks later, when the topic appears in the AKT, the information has largely been forgotten. Building a spaced repetition schedule — either through Anki or through a manual system of topic review — ensures that material stays retrievable across the full preparation period.

Interleaving

Interleaving — mixing different topics within a study session, rather than blocking all content by subject — is another evidence-based technique that feels less efficient in the moment but produces superior long-term retention. Blocked practice (doing 50 cardiology questions, then 50 respiratory questions, then 50 neurology questions) produces rapid within-session improvement but poor retention. Interleaved practice (mixing cardiology, respiratory, and neurology questions within each session) produces slower apparent progress but far better retention and transfer to novel scenarios.

The UKMLA AKT naturally uses interleaved presentation — you will not be given a block of 30 cardiology questions; you will move between topics unpredictably. Training your brain to switch between topics fluently is therefore both a learning strategy and an exam strategy. Randomising your question bank sessions (rather than doing one topic at a time) from mid-preparation onwards prepares you for the unpredictability of the real exam.

Elaboration

Elaboration means connecting new information to what you already know, asking yourself why something is true rather than simply what is true. For example, rather than memorising that atenolol is relatively contraindicated in asthma, elaborative encoding asks: why? (Beta-2 blockade in bronchial smooth muscle causes bronchoconstriction.) What are the exceptions? (Highly cardioselective agents such as bisoprolol in very low doses may be used with caution in mild, well-controlled asthma when the benefit clearly outweighs the risk, with specialist supervision.) How does this connect to other pharmacology? (Beta-2 agonists treat bronchoconstriction — they work on the same receptors in the opposite direction.) This web of connections makes the knowledge far more robust and transferable than a simple rule learned by rote.

Dual Coding

Dual coding combines verbal information with visual representations — diagrams, flowcharts, tables, timelines, and clinical algorithms. Processing the same information in two modalities (language and image) creates two distinct memory traces that reinforce each other. For UKMLA purposes, this means drawing out clinical algorithms (e.g. the NICE CKS management pathway for chest pain), creating timeline diagrams for disease progression, and using ECG and CXR images alongside textual descriptions. The evidence for dual coding in medical education is strong: students who study ECG patterns using both annotated images and verbal descriptions perform better on interpretation tasks than those who use either alone.

Official Resources: Non-Negotiable

No question bank can replace the official resources that the exam setters expect candidates to know. These include:

NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries) — The UKMLA is aligned with NICE guidelines. NICE CKS provides concise, regularly updated summaries of NICE guidance for primary care conditions, and the AKT question writers use these summaries when writing clinical management questions. Rather than using CKS as primary reading, use it to verify and refine your answers when you get questions wrong in a question bank — look up the CKS summary for the condition and check your management against the current guideline.

The BNF (British National Formulary) — The BNF is the authoritative reference for prescribing in the UK. Dose calculations, drug interactions, contraindications, and side effect profiles in UKMLA questions are based on BNF content. The BNF is freely available online and via the BNF app. Knowing which drugs are in which categories, their key interactions, and their main contraindications is essential AKT preparation. The BNF should be used as a reference to check prescribing details you are unsure of, not as revision reading.

GMC Good Medical Practice 2024 — As discussed in our UKMLA overview, GMP is the ethical framework tested across both the AKT and the CPSA. Read GMP in full at least once, and use the domain structure to organise your ethics revision.

MLA Content Map — Published by the Medical Schools Council (MSC), the MLA Content Map is the official syllabus for the UKMLA. It lists all the conditions, presentations, and professional themes that can be tested. Using the Content Map as a checklist — systematically working through each item and assessing your confidence — ensures comprehensive coverage and prevents gaps.

Textbooks and Reference Texts

The UKMLA does not require any specific textbook. The vast majority of candidates use question banks and online resources rather than reading textbooks cover to cover. However, a single integrated medical textbook can be valuable as a reference for conditions where you need deeper understanding than a question bank explanation provides. Commonly used options include:

  • Kumar and Clark's Clinical Medicine — comprehensive and well-organised; useful for reading around complex conditions
  • Davidson's Principles and Practice of Medicine — similar depth; some candidates prefer the presentation style
  • Cheese & Chalk (Oxford Handbook of Clinical Medicine) — concise and portable; good for on-the-ward revision and quick reference
  • Illustrated Colour Text (ICT) series — system-based; useful for visual learners

The key principle: use textbooks selectively and reactively, not as the primary revision activity. Read about a condition in a textbook after getting related questions wrong, not as your first engagement with the material.

Making Effective UKMLA Revision Notes

The One-Page Summary Approach

The one-page summary (or one-page A4 sheet per condition) is one of the most effective formats for UKMLA notes. The discipline of condensing everything you know about a condition onto a single page forces you to prioritise the most important information. A well-structured one-pager for a condition typically includes:

  • Definition / epidemiology — who gets it, how common, key risk factors
  • Pathophysiology — one or two sentences explaining the underlying mechanism (crucial for elaborative encoding)
  • Clinical features — the classic presentation, key discriminating features, red flags
  • Investigations — first-line, gold standard, what findings to expect
  • Management — aligned with NICE guidelines; first-line, second-line, specialist referral thresholds
  • Complications — what to watch for
  • Key differentials — one or two conditions it is commonly confused with and how to distinguish

Do not create one-pagers for every condition from the outset. Create them reactively — when you encounter a condition you consistently get wrong, or when you identify a topic that keeps appearing in your error log (see below). If you try to create comprehensive one-pagers for every condition in the MLA Content Map at the start of revision, you will spend all your time making notes and none of your time actually learning.

Comparison Tables

Comparison tables are particularly valuable for groups of conditions that are frequently confused in UKMLA scenarios: the inflammatory arthropathies (rheumatoid, psoriatic, ankylosing spondylitis, reactive, gout, pseudogout); the causes of a raised ANCA (PR3 vs MPO, GPA vs MPA vs EGPA); the antihypertensives and their specific indications and contraindications; the inherited bleeding disorders; and the different types of renal tubular acidosis. A well-constructed comparison table takes about 30 minutes to create and provides a reference you can test yourself against repeatedly.

Mnemonics: When to Use Them

Mnemonics are useful for memorising lists that have no inherent logic or structure — the causes of hypercalcaemia (CHIMPANZEES), the features of Horner's syndrome (PAM — Ptosis, Anhydrosis, Miosis), or the Wells score criteria. However, there are two important caveats. First, a mnemonic only works if you remember what each letter stands for and can immediately apply it clinically — a mnemonic that produces a list you cannot explain is worthless. Always learn the clinical meaning behind each item in a mnemonic. Second, creating your own mnemonics for the things you personally struggle to remember is more effective than borrowing someone else's, because the act of creating the mnemonic is itself a form of deep encoding.

Anki Flashcards: The Optimal UKMLA Tool

Anki is a free, open-source flashcard application that uses a spaced repetition algorithm to schedule card reviews at optimal intervals based on your individual performance. It is, by a significant margin, the most evidence-aligned digital learning tool available to medical students. For UKMLA revision, Anki offers several distinct advantages:

  • Image occlusion — the Image Occlusion Enhanced add-on allows you to cover specific labels on ECGs, chest X-rays, ABG results, or blood film images and test yourself on identification. This is particularly valuable for the CPSA, which involves interpreting clinical investigations. Creating Anki cards from real ECG strips (e.g. atrial fibrillation, STEMI, third-degree heart block) with occluded labels is one of the highest-yield things you can do for investigations-based revision
  • Spaced repetition algorithm — Anki manages the forgetting curve for you, scheduling cards for review at the point where you are about to forget them. You rate each card as Again (failed), Hard, Good, or Easy, and Anki adjusts the interval accordingly
  • Portability — Anki decks are available on iOS and Android, allowing revision during commutes, lunch breaks, or any spare time
  • Customisation — you can create cards precisely targeted at your personal weaknesses

A note on shared Anki decks: several UKMLA-specific Anki decks are available for free download from the AnkiWeb shared deck library, and some are of high quality. However, using a shared deck uncritically is risky: decks may contain errors, outdated information, or content misaligned with the current MLA Content Map and NICE guidelines. The most effective approach is to build your own deck progressively, creating new cards as you encounter gaps in knowledge through question bank practice, and supplementing with carefully reviewed shared cards only for topics you have verified for accuracy. Do not fall into the trap of downloading a 5,000-card shared deck and spending months trying to keep up with it before you have built a solid foundation through question practice.

The Error Log System

Why You Must Track Wrong Answers

The error log is one of the most powerful and most underused tools in UKMLA preparation. Every question you answer incorrectly is a diagnostic signal: it tells you exactly where your knowledge is weak. Without a systematic way of capturing and learning from these signals, you will make the same mistakes repeatedly, wasting practice questions and revision time.

An error log is a systematic record of every question you get wrong, including the root cause of the error and the corrective action you have taken. It transforms your question bank from a pass/fail scoring exercise into a personalised learning system.

How to Build an Error Log

Your error log should be a simple spreadsheet or digital document with the following columns:

  1. Date — when you answered the question
  2. Topic / condition — what the question was about
  3. Question summary — one-sentence description of the clinical scenario
  4. My answer — what you chose
  5. Correct answer — what the right answer was
  6. Root cause — why did you get it wrong? (see below)
  7. Action taken — what you did to address the gap
  8. Reviewed? — a checkbox for weekly review

The most important column is root cause. There are typically five types of error in UKMLA practice:

  • Knowledge gap — you simply did not know the information. Action: create a one-pager or Anki cards for the topic
  • Application error — you knew the facts but failed to apply them correctly to the scenario. Action: practise more scenario-based questions on this topic; review the clinical algorithm
  • Reading error — you misread the question (missed a key word such as "most likely," "best initial," or "most appropriate immediate action"). Action: practise careful question reading; flag as a technique issue
  • Distractor trap — you were drawn to a plausible-sounding distractor that contained a subtle error. Action: review the explanation carefully; understand why the distractor was wrong
  • Careless error — you knew the answer but made an error through haste or inattention. Action: slow down; review the question before confirming your answer

Classifying errors by root cause allows you to identify patterns: if 60% of your errors are knowledge gaps in respiratory medicine, you need more targeted revision in that area. If 40% of your errors are reading errors, the problem is technique rather than knowledge.

Weekly Error Log Reviews

Once per week, review your error log from the previous seven days. For each error, cover the answer column and try to recall the correct answer from memory. Errors you can now answer correctly get a tick; errors you are still getting wrong get flagged for further action (convert to a new Anki card, re-read the CKS summary, discuss with a study partner). This weekly review is itself a spaced repetition exercise and a powerful way of consolidating learning from your question bank sessions.

Converting Errors to Anki Cards

For knowledge gap errors, the most efficient corrective action is to convert the learning point into an Anki card immediately after reviewing the explanation. Do not create a long written note — create a focused, testable card. For example, if you get a question about the management of hypertension in pregnancy wrong, create a card: front — "First-line antihypertensive in pregnancy? (excluding pre-eclampsia)"; back — "Labetalol (oral); alternatives: nifedipine, methyldopa. Avoid ACE inhibitors, ARBs, thiazides." This card will appear for review according to the Anki algorithm, ensuring the learning point sticks.

Organising Revision Around the MLA Content Map

Using the Content Map as a Checklist

The MLA Content Map is the official document defining what can be tested in the UKMLA. It is publicly available on the Medical Schools Council website and is organised by clinical specialty and by professional and ethical theme. Using the Content Map as a revision checklist is the most systematic way to ensure comprehensive coverage.

Print or download the Content Map and work through each item systematically. For each condition or topic, rate your confidence on a three-point scale:

  • Red — significant knowledge gap; need active revision and question practice
  • Amber — moderate confidence; needs reinforcement through questions and spaced repetition
  • Green — confident; maintain through periodic spaced repetition reviews

Review and update this RAG (Red-Amber-Green) rating at regular intervals throughout preparation. As your revision progresses, the number of red items should decrease and the proportion of green items should increase. If items remain consistently red despite revision, they need a different approach: read the CKS summary, create detailed Anki cards, attempt more questions, or seek clinical exposure.

Colour-Coding Your Notes

Colour-coding works best when it carries semantic meaning rather than being purely aesthetic. A consistent colour scheme across all your notes makes retrieval faster. A widely used approach: red for absolute contraindications and red flags; orange for relative contraindications and cautions; green for first-line treatments; blue for investigations; purple for complications. Applied consistently across your one-pagers and comparison tables, this coding system creates visual cues that accelerate recognition during exam practice.

Digital vs Paper Revision Notes

The debate between digital and paper notes is genuine, and the evidence is nuanced. Several studies have found that handwriting notes (even in summarised form) produces better conceptual retention than typing, because the slower speed of handwriting forces more active selection and processing. For initial note creation — especially for complex topics requiring deep understanding — handwriting may be superior. For reference, searchability, and portability, digital notes have clear advantages.

A practical hybrid approach: use handwritten notes for creating one-pagers on difficult topics, and digital tools (Anki, Notion, OneNote, or a Google Doc) for your error log, Content Map checklist, and revision schedule. Avoid the trap of over-engineering your note organisation system — a perfectly structured digital note system that takes hours to maintain is a displacement activity, not revision.

Timing of Note-Making in Your Revision Calendar

When you make notes matters as much as how you make them. The optimal sequence is:

  1. Early preparation (months 4–6 before the AKT) — Focus on building foundational knowledge through reading, initial question bank practice, and creating your Content Map RAG rating. Create one-pagers for topics identified as knowledge gaps through early question practice.
  2. Mid preparation (months 2–4 before the AKT) — Shift the majority of time to question bank practice. Use your error log actively. Create Anki cards from errors. Begin spaced repetition review of early-created notes and cards. Start interleaving topics rather than blocking by specialty.
  3. Late preparation (6–8 weeks before the AKT) — Primarily question practice and Anki review. Minimise creation of new notes — at this stage, your time is better spent retrieving and refining existing knowledge than creating new material. Reserve the GMC sample questions and MSC practice exam for the final 2–4 weeks as a calibration tool.

CPSA-Specific Revision Strategies

The CPSA requires a different revision approach from the AKT because it tests applied clinical skills rather than pure knowledge recall. For CPSA-specific preparation:

  • Structured clinical examinations — practise clinical skills (history-taking, examination, procedural skills, communication scenarios) in simulated conditions with feedback. Your medical school will provide CPSA preparation time; use it fully.
  • Checklist-based revision — CPSA stations are marked using structured mark sheets. Knowing what examiners are looking for (gathering information, clinical reasoning, communication skills, professionalism) allows you to deliberately practise hitting the assessment criteria.
  • Video resources and clinical skills platforms — platforms such as Quesmed CPSA modules, Geeky Medics (for structured OSCE approaches), and your own medical school's clinical skills resources are valuable for procedural and communication skills stations.
  • ECG and CXR interpretation — this is tested in both the AKT (as image-based questions) and the CPSA. Systematic practice with labelled ECG and CXR images using Anki image occlusion cards is particularly effective.

Study Groups, Peer Teaching, and Social Accountability

Teaching is one of the most powerful consolidation strategies available. When you explain a topic to someone else, you are forced to organise your understanding, identify gaps in your explanation, and resolve inconsistencies. This is the protégé effect — you learn better when you teach. UKMLA study groups can be highly productive if they are structured: each session, one member teaches a topic from the Content Map to the group, others ask questions, and the group discusses errors from the week's question bank practice. This is far more productive than simply reading notes aloud to each other.

Social accountability is also a genuine motivational tool. Committing to a study partner that you will complete a set number of question bank questions or review a specific set of Anki cards by a specific date creates external accountability that supplements internal motivation, particularly in the low-energy periods of long revision campaigns.

Managing Note Quality Over Quantity

A critical principle of effective UKMLA note-making is that you should have fewer, higher-quality notes rather than more, lower-quality ones. Many candidates accumulate hundreds of pages of notes that they never review. The accumulation of material creates an illusion of progress without producing learning. A targeted set of 50–70 one-page summaries for high-yield conditions, a well-maintained Anki deck of 500–800 personally created cards, a systematic error log, and a completed RAG-rated Content Map is more valuable than three folders of typed notes that have never been reviewed.

Before creating any new note or summary, ask yourself: will I review this again? If the honest answer is no, do not create it. Instead, create an Anki card on the specific learning point and move on to the next question.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Revision Notes and Strategy

How many questions should I do each day in the lead-up to the AKT?

There is no universally correct answer, but a commonly recommended target for the main preparation phase (2–4 months before the exam) is 40–60 questions per day, reviewed carefully with explanations — not done at speed without reading the explanations. Quality of review matters more than raw question count. Doing 100 questions per day and skimming the explanations produces far less learning than doing 40 questions and reading each explanation carefully, cross-referencing errors against CKS, and creating Anki cards for knowledge gaps. In the final 2–4 weeks, many candidates increase to 80–100 questions per day in timed, mixed conditions to build exam stamina and pace.

Should I use Anki from the start or wait until I have a knowledge base?

You do not need a pre-existing knowledge base to benefit from Anki — the cards themselves build the knowledge base. However, the most effective approach is to integrate Anki into your question bank workflow from the start of preparation: do questions, identify errors, create cards for knowledge gaps. Starting Anki from day one of preparation maximises the time the spaced repetition algorithm has to work before the exam. Trying to build a large Anki deck in the final four weeks before the exam is ineffective because the algorithm has insufficient time to space reviews appropriately.

Is it worth buying more than one question bank?

For most candidates, one primary question bank used thoroughly is better than two question banks used superficially. The primary question bank should be whichever of Passmedicine or Quesmed you find most comfortable and well-aligned to your learning style. A second question bank (such as BMJ OnExamination or UWorld) can be valuable if you complete your primary bank and want additional practice with different question styles, or if your primary bank has weak coverage in specific areas. Do not switch between question banks repeatedly — pick one, commit to it, and use your error log to track performance.

How do I revise for the CPSA differently from the AKT?

The AKT and CPSA test overlapping but distinct skills. AKT preparation focuses on knowledge retrieval through question bank practice. CPSA preparation requires clinical skills practice in simulated settings: structured history-taking with role players, systematic examination practice (ideally filmed for feedback), communication skills scenarios (breaking bad news, gaining consent, managing disagreement), and procedural skills practice (venepuncture, cannulation, ECG recording, prescribing). The knowledge underpinning the CPSA is the same knowledge tested in the AKT, so integrated AKT preparation supports CPSA performance — but knowing the management of heart failure does not automatically mean you can conduct a structured cardiovascular examination and communicate findings to a patient. Allocate specific time for clinical skills simulation separate from question bank revision.

What is the most common mistake candidates make with UKMLA notes?

The most common mistake is spending the majority of revision time making notes rather than using notes. Notes are a tool for review, not a product in their own right. Candidates who spend months creating beautifully formatted summaries, colour-coded flowcharts, and comprehensive written resources — but who do not spend the majority of their preparation time answering questions and testing themselves — consistently underperform relative to their preparation effort. The exam tests retrieval, not recognition. Build a lean, targeted set of notes and then put them down and do questions.

Conclusion: Build a System, Not Just a Study Session

The most important insight in UKMLA revision strategy is that effective preparation is a system, not a series of individual study sessions. A system has defined inputs (question bank sessions, Anki reviews, error log updates), defined processes (retrieval practice, spaced repetition, elaboration), and defined outputs (measured progress on the Content Map RAG checklist, declining error rates, improving question bank scores). When your system is running well, each component feeds into the others: question bank errors generate Anki cards, Anki reviews reinforce weak areas identified in the error log, error log patterns direct targeted question bank sessions, and Content Map ratings track overall progress.

Candidates who build and maintain this kind of systematic approach — even imperfectly — will consistently outperform those who rely on inspiration, last-minute cramming, or passive revision. Start early, prioritise active over passive study, track your errors relentlessly, and use spaced repetition to make sure everything you learn stays learned.
