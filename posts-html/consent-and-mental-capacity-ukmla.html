Consent and Mental Capacity in the UKMLA: The Complete Guide

Consent and mental capacity UKMLA questions sit at the heart of both exam components, testing not just whether you can recite the Mental Capacity Act 2005 but whether you can apply its logic to a patient in front of you under time pressure. A confused elderly man refusing a hip X-ray, a teenager asking for contraception without her parents knowing, a man with schizophrenia declining chemotherapy for a treatable cancer — each of these is, at its core, a question about whether this particular person can make this particular decision right now, and if not, who decides for them and how. This guide works through the four elements of valid consent, the Mental Capacity Act's two-stage test and five statutory principles, best-interests decision-making, Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards, Gillick competence, and the crucial distinction between capacity and competence — all framed explicitly for both the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the communication-skills stations of the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA).

Whether you trained entirely within the UK system or are an international medical graduate (IMG) approaching this material for the first time, the underlying logic is learnable and consistent. UK medical students will typically have already sat through lectures on the Mental Capacity Act 2005 and observed consent conversations on the wards, so much of this will be revision and consolidation. IMGs, by contrast, may be encountering this exact statutory framework — with its specific two-stage test, its five principles, and its very particular vocabulary — for the first time, since capacity law varies significantly between jurisdictions. Both groups are examined identically, and both need the same depth of working knowledge: this is not a topic where a general sense of "informed consent" from another healthcare system is sufficient.

Quick Answer: What the UKMLA Expects You to Know About Consent and Capacity

Consent and mental capacity UKMLA questions test four overlapping knowledge areas: the legal elements that make consent valid, the Mental Capacity Act 2005's framework for assessing capacity, what happens when a patient lacks capacity, and how children and young people fit into the consent framework. In summary:

  • Valid consent requires four elements: voluntariness, capacity, adequate information, and specificity to the decision in question.
  • Capacity is assessed using the Mental Capacity Act 2005's two-stage test — a diagnostic test (is there an impairment or disturbance of the mind or brain?) followed by a functional test (does that impairment mean the person cannot understand, retain, use/weigh, or communicate the decision?).
  • Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific — a patient can lack capacity for one decision (say, complex surgery) while retaining it for another (say, whether to accept a blood test), and capacity can fluctuate hour to hour.
  • When a patient lacks capacity, decisions must be made in their best interests, following a structured checklist, and the least restrictive option must always be considered.
  • Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS) provide the legal authorisation required when meeting a person's care needs necessarily restricts their liberty.
  • Children under 16 can consent to treatment if they demonstrate Gillick competence — sufficient maturity and understanding for that specific decision.
  • Capacity and competence are not interchangeable terms — capacity is the statutory MCA 2005 test used for adults (16 and over), while competence (specifically Gillick competence) is the common-law test historically used for under-16s.

Consent and mental capacity are examined in the UKMLA as a single, tightly linked topic rather than two separate subjects, since the legal test for one so often determines the outcome of the other. Both the AKT and the CPSA test this material, but from different angles. The AKT presents single-best-answer scenarios that ask you to identify the legally and ethically correct next step. The CPSA tests whether you can actually hold a consent conversation with a simulated patient — explaining risks in plain language, checking understanding, and responding appropriately if the patient appears to lack capacity for the decision on the table.

The Four Elements of Valid Consent

Valid consent is not a signature on a form. It is a legal and ethical state that exists only when four conditions are simultaneously satisfied: the decision is voluntary, the patient has capacity, the patient has been given adequate information, and the consent is specific to the intervention actually being proposed. Missing any one of these four elements means the consent obtained — even if a form was signed — is not valid, and proceeding without valid consent exposes a doctor to both legal liability (battery, or negligence) and a serious professional conduct issue.

Element One: Voluntariness

A patient's decision must be free from coercion, undue pressure, or manipulation, whether from a doctor, a family member, or anyone else with influence over the patient. Voluntariness does not mean a patient must be entirely uninfluenced by advice — doctors are expected to give clear clinical recommendations — but the final decision must genuinely belong to the patient rather than being imposed on them. UKMLA scenarios frequently test this through a relative who insists a patient "must" have a particular treatment, or a patient who says "just do whatever you think is best, doctor" in a way that suggests they have not actually engaged with the decision at all. In CPSA stations, examiners are watching for whether the candidate creates space for the patient's own view rather than steering them toward a predetermined answer.

Coercion can also come from institutional or family pressure that is harder to spot than an obvious threat. A patient who agrees to a procedure only because a spouse is present and vocally in favour of it, or an older patient who consents because they do not want to "make a fuss" in front of a busy clinical team, may not be exercising genuinely voluntary consent. Recognising these subtler pressures — and, where appropriate, offering to speak with the patient alone — is itself an examined skill in CPSA stations that involve a family member accompanying the patient.

Element Two: Capacity

The patient must have the mental capacity to make the specific decision being asked of them. In England and Wales this is governed by the Mental Capacity Act 2005, covered in detail in the next section. A patient who lacks capacity for the decision in question cannot give valid consent to it — the question then shifts to how a decision should be made on their behalf, not whether their "no" or "yes" should simply be accepted at face value.

Element Three: Information

The patient must be given sufficient information to understand what they are agreeing to, in terms they can actually process. This includes the nature of the proposed treatment, its intended benefits, the material risks involved, and any reasonable alternatives (including the alternative of doing nothing). The modern legal standard for what counts as adequate information comes from Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board [2015] UKSC 11, which held that a doctor must take reasonable care to ensure a patient is aware of any risk that a reasonable person in the patient's position would consider significant, or that this particular doctor knows this particular patient would consider significant. This is a patient-centred test, not a test of what a reasonable body of doctors would have disclosed (the older Bolam standard, which Montgomery displaced for consent purposes specifically).

In practice, this means the amount and type of information a patient needs is not fixed — a professional violinist undergoing a hand procedure needs a franker conversation about the risk of reduced dexterity than a patient for whom that particular risk carries no special significance. UKMLA scenarios test whether candidates can identify which risks are material to a specific patient's circumstances, not just which risks appear on a generic list.

Element Four: Specific to the Decision

Consent must relate to the actual intervention being proposed, not a related or similar one. A patient who consents to a diagnostic laparoscopy has not thereby consented to an open laparotomy if the surgeon finds something unexpected mid-procedure, unless that possibility was specifically discussed and consented to in advance. Similarly, consent given for one procedure cannot be treated as implied consent for an entirely separate procedure performed on the same visit. This principle also underlies why capacity itself must be assessed per decision — a global label of "lacks capacity" attached to a patient's notes is not, on its own, sufficient evidence that they lack capacity for the specific choice currently being made.

The Mental Capacity Act 2005: The Two-Stage Test

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 is the statutory framework governing capacity assessment in England and Wales, and it is one of the most heavily examined pieces of legislation in the entire UKMLA. Capacity assessment under the Act proceeds in two distinct stages, and UKMLA questions frequently hinge on candidates confusing or skipping one of them.

Stage One: The Diagnostic Test

The first question is whether there is an impairment of, or a disturbance in the functioning of, the mind or brain. This impairment can be permanent (dementia, a learning disability, an acquired brain injury) or entirely temporary (acute delirium, intoxication, severe pain, the effects of anaesthesia, or even extreme fatigue in some circumstances). Critically, the diagnostic test is deliberately broad and non-judgemental about cause — a diagnosis alone is neither necessary nor sufficient. Having dementia does not automatically mean a patient fails the diagnostic test for every decision, and a patient without any formal psychiatric diagnosis can still fail it if, for example, they are acutely delirious following a urinary tract infection.

Stage Two: The Functional Test

If — and only if — stage one is satisfied, the assessor moves to the functional test: does the identified impairment mean the person is unable to make this specific decision at this specific time? A person is deemed unable to make a decision if they cannot do one or more of the following four things:

  • Understand the information relevant to the decision, including the nature of the decision, why it is needed, and the likely consequences of deciding one way, the other way, or not deciding at all.
  • Retain that information for long enough to make the decision — even briefly; the Act does not require long-term retention.
  • Use or weigh that information as part of the process of making the decision — that is, actually engage with it rather than simply parroting it back.
  • Communicate their decision, by any means, including talking, sign language, blinking, or any other method available to them.

All four elements of the functional test must be intact for a person to have capacity for that decision; failing any single one of them means the person lacks capacity. A frequent UKMLA trap involves a patient who can repeat back exactly what they have been told (satisfying "understand" and "retain") but who cannot connect that information to their own situation or genuinely weigh the risks against their own values (failing "use or weigh") — this patient still lacks capacity, even though they appear superficially informed.

The Five Statutory Principles

Section 1 of the Mental Capacity Act 2005 sets out five principles that must underpin every capacity assessment and every decision made under the Act. These principles are tested constantly, both directly (as knowledge questions) and indirectly (embedded in the correct answer to a clinical scenario).

Principle What It Means in Practice Common UKMLA Application
1. Presumption of capacity A person must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established, on the balance of probabilities, that they lack it. You cannot assume a patient with a psychiatric diagnosis or learning disability lacks capacity — capacity must be actively assessed, not inferred from a label.
2. Support to decide A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision unless all practicable steps to help them do so have been taken without success. Using an interpreter, simple diagrams, extra time, or involving a speech and language therapist before concluding a patient cannot decide.
3. Unwise decisions are allowed A person is not to be treated as lacking capacity merely because they make a decision that seems unwise. A patient refusing a clearly beneficial treatment (e.g. a blood transfusion, or amputation for a limb-threatening infection) does not automatically lack capacity — the reasoning process, not the outcome, is assessed.
4. Best interests Any act done or decision made on behalf of a person who lacks capacity must be done in their best interests. Once capacity is genuinely absent, the decision-maker must follow the best-interests checklist, not simply do what is clinically easiest.
5. Least restrictive option Before the act or decision is made, regard must be had to whether the purpose can be achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the person's rights and freedom of action. Choosing the option that best preserves the patient's autonomy and liberty among clinically appropriate alternatives — directly relevant to DoLS authorisations.

Principle three is worth dwelling on, since it is one of the most frequently misapplied concepts by UKMLA candidates. An unwise decision is not evidence of incapacity. A patient who declines chemotherapy because they would rather have a shorter life without the side effects, having clearly understood the prognosis either way, is exercising a valid, capacitous choice — however much a clinician might personally disagree with it. The correct UKMLA answer in these scenarios is almost always to respect the decision once genuine capacity has been confirmed, document the discussion thoroughly, and ensure the patient knows they can change their mind.

Capacity Is Decision-Specific and Time-Specific

One of the most important — and most frequently tested — features of the Mental Capacity Act 2005 is that capacity is never assessed as a global, permanent attribute of a person. It is always assessed in relation to one particular decision at one particular point in time. A patient can simultaneously lack capacity to consent to a complex operation with multiple treatment pathways, while retaining full capacity to decide whether to accept a peripheral cannula or a simple blood test. The complexity of the decision matters: generally, the more complex and higher-stakes the decision, the more sophisticated the reasoning required to demonstrate capacity for it — though even this is decision-specific rather than a fixed sliding scale.

Capacity can also fluctuate over hours or days. A patient who is delirious at 3am following a fall may regain capacity by the following morning once the underlying cause (infection, dehydration, opiate accumulation) is treated. Where a decision is not urgent, the correct approach is often to wait and reassess once any reversible cause of impaired capacity has been addressed, rather than proceeding immediately on a best-interests basis. UKMLA scenarios test this by presenting a patient whose capacity appears borderline and offering an option to "reassess capacity once acute confusion has resolved" — this is very often the correct answer over acting immediately, provided the clinical situation allows for the delay.

This decision-specific, time-specific framing is also why documentation matters so much in UK clinical practice: a capacity assessment recorded in the notes should specify exactly which decision was assessed, on what date, using what reasoning, rather than a vague note reading simply "lacks capacity."

Best-Interests Decision-Making

When a patient has genuinely been assessed as lacking capacity for a specific decision, the decision must then be made in their best interests — not according to what is clinically most convenient, and not according to what family members simply prefer. The Mental Capacity Act 2005 provides a structured, non-exhaustive checklist that decision-makers must work through.

The Best-Interests Checklist

  • Consider whether the person is likely to regain capacity at some point, and whether the decision can reasonably wait until then.
  • Involve the person in the decision as fully as possible, encouraging and supporting their participation even though they lack capacity to make the final choice themselves.
  • Take into account, as far as they can be ascertained, the person's past and present wishes, feelings, beliefs, and values — including anything written down in advance and anything expressed informally to family or carers.
  • Consult, where practicable and appropriate, the views of anyone named by the person as someone to be consulted, anyone engaged in caring for the person, and any close family or friends who take an interest in their welfare.
  • Never be motivated by a desire to bring about the person's death, in decisions concerning life-sustaining treatment.

Who Makes the Decision

Best-interests decisions do not have a single, universal decision-maker — the identity of the decision-maker depends on the legal context. If the patient has a valid Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) for health and welfare, the appointed attorney can make decisions within the scope of that LPA, provided the decision falls within the authority actually granted. Where no LPA exists, the treating clinician responsible for the specific decision acts as the decision-maker, in consultation with those close to the patient — this is the most common scenario tested in UKMLA questions involving a hospital inpatient. Where there is no appropriate family member or friend to consult and a serious medical treatment decision must be made, an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) must be instructed to represent the patient's interests.

A frequently tested distinction is between best interests and a related American concept, substituted judgement, which asks what the patient themselves would have chosen. English law does not formally adopt substituted judgement as the legal test, although the patient's known wishes and values are heavily weighted within the best-interests framework — in practice the two approaches often converge, but UKMLA answers should be phrased in best-interests language specifically.

Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS)

Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards exist to provide lawful authorisation whenever meeting a person's care or treatment needs, in a hospital or care home, necessarily involves restricting their liberty to a degree that amounts to a deprivation of liberty under human rights law. This arises most commonly for patients who lack capacity — for example, a patient with advanced dementia who is confused, attempts repeatedly to leave the ward, and requires continuous supervision and locked doors for their own safety.

At a conceptual level, the UKMLA expects candidates to recognise three things about DoLS. First, that restricting a person's liberty for their own safety is not automatically unlawful, but it does require a specific legal authorisation process — it is not something a ward team can simply decide to do informally, however well-intentioned. Second, that the underlying test for whether a deprivation of liberty exists (sometimes called the "acid test") asks whether the person is under continuous supervision and control and is not free to leave — a test derived from the Supreme Court's ruling in Cheshire West (P v Cheshire West and Chester Council [2014] UKSC 19), which significantly broadened what counts as a deprivation of liberty in care settings. Third, that DoLS authorisations are time-limited, must be regularly reviewed, and exist specifically to protect the human rights of people who lack capacity, rather than to make life administratively easier for staff.

For UKMLA purposes, the detail of the DoLS application process itself is less important than recognising when a scenario is describing a potential deprivation of liberty and identifying that a formal safeguard, rather than an informal restriction, is required. A patient who is confused, trying to leave, and being physically prevented from doing so by staff without any authorisation in place is a recurring red flag in AKT scenarios — the correct response usually involves recognising the need for a DoLS authorisation (or its successor framework) alongside addressing the underlying medical cause of the confusion.

Gillick Competence and Consent in Children

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 applies to people aged 16 and over — it does not apply to children under 16. For that younger group, English law uses a different, common-law test: Gillick competence, established by the House of Lords in Gillick v West Norfolk and Wisbech Area Health Authority [1986] AC 112.

What Gillick Competence Actually Tests

A child under 16 can legally consent to their own medical treatment if they have sufficient understanding and intelligence to fully comprehend the nature, purpose, and likely consequences of the proposed treatment. This is an individualised assessment of the specific child in front of you, for the specific decision being made — it is emphatically not a fixed age threshold, and two 14-year-olds might be assessed differently depending on their maturity and the complexity of the decision in question.

The Fraser Guidelines

The Fraser guidelines, arising from the same case, apply specifically to contraceptive advice and treatment for under-16s. A doctor may provide contraceptive advice or treatment to a person under 16, without parental knowledge or consent, if all of the following are satisfied:

  • The young person understands the advice being given.
  • The young person cannot be persuaded to inform a parent, or to allow the doctor to inform a parent, that they are seeking this advice or treatment.
  • The young person is very likely to begin or continue having sexual intercourse, with or without contraceptive treatment.
  • Their physical or mental health is likely to suffer unless they receive the advice or treatment.
  • It is in the young person's best interests to receive the advice or treatment without parental knowledge or consent.

Gillick competence is often used, loosely, as an umbrella term for the whole framework, but candidates should know that Fraser specifically concerns contraception and sexual health, while Gillick competence is the broader general test applied to any treatment decision made by a child under 16.

An Important Asymmetry: Consent Versus Refusal

A point that catches out a large number of UKMLA candidates is that Gillick competence works asymmetrically. A Gillick-competent child can validly consent to treatment on their own authority. However, English courts have repeatedly held that where a Gillick-competent young person refuses treatment that is considered necessary for their health, a person with parental responsibility, or the court, can override that refusal in the child's best interests — a position that does not have a direct equivalent in adult capacity law. This asymmetry reflects a protective legal stance toward children that simply does not exist once a patient turns 18. For young people aged 16 or 17, the Mental Capacity Act 2005 begins to apply, alongside a statutory presumption of capacity under the Family Law Reform Act 1969 — although even for this age group, English courts have shown willingness to override a competent refusal of life-saving treatment where the young person is not yet 18.

Capacity Versus Competence: A Distinction UKMLA Candidates Often Blur

"Capacity" and "competence" are frequently used as if interchangeable, but the UKMLA expects candidates to keep them conceptually and terminologically separate. Capacity is the specific statutory test set out in the Mental Capacity Act 2005, applying to people aged 16 and over, defined by the two-stage diagnostic-and-functional test described above. Competence, in the Gillick sense, is the common-law test historically developed for children under 16, asking whether a specific child has sufficient maturity and understanding for a specific treatment decision.

The practical difference matters because the two frameworks are not identical in structure, even though they test conceptually similar underlying questions (can this person understand, weigh, and communicate a decision). Using "capacity" when a scenario clearly concerns a 14-year-old, or "Gillick competent" to describe an adult with a learning disability, is exactly the kind of terminology error that costs marks in written AKT answers and can undermine a candidate's credibility in a CPSA station where an examiner is listening for precise, confident use of the correct legal framework. As a working rule: capacity language for 16 and over, Gillick/Fraser language for under 16, with the 16–17 group as a genuine overlap zone worth being explicit about in an exam answer.

Consent and Capacity as a CPSA Communication Station

Consent conversations are among the most commonly examined communication scenarios in the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment, precisely because they combine legal knowledge with real-time interpersonal skill. A well-prepared candidate needs to demonstrate several things simultaneously in front of the examiner and simulated patient. Consent and mental capacity stations reward candidates who treat the conversation as a genuine two-way exchange rather than a script to be delivered.

Structuring the Consent Conversation

A strong CPSA consent station typically opens by establishing rapport and checking what the patient already understands about their situation, before explaining the proposed intervention in plain, jargon-free language. The candidate should explicitly cover the nature of the procedure, its intended benefit, the material risks (tailored to what matters to this particular patient, per the Montgomery standard), and the reasonable alternatives — including doing nothing. Throughout, the candidate should check understanding using open questions ("What are your thoughts on what I've explained so far?") rather than closed, leading ones ("That all makes sense, doesn't it?"), and should leave explicit space for the patient's questions and concerns.

Recognising and Responding to a Capacity Concern Mid-Station

Some CPSA stations are deliberately designed so the simulated patient displays signs of impaired capacity partway through — repeating the same question without absorbing the answer, being unable to link the information back to their own circumstances, or giving answers that suggest they cannot retain what was just explained. A well-performing candidate notices this shift, gently probes further rather than plowing ahead with the consent process, and is prepared to explain — either to the patient or to the examiner in a follow-up question — that they would formally assess capacity using the two-stage MCA test before proceeding, and would involve family, other members of the multidisciplinary team, or seek senior support if capacity remained genuinely in doubt.

Common CPSA Pitfalls in Consent Stations

  • Rushing the information-giving — delivering a long monologue of risks and benefits without pausing to check understanding or invite questions.
  • Assuming incapacity from diagnosis alone — treating a patient with a psychiatric history or a communication difficulty as automatically lacking capacity, rather than actively assessing the specific decision.
  • Failing to address an unwise-seeming refusal appropriately — pressuring a capacitous patient to change their mind, rather than respecting their decision once genuine understanding has been confirmed.
  • Overlooking voluntariness — not noticing or addressing a family member who is answering on the patient's behalf or visibly pressuring them.
  • Conflating capacity and competence in language — using the wrong framework's terminology for the patient's age group, which examiners are specifically trained to notice.

These stations sit within a wider cluster of CPSA scenarios covering sensitive and legally complex conversations — the same underlying communication skills, careful and non-judgemental questioning, and willingness to seek senior input when the situation exceeds a candidate's competence, come up repeatedly across psychiatry communication stations and other ethically loaded encounters.

How Consent and Capacity Connect to the Wider UKMLA Ethics and Law Syllabus

Consent and mental capacity rarely appear in complete isolation from the rest of the UKMLA's professionalism and ethics content. The GMC's own professional standards, set out in Good Medical Practice, place consent and shared decision-making at the centre of the doctor-patient relationship, and questions frequently layer a capacity issue on top of another ethical theme — confidentiality, safeguarding, or end-of-life decision-making — within a single scenario. A patient who lacks capacity and is also potentially at risk of harm from a family member, for instance, draws simultaneously on capacity law and the safeguarding frameworks covered in dedicated safeguarding revision guidance.

Capacity questions also intersect with the site's broader coverage of vulnerable and marginalised patient groups. Communication with patients whose circumstances make a standard consent conversation more complex — for example, patients navigating transgender health care pathways, or those affected by domestic abuse and adult safeguarding concerns — draws on the same underlying skills of checking genuine understanding, protecting voluntariness, and never assuming incapacity from a label or a difficult social situation. Recognising these cross-topic links is itself a useful revision strategy, since the UKMLA's ethics and law content is deliberately interwoven rather than siloed by textbook chapter.

The GMC's own guidance on this topic is the authoritative reference point that underpins UKMLA question-writing: its consent guidance for doctors sets out the same four-element framework, the centrality of shared decision-making, and the expectation that doctors tailor information to the individual patient rather than working from a generic checklist. Candidates who read this guidance directly, rather than relying solely on secondary revision notes, often find that UKMLA scenario-writing style mirrors its language and worked examples closely.

A Practical Framework for Approaching Consent and Mental Capacity Questions

When faced with an AKT question involving consent or capacity, a reliable approach is to work through a fixed sequence of checks rather than jumping straight to an answer. First, ask whether the four elements of valid consent are all present — is the decision voluntary, does the patient have capacity, has adequate information been given, and is the consent specific to what is actually being proposed? Second, if capacity is in doubt, apply the two-stage test explicitly: is there an impairment of the mind or brain, and if so, does it defeat one of the four functional abilities (understand, retain, use or weigh, communicate)? Third, if capacity is genuinely absent, move to the best-interests checklist and identify the correct decision-maker for this specific context. Fourth, check whether the scenario involves a child, in which case Gillick competence and, where relevant, the Fraser guidelines replace the MCA framework entirely.

This structured approach also transfers directly to the CPSA, where verbalising this same reasoning aloud — even briefly, to the examiner or within the conversation itself — demonstrates the kind of transparent, safety-conscious clinical thinking that scoring rubrics reward. A candidate who says, in effect, "I want to check you've understood what I've explained, because it's important you're making this decision with all the information you need," is explicitly modelling the consent framework rather than merely going through the motions of an information dump.

Frequently Asked Questions About Consent and Mental Capacity in the UKMLA

The questions below reflect how consent and mental capacity UKMLA topics are most commonly phrased by candidates revising this material, with self-contained answers you can check your own understanding against.

What are the four elements of valid consent tested in the UKMLA?

The four elements are voluntariness (the decision is free from coercion), capacity (the patient can understand, retain, weigh, and communicate the decision), information (the patient has been given adequate, patient-specific information about material risks and alternatives under the Montgomery standard), and specificity (the consent relates directly to the intervention actually being proposed). All four must be present simultaneously for consent to be legally valid.

What is the two-stage test for capacity under the Mental Capacity Act 2005?

The two-stage test first asks whether there is an impairment or disturbance in the functioning of the person's mind or brain (the diagnostic test), and then, only if that is present, asks whether the impairment means the person cannot understand, retain, use or weigh, or communicate the specific decision (the functional test). A person must fail the functional test in at least one of its four elements, in addition to satisfying the diagnostic test, to be found to lack capacity.

Can a patient lack capacity for one decision but not another?

Yes, and this is one of the most important principles in Mental Capacity Act 2005 practice: capacity is always decision-specific and time-specific, never a global or permanent label. A patient may lack capacity to consent to a complex surgical procedure with several treatment options while retaining full capacity to consent to a simple blood test, and capacity can also change over hours as a reversible cause, such as delirium or intoxication, resolves.

What happens when a patient is assessed as lacking capacity for a decision?

Once a patient genuinely lacks capacity for a specific decision, that decision must be made in their best interests, following the Mental Capacity Act's structured checklist, which includes considering the person's past and present wishes and values, consulting those close to them, and choosing the option least restrictive of their rights and freedom. The decision-maker is usually the treating clinician, unless a valid Lasting Power of Attorney or, in its absence, an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate applies.

What is the difference between Gillick competence and mental capacity?

Gillick competence is the common-law test used for children under 16, asking whether a specific child has sufficient maturity and understanding to consent to a specific treatment decision, while capacity is the Mental Capacity Act 2005's statutory test, applying formally to people aged 16 and over. The two tests examine conceptually similar abilities but come from different legal sources and should not be described using each other's terminology in an exam answer.

Can a Gillick-competent young person refuse life-saving treatment?

Not necessarily. While a Gillick-competent child under 16 can validly consent to treatment on their own authority, English courts have repeatedly held that a competent young person's refusal of treatment considered necessary for their health can be overridden by a person with parental responsibility or by the court, acting in the child's best interests. This asymmetry between consent and refusal does not apply once a patient reaches 18.

What are Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards and when do they apply?

Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards provide the legal authorisation required when meeting a person's care needs in a hospital or care home necessarily involves restricting their liberty to a degree that amounts to a deprivation of liberty, most commonly for patients who lack capacity due to conditions such as advanced dementia. They exist to protect the person's human rights, require formal authorisation and regular review, and are not a substitute for informal restraint decided by ward staff alone.

Is an unwise decision by a patient evidence that they lack capacity?

No. The Mental Capacity Act 2005's third statutory principle states explicitly that a person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because they make a decision that others consider unwise. A patient who understands their situation and genuinely weighs the risks and benefits, but reaches a conclusion a clinician disagrees with, retains capacity and their decision should be respected and documented.

Conclusion

Consent and mental capacity UKMLA questions reward candidates who can move fluently between statutory detail and real-world clinical judgement — knowing the exact wording of the Mental Capacity Act 2005's two-stage test and five principles is necessary, but so is recognising when a seemingly informed patient cannot actually weigh their options, or when an "unwise" refusal is simply a capacitous patient's valid choice. Keep the four elements of consent, the diagnostic-and-functional test, the best-interests checklist, and the Gillick/Fraser framework for children distinct in your mind, and practise applying each one to realistic scenarios rather than memorising them as isolated facts. This dual fluency — legal precision on the AKT, and warm, transparent communication in the CPSA — is exactly what the exam is designed to test.
