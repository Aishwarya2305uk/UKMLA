UKMLA special circumstances is the umbrella term for the range of accommodations and flexibilities a candidate can request when something other than disability affects their ability to sit the AKT or CPSA on a scheduled date, in a standard format, or without additional support. It is easy to assume that "reasonable adjustments" only covers disability and long-term health conditions, but the GMC, medical schools, and Pearson VUE also operate separate — and often less publicised — routes for candidates who are pregnant or breastfeeding, who need to observe religious commitments around their exam, or who are dealing with a bereavement or a serious personal or family emergency close to their sitting. This guide is written for both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) who may never have needed to think about any of this before, and who are unsure exactly who to contact, what evidence is expected, and how far in advance to act.

Before going further, it is important to be precise about what this guide does not cover. If your circumstance relates to a disability, a long-term health condition, a neurodevelopmental difference such as dyslexia or ADHD, or a mental health condition, that territory is covered in full — legal framework, evidence tables, step-by-step application processes for both UK students and IMGs, and appeals — in our dedicated guide to UKMLA disability access adjustments. Read that guide first if disability or a diagnosed health condition is the reason you need support. This article picks up everything else: the circumstances that do not stem from a disability but can still, quite legitimately, mean you need the exam process to flex around you.

The Quick Answer: What Counts as a Special Circumstance

UKMLA special circumstances cover situations that are temporary, situational, or personal rather than related to a diagnosed disability or long-term health condition — and they are handled through a mixture of the GMC, your medical school, and Pearson VUE, depending on which part of the process is affected and whether you are a UK student or an IMG.

In practical terms, the categories that recur most often are:

  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding needs on exam day — things like additional rest breaks, private space to express milk, or seating close to toilet facilities.
  • Religious observance — requests around prayer time or private space during a long exam session, or, in some circumstances, asking whether an exam date can be moved because it falls on or near a significant religious observance.
  • Bereavement or a serious personal/family emergency close to an exam date — compassionate rescheduling, deferral, or in some cases a fee refund where attendance genuinely was not possible.
  • Other exceptional personal circumstances — the GMC's own published guidance uses language broad enough to include things such as sudden illness in the family, safety incidents, and other one-off events that were not foreseeable at the time of booking.

None of these are handled by a single, universal process. Where you apply, and to whom, depends on whether the affected exam is delivered by your medical school (most UK-based finals-year AKT sittings) or booked directly through GMC Online and delivered at a Pearson VUE test centre (PLAB 1/AKT for IMGs, and CPSA/PLAB 2 bookings). The rest of this guide works through each category in turn, then sets out exactly how to make a request. Throughout, we are deliberately careful to describe what is typically requestable and what bodies generally offer, rather than asserting fixed rules — these policies are reviewed periodically, evidence requirements can change, and the only way to be certain of the current position for your own sitting is to check directly with the GMC, Pearson VUE, or your medical school before you rely on anything written here.

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding Needs on Exam Day

Pregnancy is not a disability under the Equality Act 2010, but it is explicitly recognised by exam bodies as a circumstance that can justify support on exam day, and separately as one of the "exceptional circumstances" that can justify rescheduling if a candidate is genuinely unable to attend. It sits in a slightly unusual middle ground: some pregnancy-related needs are handled as day-of accommodations, and more disruptive pregnancy complications are handled as a rescheduling or refund matter.

What Is Typically Requestable on the Day

Both the GMC's PLAB guidance and Pearson VUE's own accommodations framework name pregnancy-related needs as a legitimate basis for support, alongside disability and health conditions more generally. The kinds of accommodations that are typically discussed for pregnant or breastfeeding candidates in exam settings — and that are worth explicitly requesting rather than assuming will be offered automatically — include:

  • Additional or more frequent rest breaks during a long exam session such as the AKT.
  • Seating near toilet facilities, which is a standard adjustment category already used for pregnancy, IBD, and bladder conditions.
  • Permission to bring water and light snacks into the exam room where this is not otherwise permitted.
  • A private space to express or pump breast milk during a break, for breastfeeding candidates sitting an exam away from home.

Requesting pregnancy or breastfeeding support is one of the most common forms of UKMLA special circumstances request the GMC and Pearson VUE handle, and candidates should not hesitate to raise it simply because it feels like a routine, everyday situation rather than a dramatic emergency. On the breastfeeding point specifically, Pearson VUE's published accommodations policy is more explicit than most exam providers': candidates who need an exception to breastfeed or express milk during an appointment can request this as part of the standard accommodations process, and the exam programme sponsoring the booking (in the UKMLA's case, the GMC) has to approve the request. Pearson VUE-operated test centres aim to provide a private space for this; where a candidate is testing somewhere that is not a Pearson VUE-owned centre, the stated approach is to make "reasonable efforts" to find a suitable private space rather than guaranteeing an identical setup everywhere. That distinction matters in practice — a candidate testing at a smaller or third-party venue should not assume the exact same facilities as a flagship Pearson VUE centre, and should ask specifically what will be available at their assigned venue.

When Pregnancy Affects Whether You Can Attend At All

Separately from day-of accommodations, pregnancy complications, an unexpected early labour, a difficult delivery, or a medical need to avoid travel can mean a candidate simply cannot sit on their booked date. This is where pregnancy overlaps with the GMC's exceptional circumstances process discussed later in this guide — the GMC's own published position on PLAB booking disruptions specifically lists challenges related to pregnancy and maternity among the circumstances it will consider for a rescheduled sitting, an extended eligibility window, or in some cases a refund of the cancellation fee. If you are approaching your exam date pregnant, it is worth reading that section in full and, if there is any realistic chance your circumstances could change quickly, contacting the GMC well before the date rather than waiting to see what happens.

UK Students vs IMGs: Who to Contact

For UK medical students, pregnancy-related exam support during finals-year AKT sittings is usually coordinated through the medical school's student support or occupational health service in parallel with the exam office — many universities have a specific maternity/parental leave and academic policy that interacts with assessment scheduling, and it is worth engaging with that policy early rather than treating the exam adjustment request as a standalone matter. For IMGs booking PLAB 1 (AKT) or PLAB 2 (CPSA) directly, the request goes to the GMC in the first instance, since it is the GMC that approves accommodation requests that Pearson VUE then implements at the test centre.

Postnatal Considerations and Returning to Sit an Exam After Childbirth

A related but distinct question is what happens when a candidate has already given birth and is deciding when to sit, or resit, a UKMLA component during the postnatal period. This is not the same request as day-of accommodations discussed above — it is a question of timing and eligibility windows rather than physical support in the exam room. For UK medical students, this typically overlaps with a university's maternity/parental leave academic policy, which will usually specify how long a period of interruption to study is available and how sitting dates are protected or extended around it. For IMGs, the relevant consideration is the GMC's PLAB eligibility period — the fixed window within which PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 must both be passed — and whether a period of pregnancy, childbirth, or early postnatal recovery affecting exam attendance can justify an extension to that window. As with everything else in this guide, do not assume an extension is automatic: raise the question directly with the GMC well in advance, explain the expected timeline, and ask what flexibility exists for your specific eligibility period rather than working backwards from an assumed answer.

It is also worth candidates being aware that pregnancy-related special circumstances requests do not need to be framed apologetically or treated as an imposition. Both the GMC and Pearson VUE explicitly name pregnancy and related needs as a recognised basis for support precisely because this is a common and entirely foreseeable situation among exam candidates generally, not a rare edge case. Treating the request as routine, factual, and well-documented tends to produce a smoother outcome than treating it as an exceptional favour.

Religious Observance Considerations

Religious observance is another area that is frequently assumed to be covered by "reasonable adjustments" in a general sense, but it is worth being precise about what is actually, verifiably on offer rather than assuming a specific accommodation exists. As with pregnancy and bereavement, religious observance sits firmly within the category of UKMLA special circumstances rather than disability, and it follows the same core principle: request it in writing, well in advance, through the correct body. Two distinct types of request come up here, and they are treated differently.

Prayer Time and Private Space During a Long Exam Session

For candidates whose religious practice includes fixed daily prayer times, a long single-session exam such as the AKT can create a genuine conflict, particularly where a prayer window falls in the middle of the sitting. Pearson VUE's accommodations framework and general test-centre policies allow candidates to request breaks and, where needed, a private space for religious observance as part of the standard accommodations process — this is requested in the same way as other non-disability accommodations, before the exam is scheduled, rather than assumed or arranged informally on the day. Religious apparel (headscarves, turbans, and similar items) is separately and routinely permitted at test centres as a matter of course rather than something that needs a formal accommodation request, though candidates should still expect the standard security check that applies to all headwear at a test centre.

Because this needs to be arranged before the exam is scheduled, the practical advice is the same as for every other accommodation in this guide: raise it when you book, not on the morning of the exam, and get any agreed arrangement confirmed in writing so there is no ambiguity at the test centre.

Rescheduling Around Religious Observances

This is the area where it is most important to avoid overstating what is guaranteed. Major religious observances — significant festivals, extended fasting periods, or pilgrimage obligations — can fall close to or on an exam date, and candidates sometimes ask whether they can have their sitting moved as a matter of course to avoid this. The honest answer is that this is not a blanket, automatically-granted entitlement. What does exist is the GMC's general exceptional circumstances process (covered in detail below), through which a candidate can raise a genuine, individual conflict — for example, an inability to travel or concentrate adequately during a specific observance period — as one of several types of situation the GMC will consider case by case. Whether a particular request is accommodated depends on the specifics of the individual case and the exam scheduling constraints at the time, not on a published list of religious dates that automatically triggers rescheduling.

Because published policy in this specific area can change and because the GMC and medical schools review requests individually rather than against a fixed calendar, candidates who anticipate a conflict between an exam sitting and a significant religious observance should raise it directly with the GMC (for GMC-booked exams) or their medical school's exam office (for school-delivered sittings) as early as possible, explain the nature of the conflict clearly, and ask what options are genuinely available for that specific sitting — rather than assuming in advance that a reschedule will or will not be granted.

Fasting Periods and Sustained-Concentration Exams

A specific sub-case worth addressing directly is extended fasting, since the AKT is a long, sustained-concentration assessment and fasting can affect energy levels, hydration, and focus over several hours. Candidates observing a fasting period that coincides with an exam date have a few realistic options to weigh, none of which is a single "correct" answer for everyone: sitting the exam as scheduled and, where relevant, requesting adjustments such as additional short breaks; asking whether an alternative date outside the fasting period is available, through the same exceptional circumstances channel described above; or, for candidates who know their own performance patterns well, simply proceeding without any adjustment because they do not feel their concentration is meaningfully affected. There is no obligation to request anything — this is a personal decision, and the accommodations described in this guide exist for candidates who want to use them, not a default expectation placed on anyone.

Candidates considering a request related to fasting should raise it with plenty of notice, since the exam calendar for a given sitting cycle is typically fixed well in advance and options for a genuinely alternative date may be limited depending on how close to the fasting period the request is made.

Bereavement and Serious Personal or Family Emergencies

Bereavement is arguably the most well-documented category of UKMLA special circumstances, and it is one of the circumstances most explicitly and consistently recognised across GMC guidance, and it sits alongside serious illness, safety incidents, and other sudden personal emergencies as grounds for what the GMC describes as its approach to "other exam disruptions."

What the GMC's Published Position Says

The GMC's own guidance on cancelling or amending a PLAB booking states that it will consider a full refund of exam fees where exceptional circumstances prevent a candidate from attending — and it specifically names bereavement, alongside examples such as visa refusal, the introduction of travel restrictions, serious ongoing ill health, and mandatory military service, as situations that fall within this category. Where a candidate is affected by one of these circumstances, the GMC's stated approach is to consider what support it can offer on a case-by-case basis, which can include arranging an earlier resit date, extending the candidate's eligibility period to attempt the exam again, or refunding some or all of the fees paid, depending on the specifics of the situation.

This matters because it distinguishes bereavement/emergency support from a simple missed-exam scenario: a candidate who does not attend without notifying the GMC is treated very differently from one who contacts the GMC promptly, explains a genuine emergency, and provides supporting evidence when asked. The difference between the two outcomes is almost entirely about how and when the candidate communicates with the GMC.

Compassionate Rescheduling in Practice

In practice, "compassionate rescheduling" for bereavement or a serious family emergency tends to work in one of a few ways, depending on timing and how the exam was booked:

  • If the emergency arises with enough notice before the exam date, the candidate contacts the relevant body to request a booking amendment or postponement before the sitting, ideally avoiding any late-cancellation fee that would otherwise apply.
  • If the emergency arises very close to or on the day of the exam itself, the candidate (or someone acting on their behalf) should notify the GMC, Pearson VUE, or the medical school's exam office as soon as reasonably possible, even if this is after the exam window has passed, and follow up promptly with a written explanation.
  • Where a fee has already been paid and the candidate could not attend for a bereavement or comparable emergency, the GMC's published position is that it will consider a refund of exam fees on a case-by-case basis, generally once the candidate has cancelled their booking and provided evidence of the circumstances.

Evidence requirements for bereavement and family-emergency claims are typically lighter-touch and more flexible than for disability-related adjustments, in recognition of the fact that formal documentation (a death certificate, for example) may take time to obtain and is not always the first priority for someone dealing with a sudden loss. That said, exam bodies do generally expect some form of supporting evidence in due course — this might be a death certificate, an order of service, a letter from a hospital or GP confirming a family medical emergency, or comparable documentation — and providing this as soon as it is reasonably available strengthens the request and speeds up processing.

UK Students: Mitigating Circumstances Processes

UK medical schools run their own "mitigating circumstances" or "extenuating circumstances" processes, which is the internal university mechanism most bereavement and family-emergency requests for school-delivered AKT sittings go through, rather than a direct approach to the GMC. These processes typically require a claim to be submitted within a set window (often within one or two weeks of the affected assessment, though this varies by institution) along with whatever evidence is available at the time, with the option to supply further evidence later. If you are a UK student affected by a bereavement or emergency close to an exam, your first point of contact should be your medical school's student support office or the specific mitigating circumstances panel/committee that handles assessment-related claims — they will be able to confirm the exact deadline and evidence format your institution uses.

Other Exceptional Personal Circumstances

Beyond the three categories above, the GMC's exceptional circumstances process is deliberately written to be broad rather than an exhaustive checklist. Alongside bereavement, pregnancy/maternity challenges, and travel disruption, the GMC's published guidance references situations such as the outbreak of war affecting a candidate's home region, ongoing and significant travel restrictions, and mandatory military service as examples of circumstances it will consider. This is illustrative rather than a closed list — the underlying test the GMC applies is whether a genuinely exceptional, largely unforeseeable circumstance has prevented a candidate from attending or adequately preparing for their exam, not whether the specific situation matches a name on a published list. Because the list is illustrative rather than exhaustive, candidates facing a circumstance that does not neatly match one of the named examples — a house fire, a sudden and serious safety incident, an acute mental health crisis that falls short of an ongoing diagnosed condition, a visa or immigration complication outside the candidate's control — should still contact the GMC or their medical school and explain the situation in full. The worst outcome is generally not asking at all and assuming nothing can be done.

What Is Not Typically Covered

It is worth being equally clear about what generally falls outside this process, since setting realistic expectations avoids wasted time and disappointment close to an exam date. Routine, foreseeable scheduling conflicts — a holiday already booked, a clash with a non-urgent family event, general exam stress or nerves that do not amount to a diagnosed and evidenced condition, or simply feeling underprepared — are not treated as exceptional circumstances by the GMC or medical schools. If ordinary exam-related stress and anxiety, rather than a specific acute emergency, is the issue, our guide on managing pre-exam nerves and anxiety covers preparation and coping strategies that are more appropriate for that situation than a special circumstances request.

How to Actually Request a Special Circumstance Accommodation

The single most important piece of practical advice in this entire guide is timing: contact the relevant body as early as possible, ideally when you first become aware of the need, and always before your exam date wherever the circumstance allows for advance notice. Bereavement and sudden emergencies are, by definition, often impossible to anticipate — but pregnancy, planned religious observances, and known upcoming commitments are not, and requests for these should be raised at the earliest realistic point, not in the final week before the exam.

If you are unsure which category applies to you, the safest default is to contact the GMC directly (for any GMC-booked component) or your medical school's exam administration team (for a school-delivered sitting) and ask them to confirm the correct process — do not guess or assume the same process applies as for a friend or colleague at a different institution or in a different situation, since the details do genuinely vary.

Step 2: Explain the Circumstance Clearly and in Writing

Whichever body you contact, put the request in writing (email is standard) and be specific: what the circumstance is, how it affects your ability to attend or perform on the scheduled date, what outcome you are asking for (a rescheduled date, a specific accommodation on the day, a fee refund, or a combination), and your exam booking reference or candidate number. Vague requests that do not specify what outcome is being sought tend to take longer to process, simply because the receiving team has to come back and ask for clarification before they can act.

Step 3: Gather Supporting Evidence Where Relevant

Evidence requirements vary by circumstance and are, again, not something this guide should assert as fixed — but as a general pattern, exceptional circumstances such as bereavement or a family medical emergency tend to require lighter, more flexible evidence (which can often follow after the initial request), while planned accommodations such as pregnancy-related support or a religious observance request tend to be easier to arrange smoothly when supporting detail is provided upfront, even if formal documentary evidence is not strictly demanded in every case. When in doubt about what evidence is expected for your specific circumstance, ask the GMC or your medical school directly what they need rather than guessing and submitting the wrong type of documentation.

Step 4: Confirm the Outcome in Writing Before Your Exam

Once a request has been considered, make sure you have a written confirmation of the outcome — whether that is confirmation of a rescheduled date, confirmation of an approved day-of accommodation, or confirmation of a refund — before your original or amended exam date arrives. If you are approved for an accommodation such as a private space or additional breaks, it is worth carrying a copy of that confirmation with you to the test centre, in case there is any discrepancy between what was agreed and what the invigilation team at the venue is expecting on the day. This mirrors the advice given for disability accommodations, and the same principle applies regardless of the underlying reason for the accommodation: get it in writing, keep a copy, and be ready to produce it if needed.

How This Interacts With the Wider Exam Process

None of the categories above happen in isolation from the rest of the UKMLA process. If your special circumstance affects your exam booking itself, it is worth reviewing our guide to the GMC Online exam booking process so you understand exactly where in that process a rescheduling request or cancellation needs to be actioned. And if your concern is less about a specific circumstance and more about knowing what to generally expect from the exam day itself — timings, what to bring, what the venue looks like — our guide on what to expect on UKMLA exam day is a useful companion piece, since many special circumstances requests (extra breaks, private space, seating position) only make full sense once you understand the standard exam-day structure they are being requested against.

It is also worth noting that special circumstances are not limited to the AKT. Candidates sitting the CPSA clinical skills exam — the component built around simulated patient encounters — can equally request pregnancy, religious observance, or bereavement-related accommodations for that exam. Because the CPSA also involves scenarios where candidates themselves must communicate sensitively (for example, our guide on breaking bad news in the CPSA covers how candidates are assessed on handling difficult conversations with simulated patients), it is worth flagging to the GMC in advance if a recent bereavement or ongoing personal circumstance means a specific station topic would be particularly difficult to sit — while stations cannot generally be removed or substituted, the GMC and exam centre may be able to offer additional pastoral support around the sitting, or, in significant cases, consider whether a short postponement is appropriate.

Common Mistakes Candidates Make With Special Circumstances Requests

A recurring pattern across pregnancy, religious observance, and bereavement requests alike is that candidates tend to make the same handful of avoidable mistakes with a UKMLA special circumstances request, regardless of which category their situation falls into. Being aware of these in advance can save real time and stress at what is often already a difficult moment.

Waiting Until Very Close to the Exam Date

The single most common mistake is delay. Even for circumstances that genuinely could not have been anticipated, there is a meaningful difference between contacting the GMC or a medical school the moment a situation becomes clear versus waiting a week or two to see how things develop. Exam bodies cannot always guarantee a resolution on short notice, particularly where a rescheduled sitting depends on space at an already-booked test centre or an administrative process that has its own processing time. Where any element of a special circumstance is foreseeable — a due date, a known religious observance period, a planned medical procedure — raise it as early in the exam cycle as possible, ideally at the point of booking rather than afterwards.

Assuming an Informal Conversation Is Enough

Mentioning a circumstance verbally to an invigilator on the morning of the exam, or in an informal conversation with a tutor, is not the same as making a formal request through the correct channel. Without a written record, there is nothing for the GMC, Pearson VUE, or a university's exam office to act on, and no confirmation for the candidate to rely on if something goes wrong on the day. Every request in this guide should be made in writing, to the correct body, with enough detail for a decision to be made.

Not Following Up for Written Confirmation

Submitting a request is only the first half of the process. Candidates sometimes assume that because they have emailed a request, it has automatically been approved, and are then caught out when the accommodation is not actually in place on the day. Always chase a specific written confirmation of the outcome, and do not treat the absence of a reply as an implicit yes.

Conflating Special Circumstances With General Exam Stress

As noted above, ordinary nervousness, feeling underprepared, or general exam-related anxiety that does not amount to a specific, evidenced circumstance is not what this process is designed for, and framing a request this way is unlikely to succeed. That is not to dismiss exam anxiety as unimportant — it is a real and common experience — but it is addressed through preparation, support, and coping strategies rather than through a rescheduling or accommodation request. See the anxiety-management guide linked earlier in this article for that side of things.

Not Checking Whether the Circumstance Sits With the University or the GMC

Because UK medical students and IMGs go through different administrative routes, a mistake that costs real time is contacting the wrong body first — for example, a UK student emailing the GMC directly about a school-delivered AKT sitting, when the request actually needs to go through the university's own mitigating circumstances process. If in doubt, ask either body which one is responsible for your specific exam component before submitting a formal request, so the request reaches the right team the first time.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Special Circumstances

Is a UKMLA special circumstances request the same as a disability adjustment?

No. Disability adjustments are a separate, legally distinct process under the Equality Act 2010 for candidates with a diagnosed disability or long-term health condition, covered in full in our disability access adjustments guide. Special circumstances cover situational, temporary, or personal factors — pregnancy, religious observance, bereavement, and comparable emergencies — that are not disabilities but can still justify a rescheduled date, a day-of accommodation, or a fee refund.

Can I get my UKMLA exam date moved because of a bereavement?

The GMC's published approach to exam disruptions states that it will consider support, including a rescheduled date, an extended eligibility window, or a refund, where a bereavement or similarly exceptional circumstance genuinely prevents a candidate from attending. This is considered case by case rather than automatically granted, so contact the GMC as soon as possible, explain the situation, and expect to provide supporting evidence in due course.

Does the GMC allow rescheduling around religious holidays?

There is no published, automatic entitlement to have an exam moved simply because it falls near a religious observance. Genuine individual conflicts can be raised through the GMC's general exceptional circumstances process and will be considered case by case, but candidates should not assume in advance that a reschedule will be granted and should contact the GMC directly, well ahead of the exam date, to discuss their specific situation.

Can I request breaks to breastfeed or pump during the AKT?

Yes, this is a recognised category under Pearson VUE's accommodations framework, which candidates can request as part of the standard pre-booking accommodations process, subject to approval by the GMC as the exam sponsor. A private space is generally provided or reasonable efforts are made to arrange one, though facilities can vary by test centre, so it is worth confirming what will specifically be available at your assigned venue.

What evidence do I need for a bereavement-related exam rescheduling request?

Evidence requirements are generally more flexible for bereavement than for planned accommodations, since formal documentation such as a death certificate may not be immediately available. Contact the GMC or your medical school as soon as possible to explain the situation, and expect to be asked to provide some form of supporting evidence, such as a death certificate or order of service, once it becomes reasonably available.

Who do I contact for a special circumstances request if I am an IMG?

IMGs sitting PLAB 1 (AKT) or PLAB 2 (CPSA) should contact the GMC directly, since the GMC both owns the booking relationship through GMC Online and approves any accommodations that Pearson VUE then implements at the test centre. This differs from UK medical students, whose school-delivered AKT sittings are usually handled through the university's own exam office or mitigating circumstances process.

Will a special circumstances request affect my UKMLA result or how I am assessed?

No. Special circumstances accommodations and rescheduling exist to ensure candidates can access the exam fairly despite a genuine, non-academic disruption — they do not change the pass standard, the content of the exam, or how your performance is marked. The purpose is to remove a barrier to attending or performing under normal conditions, not to adjust the assessment itself.

Conclusion

Handling UKMLA special circumstances well comes down to two things: knowing that pregnancy, religious observance, bereavement, and comparable personal emergencies are genuinely recognised categories separate from disability, and acting early enough that the GMC, Pearson VUE, or your medical school actually has time to help. Policies, evidence requirements, and the exact accommodations on offer can and do change, so always check the current official guidance before relying on anything in this article, and put every request in writing with enough detail for the receiving team to act on it.
