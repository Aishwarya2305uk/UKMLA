UKMLA for Indian doctors means passing the two GMC-recognised assessments that sit under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, commonly called the AKT and CPSA, then completing GMC registration through EPIC verification, an accepted internship and an approved English language score. For most Indian medical graduates the whole journey, from booking the first test to holding full GMC registration, takes roughly twelve to eighteen months and costs somewhere between £1,345 and £1,800 in exam and registration fees alone, before English tests, travel and visa costs are added. This guide walks through every stage of that route in detail, using the General Medical Council's own Medical Licensing Assessment guidance as the primary source, so that a final-year MBBS student in Pune and a working doctor in Mumbai can both use it as a single reference.

Key takeaways

  • Indian doctors follow the same UKMLA-aligned route as every other international medical graduate (IMG): a knowledge test (AKT) and a clinical and professional skills assessment (CPSA), both set to Medical Licensing Assessment standards.
  • From 1 April 2026, the AKT costs £283 and the CPSA costs £1,036, according to the GMC's official fee schedule; registration adds a further £26 (provisional) or £481 (full).
  • The AKT can be sat at Pearson VUE centres in India; the CPSA is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester, so every Indian candidate must travel to the UK at least once.
  • EPIC verification, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform, must confirm your Indian MBBS before the GMC will process your registration application.
  • Most Indian candidates need IELTS 7.5 overall (7.0 in each band) or OET Medicine grade B in every component, unless they qualify for an exemption.
  • Registering with the GMC is the last step before you can apply for NHS jobs, but it is not the same as finding a post: budgeting for the Health and Care Worker visa and NHS-facing job applications is a separate, later stage.

Who this route is for: Indian doctors and the UKMLA

The Medical Licensing Assessment, or MLA, is the GMC's overarching quality bar for anyone joining the UK medical register, whether they trained at a UK medical school or overseas. UK final-year students sit the MLA directly as part of their degree. International medical graduates, including doctors who completed their MBBS in India, continue to sit what the GMC still formally calls PLAB, which has been restructured so that its two parts are aligned with, and equivalent to, the MLA's AKT and CPSA components. In practice, therefore, when this site and most independent guides refer to "UKMLA for Indian doctors," they mean the AKT-and-CPSA route that Indian IMGs use to reach the same licensing standard as a UK graduate.

This distinction matters because it explains a detail that confuses many Indian applicants: your GMC paperwork may still reference "PLAB 1" and "PLAB 2" fees, while revision providers, question banks and this site describe the same two assessments as "UKMLA AKT" and "UKMLA CPSA." They are the same tests, viewed through two different naming conventions. If you want the deepest possible breakdown of the format itself, read our dedicated guides to the UKMLA AKT format and preparation and the UKMLA CPSA clinical assessment, both of which apply equally to Indian candidates.

Consequently, an Indian MBBS graduate's path into UK practice runs through exactly the same eligibility gateway as every other IMG: an accepted primary medical qualification, a completed internship, an approved English language score, EPIC/MyIntealth verification and a pass in both the AKT and the CPSA. What differs for India specifically is the volume of candidates, the availability of AKT test centres inside the country, the currency conversion on every fee, and a handful of India-specific comparison questions, such as how this route stacks up against NEET PG. The rest of this guide works through each of those in turn.

Step 1: Confirm your eligibility as an Indian IMG

Before spending money on tests, an Indian doctor should confirm four eligibility pillars, because failing any one of them stalls the whole application regardless of exam results.

1. Your primary medical qualification (PMQ)

Indian medical schools are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC checks as part of primary source verification, so an Indian MBBS is generally accepted as a valid PMQ. Acceptance of the qualification itself is a separate question from whether you have passed the UKMLA route or completed an internship, so do not assume a listed degree means you are automatically eligible to register.

2. Your internship

The GMC expects an acceptable period of supervised clinical practice completed immediately before or after your degree, generally consisting of at least twelve months split across medicine and surgery placements, with defined minimum periods in each. Most Indian doctors satisfy this through their compulsory rotating internship, but because internship structures and durations can differ between Indian medical colleges and change over time, always confirm your specific internship meets current GMC criteria via the internship requirement guide before assuming it counts.

3. English language evidence

Almost every Indian doctor needs to provide English language evidence unless their medical education was conducted and assessed substantially in English and meets the GMC's specific exemption criteria. In practice, this means most Indian candidates sit IELTS Academic or OET Medicine regardless of how confident they are in spoken English, simply because the GMC needs a standardised score on file. See the full breakdown, including exemption routes, in our English language requirements guide.

4. EPIC / MyIntealth primary source verification

Historically, this verification step ran through a service called EPIC (the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials). ECFMG, now operating as Intealth, has since moved candidates onto a newer platform called MyIntealth, although many guides, forums and even some GMC-facing paperwork still refer informally to "EPIC verification." Whichever name you see, the underlying requirement is unchanged: your Indian medical school must confirm directly to Intealth that your degree is genuine before the GMC will accept your registration application. Because this step depends on your university responding to a verification request, it is often the single biggest source of delay in the whole process, so Indian doctors should start it as early as possible, ideally before booking the AKT. Our dedicated GMC registration and EPIC verification guide for doctors from India walks through the account setup and document requirements step by step.

Where the National Medical Commission fits in

Since 2020, the National Medical Commission (NMC) has replaced the Medical Council of India as the body governing medical education and registration inside India, and your State Medical Council registration certificate, issued once you complete your internship, is one of the documents Indian doctors are typically asked to produce during GMC registration. Because the NMC and its predecessor have changed names, structures and even MBBS curricula over the years, an Indian doctor's exact paperwork can look different depending on when they graduated. If in doubt, contact your medical college and State Medical Council well in advance so that certified copies of your degree, internship completion certificate and state registration are ready before you need them, rather than requesting them for the first time under time pressure.

Building a documents checklist early

Because so many of the delays Indian doctors experience come from missing or slow-to-obtain paperwork rather than exam performance, it helps to assemble a documents folder before you book anything. At minimum, most Indian applicants need: their MBBS degree certificate, internship completion certificate, State Medical Council or NMC registration certificate, passport, English test certificate (IELTS or OET), and their EPIC/MyIntealth verification report once it is issued. Keeping certified, dated copies of each of these ready, rather than requesting them piecemeal as the GMC asks for them, is one of the simplest ways an Indian doctor can shorten the overall timeline.

Step 2: Understand the AKT and CPSA as an Indian candidate

Once eligibility is confirmed, the practical work begins with the two assessments. The AKT is a computer-based knowledge test covering the breadth of the MLA content map, delivered in a single sitting of multiple-choice-style questions. Because it is Pearson VUE-delivered, Indian candidates can typically sit the AKT at test centres within India, which meaningfully reduces the cost and logistics of the first stage compared with candidates from countries with no local centre. For the exact list of currently available cities and how to check seat availability, see our UKMLA AKT test centres guide covering India, Pakistan, the UK and worldwide.

The CPSA is different in kind. It tests clinical examination skills, practical procedures, data interpretation and communication across a circuit of stations, and it is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester. This means every Indian candidate, however strong their AKT result, must travel to the UK at least once to complete the CPSA. Because of that, most experienced advisers recommend Indian doctors treat the CPSA trip as a single, well-prepared visit: booking flights and CPSA-specific short courses together, rather than travelling more than once if it can be avoided. Our companion guide to the CPSA clinical assessment explains station types and scoring in detail, while the clinical examination stations guide is worth revising alongside your AKT preparation, not after it, since several CPSA skills reinforce AKT topics.

How Indian doctors typically prepare for the AKT

Because the AKT draws on the same content map as UK final-year students use, most Indian doctors combine a recognised question bank with the official MLA content map as a syllabus checklist, rather than relying on general MBBS revision alone, since UK prescribing conventions, NICE-aligned guidelines and NHS-specific processes are tested in ways that differ from Indian undergraduate exams. A structured three-to-six-month preparation window is typical, with many candidates layering in mock papers in the final six to eight weeks. Our three-months-to-AKT countdown guide and the broader Preparation pillar both give a realistic week-by-week structure that Indian candidates can adapt around work or internship commitments.

Preparing for the CPSA as an Indian doctor

The CPSA also tests communication style and professionalism in ways that catch some Indian candidates off guard, not because clinical knowledge is lacking, but because UK patient-communication norms, such as explicit consent-seeking language and structured breaking-bad-news frameworks, are assessed quite specifically. Reviewing the GMC's own Good Medical Practice standards alongside CPSA-specific practice stations, rather than assuming general clinical competence will carry the station, tends to make a measurable difference. Our Good Medical Practice and the UKMLA guide and psychiatry communication stations guide are both useful preparation companions for this reason.

Because many Indian doctors are not yet earning a UK-level salary at the point of applying, this discount is genuinely worth checking on the GMC's fees for doctors page before assuming you must pay the full rate.

Step 4: The application and registration process, step by step

Although every candidate's exact order varies slightly, most Indian doctors follow a recognisable sequence. Working through it as a checklist helps avoid the common trap of booking an exam before a prerequisite is actually in place.

  1. Confirm eligibility. Check your PMQ, internship and English language position against current GMC criteria before spending money on anything else.
  2. Start EPIC/MyIntealth verification early. Because your Indian medical school must respond to a verification request, this step has the least predictable timeline and should be initiated months, not weeks, before you plan to register.
  3. Sit IELTS or OET and receive your certified score, allowing time for a resit if your first attempt falls short of the required bands.
  4. Book and sit the AKT at a Pearson VUE centre in India (or elsewhere), typically after several months of structured revision. See our UKMLA preparation guide for IMG candidates for a realistic study timeline.
  5. Book and travel for the CPSA in Manchester, usually scheduling a short UK visit that combines final preparation with the assessment itself.
  6. Submit your GMC registration application once your AKT and CPSA results, EPIC/MyIntealth report, English language evidence and internship confirmation are all in hand.
  7. Pay the relevant registration fee (provisional or full, per the table above) and await GMC processing.
  8. Receive GMC registration with a licence to practise, at which point you can legally begin applying for NHS posts.
  9. Arrange your visa once you have a confirmed job offer, most commonly the Health and Care Worker visa (see below).

Because several of these steps run in parallel rather than strictly one after another, experienced candidates and the UKMLA crash course guide for doctors both recommend starting EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English testing as early as possible, so that by the time your AKT and CPSA results arrive, the rest of the paperwork is not the bottleneck.

Provisional or full registration: which should an Indian doctor apply for?

Many Indian doctors are unsure whether to apply for provisional or full registration first, and the answer depends mainly on whether your internship is already complete and confirmed at the point you apply. Provisional registration, at the lower £26 application fee, is generally for doctors who still need to complete part of their internship requirement under supervision, while full registration, at £481, is for doctors who can already evidence a complete, GMC-acceptable internship alongside their other documents. Applying for the wrong category can cost an Indian doctor weeks of otherwise avoidable delay, so it is worth reading our Types of GMC Registration Explained guide before submitting anything, particularly if your internship finished some years ago and you are unsure how the GMC will treat the gap.

Most Indian doctors report a total timeline of roughly twelve to eighteen months from the point they begin serious AKT preparation to the point they hold full GMC registration, though this varies considerably depending on how quickly EPIC/MyIntealth verification completes and whether a resit is needed at either stage. Sitting the CPSA in the UK: visas, travel and what to expect

Because the CPSA takes place in Manchester, Indian candidates need a UK visa purely to attend the assessment, even though this is not yet an employment visa. Most Indian doctors travelling for the CPSA use a Standard Visitor visa, since the trip is for an examination rather than paid work. This is a different visa entirely from the one you will need later, once you have an NHS job offer: at that stage, most Indian doctors move onto the Health and Care Worker visa, a reduced-fee, sponsored route with no Immigration Health Surcharge, specifically designed for NHS-facing roles. Our full UK visa guide for international doctors explains both visa types and when each one applies, so you are not caught applying for the wrong category at the wrong stage.

Because CPSA travel adds a real cost layer beyond the exam fee itself, most advisers suggest budgeting separately for flights, UK accommodation for several days, and, ideally, a short CPSA-focused preparation course taken either online beforehand or in person just before your sitting. Treat this as part of your overall UKMLA budget rather than an afterthought.

UKMLA route vs NEET PG: a genuine decision for Indian graduates

A significant share of Indian medical graduates weigh the UKMLA route against NEET PG, India's own postgraduate entrance examination, because both represent the next major step after MBBS and internship, yet they lead in very different directions. NEET PG opens the door to postgraduate specialty training inside India; the UKMLA route (AKT and CPSA) opens the door to GMC registration and NHS employment in the UK. Neither is inherently "better" than the other: the right choice depends on whether you want to build a career inside India's public and private hospital systems, or whether NHS employment, UK-based specialty training and eventual UK consultant or GP status better fit your goals.

Some Indian doctors sit both, either because they are undecided or because they want to keep both routes open while deciding. If that describes your situation, our dedicated UKMLA vs NEET PG comparison walks through the practical trade-offs, including typical timelines, costs and career outcomes for each path, in far more depth than this section can cover.

It is worth remembering, too, that domestic Indian licensing requirements, such as your MBBS, internship and State Medical Council or NMC registration, exist independently of whichever postgraduate route you eventually choose. In other words, satisfying India's own requirements to practise is not itself the UKMLA route, but rather the foundation that both the UKMLA route and NEET PG are built on top of; the choice between them is genuinely about your postgraduate career, not about which country's basic licensing you must satisfy first.

What about the USMLE and other countries?

A smaller group of Indian doctors also weigh the US route (the USMLE) alongside the UK route described in this guide, since both are common destinations for Indian IMGs. The two exams test different content in different formats, lead to different visa and residency-matching systems, and typically involve different total timelines and costs. Is an Indian MBBS actually recognised for UK practice?

This is one of the most common anxieties among Indian doctors considering the UKMLA route, and the honest answer has two parts. First, an Indian MBBS from a school listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools is generally accepted by the GMC as a valid primary medical qualification, meaning the degree itself is not the obstacle. Second, holding an accepted degree does not by itself entitle you to practise in the UK: you must still pass the AKT and CPSA (or hold a recognised exemption), complete an accepted internship, provide English language evidence and pass EPIC/MyIntealth verification. In other words, the question "is my Indian MBBS valid in the UK" and the question "can I practise in the UK yet" have different answers, and conflating them is a common source of confusion. Our dedicated explainer, Is an Indian MBBS Valid in the UK? UKMLA Recognition and Degree Validity Explained, unpacks this distinction in full.

It is also worth separating this pathway from a different one some Indian families consider: enrolling directly in a UK medical school as an undergraduate, which follows an entirely different admissions and funding process rather than the IMG registration route described in this guide. What happens after you register: NHS jobs, salary and career progression

Once you hold GMC registration with a licence to practise, you are legally able to apply for NHS posts, but registration itself is not a job offer, and Indian doctors sometimes underestimate how much separate work the job search stage requires. Realistically, most Indian IMGs move into their first NHS role either through direct trust-level applications, through NHS-facing recruitment campaigns aimed specifically at overseas doctors, or, in some cases, via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive post over the following years.

Salary expectations matter here too, since many Indian doctors significantly overestimate or underestimate likely UK earnings. Foundation-level and equivalent junior doctor posts sponsored under the Health and Care Worker visa typically start in the region of £32,000 per year, rising considerably as you move into specialty training and, eventually, consultant or GP-partner level roles, though exact figures depend on your grade, specialty, location and whether you are on a training or non-training contract. Foundation Programme, direct NHS posts, or specialty training first?

Not every Indian IMG follows the same entry point into NHS work, and this is a genuine decision rather than a formality. Some Indian doctors, particularly those who register relatively early after completing their internship, pursue routes connected to the Foundation Programme or apply for Foundation-equivalent posts; others move straight into a suitable non-training NHS role, often described as a "trust grade" or "clinical fellow" post, and gain UK clinical experience before applying into formal specialty training later. Because entry requirements, competition ratios and the Multi-Specialty Recruitment Assessment (MSRA) differ across these routes, Indian doctors should decide deliberately rather than defaulting to whichever job appears first. Common mistakes Indian applicants make on this route

Several avoidable mistakes recur often enough among Indian applicants that they are worth naming directly.

  • Leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification until after the AKT. Because this step depends on your medical school responding to a verification request, starting it only once exam preparation is already underway is the single most common cause of an otherwise avoidable delay. Start it as soon as you have decided to pursue the UKMLA route, not once you already hold an AKT pass.
  • Assuming an Indian MBBS being listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools means the whole route is automatic. A listed degree satisfies one eligibility pillar; internship, English language evidence, EPIC/MyIntealth verification and passing the AKT and CPSA are all still required separately.
  • Booking CPSA travel before a confirmed test date. Because the CPSA is sat only in Manchester, booking flights or accommodation speculatively, before your assessment slot is confirmed, risks wasted money if the date changes.
  • Underestimating how much the CPSA differs from Indian clinical exams. Indian candidates with strong clinical knowledge sometimes underperform on CPSA communication stations because UK-specific consent language and structured consultation frameworks are assessed quite specifically, and this benefits from dedicated practice rather than general confidence in one's clinical ability.
  • Treating GMC registration as equivalent to having an NHS job. Registration is the regulatory gateway, not a job offer; budgeting time and effort for the separate job-search stage, including CVs, interviews and visa sponsorship, avoids an unwelcome surprise after registration is granted.
  • Not checking the income-based fee discount. Many Indian doctors pay the full annual retention fee without realising they may qualify for the GMC's 50% income-based discount if their worldwide income sits below the current threshold.

Frequently asked questions

Do Indian medical graduates have to take the UKMLA?

Yes. If you qualified outside the UK, including in India, you must pass the two GMC-recognised assessments that sit under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, commonly called the AKT and the CPSA, before you can register with the GMC and practise medicine in the UK, unless you already hold an exemption such as specialist or GP registration through a recognised route.

What is the UKMLA fee for Indian doctors in 2026?

As of April 2026, the GMC charges £283 for the AKT and £1,036 for the CPSA, plus a further registration application fee of £26 for provisional registration or £481 for full registration. Total exam and registration costs typically fall between roughly £1,345 and £1,800, before English test, travel and visa costs, so always check the current figures on gmc-uk.org before budgeting.

Can Indian doctors sit the AKT in India?

Yes. The AKT is a computer-based test delivered through Pearson VUE test centres, and India has multiple test centre locations, which removes the need to travel to the UK just for the knowledge test. The CPSA, however, is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester, so every candidate must travel to the UK for that stage.

What is EPIC verification and do Indian doctors need it?

EPIC, now run on the MyIntealth platform, is the primary source verification service operated by ECFMG/Intealth that confirms your Indian medical degree is genuine by checking directly with your university. GMC registration cannot proceed without a valid EPIC/MyIntealth verification report, so Indian doctors should start this process early, ideally before booking the AKT.

What English language test do Indian doctors need for the UKMLA route?

Most Indian doctors sit IELTS Academic, needing an overall score of 7.5 with at least 7.0 in each of the four components, or OET Medicine with at least grade B in every section. Some Indian doctors qualify for an exemption if their medical education was conducted and assessed substantially in English.

Is an Indian MBBS accepted by the GMC?

An Indian MBBS is generally accepted as a primary medical qualification because Indian medical schools are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC checks as part of registration. Acceptance of the degree itself is separate from passing the UKMLA route and completing the required internship.

How long does the whole UKMLA route take for an Indian doctor?

Most Indian candidates take roughly twelve to eighteen months from starting AKT preparation to receiving full GMC registration, once English test scores, EPIC verification, AKT, CPSA, visa processing and the internship requirement are all accounted for. Candidates who are well prepared and organised can sometimes move faster, while others take longer if a resit is needed.

Do Indian doctors need a UK visa to sit the CPSA?

Indian nationals normally require a Standard Visitor visa to travel to the UK for the CPSA sitting in Manchester, since it is a short assessment visit rather than employment. A different visa, the Health and Care Worker visa, becomes relevant later once you have a confirmed NHS job offer.

What is the difference between UKMLA and NEET PG for Indian doctors?

NEET PG is India's entrance examination for postgraduate specialty training within India, while the UKMLA route (AKT and CPSA) is the licensing pathway that lets an Indian-qualified doctor register with the GMC and work in the UK. They serve different countries and different purposes, and many Indian doctors weigh both before deciding where to train next.

Can Indian doctors get a discount on GMC fees?

The GMC offers a 50% discount on annual retention fees and on the full registration fee for doctors whose worldwide income falls below the GMC's income discount threshold, which is £38,500 from April 2026. Indian doctors who are not yet earning UK-level income at the time of applying may be eligible, so it is worth checking the current criteria on gmc-uk.org.

What happens after an Indian doctor passes the UKMLA route and registers with the GMC?

Once registered, most Indian IMGs move into NHS employment, either directly into a suitable post or, in some cases, via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive NHS role and eventually applying for specialist or GP registration if they wish to practise independently.

Do experienced Indian consultants also need to sit the UKMLA route?

Not always. Experienced doctors applying through the specialist or GP portfolio pathway, or those who already hold a recognised postgraduate qualification accepted by a UK royal college, may be exempt from sitting the AKT and CPSA, though they must still meet the GMC's other registration requirements.
