Trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions test one of the most practical, high-frequency skill sets a newly qualified doctor needs: recognising a limb-threatening emergency, reading a fracture on an X-ray, and knowing exactly what to do in the first sixty minutes after major trauma arrives in the emergency department. Unlike the inflammatory joint disease tested in rheumatological conditions, this topic is overwhelmingly about mechanical injury — broken bones, torn ligaments, and the vascular and neurological structures that run alongside them. Every foundation doctor will be called to see a patient with a suspected fracture, a swollen limb after trauma, or a confused elderly patient who has fallen, and the Applied Knowledge Test reflects that reality with a heavy weighting toward musculoskeletal injury. This guide covers the ATLS-style primary survey for major trauma, the classic fracture patterns you are expected to recognise on sight, compartment syndrome as a genuine surgical emergency, the principles of open fracture management, hip fracture as one of the single most heavily tested topics in the entire syllabus, and the complications of fracture healing that examiners love to hide in extended-matching questions. Whether you are a UK medical student revising for finals or an IMG building an AKT study plan from scratch, trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision repays focused, structured effort more than almost any other content area.

Trauma and Orthopaedics UKMLA: The Quick-Answer Summary

Trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions assess a candidate's ability to manage major trauma using a structured primary survey (ABCDE), recognise common fracture patterns from history and examination alone, identify compartment syndrome and open fractures as time-critical emergencies, and apply NICE guidance to the management of hip fractures in older adults. The topic sits within the wider Musculoskeletal Health content area of the GMC's MLA content map and overlaps with general surgical presentations only at the margins — this is fracture- and soft-tissue-injury-focused content, not abdominal or perioperative surgery.

For a foundation doctor, the highest-yield facts are these: a suspected neck of femur fracture in an older adult requires urgent analgesia, a full set of bloods and an ECG, and same-day or next-day surgery wherever medically fit; compartment syndrome is a clinical diagnosis that cannot wait for imaging; and any open fracture needs antibiotics, tetanus assessment, and early orthoplastic input to avoid catastrophic infection. Candidates should also be comfortable describing a fracture in radiological terms (site, pattern, displacement, angulation) because image-based single-best-answer questions are common throughout this content area, and the skill of describing a plain film accurately overlaps closely with the site's dedicated imaging interpretation guide. Building this skill early makes the rest of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision considerably faster, since so many questions hinge on correctly reading the film before applying any management algorithm.

The Primary Survey: ATLS Principles in Major Trauma

Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) provides the internationally recognised framework for the first assessment of any trauma patient, and trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions frequently open with a trauma call scenario before narrowing down to a specific limb injury. The primary survey follows the ABCDE sequence, and every step is designed to identify and treat immediately life-threatening problems before moving on to the next letter. This sequence matters just as much for orthopaedic trauma as it does for major polytrauma, since a dramatic-looking limb deformity can distract from a more immediately dangerous airway or breathing problem — a recurring examiner trick in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA scenarios.

Airway with Cervical Spine Control

Airway assessment always comes first, and in trauma it is inseparable from cervical spine protection. Any patient with a mechanism of injury capable of causing spinal injury — a fall from height, a road traffic collision, a diving accident, or a blow to the head or neck — should have manual in-line stabilisation applied immediately, followed by a rigid collar, blocks, and tape once the airway is secured. Look, listen, and feel for airway patency: can the patient talk in full sentences, is there stridor or gurgling, is there facial or airway trauma that might obstruct the airway as swelling progresses? A jaw thrust (not a head tilt, which risks worsening a cervical spine injury) is the manoeuvre of choice if the airway needs opening manually. Definitive airway management (rapid sequence induction and intubation) is required if the patient cannot maintain their own airway, has a Glasgow Coma Scale of 8 or less, or has evidence of expanding airway compromise such as an expanding neck haematoma.

Breathing and Ventilation

Once the airway is secured, expose the chest fully and assess breathing. Look for asymmetrical chest movement, use of accessory muscles, and visible wounds; feel for surgical emphysema, tracheal deviation, and chest wall tenderness or crepitus suggesting rib fractures; listen for equal air entry bilaterally. Immediately life-threatening chest injuries that must be identified and treated in the primary survey include tension pneumothorax (needle or finger thoracostomy followed by a chest drain), open pneumothorax (three-sided occlusive dressing), massive haemothorax, flail chest, and cardiac tamponade. High-flow oxygen is given to all major trauma patients at this stage regardless of saturation readings, since pulse oximetry can be unreliable in shock.

Circulation and Haemorrhage Control

Circulation assessment in trauma means controlling external haemorrhage first, then assessing for internal bleeding. Direct pressure is the first-line measure for external bleeding from a limb wound; a tourniquet is applied proximally without delay if direct pressure fails to control life-threatening limb haemorrhage, since prehospital and military data consistently show tourniquets save lives when used correctly. A pelvic binder is applied at the level of the greater trochanters for any suspected pelvic fracture with haemodynamic instability, since pelvic fractures can cause massive concealed haemorrhage into the retroperitoneal space. Two large-bore cannulae are sited, bloods sent for group and save/crossmatch, venous blood gas, and clotting, and balanced, ratio-based blood product resuscitation (packed red cells, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets, typically 1:1:1) is favoured over large volumes of crystalloid in haemorrhagic shock, since crystalloid dilutes clotting factors and worsens coagulopathy. Tranexamic acid, given within three hours of injury, reduces mortality from traumatic haemorrhage and should not be delayed while awaiting definitive imaging. Interpreting drug interactions and perioperative medication changes for a trauma patient who also needs urgent surgery draws directly on the site's separate perioperative medicine revision guide, which is worth reading alongside this one.

Disability and Exposure

Disability is assessed using the Glasgow Coma Scale, pupillary responses, and a rapid blood glucose to exclude hypoglycaemia as a cause of altered consciousness. Exposure means fully undressing the patient to identify all injuries, including on the back (log-roll technique, protecting the spine throughout), while actively preventing hypothermia with warmed blankets and fluids — hypothermia worsens the "lethal triad" of trauma (hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy) that drives ongoing bleeding and death. Once the primary survey is complete and immediately life-threatening problems addressed, a secondary survey — a systematic head-to-toe examination — identifies the full range of injuries, including the fractures and dislocations that form the bulk of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA content. It is worth noting that the general approach to the acutely unwell surgical patient, including the WHO checklist and drug interactions around theatre, is covered in more depth in the site's surgical presentations revision guide, while this article focuses specifically on the musculoskeletal injuries themselves.

Common Fracture Patterns and Their Classic Presentations

Recognising a fracture from its mechanism, examination findings, and typical patient demographic is one of the most reliably tested skills in this content area. Examiners often describe a mechanism of injury and expect the candidate to predict the fracture pattern, or vice versa. The table below summarises the fracture patterns most frequently featured in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions, but a narrative walk-through of each is equally important for the exam. Pattern recognition of this kind is exactly the sort of high-yield, repeatable content worth building into a structured UKMLA preparation plan well before exam day.

Hip Fracture (Neck of Femur)

Hip fracture in the elderly is arguably the single most important topic in the whole of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision, both because of its sheer clinical frequency in UK hospitals and because of how tightly NICE guidance defines its management (see the dedicated section below). The classic presentation is an older patient, often with osteoporosis, who has had a low-energy fall (commonly a simple trip or a fall from standing height) and now cannot weight-bear. On examination, the affected leg is characteristically shortened and externally rotated, with pain on any attempted hip movement. Intracapsular fractures (subcapital or transcervical, within the joint capsule) carry a real risk of disrupting the retrograde blood supply to the femoral head via the medial circumflex femoral artery, predisposing to avascular necrosis — this is why displaced intracapsular fractures in fit older adults are usually treated with arthroplasty (hemiarthroplasty or total hip replacement) rather than fixation. Extracapsular fractures (intertrochanteric or subtrochanteric, outside the capsule) do not threaten the femoral head's blood supply in the same way and are typically fixed with a dynamic hip screw or an intramedullary nail.

Wrist Fracture (Colles' and Smith's)

Wrist injuries are among the most commonly examined presentations in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA papers, precisely because they are so common in real emergency departments. A Colles' fracture is a fracture of the distal radius with dorsal displacement and dorsal angulation of the distal fragment, classically following a fall onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH). It produces the characteristic "dinner fork deformity" on lateral examination of the wrist and is extremely common in postmenopausal women with osteoporosis, though it also occurs in younger patients after high-energy trauma such as sporting falls. A Smith's fracture is the reverse injury — volar (palmar) displacement and angulation of the distal fragment, usually caused by a fall onto a flexed wrist or a direct blow to the dorsum of the wrist — and produces a "garden spade deformity." Both are usually managed with closed reduction and cast immobilisation if undisplaced or successfully reduced, with surgical fixation (open reduction and internal fixation) reserved for intra-articular involvement, significant comminution, or failure to maintain reduction in a cast.

Scaphoid Fracture

Scaphoid fractures deserve specific mention alongside wrist injuries because they are commonly missed on initial X-ray and are a classic examiner trap. The mechanism is again a fall onto an outstretched hand, and the key examination finding is tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox, along with pain on axial loading of the thumb. Because scaphoid fractures are frequently occult on the initial plain film, any patient with anatomical snuffbox tenderness after a FOOSH injury should be treated as having a scaphoid fracture (immobilised in a scaphoid cast or splint) even with a normal initial X-ray, with repeat imaging or MRI at 10–14 days if symptoms persist. This precaution exists because the scaphoid's blood supply enters distally and runs retrograde, meaning proximal pole fractures carry a significant risk of avascular necrosis and non-union if missed or inadequately immobilised.

Ankle Fractures

Ankle injuries form another consistently tested slice of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA content. Ankle fractures typically follow a twisting injury to the ankle and are classified anatomically by which malleolus (or malleoli) is fractured — unimalleolar, bimalleolar, or trimalleolar (medial, lateral, and posterior malleolus all involved), with trimalleolar fractures generally considered unstable and requiring surgical fixation. The Weber classification further describes fibular fractures relative to the level of the syndesmosis (the fibrous joint between tibia and fibula): Weber A (below the syndesmosis, usually stable), Weber B (at the level of the syndesmosis, variable stability), and Weber C (above the syndesmosis, usually unstable and associated with syndesmotic disruption). The Ottawa Ankle Rules are the key decision tool taught for whether an ankle or foot X-ray is indicated at all: an X-ray is required if there is bone tenderness at the tip or posterior edge of either malleolus, bone tenderness at the base of the fifth metatarsal or the navicular, or an inability to weight-bear for four steps both immediately after the injury and in the emergency department. This rule reduces unnecessary radiation exposure while maintaining very high sensitivity for clinically significant fractures.

Long Bone Fractures (Femoral Shaft, Tibial Shaft, Humeral Shaft)

Long bone fractures of the femoral or tibial shaft are usually the result of high-energy trauma in younger patients (road traffic collisions, falls from height) and are important because of the volume of blood that can be lost into the thigh (up to 1.5–2 litres from a closed femoral shaft fracture alone) and the associated risk of fat embolism syndrome. Immediate management includes analgesia, splinting (traction splints for femoral shaft fractures reduce pain, bleeding, and further soft-tissue damage), and neurovascular assessment distal to the injury before and after any manipulation. Humeral shaft fractures carry a specific association with radial nerve injury, since the radial nerve runs directly against the bone in the spiral groove — a wrist drop and sensory loss over the dorsal first web space after a humeral shaft fracture should always prompt assessment for radial nerve palsy, which is often neurapraxic and recovers spontaneously over weeks to months but must be documented and monitored.

Paediatric Fracture Patterns

Paediatric injuries add a further dimension to trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions, since the same mechanism can produce a very different fracture pattern in a child than in an adult. Children's bones behave differently from adult bone because of the presence of the growth plate (physis) and a thicker, more flexible periosteum. Greenstick fractures (an incomplete fracture where one side of the bone bends and the other cracks) and buckle (torus) fractures (a compression injury causing the cortex to bulge outward without a clear fracture line) are unique to the paediatric skeleton and generally heal quickly with simple immobilisation. Growth plate injuries are classified using the Salter-Harris system, which matters clinically because fractures through the growth plate itself carry a risk of growth arrest or angular deformity as the child grows — candidates should recognise that any paediatric fracture involving the physis warrants careful follow-up, and any unexplained fracture pattern inconsistent with the reported mechanism should always prompt consideration of non-accidental injury per UK safeguarding guidance.

Compartment Syndrome: A Limb-Threatening Emergency

Compartment syndrome is defined as raised pressure within a closed fascial compartment that compromises the perfusion of the muscles and nerves contained within it, and it is one of the true limb-threatening emergencies covered in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision. It most commonly follows tibial shaft fractures and forearm fractures, but can also occur after crush injuries, reperfusion following vascular repair, tight casts or dressings, and prolonged limb compression (for example after a period lying on a hard floor following a fall or collapse).

Recognising Compartment Syndrome Clinically

Compartment syndrome is a clinical diagnosis, and the single most important teaching point for the exam is that pain out of proportion to the injury, and pain on passive stretching of the muscles within the affected compartment, are the earliest and most sensitive signs — both should be actively sought in any patient with a high-risk fracture, and neither should be dismissed as "just needing more analgesia." The traditional "5 Ps" (pain, paraesthesia, pallor, pulselessness, paralysis) are unreliable as an early screening tool because pulselessness and paralysis are late findings that indicate the process is already well advanced and irreversible damage may already be occurring; waiting for an absent distal pulse before acting is a significant and recurring error. Increasing analgesia requirement out of proportion to the apparent injury, a tense and swollen compartment on palpation, and pain with passive movement of the digits are the findings that should prompt urgent escalation. Where the clinical picture is equivocal, intracompartmental pressure monitoring can be used, with a difference between diastolic blood pressure and measured compartment pressure of less than 30 mmHg (or an absolute compartment pressure above roughly 30–40 mmHg, depending on local protocol) supporting the diagnosis.

Managing Compartment Syndrome

Once compartment syndrome is suspected, all circumferential dressings, bandages, and casts must be removed or split immediately, and the limb should be kept at the level of the heart rather than elevated (elevation above heart level can actually reduce arterial inflow pressure and worsen perfusion, which is a commonly tested point of confusion). If symptoms and signs do not resolve promptly after removing external constricting factors, emergency fasciotomy — surgical division of the fascia to decompress the affected compartments — is required, and this should not be delayed pending imaging, since muscle necrosis begins within around four to six hours of ischaemia and the window for salvaging viable tissue is narrow. Delayed or missed compartment syndrome can progress to irreversible muscle necrosis, Volkmann's ischaemic contracture (a permanent flexion deformity most classically described in the forearm and hand after missed forearm compartment syndrome), limb loss, and rhabdomyolysis with associated acute kidney injury and hyperkalaemia. Because the consequences of a delayed diagnosis are so severe, compartment syndrome is one of the highest-stakes single topics in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision, and candidates should be able to state confidently that it is diagnosed clinically, not by waiting for investigations.

Open Fracture Management Principles

Open fracture management is one of the most protocol-driven areas of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision, since the sequence of actions matters as much as the actions themselves. An open fracture is one in which the fracture site communicates with the external environment through a break in the overlying skin, and it represents a substantially higher-risk injury than an equivalent closed fracture because of the risk of contamination, infection, and soft-tissue compromise. Open fractures are frequently classified using the Gustilo-Anderson system, which grades severity from Type I (a wound under 1 cm, minimal contamination and soft-tissue damage) through Type II (a wound over 1 cm without extensive soft-tissue damage) to Type III (extensive soft-tissue damage, often with vascular injury or gross contamination, further subdivided into IIIA, IIIB, and IIIC based on the degree of periosteal stripping and whether vascular repair is needed).

Immediate Management Priorities

The immediate priorities in an open fracture follow a consistent sequence: control catastrophic haemorrhage first, assess and document the neurovascular status of the limb before any manipulation, cover the wound with a saline-soaked or antiseptic-soaked dressing (repeated probing or washing of the wound in the emergency department is discouraged, since this can push contamination deeper and should be reserved for the operating theatre), and realign grossly deformed limbs to restore perfusion and reduce soft-tissue tension, splinting afterward. Photographic documentation of the wound at first assessment reduces the need for repeated dressing removal and re-exposure of the wound to the environment. Intravenous antibiotics should be given as early as possible, ideally within an hour of injury, following local trust protocols (commonly co-amoxiclav or cefuroxime, with additional gram-negative and anaerobic cover for heavily contaminated wounds), since early antibiotic administration is one of the most consistent predictors of reduced infection rates in the trauma literature. Tetanus immunisation status must be checked and a booster or immunoglobulin given according to the wound's tetanus-prone classification and the patient's vaccination history.

Definitive Management and the Orthoplastic Approach

Definitive management of open fractures increasingly follows a combined orthoplastic approach, where orthopaedic surgeons and plastic surgeons plan debridement, skeletal stabilisation, and soft-tissue coverage together rather than sequentially, since outcomes are significantly better when both specialties are involved from the outset for higher-grade injuries. Surgical debridement of all non-viable tissue is performed as soon as safely possible — traditionally within six hours of injury for highly contaminated wounds, though the more recent evidence base supports a slightly more flexible window provided the patient is adequately resuscitated and antibiotics have already been given. Wounds are often left open initially (with negative-pressure wound therapy commonly used as a temporising measure) and closed definitively at a planned second procedure once the soft-tissue envelope is judged safe, rather than closed under tension at the first operation, which significantly increases infection risk. The British Orthopaedic Association and the British Association of Plastic, Reconstructive and Aesthetic Surgeons jointly publish standards for the management of open fractures, and these standards underpin most UK major trauma centre protocols.

Hip Fracture: A High-Yield NICE-Guided Topic

No discussion of trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA revision would be complete without a dedicated look at hip fracture, since it is tested more consistently than almost any other single condition in this content area. Hip fracture management deserves its own dedicated section because it combines extremely high clinical prevalence in the NHS — well over 70,000 hip fractures occur in the UK each year, overwhelmingly in older adults with osteoporosis — with a detailed, frequently updated NICE guideline that examiners draw on directly. NICE guideline CG124, "Hip fracture: management," sets out the evidence-based standard of care from admission through to rehabilitation, and familiarity with its key recommendations is one of the most efficient ways to prepare for this part of the syllabus.

Assessment and Preoperative Care

NICE recommends that patients with a suspected hip fracture receive prompt, effective analgesia, with regular paracetamol as the mainstay supplemented by opioids as required, and nerve blocks (such as a fascia iliaca block) considered to reduce opioid requirements and delirium risk. A full set of baseline investigations is expected on admission, including full blood count, urea and electrolytes, group and save, clotting, and an electrocardiogram, alongside a chest X-ray if clinically indicated. NICE guidance is explicit that surgery should take place on the day of, or the day after, admission wherever possible, since delays to surgery are consistently associated with worse outcomes, including higher rates of pressure sores, pneumonia, delirium, and mortality. Preoperative optimisation should not be used as a reason for prolonged delay unless a specific, reversible medical issue genuinely needs correcting first (for example a critically abnormal electrolyte or a rate-uncontrolled arrhythmia); "waiting for a normal ECG" or similar low-yield delays are explicitly discouraged.

Surgical Management Choices

The choice of operation depends primarily on whether the fracture is intracapsular or extracapsular and, for intracapsular fractures, on the patient's baseline mobility and cognitive status. Displaced intracapsular fractures in patients who were mobile outdoors independently, without cognitive impairment, are generally offered a total hip replacement, since NICE evidence shows better functional outcomes and lower reoperation rates than hemiarthroplasty in this fitter group. Displaced intracapsular fractures in less fit or cognitively impaired patients are more often managed with hemiarthroplasty, which is a technically simpler and shorter procedure. Undisplaced intracapsular fractures can often be managed with internal fixation (screws), preserving the femoral head, since the blood supply has not been significantly disrupted. Extracapsular fractures (intertrochanteric and subtrochanteric) are managed with fixation devices — a sliding (dynamic) hip screw for most intertrochanteric fractures, and an intramedullary nail for subtrochanteric or reverse oblique fractures, where the biomechanics favour a load-sharing intramedullary construct.

Postoperative Care and the Multidisciplinary Approach

NICE recommends a formal multidisciplinary approach to hip fracture care from admission, combining orthopaedic surgery with orthogeriatric medical input, since patients with hip fractures are typically frail, often have multiple comorbidities, and are at high risk of delirium, pressure injury, and further falls. Early mobilisation, ideally on the day after surgery, is a core recommendation, alongside falls and bone health assessment before discharge to reduce the risk of a further fragility fracture — this means assessing for osteoporosis and considering bone-protection treatment (such as a bisphosphonate) as part of the same admission, not as a separate, easily missed outpatient task. A comprehensive geriatric assessment addressing cognition, continence, nutrition, and discharge planning is central to reducing the substantial one-year mortality associated with hip fracture, which remains around 20–30% in UK cohorts despite improvements in care pathways. The National Hip Fracture Database, run in partnership with the British Orthopaedic Association, audits hospital performance against these standards and is itself a useful reference point for understanding how UK hip fracture care is measured and benchmarked nationally.

Fracture Healing and Its Complications

Complications of fracture healing are a favourite source of difficult, discriminating single-best-answer questions in trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA papers, since they require candidates to link the biology of bone repair to a specific clinical picture. Fracture healing progresses through four overlapping biological phases: haematoma formation (within hours of injury, as bleeding from the fracture ends and surrounding soft tissue forms a stabilising clot), inflammation (recruitment of inflammatory cells and growth factors over the first few days), callus formation (soft cartilaginous callus over one to two weeks, progressively replaced by harder woven bone), and remodelling (over months to years, as woven bone is remodelled into mature lamellar bone along the lines of mechanical stress). Understanding this timeline helps explain why immobilisation periods vary by fracture site and patient factors, and why certain complications arise at predictable stages.

Non-Union and Delayed Union

Delayed union describes fracture healing that is progressing more slowly than expected for that particular bone and patient, while non-union describes a fracture that has definitively failed to heal, usually defined as no radiographic progression over a period of several months (commonly six to nine months, depending on the bone). Risk factors for non-union include smoking (one of the strongest modifiable risk factors, through impaired vascularity and oxygen delivery to the healing site), inadequate immobilisation or excessive movement at the fracture site, infection, poor blood supply to the fracture site (as seen classically in scaphoid and talus fractures), significant soft-tissue injury, and patient factors such as diabetes, malnutrition, and long-term steroid or NSAID use. Non-union is classified radiologically as hypertrophic (excessive callus forms but fails to bridge the fracture, usually reflecting a biomechanically unstable fracture with an adequate blood supply — treated by improving mechanical stability) or atrophic (little to no callus forms, usually reflecting a biological problem such as poor blood supply or infection — often requiring bone grafting alongside improved fixation).

Avascular Necrosis

Avascular necrosis (AVN) is death of bone tissue due to disruption of its blood supply, and it is a classic, specifically testable complication of fractures at sites with a precarious, largely retrograde blood supply — the femoral head after intracapsular neck of femur fracture, the proximal pole of the scaphoid after scaphoid fracture, and the talus after talar neck fracture are the three sites examiners return to repeatedly. Clinically, AVN presents with pain that may develop months to years after the initial injury, sometimes after a period of apparent healing, and plain X-rays can be normal in the early stages — MRI is the most sensitive early imaging investigation, while later disease shows characteristic subchondral collapse and eventual secondary osteoarthritis on plain film. Management ranges from conservative measures and joint-preserving surgery in early disease to joint replacement once significant collapse or secondary arthritis has developed.

Fat Embolism Syndrome

Fat embolism syndrome is a systemic complication most classically associated with long bone fractures (particularly the femur) and pelvic fractures, especially after high-energy trauma or intramedullary nailing, where fat globules from the bone marrow enter the venous circulation and embolise to the lungs, brain, and skin. It classically presents within 24 to 72 hours of injury with a triad of respiratory distress (hypoxia, tachypnoea, and a chest X-ray that may show diffuse bilateral infiltrates resembling acute respiratory distress syndrome), neurological changes (confusion, reduced consciousness, or focal deficits, which can occur even without a direct head injury), and a petechial rash, classically distributed over the chest, axillae, and conjunctivae. There is no specific reversal treatment for fat embolism syndrome; management is supportive, including oxygen therapy or ventilatory support as needed, careful fluid management, and early fracture stabilisation, which itself appears to reduce the risk of the syndrome developing in the first place. Recognising this triad promptly matters because the differential in a trauma patient with new confusion and breathlessness a day or two after a long bone fracture is often initially — and wrongly — assumed to be a pulmonary embolism or a head injury rather than fat embolism syndrome.

Other Recognised Complications

Beyond non-union, avascular necrosis, and fat embolism syndrome, candidates should be aware of a handful of other fracture complications that recur in questions: malunion (healing in an unsatisfactory anatomical position, causing deformity or functional impairment), infection (particularly relevant after open fractures or surgical fixation, and a major driver of the orthoplastic management approach described above), complex regional pain syndrome (a poorly understood chronic pain condition that can follow even relatively minor limb trauma, characterised by disproportionate pain, swelling, and autonomic changes), and joint stiffness or contracture following prolonged immobilisation, which is one of the reasons early, safe mobilisation is emphasised so strongly in both general trauma and specifically in hip fracture care.

Frequently Asked Questions About Trauma and Orthopaedics in the UKMLA

What is the correct first step in managing a patient with a suspected compartment syndrome?

The first step is to remove any circumferential dressings, bandages, or casts immediately and reassess the limb, keeping it at heart level rather than elevated. Compartment syndrome is diagnosed clinically on the basis of pain out of proportion to the injury and pain on passive stretch of the affected muscles — it should never be excluded simply because distal pulses are still present, since pulselessness is a late and unreliable sign. If symptoms persist after removing external constricting factors, urgent surgical review for fasciotomy is required without waiting for imaging.

Why do elderly patients with a hip fracture classically present with a shortened and externally rotated leg?

The shortening and external rotation result from the unopposed pull of the strong muscles attaching around the hip once the bony continuity of the femoral neck is disrupted, particularly the iliopsoas and the short external rotators. This combination, alongside an inability to straight-leg raise or weight-bear after a fall, is the classic clinical picture that should prompt urgent hip X-rays in any older adult presenting after a fall, even when the history seems relatively minor. NICE's CG124 guideline recommends prompt analgesia and investigation as soon as this presentation is recognised.

How soon should surgery happen after a hip fracture diagnosis according to NICE guidance?

NICE recommends that surgery for a hip fracture should take place on the day of admission or the day after, since delays beyond this point are associated with higher rates of complications including pressure sores, delirium, pneumonia, and death. Preoperative optimisation of reversible medical problems is appropriate, but it should not be used as a routine justification for delaying surgery beyond this window. Early, well-planned surgery combined with orthogeriatric input and early mobilisation forms the backbone of NICE's recommended hip fracture pathway.

What is the difference between a hypertrophic and an atrophic fracture non-union?

A hypertrophic non-union forms abundant callus at the fracture site, but the callus fails to bridge the gap because the fracture remains mechanically unstable, even though the local blood supply is adequate — treatment focuses on improving stability, usually with revised fixation. An atrophic non-union shows little or no callus formation at all, usually reflecting a biological problem such as poor blood supply, infection, or a large bone gap, and typically requires bone grafting in addition to improved mechanical fixation. Distinguishing the two on X-ray is a classic higher-difficulty question in this content area.

What are the earliest warning signs of fat embolism syndrome after a long bone fracture?

The earliest and most useful clinical clue is new respiratory distress or hypoxia developing within 24 to 72 hours of a long bone or pelvic fracture, often accompanied by confusion or other neurological changes and a petechial rash over the chest, axillae, or conjunctivae. Because this triad can mimic other post-trauma complications such as pulmonary embolism or a primary head injury, it is frequently used in single-best-answer questions to test whether candidates recognise fat embolism syndrome as a distinct diagnosis with its own supportive-only management approach. Early stabilisation of the fracture is thought to reduce the risk of the syndrome developing.

Why is anatomical snuffbox tenderness treated so seriously after a fall onto an outstretched hand?

Anatomical snuffbox tenderness after a FOOSH injury raises strong suspicion of a scaphoid fracture, which is notoriously difficult to see on an initial plain X-ray because the fracture line can be extremely subtle or entirely absent for the first one to two weeks. Because the scaphoid's blood supply enters at its distal pole and runs backward (retrograde) through the bone, missing a fracture and failing to immobilise the wrist can lead to non-union or avascular necrosis of the proximal pole. For this reason, the standard of care is to immobilise the wrist and arrange repeat imaging at 10–14 days even when the first X-ray looks normal.

Do trauma and orthopaedics questions overlap with general surgical presentations in the UKMLA?

There is some overlap in the initial ABCDE trauma approach and in general principles such as consent and perioperative drug management, both of which are shared foundations across surgical specialties. However, trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA content is distinctly focused on fractures, dislocations, and soft-tissue musculoskeletal injury, whereas the separate surgical presentations content area concentrates on acute abdominal emergencies, hernias, and postoperative complications. Candidates should revise both areas, since together they cover the bulk of acute care a foundation doctor is expected to manage, and doing so also broadens the base of scored questions reflected in how UKMLA results and scoring work overall.

Conclusion

Trauma and orthopaedics UKMLA questions reward candidates who can apply a structured ABCDE approach under pressure, recognise classic fracture patterns from mechanism and examination alone, and treat compartment syndrome and open fractures with the urgency they deserve. Hip fracture management, underpinned by NICE guideline CG124 and audited nationally through the National Hip Fracture Database, remains one of the highest-yield single topics in the entire content area given how common it is in day-to-day NHS practice. Building confidence in fracture-healing complications — non-union, avascular necrosis, and fat embolism syndrome — rounds out a revision plan that will serve candidates well whether they are UK medical students preparing for finals or international medical graduates approaching the AKT for the first time.
