UKMLA for Gulf and Saudi doctors is one of the most frequently searched but least clearly explained international pathways to UK medical registration, largely because the Gulf itself is not one regulatory system but several. Doctors currently licensed in Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, or elsewhere across the GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council) often originally trained in India, Pakistan, Egypt, Sudan, or another country entirely, and later moved to the Gulf on a work visa and a locally issued medical licence. That layered history — an original medical degree, a period of postgraduate work, and a current Gulf licence — raises a question this guide answers directly: which of those credentials actually matters to the GMC?

The short answer is that UKMLA for Gulf and Saudi doctors hinges almost entirely on the doctor's original primary medical qualification — where the medical degree itself was awarded — rather than on the country where the doctor currently holds a licence to practise. A Saudi Commission for Health Specialties (SCFHS) classification, a Dubai Health Authority (DHA) licence, or a Ministry of Health and Prevention (MOHAP) registration confirms that a doctor is entitled to practise within that specific Gulf jurisdiction; none of these Gulf licences transfers, converts, or substitutes for GMC registration. However, a current Gulf licence is far from irrelevant — it is often valuable supporting evidence of ongoing clinical practice, continuing fitness to practise, and up-to-date clinical skills, all of which strengthen an application once the more fundamental eligibility question is settled.

This guide walks through the entire pathway as it applies to doctors currently practising anywhere in the Gulf region, covering how GMC eligibility is actually assessed, how primary source verification works regardless of your current country of practice, where Gulf-based candidates can sit the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), the mandatory in-person Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) in the UK, and a full fee breakdown converted into both Saudi Riyal (SAR) and UAE Dirham (AED) so candidates across the region can budget realistically. Because GCC medical licensing is genuinely fragmented — Saudi Arabia, the UAE's individual emirates, and other Gulf states each run separate systems — this guide hedges deliberately wherever local detail varies, and points to the correct official body to confirm current requirements.

The UKMLA Route From the Gulf: A Quick Overview

Before examining each stage in detail, it helps to see the whole pathway at a glance. Because so many Gulf-based doctors originally qualified elsewhere, the practical route generally runs as follows:

  1. Identify your original primary medical qualification — the country and medical school where you actually earned your basic medical degree, since this, not your current Gulf licence, is what the GMC assesses for eligibility.
  2. Confirm that qualification is GMC-eligible, typically by checking that your medical school appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year and that you hold full, unrestricted registration with your home country's medical regulator.
  3. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your original medical qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) — a process that is universal and does not vary by your current country of residence.
  4. Sit and pass an approved English language test — IELTS Academic or OET Medicine — unless an accepted exemption applies.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT, sat at a Pearson VUE test centre, with sitting locations available in several Gulf cities.
  6. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA, which can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.
  7. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise, using your current Gulf employment and licensure history as supporting evidence of ongoing clinical competence where relevant.
  8. Apply for NHS posts — commonly trust-grade or clinical fellow roles — and begin the process of relocating from the Gulf to the UK.

Each stage carries its own processing time, cost, and common pitfalls, covered in detail below. Understanding the full sequence early prevents the single most common misunderstanding among Gulf-based applicants: the assumption that a current SCFHS classification or DHA licence somehow shortcuts GMC recognition. It does not. The GMC's assessment always traces back to the primary medical qualification, and a doctor who has spent a decade practising competently in Riyadh or Dubai still needs to verify their original degree exactly as a first-time applicant fresh out of medical school would. Consequently, treating credential verification of the original degree as a separate, parallel project — rather than something a current Gulf licence can bypass — is the most important planning decision a Gulf-based candidate can make.

Gulf Medical Licensing and UKMLA Eligibility: What Actually Counts

The single most important concept for Gulf-based doctors to understand is the distinction between current licensure and original qualification recognition. GCC licensing bodies — the Saudi Commission for Health Specialties (SCFHS) in Saudi Arabia, and in the UAE the Ministry of Health and Prevention (MOHAP), the Dubai Health Authority (DHA), and the Department of Health Abu Dhabi (DOH), each licensing separately depending on the emirate — assess whether a doctor is fit to practise within their specific jurisdiction, often based on the doctor's original degree, postgraduate qualifications, and years of clinical experience combined. The GMC's assessment process is entirely separate and does not accept a Gulf classification, licence number, or professional grading as a substitute for its own eligibility checks.

In practice, this means a Gulf-based doctor's GMC eligibility is determined by three things: whether their original medical school appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant graduation year, whether their original primary medical qualification meets the GMC's minimum duration and curriculum standards, and whether they hold full, unrestricted registration with the medical regulator of the country where they originally qualified — not necessarily the Gulf country where they currently work. A doctor who originally trained in India, for example, would need to confirm eligibility against the same criteria as any other Indian-trained doctor; a doctor who originally trained in Egypt would follow the criteria that apply to Egyptian-trained doctors. The Gulf licence sits alongside this as evidence of subsequent practice, not as the qualification being assessed.

Because GCC licensing is genuinely fragmented by country and, within the UAE, by emirate, candidates should not assume that requirements for an SCFHS classification map neatly onto DHA or DOH requirements, or vice versa. Saudi Arabia's SCFHS operates a nationwide system with defined professional classifications (General Practitioner, Specialist, Consultant) tied to years of experience and postgraduate qualifications, whereas the UAE licenses separately through MOHAP for federal facilities, DHA for Dubai, and DOH for Abu Dhabi, each with its own documentation and examination requirements. This guide describes the general shape of each system where it is relevant to a doctor's UK plans, but Gulf-based candidates should always confirm current, jurisdiction-specific requirements directly with the relevant licensing body rather than relying on a general summary, since local rules are updated periodically and vary meaningfully between Gulf states.

For the broader eligibility framework that applies across every international medical graduate, regardless of current country of practice, see the site's guide to UKMLA eligibility requirements. It sets out the baseline criteria the GMC applies before any country-specific detail comes into play.

Primary Source Verification via EPIC Is Universal

Once basic eligibility against the original qualification is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of that qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials). This is worth stating plainly: the EPIC process is identical regardless of where a doctor currently practises. A doctor working in Riyadh, Dubai, Doha, or Kuwait City follows exactly the same EPIC verification route as a doctor applying directly from India or Pakistan, because EPIC verifies the original degree-granting institution, not the doctor's current country of residence or licensure. The practical implication is that a Gulf-based doctor should approach their home country's medical school and regulator for verification, not the Gulf authority that currently licenses them.

Response times vary considerably by medical school and home regulator rather than by the doctor's current Gulf location. Well-established medical schools with dedicated international relations offices tend to respond within four to eight weeks, whereas others can take three months or longer, particularly during academic holidays. Full detail on how primary source verification works, what documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting evidence is available directly from the regulator: see the GMC's primary source verification requirements for international medical graduates. Gulf-based candidates are strongly advised to read this guidance directly rather than assuming their Gulf licensing authority's own verification standards are equivalent, since the two processes are not interchangeable.

How a Current Gulf Licence Can Still Help

Although a Gulf licence does not itself transfer to GMC registration, it is far from useless in an application. A current SCFHS classification, DHA licence, or MOHAP registration demonstrates ongoing, verifiable clinical practice, which can be genuinely useful supporting evidence of recent experience and continuing fitness to practise, particularly for doctors whose original medical degree dates back many years. Employment references from Gulf hospitals, evidence of continuing professional development logged with the relevant Gulf authority, and specialist or consultant classifications awarded by SCFHS can all strengthen the broader picture of a candidate's clinical standing, even though none of them substitutes for EPIC verification of the original degree itself. Candidates should therefore keep detailed, well-organised records of their Gulf employment and licensing history throughout their time in the region, since this evidence becomes far harder to reconstruct once a doctor has already relocated to the UK.

Documents Gulf-Based Candidates Should Gather Early

Because Gulf-based doctors are often juggling documentation from two or three countries — their original training country, their current Gulf jurisdiction, and the UK application itself — gathering documents in parallel rather than sequentially saves considerable time:

  • Original primary medical degree certificate and full academic transcripts from the medical school where you originally qualified, not from any Gulf institution.
  • Certificate of Good Standing from the medical regulator of your original training country, confirming your registration status and disciplinary record.
  • Current Gulf licence documentation — SCFHS classification certificate, DHA/MOHAP/DOH licence, or equivalent — as supporting evidence of ongoing practice, even though it is not the primary eligibility document.
  • Employment verification letters from Gulf hospitals or clinics, confirming dates, grade, and scope of practice.
  • Valid international passport, ideally covering the full expected duration of the application and any subsequent UK visa process.
  • Evidence of any name variations across documents issued in different countries, which is common for candidates who have worked across multiple jurisdictions with different transliteration conventions for names.
  • Certified translations of any document not already issued in English, from a recognised professional translation service — relevant for candidates whose original degree documents or Gulf licensing paperwork are in Arabic or another language.

Requesting the Certificate of Good Standing from your original training country's regulator early is particularly important, since many Gulf-based doctors have not needed to contact that regulator for years and processing requests from overseas can take longer than expected.

How to Apply for the UKMLA From the Gulf: Step by Step

With eligibility confirmed against the original qualification, the practical application process for a Gulf-based doctor follows six sequential steps.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

Registration begins with creating a GMC Online account through the GMC's official website. This account becomes the central hub for the entire process — tracking EPIC verification status, booking both the AKT and CPSA, submitting English language evidence, and eventually completing the registration application. Gulf-based candidates should use a personal email address they will retain long after leaving their current Gulf employer, since GMC Online correspondence can span well over a year, and hospital-issued email addresses in the Gulf are frequently deactivated the moment employment ends.

Step 2: Begin EPIC Verification of Your Original Degree Early

As covered above, EPIC verification of the original medical qualification is usually the single longest step in the entire pathway, and it should begin as early as possible — ideally before English test dates or exam bookings are finalised. Because this process runs through your original training country's medical school rather than through any Gulf authority, it does not require your current Gulf employer's involvement at all. Keep a written record of every communication with your original medical school's registrar, since this becomes useful evidence if a delayed response needs escalating later.

Step 3: Gather and Submit English Language Evidence

In parallel with EPIC verification, sit an approved English language test and upload the certificate to GMC Online once results are issued. This requirement is universal and does not vary based on current country of practice — a doctor practising in Dubai under a DHA licence follows exactly the same English testing rules as a doctor applying directly from their original training country. Full detail is covered in the dedicated section below.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once the GMC Online account is active, candidates can book the AKT through the linked Pearson VUE booking system. Gulf-based candidates benefit from strong regional test centre availability, discussed in the next section, though booking windows can fill quickly during popular sitting periods. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines exactly what conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas are examinable, alongside a structured question bank — see the guidance on choosing a good AKT question bank for a comparison of the main preparation resources available to candidates across the Gulf.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once the AKT has been passed. Because the CPSA is available only in Manchester, Gulf-based candidates need to plan a UK trip around this stage, factoring in visa arrangements, flights, and several days of accommodation for final preparation immediately before the assessment. Many candidates already hold or can readily obtain a UK visitor visa given typical Gulf residency and employment status, though requirements should always be confirmed against current UK Home Office guidance rather than assumed from general reputation.

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and EPIC verification, English language evidence, and any outstanding documents are all confirmed, candidates can submit their full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage, including document checklists and processing times, is available in the site's GMC registration process guide.

UKMLA AKT Test Centres for Gulf-Based Candidates

One of the most practical advantages for doctors sitting the UKMLA from the Gulf is that the AKT does not require leaving the region at all. The AKT is delivered through the global Pearson VUE test centre network, which includes locations across major Gulf cities, and candidates should always confirm current centre availability directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal, since specific centres and open dates shift between sitting periods and are best verified close to the time of booking. A full breakdown of test centre options — including exactly which UAE and Saudi cities currently host sittings — is covered in the dedicated guide to AKT test centre locations, which is worth reading before committing to a specific booking window.

This is a significant contrast with the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in the Gulf, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — it is held exclusively at the GMC's purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. This means every Gulf-based candidate must, at minimum, make one trip to the UK to complete their UKMLA pathway, regardless of how much of the rest of the process can be completed from Riyadh, Jeddah, Dubai, or Abu Dhabi. Budgeting for this mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visitor visa if required, Manchester accommodation, and living costs for the preparation and exam period — should be built into financial planning from the very start rather than left until after the AKT is passed.

English Language Requirements for Gulf-Based Doctors

English language testing is a universal requirement for the great majority of international medical graduates, and this does not change based on current country of practice. A doctor who has worked for years in an English-medium hospital environment in Dubai or Riyadh, using English as the primary language of clinical documentation and multidisciplinary communication, may reasonably assume this satisfies the GMC's requirement without further evidence — in practice, formal testing is still required in almost all cases, and years of English-language clinical work in the Gulf does not itself constitute accepted evidence.

Two tests are accepted: IELTS Academic, where the GMC requires an overall score of 7.0, with no individual component (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the GMC requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. Both tests are available at test centres across the Gulf, including in Saudi Arabia and the UAE, so candidates do not need to travel outside the region to sit either option. OET is often more intuitive for practising clinicians because its scenarios are built entirely around healthcare communication, whereas IELTS Academic draws on a broader range of general academic subject matter. This detail — along with how the process is universal regardless of a candidate's current country — is covered further in the site's dedicated guide to English language testing requirements, which applies equally to Gulf-based candidates as to those applying directly from their original training country.

Both certificates carry a defined validity period from the test date, so timing matters: sitting the test too early relative to the overall pathway risks the certificate expiring before the registration application is submitted, which forces a costly re-sit. Full detail on accepted scores and how evidence is submitted is available directly from the regulator at the GMC's English language evidence requirements. This page is updated periodically and should always take precedence over any general summary.

Life After Registration: Moving From the Gulf to the NHS

Doctors relocating from the Gulf to the UK are, in one sense, making a second international move rather than a first — many will have already navigated relocating from their original training country to Saudi Arabia, the UAE, or another Gulf state earlier in their career. This experience of adapting to a new health system, new documentation standards, and a new professional culture is genuinely useful preparation for the transition to the NHS, even though the specific systems differ considerably.

Career progression after GMC registration generally follows one of several routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post, the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs, offering Foundation Year 2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place; a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK experience and portfolio evidence are in place; or locum work through NHS staff banks and agencies for doctors who want flexibility while building a UK clinical track record. Gulf-based doctors who have held specialist or consultant-level SCFHS classifications, or equivalent DHA/MOHAP grading, sometimes find this experience strengthens their portfolio for UK Specialty Training applications, though UK training pathways still require the standard competency evidence regardless of prior Gulf seniority.

Clinically, the biggest early adjustments for Gulf-trained doctors moving into the NHS tend to involve NHS-specific documentation standards, such as structured handover formats, a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary team working, and a more codified approach to consent and capacity under UK law than some Gulf healthcare systems require. It is worth noting that many doctors making this move originally trained in Egypt, India, or Pakistan before working in the Gulf, and the broader shape of their eventual UK journey mirrors the equivalent route for Egyptian-trained doctors or the pathway followed by Pakistani-trained doctors, even though the specific verification timelines and Gulf employment history layered on top differ from doctor to doctor. Salary progression follows the standard NHS pay scale for the relevant grade, and while Gulf salaries are frequently higher in absolute terms, many doctors making the move cite the structured nature of UK training — clear competency frameworks, protected training time, and defined progression points — as a significant non-financial factor in the decision.

Common Mistakes Gulf-Based Doctors Make on This Route

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among Gulf-based candidates pursuing the UKMLA pathway, and most of them stem from the same underlying misunderstanding: treating the current Gulf licence as more relevant to GMC eligibility than it actually is.

  • Assuming a Gulf licence transfers or shortcuts GMC recognition. An SCFHS classification, DHA licence, or MOHAP registration does not convert into GMC registration under any circumstances. The GMC always assesses the original primary medical qualification, and candidates who delay contacting their original medical school because they assume their Gulf credential is sufficient lose valuable time.
  • Contacting the wrong regulator for verification. EPIC verification runs through the original degree-granting medical school and its national regulator, not through SCFHS, DHA, MOHAP, or DOH. Candidates sometimes waste weeks attempting to route verification requests through their current Gulf licensing authority before realising this is not the correct channel.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Flights from the Gulf, several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, food, and often a short UK preparation course before the exam can together add £1,000 to £2,000 on top of the fee. Candidates who budget only for the headline fee frequently scramble for additional funds close to their exam date.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. Both IELTS and OET certificates are valid for a defined period from the test date. Candidates who sit their English test early in the process, before EPIC verification or AKT preparation is complete, sometimes find their certificate has expired by the time they submit their full registration application, forcing a costly re-sit.
  • Underestimating employer notice periods. Many Gulf hospitals and clinics require notice periods of several months, and some employment contracts include exit conditions or repatriation clauses. Candidates who pass both exams but have not planned their exit from current Gulf employment often face an unexpectedly long gap between GMC registration and actually starting an NHS post.
  • Treating document translation and certification casually. Any document not already issued in English — whether from the original training country or from Gulf employment records — requires a certified professional translation, not an informal translation from a bilingual colleague. Underestimating the time this takes is a common cause of application delay.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a Saudi SCFHS classification count towards GMC registration?

No, not directly. The Saudi Commission for Health Specialties (SCFHS) classification confirms a doctor's standing to practise within Saudi Arabia, but the GMC does not accept it as a substitute for verifying the doctor's original primary medical qualification. It can, however, serve as useful supporting evidence of ongoing clinical practice once the more fundamental eligibility question — based on the original medical degree — has been confirmed.

Can a doctor with a DHA or MOHAP licence in the UAE apply for GMC registration directly?

A DHA, MOHAP, or DOH licence confirms a doctor's entitlement to practise within a specific UAE jurisdiction, but it does not itself transfer to GMC registration. Eligibility is instead assessed against the doctor's original primary medical qualification, wherever that degree was awarded, following exactly the same primary source verification process any other international medical graduate would follow.

Where can Gulf-based doctors sit the UKMLA AKT?

The AKT is delivered through the global Pearson VUE test centre network, which includes locations across several Gulf cities, meaning many Gulf-based candidates can sit the exam without leaving the region. Exact centre availability changes between sitting periods, so candidates should confirm current locations and open dates directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal before finalising travel plans.

Do Gulf-based doctors still need to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, this cannot be avoided. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand, regardless of how much of the rest of the pathway is completed from within the Gulf. Every Gulf-based candidate must budget for at least one trip to the UK to complete their UKMLA pathway.

How much does the whole UKMLA process cost in SAR or AED?

The core GMC and exam fees together total approximately £1,800, which converts to roughly SAR 8,495 or AED 8,335 at broadly indicative exchange rates, though actual figures move with the market. Once English testing, EPIC verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget rises to approximately £3,300 to £4,800. Candidates should always verify current GMC fees and live exchange rates before making any payment rather than relying on an estimate calculated months in advance.

Does my original medical degree matter more than my current Gulf licence?

Yes, considerably more. The GMC's eligibility assessment is built entirely around the doctor's original primary medical qualification — the country and medical school where the basic medical degree was awarded — rather than any subsequent licence held in the Gulf. A current SCFHS, DHA, MOHAP, or DOH licence is useful supporting evidence of ongoing clinical practice, but it plays no role in determining whether the underlying medical degree itself satisfies GMC requirements.

Do doctors practising in the Gulf need an English language test if they already work in English daily?

In almost all cases, yes. Working in an English-medium clinical environment in the Gulf does not itself constitute accepted evidence of English proficiency for GMC purposes. Most candidates still need to submit a valid IELTS Academic score of 7.0 overall with no component below 7.0, or an OET Medicine result of Grade B minimum across all four components.

What is the realistic timeline from working in the Gulf to registering with the GMC?

Most candidates should plan for 18 to 30 months from starting EPIC verification of their original degree to beginning a first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. This accounts for original-country document processing, EPIC verification delays, English test preparation, exam preparation for both assessments, the GMC registration application itself, and a realistic notice period with a current Gulf employer.

Conclusion

The route to UKMLA for Gulf and Saudi doctors is entirely achievable, but it depends on correctly separating two things that are easy to conflate: a current Gulf licence, which demonstrates ongoing clinical practice, and the original primary medical qualification, which is what the GMC actually assesses for eligibility and primary source verification. Doctors who start EPIC verification of their original degree early, treat English testing and exam preparation as universal requirements unaffected by their Gulf employment, and plan realistically for both the mandatory Manchester trip and their notice period with a current employer consistently move through the pathway with fewer costly surprises. For doctors who originally trained in India, the equivalent process — including how EPIC verification and GMC registration work — follows the same underlying logic described throughout this guide, and the same is true for those who trained via the broader route from India to UK medical practice. Understanding the Applied Knowledge Test and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment formats in detail before booking either exam remains the best single preparation step available.
