Finding the right dependent visa for doctors' families can feel like a second, unfamiliar exam layered on top of everything else an international medical graduate is already managing — GMC registration, the UKMLA, job hunting, and the doctor's own Health and Care Worker visa. Yet for the great majority of doctors relocating to the NHS, bringing a spouse or partner and children to the UK is a well-established, workable process, not a bureaucratic obstacle course. This guide is written specifically for that purpose: it does not repeat the mechanics of the doctor's own visa application, which the site's existing general guide to the UK visa for international doctors already covers in depth, and it does not revisit the 2026 salary-threshold changes affecting the main applicant, which are explained separately in the site's article on the 2026 visa salary threshold changes for doctors. Instead, this article focuses entirely on the dependant side of the process — the spouse, partner, and children who join the doctor in the UK.

Because immigration rules affecting dependants have been reviewed more than once in recent years, and because eligibility now genuinely differs depending on the sponsored worker's occupation and the date their sponsorship began, this guide is deliberately careful about which figures and rules it states as fixed and which it flags as subject to change. Wherever a detail could plausibly have moved since this article was written, the guide says so explicitly and points to the live gov.uk guidance, since that is the only source that is updated the moment a rule changes. Doctors and their families should treat this article as a structured starting point for understanding the process, not as a substitute for checking the current position before submitting an application.

As with all immigration content on this site, a standing caveat applies throughout: this is not legal or immigration advice. Rules, financial thresholds, and fees are reviewed periodically by the Home Office, sometimes with limited notice, and every figure below should be verified directly on gov.uk's official guidance on partners and children joining a Health and Care Worker visa holder before an application is prepared, or with a regulated immigration adviser or the sponsoring Trust's HR and international recruitment team for anything specific to an individual family's circumstances.

Quick Answer: Can Doctors Bring Their Family to the UK?

Yes. A doctor holding, or successfully applying for, a Health and Care Worker visa can generally apply for a dependent visa for doctors' families covering a spouse, civil partner, unmarried partner, and dependent children under 18, either as part of the same application or as a later, separate one. Dependants who qualify are generally granted permission to work in the UK in almost any occupation, to access NHS care, and — for children — to attend UK state schools, on broadly the same footing as a UK resident family.

The position is more nuanced than a single blanket "yes," however, and the nuance matters enormously depending on the sponsored doctor's specific occupation. Since 11 March 2024, care workers and senior care workers have faced significant new restrictions on bringing dependants, and since 22 July 2025 further restrictions apply to some medium-skilled health and social care roles classified at RQF levels 3 to 5. Doctors sponsored as qualified medical practitioners sit at a materially higher skill classification than either of these restricted groups, and — based on the current published guidance — generally retain full dependant rights. Even so, because these rules were introduced specifically to narrow dependant eligibility and could be reviewed again, every doctor planning to bring family to the UK should personally confirm their own occupation code's current dependant eligibility on gov.uk's dependant guidance page before assuming the general position described in this article still applies unchanged to their own case. The sections below walk through who qualifies as a dependant, the application process and documents required, the financial requirement and its exemptions, the Immigration Health Surcharge, dependants' right to work, and practical timing advice for families deciding whether to travel together or in stages.

Who Counts as a Dependant on a Doctor's Visa?

The Home Office uses a specific, fairly narrow definition of who qualifies as a dependant partner or child, and understanding this definition precisely — rather than assuming it matches a country's own domestic definition of family — is the first step for any doctor planning a family relocation. Getting this definition right early avoids wasted effort preparing a dependent visa for doctors' families application for a relative who, however close, simply does not meet the Home Office's qualifying criteria for this specific route.

Partners: Spouse, Civil Partner, or Unmarried Partner

A dependant partner can be a husband, wife, or civil partner, or an unmarried partner in a relationship that is genuinely equivalent to marriage. For a married or civil partnership couple, the core evidence requirement is straightforward: a valid marriage or civil partnership certificate, generally recognised as legally valid both in the country where it took place and in the UK. For unmarried partners, the position requires more documentary evidence, since the couple must demonstrate they have been living together in a relationship akin to marriage or civil partnership for a qualifying period — commonly cited as at least two years — and that any periods of separation were reasonable in the circumstances, for example due to work or study commitments, rather than a sign the relationship has ended.

Couples who have spent time apart, which is common where one partner has already moved to the UK to begin work while the other remains at home temporarily, should keep evidence of ongoing communication, financial support sent between them, and visits or shared time together during any period of separation. This evidence becomes important precisely because the couple is not living under one roof at the point the application is prepared, and the Home Office will want to see that the relationship has continued rather than lapsed.

Children Under 18

Dependent children generally include any child under 18 at the date of application, including a child born in the UK during the sponsored doctor's stay. A child does not need to be biologically related to both parents to qualify, but the relationship — whether biological, adopted, or otherwise legally recognised — must be demonstrable through documentation such as a birth certificate or adoption order. Children must generally live with the sponsoring parent, other than where they are away at boarding school or in full-time education elsewhere in the UK, and a child who is already married or in a civil partnership does not qualify as a dependant under this route.

Children Over 18

A young adult child over 18 can, in more limited circumstances, continue to qualify as a dependant if they already hold permission to be in the UK as a dependant of the same parent — typically because they arrived as a minor and have remained in the family unit without a break in lawful status. A child over 18 who has never previously held dependant status generally cannot newly join a parent's visa as a dependant purely on the basis of age and relationship; this route is intended to allow existing dependant children to remain within the family unit as they turn 18, not to bring in an adult child for the first time.

Occupation-Based Restrictions: Why a Doctor's Specific Role Matters

The single most important recent change to this area of the rules is that dependant eligibility is no longer occupation-neutral across the whole Health and Care Worker visa route. Two distinct restrictions now apply to certain roles:

  • Care workers and senior care workers sponsored under the Health and Care Worker visa have, since 11 March 2024, generally been unable to bring dependants at all, unless a specific listed exception applies — most commonly where the worker has been continuously employed in the UK as a care worker or senior care worker on a Health and Care Worker or Skilled Worker visa since before that date, or where a child was born in the UK during the family's stay.
  • Certain medium-skilled roles classified at RQF levels 3 to 5 — a wider category than care work alone, covering some roles on the Immigration Salary List or Temporary Shortage List — have faced a broadly similar restriction since 22 July 2025, again subject to transitional exceptions for workers already sponsored before that date.

Qualified medical practitioners — the occupation code doctors are sponsored under — sit at a substantially higher skill classification (RQF level 6 and above, degree-level and beyond) than either restricted category, and the published guidance treats doctors, registered nurses, and other RQF 6+ health professionals as retaining full dependant rights, distinct from the care-worker and medium-skilled restrictions described above. This is a genuinely important, and genuinely favourable, distinction for doctors specifically, since it means the tightening of dependant rules seen elsewhere in the health and social care visa system has, on current published guidance, not been extended to the medical profession. Because these restrictions were introduced through incremental policy changes and the Home Office has shown a willingness to adjust dependant eligibility further, doctors should not treat this as a permanently fixed position — confirming current eligibility for the specific occupation code on the Certificate of Sponsorship, via gov.uk's dependant guidance, remains the only reliable way to be certain before relying on it for a real application.

How to Apply: The Dependant Visa Application Process

Applying for a spouse, partner, or child to join a doctor in the UK follows a defined process that can run either alongside the doctor's own application or afterwards, once the doctor is already settled into their post.

Applying Together vs. Applying Later

A doctor applying for their own Health and Care Worker visa for the first time, whether from overseas or switching from within the UK, can generally include a partner and children in the same application round, provided the doctor's own Certificate of Sponsorship and supporting evidence are ready. Where the doctor is the first family member to apply, they can generate a "family linking code" once their own application is underway, which the partner and each child then use to connect their separate applications to the main applicant's case. This linking mechanism exists precisely because each family member submits an individual application form, even when the family intends to travel and arrive together.

Alternatively, a doctor can apply alone first and bring family to the UK later, once they have started work, found suitable housing, and settled into the practical realities of NHS life. In this scenario, the dependant's later application still references the main applicant's visa details rather than starting from nothing, and the family relationship and financial requirements must still be satisfied at the time the dependant applies, not merely at the time the doctor's own visa was granted.

Documents Typically Required

While the exact document checklist depends on individual circumstances and should always be confirmed against the live application guidance, a typical dependent visa for doctors' families application commonly requires:

  • A valid passport for each dependant, with sufficient remaining validity to comfortably outlast the intended visa period.
  • Proof of relationship — a marriage or civil partnership certificate for a partner, or a birth certificate (and adoption order, where relevant) for a child.
  • Evidence supporting an unmarried partner relationship, where applicable — such as joint tenancy or utility documents, correspondence addressed to both partners at the same address, or evidence of the relationship's duration and continuity through periods of separation.
  • Evidence of the financial requirement, unless an exemption applies (see below).
  • A criminal record certificate covering recent years of residence overseas, for dependants over the relevant age threshold, in some cases.
  • Certified translations of any document not originally issued in English or Welsh.
  • Biometric information — a photograph and, in most cases, fingerprints, gathered either at a visa application centre or, for some applicants switching status from inside the UK, via a smartphone identity-verification app.

Because document requirements vary by nationality, age, and individual family circumstances, the safest approach is to complete the guided questions on the official online application, which tailors the exact document list to the specific case, rather than working from a generic checklist alone.

English Language Requirements for Adult Dependants

Unlike the doctor's own visa, which carries its own English language requirement entirely separate from the GMC's clinical English test, a dependant partner generally does not need to prove English language ability at the point of the initial dependant visa application under the Health and Care Worker route. However, an adult dependant partner who goes on to apply for an extension of their own dependant status, or ultimately for settlement, may be required to demonstrate English language ability at that later stage, depending on the specific route and how long they have held dependant status. Because this requirement can apply at a different stage of the process than many families expect, and because the specific thresholds and exemptions are reviewed periodically, dependants planning a multi-year stay should check the current English language requirement for extensions and settlement directly on gov.uk well before it becomes relevant, rather than assuming no English requirement will ever apply simply because none applied at the outset. Dependent children are not subject to an English language requirement for their visa application.

Where and How to Submit the Application

Dependant applications are submitted through the same official online visa application service used for the main applicant, with the process differing slightly depending on whether the dependant is applying from outside the UK or, in the case of a family already partly in the UK, switching or extending status from within the country. Processing times for applications made from outside the UK have historically run to a few weeks under standard service, with a faster priority service available at an additional cost, while applications made from inside the UK typically take longer under standard processing. Because processing times move with application volumes and are not fixed, a family planning a specific arrival date — for example, timed around school term dates — should build in a realistic buffer rather than assuming the fastest historical processing time will apply to their own case.

The Financial Requirement for Dependants

Historically, one of the most confusing aspects of a dependent visa for doctors' families has been understanding exactly what financial evidence, if any, is required — and this is precisely the area where doctors should be most careful to check the current position, since financial requirements for dependants have been reviewed and adjusted more than once.

The Maintenance Funds Requirement

Where the financial requirement applies and no exemption is available, a dependant partner and each dependant child generally need to show a specified amount of personal savings held for a continuous qualifying period — commonly 28 consecutive days — ending shortly before the application is submitted. As a general guide to the shape of this requirement, published figures have specified an amount for a partner, a further amount for one child, and a smaller additional amount for each further child beyond the first, with the total rising as family size increases. Because the specific figures are reviewed periodically and this guide does not want to state an amount that may already be out of date by the time it is read, doctors and their families should treat gov.uk's dependant guidance page as the only reliable source for the exact current figures required for their specific family size.

Common Exemptions From the Financial Requirement

Several circumstances can exempt a family from needing to show the maintenance funds requirement at all, and doctors should check whether any apply before assuming they need to assemble 28 days of qualifying bank statements:

  • Sponsor certification. Where the sponsoring employer formally certifies, via the Certificate of Sponsorship, that it will cover a dependant's maintenance costs for a period after arrival (commonly the opening weeks in the UK), the personal savings requirement may not apply in the same way.
  • Length of prior lawful residence. Where the family has already held valid UK immigration permission for a continuous period — commonly cited as 12 months or more — before the dependant application is made, the financial requirement may no longer apply in the same form, on the basis that the family's ability to support itself in the UK has already been demonstrated in practice.
  • Combined household income considerations. Some families are able to rely on a combination of the sponsoring doctor's salary and independent savings to satisfy the requirement, rather than needing the dependant's own separate savings pot, depending on how the specific figures are calculated for the family's circumstances.

Because eligibility for these exemptions depends on precise dates, employer certification details, and the family's specific immigration history, doctors should not assume an exemption applies without checking it against their own Certificate of Sponsorship and immigration history, ideally with input from the sponsoring Trust's HR or international recruitment team, who are generally familiar with how the financial requirement has applied to other doctors they have sponsored.

Historical Context: Why This Area Has Been More Favourable for Health and Care Roles

It is worth understanding, in general terms, why doctors and other health professionals have often found the financial side of this route more manageable than the equivalent general Skilled Worker dependant process. The Health and Care Worker visa was created specifically to support NHS and social care recruitment, and policy makers have periodically built in flexibilities — around fees, the Immigration Health Surcharge, and in some cases financial requirements — intended to reduce the total cost of relocating a family into a health or care role. However, precisely because this area has been adjusted more than once already, and because the direction of travel in recent years has, if anything, been toward tightening dependant rules for some occupations within the same visa route (as seen in the care-worker and RQF 3–5 restrictions described earlier), this guide deliberately avoids stating that doctors are permanently guaranteed a more favourable financial position indefinitely. The safest working assumption for a doctor planning a family relocation is: check the current financial requirement and any applicable exemption for the specific occupation code on the Certificate of Sponsorship, at the time of application, rather than relying on what applied to a colleague who moved a year or two earlier.

The Immigration Health Surcharge for Dependants

The Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) is a charge most UK visa applicants must pay upfront, calculated per year of the visa granted, to access NHS services during their stay. For families relocating together, this charge can add a very substantial sum to the overall cost of the move if it applies in full to every family member.

Doctors on the Health and Care Worker visa benefit from a specific exemption from the surcharge for themselves, and — importantly for families — this exemption generally extends to their dependant partner and children as well, provided the dependant's application is correctly linked to the doctor's eligible Health and Care Worker sponsorship. In practice, this means a doctor's spouse and children joining them under this route typically do not pay the Immigration Health Surcharge that would otherwise apply to dependants of most other UK visa categories, which represents one of the more significant, concrete savings available to a relocating medical family.

This exemption is not automatic in the sense of requiring no verification at all — it depends on the dependant's application being correctly processed against the main applicant's eligible occupation code, and doctors should confirm the exemption is applied correctly during the application process rather than assuming it retroactively if an error occurs. As with every other financial detail in this guide, the definitive current position should always be checked on gov.uk's dependant guidance page, since surcharge rates, exemptions, and the visa categories they apply to have all been adjusted by government more than once in recent years.

Dependants' Right to Work and Access NHS Services

One of the most practically important features of a dependent visa for doctors' families, for families weighing up whether a UK relocation makes sense, is what a dependant partner can actually do once in the country — beyond simply accompanying the sponsored doctor.

Right to Work

A dependant partner on a Health and Care Worker visa is generally permitted to work in the UK in almost any occupation, with only a narrow set of exceptions such as working as a professional sportsperson or sports coach. This is a genuinely significant practical advantage compared with some other countries' medical relocation visas, where an accompanying spouse is often barred from working altogether or restricted to a narrow list of permitted roles. In practice, this means a doctor's partner can pursue their own career in the UK — whether that is continuing in their existing profession, retraining, or taking up study — rather than being confined to an unpaid, dependent status for the duration of the visa.

Dependent children do not have a separate "right to work" consideration in the same sense, but older dependent children who reach working age while in the UK on this status are generally able to take up employment or part-time work in the same way as other young people resident in the UK, subject to standard employment law protections for minors.

Access to NHS Services

Because dependants of a Health and Care Worker visa holder generally benefit from the same Immigration Health Surcharge exemption as the main applicant, they are generally entitled to access NHS services on the same basis as a UK resident, without paying the surcharge that funds free NHS access for most other visa categories. This does not mean every single healthcare service becomes entirely free — prescriptions, dental treatment, and optical care, for example, generally carry the same charges (or exemptions) that apply to any UK resident under the relevant NHS charging rules for those specific services, quite separate from the Immigration Health Surcharge itself. Families should register with a local GP practice promptly after arriving in the UK, since GP registration is generally what triggers access to the wider NHS referral pathway for both routine and urgent care.

Access to Education for Dependent Children

Dependent children of a Health and Care Worker visa holder are generally entitled to attend UK state schools on the same basis as other resident children, without paying the international or overseas fees that apply to some other visa categories' dependants in certain circumstances. Families relocating with school-age children should contact their local council's admissions team as early as possible once a UK address is confirmed, since school place allocation in popular areas can take weeks, and starting the admissions process before arrival — where the local authority allows it — can meaningfully reduce the gap between a family's arrival and a child actually starting school.

Practical Timing: Applying Together vs. the Doctor Coming First

Many doctors face a genuinely difficult practical decision when planning a UK relocation: apply for a dependent visa for doctors' families covering the whole household from the outset, or have the doctor travel first, get settled into the job and find housing, and bring the family over in a second phase. There is no single universally correct answer, and the right choice depends heavily on individual family circumstances, but several practical considerations consistently come up.

The Case for Applying Together

Applying and travelling together avoids a period of family separation, which many doctors and their partners consider the single most important factor, particularly where children are young and a lengthy separation from one parent would be genuinely disruptive. It also means the family only goes through the disruption of relocating once, rather than experiencing an initial move followed by a second, separate move for the rest of the family some months later. From a purely administrative point of view, applying together allows the family linking code process to connect every application cleanly from the start, which some families find simpler than managing a follow-on application months later.

The Case for the Doctor Coming First

On the other hand, a substantial number of doctors choose to travel first alone, particularly where housing, schooling, and the practicalities of a new city are genuinely uncertain until the doctor has actually started the job and can assess the local area in person. Coming first allows the doctor to secure suitable long-term housing (rather than committing a family to a rental sight-unseen), research schools and register children in advance of their actual start date, and get a first payslip and NHS induction underway before adding the logistics of an international family move on top of an already demanding first few months in a new health system. Some Trusts' relocation packages and NHS staff accommodation arrangements are also easier to navigate for a single arriving worker in the first instance, with family accommodation confirmed once the doctor has a clearer picture of their department, working pattern, and preferred location.

A Balanced Practical Recommendation

Most doctors who have gone through this process report that the right choice depends primarily on how settled the destination city and housing situation already are before the move, and how young any children are. Where a doctor already has confirmed, family-suitable housing arranged before departure — for example through a Trust's staff accommodation scheme or a previously visited rental — applying together often works well. Where housing, school catchment areas, or even the exact city are still uncertain at the point of the doctor's own visa application, a growing number of doctors choose a staged approach: travel alone for the first few weeks to a few months, use that time to secure stable housing and confirm school places, and then bring the family over once those practicalities are settled rather than adding them to an already complex initial transition. Whichever path a family chooses, the financial requirement, if it applies, and the Immigration Health Surcharge exemption both apply in essentially the same way regardless of whether the family applies together or in stages — timing is a practical and emotional decision for the family, not one that changes the underlying immigration rules.

Common Mistakes Families Make With Dependant Visas

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among doctors and their families navigating a dependent visa for doctors' families application for the first time, and almost all of them are planning mistakes rather than genuine ineligibility.

  • Assuming every family member automatically qualifies as a dependant. An adult child who has never previously held dependant status, or a partner who cannot yet evidence two years of cohabitation, may not qualify under this specific route without a different immigration category being considered instead.
  • Underestimating the total number of separate applications and fees involved. Because the visa application fee is charged per family member rather than as a single household fee, a family of four should expect to plan, budget, and document four broadly separate applications, even where the applications are submitted together and linked by a family code.
  • Assuming a fixed financial requirement figure without checking it is current. Because maintenance fund amounts are reviewed periodically, relying on a figure seen in an older article or a forum post risks a shortfall that only becomes apparent when the application is already being assessed.
  • Not checking the doctor's specific occupation code against the current dependant restrictions. While qualified doctors generally retain full dependant rights under the current published guidance, a doctor sponsored under an unusual or borderline occupation code should confirm this directly rather than assuming it applies without checking, particularly given how the care-worker and RQF 3–5 restrictions were introduced with relatively limited advance notice.
  • Leaving school admissions until after arrival. Families who wait until they have physically arrived in the UK before contacting a local council about school places often find the process considerably slower than families who begin research and, where possible, initial contact before the move.
  • Assuming the English language requirement never applies to a dependant partner. While it typically does not apply at the point of the initial application, it can become relevant at extension or settlement stage, and families planning a multi-year stay should not be caught off guard by this later requirement.

Avoiding these specific, well-documented mistakes will not eliminate every source of delay, since processing times and document verification depend on external bodies beyond any individual family's control. Nevertheless, sidestepping these particular errors removes a substantial share of the friction that families otherwise experience entirely unnecessarily during what is already an emotionally significant life transition.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a doctor bring their spouse and children to the UK on a Health and Care Worker visa?

Yes. A doctor can generally apply for a spouse or partner and dependent children to join them, either as part of the same application or later, provided the relationship and any applicable financial requirements are met. Doctors currently retain full dependant rights under the published guidance, distinct from the restrictions that now apply to care workers and some other roles on the same visa route.

Do dependants of a Health and Care Worker visa holder pay the Immigration Health Surcharge?

Generally, no. Dependants of an eligible Health and Care Worker visa holder typically benefit from the same surcharge exemption as the main applicant, provided the dependant application is correctly linked to the doctor's eligible sponsorship. This should be confirmed as part of the application, since it depends on the sponsoring occupation code being correctly recorded.

Is there a financial requirement for a doctor's family to move to the UK?

There can be, unless an exemption applies — such as employer certification of maintenance costs via the Certificate of Sponsorship, or the family already having held valid UK permission for a qualifying period. Because the specific savings figures required are reviewed periodically, families should always check the current amount on gov.uk's dependant guidance rather than relying on a previously quoted figure.

Can a doctor's partner work in the UK on a dependant visa?

Yes, in almost any occupation, with only a narrow set of exceptions such as professional sport. This is one of the most practically valuable features of this route for relocating families, since it allows a partner to pursue their own career rather than being confined to a narrow or unpaid status.

Should a doctor bring their family at the same time or travel to the UK first?

Both approaches are common and workable. Applying together avoids a period of family separation, while the doctor travelling first can allow time to secure suitable housing and confirm school places before the rest of the family arrives. The right choice depends on how settled housing and schooling arrangements already are and the ages of any children involved.

Do children need a visa to attend UK schools as a doctor's dependants?

Yes, dependent children generally need their own dependant visa linked to the sponsoring parent, but once granted, they are generally entitled to attend UK state schools on the same basis as other resident children, without the additional international fees that apply under some other visa categories.

Does a partner need to pass an English test to join a doctor in the UK?

Generally not at the point of the initial dependant application under this route, though an English language requirement can become relevant later, at extension or settlement stage. Dependent children are not subject to an English language requirement for their visa.

Are care workers allowed to bring their families to the UK in the same way as doctors?

No, not in the same way. Since 11 March 2024, care workers and senior care workers have faced significant restrictions on bringing dependants, generally unable to do so unless a specific exception applies. Doctors, as higher-skilled medical practitioners, are not currently subject to this same restriction under the published guidance, though this position should always be checked against the doctor's specific occupation code before relying on it.

Conclusion

For the great majority of doctors relocating to the NHS, the process of securing a dependent visa for doctors' families is a well-established and genuinely workable one — spouses, partners, and children can generally join a sponsored doctor, work or study once in the UK, and access NHS services largely free from the Immigration Health Surcharge that adds significant cost to many other UK visa categories. The areas that most reward careful preparation are confirming the current financial requirement and any exemption that applies, gathering relationship and translation documents early, and making a considered, honest decision as a family about whether to travel together or in stages.
