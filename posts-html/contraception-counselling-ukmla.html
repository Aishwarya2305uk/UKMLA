Contraception UKMLA questions are among the most reliably tested topics across both papers, because contraception counselling sits at the intersection of clinical knowledge and communication skill — exactly the combination the Medical Licensing Assessment is designed to sample. In the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) it appears as single-best-answer questions on UK Medical Eligibility Criteria (UKMEC) categories, method selection, and emergency contraception timing windows. In the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — see the full CPSA exam format and station breakdown for how communication stations are structured and marked — it appears as a full communication station: a simulated patient wants to discuss starting, switching, or stopping contraception, and you are marked on your ability to explore ideas and concerns, offer genuinely informed choice, and reach a shared decision safely. This guide brings both angles together in one place, with the UKMEC framework explained in detail, every major method category compared side by side, emergency contraception timing spelled out precisely, a practical structure for the counselling conversation itself, and the contraindications examiners return to again and again — everything needed to answer contraception UKMLA questions confidently in both the AKT and the CPSA.

Because contraception counselling depends so heavily on individual risk factors — age, smoking status, migraine history, BMI, breastfeeding status, and personal preference — it rewards candidates who can combine a clear mental model of UKMEC categories with a natural, patient-centred conversation style. UK medical students meet this content first through primary care and sexual health placements and then again as a dedicated CPSA station; international medical graduates (IMGs) often find the clinical facts more familiar than the communication expectations, since the UK's shared decision-making model and its explicit "no judgement, no assumptions" approach to sexual history taking can differ from training received elsewhere. This guide is written for both audiences and flags where the expectations genuinely differ.

The UKMEC Framework: How Suitability Is Actually Assessed

Any serious contraception UKMLA revision plan has to start with the UKMEC framework, since almost every method-selection question in the exam is really a UKMEC question in disguise. The UK Medical Eligibility Criteria (UKMEC) is the framework every clinician in the UK uses to judge whether a given contraceptive method is safe for a given patient, and it is the single most important structure to master for contraception UKMLA questions. UKMEC is maintained by the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH), adapted from World Health Organization Medical Eligibility Criteria guidance to reflect UK prescribing practice, and it classifies every method against every relevant medical condition using four numbered categories.

UKMEC Categories 1 to 4 Explained

UKMEC Category 1 means there is no restriction on the use of the method for that condition — it can be used with no additional caution. Category 2 means the advantages of the method generally outweigh the theoretical or proven risks — the method can be used, and it is often the pragmatic first choice, but the clinician should be aware of the underlying risk. Category 3 means the theoretical or proven risks usually outweigh the advantages of the method — the method is not usually recommended unless other, more suitable methods are unavailable or unacceptable to the patient, and if used, requires careful clinical judgement and follow-up. Category 4 represents an unacceptable health risk if the method is used — this is an absolute contraindication and the method must not be prescribed.

A helpful shorthand used widely in UK sexual and reproductive health training is: Category 1 — use it; Category 2 — use it, but know why it is not risk-free; Category 3 — think very carefully, and usually choose something else; Category 4 — never use it. Examiners frequently construct AKT questions by describing a patient's risk factors in a vignette and asking which UKMEC category a given method falls into, or which method is the most appropriate given a stated contraindication — so the ability to reason through the category system, rather than just memorise isolated facts, is what actually earns marks.

Why UKMEC Matters More for Some Methods Than Others

UKMEC categorisation matters most for combined hormonal contraception (CHC), because oestrogen is the component responsible for the majority of serious contraindications — principally increased venous thromboembolism (VTE) and arterial (stroke, myocardial infarction) risk. Progestogen-only methods carry a much shorter list of Category 3 and 4 conditions, and intrauterine and barrier methods carry the fewest of all, since they act locally rather than systemically. This is precisely why LARC methods are so often the correct answer in AKT vignettes describing a patient with multiple relative contraindications to hormonal contraception — they sidestep most of the systemic risk altogether.

Applying UKMEC in Practice

In real UK general practice and sexual health clinics, UKMEC categories are looked up condition-by-condition using the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare's published UKMEC summary tables, not recalled from memory for every possible permutation — and it is entirely reasonable, and expected, for a candidate in a CPSA station to say they would "check the current UKMEC guidance" for an unusual combination of risk factors rather than guess. What the exam does expect candidates to know from memory are the handful of high-yield, frequently retested Category 4 contraindications covered in detail later in this guide, because these represent genuine patient-safety red flags a foundation doctor must recognise instantly, without needing to look anything up.

Contraceptive Methods by Category: A Complete Breakdown

Contraceptive methods are conventionally grouped into six categories for teaching purposes: combined hormonal contraception, progestogen-only methods, long-acting reversible contraception (LARC), barrier methods, emergency contraception, and permanent (sterilisation) methods. Each has a distinct mechanism, side-effect profile, and set of UKMEC considerations, and contraception UKMLA questions test all six categories at some point across the AKT and CPSA combined.

Combined Hormonal Contraception (CHC)

Combined hormonal contraception is the first method category most contraception UKMLA questions test, and it contains both an oestrogen (usually ethinylestradiol) and a progestogen, and is available as the combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP), the transdermal patch (changed weekly), and the vaginal ring (left in place for 21 days, then removed for a 7-day break). All three work primarily by inhibiting ovulation, with additional effects on cervical mucus and endometrial receptivity. Typical-use failure rates across all three are similar, at around 9% per year, because effectiveness is highly dependent on consistent, correct use — perfect use failure rates are much lower, closer to 0.3%.

CHC offers non-contraceptive benefits worth discussing during counselling: lighter, more regular, less painful periods; improved acne and hirsutism (particularly with anti-androgenic progestogens such as drospirenone); reduced ovarian and endometrial cancer risk persisting for years after stopping; and reduced functional ovarian cysts. Its major risks are venous thromboembolism (roughly 5–12 per 10,000 woman-years versus a baseline of around 2 per 10,000 in non-users), a small increase in ischaemic stroke and myocardial infarction risk (particularly in smokers, those with migraine with aura, or uncontrolled hypertension), and a small increase in breast and cervical cancer risk that reduces after stopping — a genuine contraception UKMLA exam point examiners use to test risk-benefit reasoning.

Progestogen-Only Methods

Progestogen-only methods form the second major contraception UKMLA method category and avoid oestrogen entirely, making them suitable for many patients in whom CHC is contraindicated. The progestogen-only pill (POP) is taken daily with no pill-free interval; traditional POPs (e.g. levonorgestrel- or norethisterone-based) must be taken within a strict 3-hour window each day, while desogestrel-based POPs have a more forgiving 12-hour window, matching the injection and implant's reliability profile more closely. The progestogen injection (medroxyprogesterone acetate, branded Depo-Provera) is given every 13 weeks by intramuscular or subcutaneous injection and, alongside the implant, is one of the most effective user-independent methods available short of an intrauterine device.

Progestogen-only methods work mainly by thickening cervical mucus and, at the doses used in the injection and implant, by also reliably inhibiting ovulation. Because they contain no oestrogen, they carry a much shorter contraindication list — safe in most women with migraine (including with aura), most cardiovascular risk factors, and typically startable immediately postpartum, including while breastfeeding. Common counselling points are unpredictable bleeding patterns (a major reason for discontinuation), a modest delay in return of fertility after stopping the injection (up to 12 months), and a small, reversible reduction in bone mineral density with prolonged Depo-Provera use.

Long-Acting Reversible Contraception (LARC): Coil, Implant, and Injection

LARC is the contraception UKMLA topic most closely tied to real-world effectiveness data, and it is the umbrella term for the contraceptive implant, the intrauterine device (copper coil), the intrauterine system (hormonal coil), and — often included in UK teaching, though technically requiring more frequent user action than the others — the progestogen injection. All LARC methods share the key clinical advantage of removing user error from the effectiveness equation, which is why their typical-use and perfect-use failure rates are almost identical and dramatically lower than user-dependent methods.

The contraceptive implant (Nexplanon) is a single flexible rod inserted subdermally into the inner upper arm under local anaesthetic, releasing etonogestrel for up to 3 years, with a failure rate below 0.1% — making it, alongside the intrauterine methods, one of the most effective reversible contraceptives available. The copper intrauterine device (IUD) is hormone-free and works primarily by a direct toxic effect on sperm and ova, remaining effective for 5–10 years depending on the specific device, and is also the single most effective form of emergency contraception when fitted after unprotected intercourse. The levonorgestrel intrauterine system (IUS, e.g. Mirena, Kyleena, or Jaydess depending on dose and licensed duration) combines a local progestogen effect on the endometrium with the mechanical presence of a coil, typically produces much lighter or absent periods over time, and is licensed for both contraception and, at the higher-dose formulation, management of heavy menstrual bleeding.

Counselling for LARC insertion should cover the small but important procedural risks: uterine perforation (rare, higher risk during breastfeeding or shortly postpartum), expulsion (most likely in the first 3 months), and a small increased risk of pelvic infection in the first 20 days after insertion, usually related to a pre-existing undiagnosed sexually transmitted infection rather than the procedure itself. Intrauterine methods do not increase the background risk of ectopic pregnancy, but if pregnancy does occur despite a coil in place, a higher relative proportion are ectopic, since the coil prevents intrauterine implantation more effectively than it prevents fertilisation.

Barrier Methods

Barrier methods round out the contraception UKMLA method list — male condoms, female (internal) condoms, diaphragms, and cervical caps — work by physically preventing sperm from reaching the cervix, and the male condom remains the only contraceptive method that also provides meaningful protection against sexually transmitted infections. Typical-use failure rates for condoms are relatively high (around 13% per year for male condoms and around 21% for female condoms) because correct and consistent use is difficult to sustain, but perfect-use failure rates are much lower. Diaphragms and caps must be used with spermicide and fitted correctly, and are less commonly prescribed in current UK practice than in previous decades, but still appear in the exam as an option for patients who wish to avoid hormonal methods entirely and do not want an intrauterine device.

Barrier methods carry essentially no UKMEC Category 3 or 4 contraindications related to systemic medical conditions, since they act locally and are not absorbed — the main clinical caveat is a latex allergy, for which non-latex alternatives exist. Because condoms are the only method offering STI protection, dual method use — a highly effective method such as the implant or coil alongside condoms — is the standard advice for patients not in a mutually monogamous relationship, and this dual-protection message is a common thread examiners expect to see raised spontaneously in a CPSA contraception station.

Emergency Contraception: Methods and Timing Windows

Emergency contraception (EC) is one of the highest-yield topics in the entire contraception UKMLA syllabus because the timing windows are precise, testable, and clinically consequential if got wrong — and it is worth revising this subsection until the exact hour thresholds are automatic rather than approximate. Three methods are available in the UK, and choosing correctly between them depends on time since unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI), cycle timing, weight, and any recent hormonal contraception use.

Every emergency contraception consultation should also include a discussion of ongoing contraception, since a single dose does not protect against pregnancy from any further unprotected intercourse later in the same cycle.

Sterilisation: Permanent Contraception

Sterilisation is offered to patients, of either sex, who have completed their family or are otherwise certain they do not wish to retain fertility, and must always be discussed as a permanent method with a small but real failure rate, rather than as a guaranteed or easily reversible option. Female sterilisation (tubal occlusion, most commonly by laparoscopic clip application) has a lifetime failure rate of approximately 1 in 200, and if pregnancy does occur after sterilisation, there is a higher relative chance that it is ectopic. Vasectomy (male sterilisation) is simpler, can be performed under local anaesthetic, and has a lower failure rate of around 1 in 2,000 once post-vasectomy semen analysis has confirmed azoospermia — patients must continue using effective contraception until clearance is confirmed, since vasectomy is not immediately effective.

UK guidance is explicit that sterilisation counselling must include a realistic discussion of regret, particularly in younger patients or those without children, since reversal surgery is not routinely funded on the NHS and is not reliably successful even when performed privately. This makes sterilisation counselling a genuinely useful CPSA scenario to prepare for in its own right, since it tests whether a candidate can explore the patient's certainty and circumstances non-judgementally, without either discouraging a fully competent adult's informed decision or rushing them toward an irreversible procedure.

Structuring a Contraception Counselling Conversation for the CPSA

This is the section that most contraception UKMLA guides skip over in favour of pure clinical facts, yet it is exactly what a CPSA station actually scores. Contraception counselling is a genuinely common CPSA station, and it is marked less on reciting UKMEC categories from memory and more on demonstrating a structured, patient-centred communication process that arrives at a safe, informed decision. The station typically presents a patient who wants to start, switch, or stop a method, sometimes with a specific worry or myth they want addressed, and sometimes with an underlying risk factor the candidate must actively elicit through history-taking.

Opening and Establishing the Agenda

Begin by introducing yourself, confirming the patient's name and date of birth, and explicitly inviting them to explain what has brought them in today, rather than assuming the reason from the case notes alone. Establishing the agenda early — "What were you hoping we could cover about contraception today?" — signals genuine patient-centred practice and avoids launching straight into a rehearsed explanation of a method the patient may not actually want.

Taking a Focused, Non-Judgemental History

A contraception consultation requires a focused but sensitive history covering: menstrual history (last menstrual period, cycle regularity), current and past contraceptive use, relationship status and number of partners (relevant to STI risk and dual protection advice, not for moral judgement), smoking status, migraine history — specifically asking about aura, since patients rarely volunteer this unprompted — personal and family history of venous thromboembolism or cardiovascular disease, breastfeeding status if postpartum, BMI, and any current medications that may interact with hormonal methods (notably enzyme-inducing drugs such as certain anticonvulsants and rifampicin, which reduce hormonal contraceptive efficacy). This history-taking step is where UKMEC contraindications are actually uncovered in a CPSA station, and examiners commonly embed one deliberately in the patient's answers — for example, a patient requesting the combined pill who, only when asked directly, mentions migraines with visual disturbance before the headache.

Presenting Options Through Informed Choice

Once relevant risk factors are established, the candidate should present the range of suitable methods neutrally, explaining mechanism, effectiveness, and realistic side effects for each, rather than steering the patient toward the clinician's own preferred option. Good practice is to briefly describe each broad category (combined hormonal, progestogen-only, LARC, barrier) in plain language, check the patient's reaction, and let their priorities — how much they mind an irregular bleeding pattern, whether they want something reversible immediately, whether they are averse to having anything fitted internally — guide which option gets explored in more depth. This is the single most heavily marked communication skill in a contraception CPSA station: genuine informed choice, not a lecture.

Addressing Myths and Misconceptions

Patients frequently raise specific worries that examiners expect to be addressed factually and reassuringly rather than dismissed. Common myths include the belief that the combined pill causes long-term infertility (fertility returns to baseline almost immediately after stopping, though the injection can delay return of fertility by several months), that a "pill-free break" is medically necessary rather than a historical convention (continuous or tricycling regimens are increasingly recommended and are not less safe), that intrauterine devices are unsuitable for women who have never had children (they are suitable and increasingly first-line for nulliparous women), and that emergency contraception is a form of abortion (it acts before implantation and does not disrupt an established pregnancy). Addressing a myth well means acknowledging why the patient may have heard it, correcting it clearly and kindly, and checking they feel reassured — rather than simply stating the correct fact and moving on.

Considering the Patient's Individual Circumstances

The best-performing candidates explicitly tailor the conversation to the patient in front of them rather than delivering a generic script. This means acknowledging lifestyle factors that affect adherence (a patient who frequently forgets tablets is a strong candidate for LARC), cultural or religious considerations the patient raises themselves, confidentiality concerns in a young person (applying Gillick competence and Fraser guidelines where relevant, and being explicit about the limits of confidentiality only where a genuine safeguarding concern exists), and the patient's own stated priorities — some patients prioritise cycle control, others prioritise avoiding any hormones, others prioritise "set and forget" convenience. Reflecting these priorities back ("It sounds like not having to remember a daily pill matters most to you") demonstrates the individualised, shared decision-making approach the CPSA is designed to test.

Closing Safely: Safety-Netting and Follow-Up

Every contraception counselling station should close with clear safety-netting: what to do if a method fails or is used incorrectly (missed pill rules, when emergency contraception is needed), what side effects would warrant returning sooner than a routine follow-up, and when routine follow-up should occur (for example, a follow-up appointment after starting a new method, or a check after an intrauterine device insertion). Checking the patient's understanding before ending — "Just to make sure I've explained things clearly, could you tell me in your own words what we've agreed?" — is a well-recognised communication technique examiners specifically look for and is easy to build into a closing routine.

Key UKMEC Contraindications Every Candidate Must Know

While the full UKMEC tables cover dozens of conditions, a small number of contraindications are tested so frequently in both the AKT and the CPSA that they should be committed to memory precisely, not approximately — treat this section as the non-negotiable core of contraception UKMLA preparation.

Migraine with Aura and Combined Hormonal Contraception

Migraine with aura is a UKMEC Category 4 contraindication to combined hormonal contraception at any age, because aura is associated with an increased background risk of ischaemic stroke, and CHC's oestrogen component further increases that risk. This applies regardless of how infrequent the migraines are or how long ago the patient last experienced aura, and it applies to all forms of CHC — pill, patch, and ring alike. Migraine without aura is a much less restrictive Category 2 below age 35, becoming Category 3 at age 35 or older. Progestogen-only methods and intrauterine devices are UKMEC Category 2 or 1 in patients with migraine with aura and remain entirely appropriate alternatives, which is why this contraindication is so often paired in AKT vignettes with "which method would you recommend instead" as the actual test of clinical reasoning.

Smoking and Age as a Combined Risk Factor

Smoking status and age interact to determine CHC eligibility in a specific, testable way: smoking of any amount is UKMEC Category 3 for CHC in women aged 35 or over, and smoking 15 or more cigarettes per day at age 35 or over is UKMEC Category 4 — an absolute contraindication. Below age 35, smoking status alone does not usually push CHC beyond Category 2, though the combination with other cardiovascular risk factors can. This age-and-quantity threshold is one of the most commonly misremembered UKMEC facts, and precision matters here: the exam distinguishes between "35 or over, any smoking" (Category 3) and "35 or over, heavy smoking" (Category 4).

Venous Thromboembolism, Obesity, and Cardiovascular Risk

A personal history of venous thromboembolism, whether provoked or unprovoked, is UKMEC Category 4 for CHC, as is known thrombophilia. A BMI of 35 or above is UKMEC Category 3 for CHC, reflecting a meaningfully elevated VTE risk that increases further with BMI above 40 (which some guidance treats as approaching Category 4 territory in combination with other risk factors). Uncontrolled hypertension (systolic 160 mmHg or above, or diastolic 100 mmHg or above) is UKMEC Category 4 for CHC, while well-controlled hypertension is typically Category 3. In all of these scenarios, progestogen-only and intrauterine methods again carry substantially fewer restrictions, reinforcing the exam's recurring pattern: know the CHC contraindication precisely, and know which non-oestrogen-containing alternative becomes the safe default.

Breastfeeding and the Early Postpartum Period

Combined hormonal contraception is UKMEC Category 4 in the first 6 weeks postpartum for breastfeeding women, reflecting both a theoretical effect on milk supply and an elevated postpartum VTE risk, before falling to Category 3 between 6 weeks and 6 months while breastfeeding continues. Progestogen-only methods, by contrast, can be started immediately postpartum regardless of breastfeeding status, and this is a common reason LARC or the progestogen-only pill are recommended for postpartum contraception discussions, which often occur alongside the topics covered in a dedicated guide to early pregnancy complications when postnatal care is being revised as a connected block.

Breast Cancer and Hormone-Sensitive Conditions

Current breast cancer is UKMEC Category 4 for all hormonal methods, including progestogen-only options and the hormonal coil, reflecting the hormone-sensitive nature of most breast cancers. A past history of breast cancer with no evidence of disease for 5 years is generally Category 3 rather than Category 4. This is a useful contrast to keep in mind against migraine with aura and smoking, both of which are CHC-specific contraindications — breast cancer is one of the few conditions that restricts progestogen-only and intrauterine hormonal methods as well, which is why the non-hormonal copper IUD remains an option even for this patient group.

Preparing for the CPSA Contraception Station: Practical Tips

Effective contraception UKMLA revision for the CPSA looks different from AKT revision, since rehearsal and delivery matter as much as recall. Candidates preparing specifically for a contraception CPSA station benefit from rehearsing the history-taking questions out loud until they feel natural rather than scripted, since a hesitant or visibly uncomfortable approach to questions about sexual history or smoking status is itself marked down, independent of clinical accuracy. It is worth preparing a mental checklist — age, smoking, migraine and aura specifically, personal and family VTE history, BMI, breastfeeding status, and current medications — so that the relevant UKMEC risk factors are elicited systematically rather than by chance. IMGs in particular should practise the UK's explicit, non-directive informed-choice model, since some training systems place more emphasis on the clinician recommending a single "best" method rather than presenting a neutral range of options; examiners specifically credit candidates who visibly hand the decision to the patient rather than steering it.

UK medical students, who are often clinically comfortable with the UKMEC facts from placements, should focus preparation time on the parts of the station that are easy to underrate: opening with a genuine invitation to set the agenda; explicitly naming and normalising sensitive questions before asking them ("I'm going to ask some routine questions about your health and lifestyle that we ask everyone in this kind of appointment"); and closing with clear safety-netting rather than ending abruptly once a method has been agreed. Both groups benefit from timing practice runs strictly, since a full history, options discussion, myth-busting, and safety-netting does not fit into a CPSA station's time allowance without a clear internal structure.

Frequently Asked Questions About Contraception in the UKMLA

The questions below cover the points candidates most commonly search for when revising contraception UKMLA content, phrased exactly as they tend to be asked, with self-contained answers.

What is the most important UKMEC contraindication to remember for the UKMLA?

Migraine with aura is the single most frequently tested UKMEC Category 4 contraindication to combined hormonal contraception, applying at any age and to the pill, patch, and ring alike, because of the elevated ischaemic stroke risk. The second most commonly tested contraindication is smoking 15 or more cigarettes a day at age 35 or over, which is also UKMEC Category 4 for combined hormonal methods. Both facts should be known precisely, including the exact age and cigarette thresholds, since AKT questions frequently test the boundary values directly.

How long after unprotected sex can emergency contraception be used?

Levonorgestrel emergency contraception is licensed for use up to 72 hours after unprotected sexual intercourse, ulipristal acetate up to 120 hours, and the copper intrauterine device up to 120 hours after intercourse or up to 5 days after the earliest likely date of ovulation, whichever is later. Effectiveness of the two oral methods decreases the longer the interval since intercourse, while the copper IUD remains highly effective throughout its licensed window and is the most effective option overall when a patient is a suitable candidate for insertion.

Which contraceptive methods are safest for a patient who smokes and is over 35?

Progestogen-only methods (the pill, injection, and implant) and intrauterine methods (copper IUD and hormonal IUS) carry no age-and-smoking restriction, since the elevated cardiovascular and thrombotic risk associated with smoking after age 35 relates specifically to the oestrogen component of combined hormonal contraception. A patient over 35 who smokes heavily should not be offered the combined pill, patch, or ring, and should instead be counselled on one of these oestrogen-free alternatives, with the contraceptive implant or an intrauterine method often recommended given their high effectiveness.

Does emergency contraception count as a form of abortion?

No — all three licensed forms of emergency contraception in the UK (levonorgestrel, ulipristal acetate, and the copper intrauterine device) act before implantation, either by delaying or inhibiting ovulation or by preventing fertilisation, and none of them disrupt an established pregnancy. This is an important point to state clearly and confidently during counselling, since patients sometimes raise this specific misconception and expect a direct, reassuring, factual answer rather than a vague response.

Can intrauterine devices be fitted in women who have never had children?

Yes — both the copper intrauterine device and the hormonal intrauterine system are suitable for nulliparous women, and current UK guidance actively supports offering them as first-line options regardless of whether a woman has previously given birth. The historical belief that intrauterine methods are unsuitable for nulliparous women is an outdated misconception that should be actively corrected during a contraception counselling conversation if a patient raises it.

How should a candidate approach a CPSA station where the patient wants a method that turns out to be contraindicated?

The candidate should take a focused history to establish why the requested method is contraindicated (for example, migraine with aura when a patient asks specifically for the combined pill), explain the risk clearly without alarming the patient, and then present suitable alternatives through the same informed-choice process used for any other consultation. Examiners mark this scenario on whether the candidate identifies the contraindication, explains it accurately, and still leaves the patient feeling a genuine, collaborative decision was reached, rather than simply being told what they cannot have.

What is the difference between UKMEC Category 3 and Category 4?

UKMEC Category 3 means the risks of a method generally outweigh its advantages for a given condition, so the method is not usually recommended unless no more suitable alternative is available or acceptable to the patient, and if used, requires careful follow-up. UKMEC Category 4 means the method poses an unacceptable health risk for that condition and must never be prescribed under any circumstances. This distinction — "avoid if possible" versus "never use" — is frequently the exact point an AKT question is testing when it presents a borderline risk factor.

Conclusion

Contraception UKMLA questions reward candidates who combine a precise understanding of the UKMEC framework with a genuinely patient-centred approach to counselling, since the exam tests both the clinical facts and the communication skill needed to apply them safely in a real consultation. Knowing that migraine with aura and heavy smoking after age 35 are UKMEC Category 4 contraindications to combined hormonal contraception, that emergency contraception timing windows run from 72 hours (levonorgestrel) to 120 hours (ulipristal acetate and the copper coil), and that progestogen-only and intrauterine methods sidestep most oestrogen-related risk, covers the highest-yield AKT content. Structuring a CPSA contraception station around agenda-setting, focused non-judgemental history-taking, neutral presentation of suitable options, myth-busting, and clear safety-netting covers the communication skill the station is actually designed to assess. Revising this topic alongside the wider women's health curriculum, rather than in isolation, is the most efficient route to genuine confidence in both papers.
