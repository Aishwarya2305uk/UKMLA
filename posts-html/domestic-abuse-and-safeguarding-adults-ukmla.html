Domestic abuse UKMLA questions sit at the intersection of clinical recognition, communication skill, and professional judgement, and they appear far more often across the Applied Knowledge Test and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment than most candidates expect. This guide goes beyond the broad legal framework covered in our general safeguarding revision guide and focuses specifically and deeply on recognising and responding to domestic abuse in adults — the enquiry techniques, the presentation patterns, the concept of coercive control, the documentation standards, and the referral pathways you are expected to know cold before you sit either exam. Whether you trained in the UK or are an international medical graduate preparing for registration, this is one of the professional-skills topics where examiners consistently reward candidates who show warmth, structure, and restraint in equal measure.

Domestic abuse is common, under-disclosed, and frequently missed in ordinary clinical encounters — which is precisely why the UKMLA tests it so heavily. Candidates are expected to know how to ask about it sensitively, how to recognise it when a patient does not use the words "abuse" or "violence" at all, how to document what they hear and see in a way that could later support a legal process, and how to connect a disclosure to the right specialist support without overstepping into an investigative role that does not belong to a doctor. This article works through each of those skills in turn, with the scenario-based detail that both the AKT and the CPSA reward.

Quick Answer: What the UKMLA Expects You to Know About Domestic Abuse

Domestic abuse UKMLA scenarios test whether you can ask sensitively, recognise abuse that does not look like a single violent incident, document accurately, and refer to the right specialist service — all without acting as an investigator or a judge of the relationship. In summary, a well-prepared candidate should be able to:

  • Use routine enquiry and targeted (selective) enquiry appropriately, always asking in private, without the suspected perpetrator present, and ideally with a professional interpreter rather than a family member if a language barrier exists
  • Recognise indirect presentation patterns — frequent attendances, vague or somatic symptoms, delayed presentation, and injuries inconsistent with the explanation given
  • Understand coercive control as a real, legally defined form of abuse in its own right, not merely a precursor to physical violence
  • Document findings accurately, non-judgementally, and contemporaneously, using the patient's own words wherever possible, because clinical records may later be used in criminal or family court proceedings
  • Know the referral pathway to an Independent Domestic Violence Advisor (IDVA) and the national helplines that exist specifically for this purpose
  • Understand the boundary of the doctor's role — recognise, document, and refer, rather than investigate, confront, or promise outcomes that are not within a clinician's control

Each of these areas is explored in depth below, with the level of clinical and legal precision the AKT and CPSA both expect.

Routine Enquiry and Targeted Enquiry: Asking the Question Well

Asking about domestic abuse well is a discrete clinical skill, and the UKMLA tests the mechanics of the conversation as closely as it tests the underlying facts. Two related but distinct approaches are described in UK safeguarding practice, and candidates are expected to be able to tell them apart.

Routine Enquiry

Routine enquiry means asking every patient, or every patient within a defined group (for example, all patients attending antenatal booking, or all new patients registering at a GP practice), a standard question about their safety at home, regardless of whether there is any specific reason to suspect abuse. The rationale is straightforward: domestic abuse is common and frequently invisible, and a patient who has never been asked directly may never volunteer the information unprompted. Maternity services in the UK have used routine enquiry at booking and subsequent antenatal contacts for years, precisely because pregnancy is a recognised period of elevated risk, and because the environment of a private antenatal appointment offers a rare, protected opportunity to ask without a partner necessarily being present.

Routine enquiry only works, however, if the conditions around it are right. Asking the question in front of a partner, a family member, or anyone else who is not a professional interpreter defeats the purpose entirely and can put the patient at real risk once they leave the appointment. This is why UKMLA CPSA stations that test routine enquiry almost always begin with the candidate needing to engineer a private moment — for example, sending a partner to the waiting room on a pretext, or asking to examine the patient alone as standard practice, before the safety question is ever asked.

Targeted (Selective) Enquiry

Targeted enquiry, sometimes called selective enquiry, means asking directly about domestic abuse when something in the presentation raises the clinician's suspicion — an injury that does not match the story given, a pattern of repeated attendances, a partner who insists on staying in the room and answering for the patient, or simply a gut sense that something is being left unsaid. Targeted enquiry is reactive rather than universal: it is prompted by a specific clinical cue rather than applied to every patient as a matter of course.

In practice, most UK guidance recommends a combination of both approaches: routine enquiry in specific high-yield settings (maternity, sexual health, mental health, substance misuse services), and targeted enquiry triggered by clinical suspicion in every other setting. UKMLA candidates should be comfortable identifying which approach a given scenario is testing and should never conflate the two — a question bank item describing a GP asking every new patient about home safety at registration is testing routine enquiry; one describing a doctor noticing a bruising pattern and then asking is testing targeted enquiry.

How to Ask: Technique Matters as Much as Content

The UKMLA rewards specific communication behaviours when a candidate asks about domestic abuse, whether in a CPSA station or in the reasoning behind an AKT single-best-answer option. The core technique includes:

  • Always ask in private, with the suspected perpetrator not present and, ideally, out of earshot — this may mean separating a patient from a partner, a family member, or even an adult child under a clinical pretext such as taking observations or arranging a scan
  • Use a professional interpreter, never a family member or the partner themselves, if there is a language barrier — a partner acting as interpreter can distort disclosure entirely and may itself be a marker of controlling behaviour
  • Normalise the question before asking it directly — for example, explaining that this is a question asked of all patients in this clinic, which reduces the sense of being singled out or accused of something
  • Use open, non-leading language — "How are things at home?" or "Do you ever feel frightened by anyone at home?" rather than closed questions that invite a reflexive "no"
  • Avoid appearing rushed or performative — patients are highly attuned to whether a question is being asked out of genuine concern or as a box-ticking exercise, and a rushed delivery reduces disclosure rates
  • Respond to disclosure without shock, judgement, or visible distress — a calm, validating response ("Thank you for telling me. I believe you, and this is not your fault") is what UKMLA mark schemes consistently reward in CPSA stations
  • Never promise confidentiality you cannot guarantee — be honest that information may need to be shared if there is a risk to life or if children are involved, while reassuring the patient that this will always be discussed with them first wherever possible

A frequent trap in AKT questions is an answer option describing a doctor confronting a suspected abusive partner directly, or promising the patient that "nothing will happen unless you want it to." Both are wrong: the first endangers the patient by tipping off the perpetrator, and the second is not strictly true, since certain risks (particularly to children or where there is a threat to life) may require action regardless of the patient's initial wishes. The correct answer nearly always centres on privacy, validation, and a calm offer of ongoing support.

Recognising Indirect Presentation: When Domestic Abuse Does Not Look Like Domestic Abuse

One of the most heavily tested skills in domestic abuse UKMLA content is pattern recognition — because most patients experiencing domestic abuse do not present saying "my partner hits me." They present with something else entirely, and the abuse only becomes apparent once a clinician notices a pattern across multiple contacts or looks past the presenting complaint.

Frequent and Unexplained Attendances

Patients experiencing domestic abuse often have a markedly higher rate of contact with health services than the general population, frequently for reasons that seem loosely connected or entirely unconnected to abuse. Repeated GP attendances for headaches, abdominal pain, back pain, or "just not feeling right," repeated attendances at the emergency department for minor injuries, and a pattern of cancelled or rearranged appointments can all be markers worth pausing on. No single attendance is diagnostic, but a doctor who notices a pattern across the records — five attendances in six months where none previously occurred — should treat that pattern itself as a clinical finding worth exploring, not simply as background noise to be worked around.

Vague or Medically Unexplained Symptoms

Chronic pain without a clear organic cause, persistent fatigue, non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms, headaches, and other presentations that resist a straightforward diagnostic label are recognised markers associated with experiences of abuse, including domestic abuse. This overlaps closely with the territory covered in our guide to assessing symptoms that do not fit a clear diagnostic pattern, and UKMLA candidates should hold both possibilities in mind simultaneously: a patient with unexplained symptoms may be experiencing a somatic symptom disorder, may be experiencing domestic abuse, or, not infrequently, both, since chronic stress and abuse are recognised contributors to somatic presentation. The correct clinical approach is not to choose one explanation prematurely but to keep the door open for either, including a private, sensitively asked question about safety at home as part of a full biopsychosocial assessment.

Injuries Inconsistent with the Explanation Given

As with non-accidental injury in children, an injury pattern that does not match the mechanism described is one of the clearest and most exam-relevant red flags in adult domestic abuse. Features that should raise concern include:

  • Bruising or injuries at multiple stages of healing, suggesting repeated rather than single episodes of harm
  • Injuries to areas typically covered by clothing (upper arms, torso, inner thighs) rather than the exposed limbs typical of accidental injury
  • A vague, inconsistent, or minimising account of how the injury occurred, or an account that shifts on repeated questioning
  • Delay between the injury occurring and the patient seeking medical attention, without a plausible reason for the delay
  • Defensive-pattern injuries — bruising or fractures to the forearms consistent with warding off blows

The Partner Who Will Not Leave the Room

A partner who insists on accompanying the patient into every part of the consultation, answers questions directed at the patient, minimises or explains away the patient's symptoms on their behalf, or becomes visibly agitated when asked to step out, is displaying a recognised behavioural marker of controlling behaviour. UKMLA CPSA mark schemes reward candidates who notice this dynamic and actively engineer a way to speak to the patient alone — for example, using a clinical justification such as needing to perform an intimate examination, take a set of observations privately, or discuss test results confidentially, rather than directly accusing the partner of anything, which would escalate risk without necessarily achieving a private conversation.

Non-Attendance and Disengagement

Equally important, and easy to overlook, is the opposite pattern: a patient who repeatedly misses appointments, disengages from follow-up, or becomes suddenly uncontactable after a period of frequent contact. This can represent a partner restricting access to healthcare, a patient's own withdrawal driven by shame or fear, or an escalation in risk that has made attending appointments unsafe. A missed appointment in a patient with a previous safeguarding concern on record should prompt active follow-up, not simply administrative closure of the referral.

Coercive Control: A Real, Defined Form of Abuse

A concept that UKMLA candidates are increasingly expected to understand in depth, rather than as a footnote to physical violence, is coercive control. Many candidates — particularly those trained in systems where domestic abuse is understood mainly in terms of physical assault — underestimate how heavily the exam now tests this non-physical dimension of abuse.

What Coercive Control Actually Is

Coercive control is a pattern of controlling, threatening, and manipulative behaviour used by one person to dominate and instil fear in another within an intimate or family relationship, without necessarily involving a single act of physical violence at all. It has been a specific criminal offence in England and Wales since 2015 under the Serious Crime Act, recognising that a sustained pattern of control can be as harmful, and in some cases as dangerous, as isolated physical assaults. Coercive control commonly includes:

  • Isolating the person from friends, family, and other sources of support
  • Monitoring their time, movements, and communications, including checking phones, restricting access to transport, or requiring the person to account for every minute away from home
  • Controlling financial resources, including withholding money, restricting access to bank accounts, or requiring receipts and justification for every purchase
  • Degradation and humiliation, both in private and in front of others, undermining self-worth and confidence over time
  • Threats — to the person themselves, to children, to pets, or to reveal private information — used to compel compliance
  • Regulating everyday behaviour, such as what the person wears, eats, or says, and who they are permitted to see

The defining feature of coercive control is not any single act, but the cumulative pattern: a relationship in which one partner's freedom, autonomy, and sense of self have been systematically eroded. This matters clinically because a patient experiencing coercive control may present with no visible injury at all, and a doctor looking only for bruises or fractures will miss it entirely.

Why Coercive Control Is Hard to Recognise Clinically

Coercive control is difficult to identify in a single consultation because its evidence is behavioural and relational rather than physical. Clues that should prompt further, gentle enquiry include a patient who seems to defer every decision to their partner, who appears anxious about the time (frequently checking a phone or clock, worried about "getting home"), who gives contradictory or overly rehearsed answers, or whose account of their own daily life sounds unusually restricted for no obvious reason. None of these signs is diagnostic in isolation, but together, and particularly when several appear at once, they should raise clinical suspicion in the same way that a cluster of red-flag symptoms would in a purely physical presentation.

Why This Matters for the UKMLA Specifically

Because coercive control is now a defined criminal offence and a defined concept in UK safeguarding and clinical guidance, the UKMLA treats it as core, examinable content rather than background context. AKT items may describe a patient with no physical injury at all, where the correct answer still involves recognising a pattern of controlling behaviour as a safeguarding concern warranting the same sensitive enquiry, documentation, and referral pathway as a case involving visible violence. Candidates who mentally equate "domestic abuse" with "physical assault" risk missing these questions entirely, so it is worth deliberately revising coercive control as its own topic rather than folding it into general physical abuse recognition.

Documentation Principles: Writing Records That Protect the Patient

Documentation is one of the most concretely testable skills within this topic, because the standard expected is precise, consistent, and directly examinable in both AKT single-best-answer questions and CPSA data-gathering or written-communication stations.

Why Documentation Standards Are So High in This Context

Clinical records made at the time of a domestic abuse disclosure or examination may later be used as evidence in criminal proceedings, family court proceedings (including child arrangements and non-molestation orders), or a coroner's inquest in the most serious cases. A record that is vague, judgemental, speculative, or incomplete can weaken a legal case months or years later, long after the clinician's own memory of the consultation has faded. This is why the standard for documentation in this area is deliberately higher than for a routine clinical note.

Core Principles of Good Documentation

  • Be accurate and specific. Record exact measurements, locations, and descriptions of any injury (for example, "3cm x 2cm bruise, purple, over the left forearm" rather than "some bruising"), rather than vague summaries
  • Use the patient's own words wherever possible, in quotation marks, rather than paraphrasing or interpreting what they meant — "He grabbed my arm and wouldn't let go" is far more useful as a contemporaneous record than a clinician's summarised version of the same disclosure
  • Be non-judgemental and factual. Record what was seen, heard, and done, not opinions about the patient's choices, the credibility of their account, or predictions about the relationship. Phrases implying disbelief, blame, or moral judgement have no place in a clinical record and can actively undermine a later legal process
  • Record who was present during the consultation and, separately, during any private conversation — this can matter significantly if a partner's presence or absence is later relevant to a court's understanding of the circumstances
  • Document contemporaneously — write the record at the time or as close to the time as possible, since a note written days later, from memory, carries far less evidential weight and is more easily challenged
  • Use a body map or diagram for injuries where appropriate, in addition to written description, and photograph injuries with the patient's informed consent and in line with local information governance policy
  • Record the safety plan and referral actions taken, including what information was given to the patient, what services were discussed, and what the patient consented to (or did not consent to) in terms of onward referral
  • Avoid destructive or dismissive shorthand — abbreviations or throwaway phrases that might read as flippant in a legal context (for example, casual slang) should never appear in a safeguarding-related entry

Documenting Capacity and Consent Discussions

Where a patient with capacity chooses not to pursue a referral or not to leave a relationship, this should be documented clearly and neutrally: that the risks were discussed, that information about support services was provided, that the patient made an informed choice, and that the door remains open for future contact. This protects both the patient's autonomy and the clinician, and it mirrors the standard expected in the broader capacity and consent framework covered in our dedicated guide to assessing whether a patient has the capacity to make a specific decision. It is worth being explicit in the record that respecting a patient's decision is not the same as taking no action — offering information, providing safety planning advice, and leaving a route back into support all count as active clinical steps, and should be recorded as such.

Safety Planning and Referral Pathways: IDVA Services and National Helplines

Recognising domestic abuse and documenting it well are necessary but not sufficient — the UKMLA also expects candidates to know what happens next, and specifically which services exist to support a patient once a disclosure has been made.

Safety Planning: What It Actually Involves

A safety plan is a practical, individualised plan developed with the patient (never imposed on them) that addresses immediate and ongoing safety. Basic elements a clinician can discuss even without specialist safeguarding training include:

  • Whether the patient feels safe to return home today, and if not, whether they have somewhere else to go
  • Whether children in the household are safe, which may trigger a separate safeguarding referral in its own right
  • Simple practical measures — keeping a phone charged and accessible, knowing a safe exit route from the home, agreeing a code word with a trusted friend or family member to signal that help is needed
  • Ensuring the patient knows how to access emergency services (999) if there is an immediate threat to life
  • Providing information about specialist services discreetly — for example, a small card or a note on a phone rather than a visible leaflet if a partner might check the patient's belongings

Safety planning in detail is properly the role of a trained specialist, not something a doctor is expected to complete alone in a ten-minute consultation — which is precisely why referral to specialist services is the pivotal next step.

The Independent Domestic Violence Advisor (IDVA)

An Independent Domestic Violence Advisor (IDVA) is a trained specialist who works specifically with people at high risk of harm from domestic abuse, providing tailored safety planning, emotional support, and practical help navigating the criminal justice system, housing services, and civil protection orders. IDVAs are the specialist link between a clinical disclosure and the wider system of protection and support that exists outside the health service. In UKMLA terms, once a doctor has recognised a domestic abuse concern, made the patient safe in the immediate consultation, and documented appropriately, referral to (or discussion of) IDVA support is usually the single most important next action tested in exam scenarios, alongside completion of a validated risk assessment where the clinician is trained to do so.

Referral pathways to an IDVA vary by local area but commonly run through a local domestic abuse charity, a hospital-based Independent Domestic Violence Advisor service (many acute trusts now employ IDVAs directly within the emergency department or maternity service), or a referral from the GP or specialist safeguarding team. UKMLA candidates are not expected to memorise local referral forms, but are expected to know that IDVA involvement exists as a distinct, specialist step beyond a doctor's own consultation, and to select it correctly among answer options in preference to vaguer alternatives such as "advise the patient to contact the police" as a first-line, universal response.

National Helplines and Signposting

Doctors are expected to know that dedicated, confidential national support exists and to be able to point a patient toward it, even without memorising every regional service. The National Domestic Abuse Helpline, run in partnership between Refuge and Women's Aid, provides free, confidential, 24-hour support, advice, and safety planning for anyone experiencing domestic abuse, and can also advise on next steps for concerned friends, family, or professionals. The UK government maintains an official directory of national and regional support services, which is a useful reference for candidates who want to understand the full landscape of helplines, apps, and safe spaces available to patients across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. Providing this kind of signposting information, discreetly and without pressure, is a core, testable action in both AKT and CPSA scenarios — the correct answer is very rarely "do nothing until the patient asks for help unprompted," and very rarely "take control of the situation and act without the patient's knowledge unless there is an immediate risk to life or a child."

When Urgent Escalation Is Required Regardless of Consent

While respecting patient autonomy is a consistent theme throughout this topic, the UKMLA also expects candidates to recognise the narrow set of circumstances where action must be taken regardless of the patient's wishes: an immediate and serious risk to life, a child in the household who may be at risk of harm (which triggers the child safeguarding pathway discussed in our broader safeguarding revision guide), or a patient who lacks capacity to make an informed decision about their own safety. Outside these narrow circumstances, the correct approach is to offer information and support, respect the patient's autonomy, and leave the door open for future contact, rather than overriding their wishes.

The Doctor's Role and Its Limits: Recognise, Document, Refer — Not Investigate

A recurring examiner trap in domestic abuse UKMLA questions is an answer option that casts the doctor as an investigator, an interrogator, or a decision-maker about the relationship itself. Understanding exactly where a clinician's role ends is as important as understanding where it begins.

What Is Within the Doctor's Remit

  • Recognising the clinical and behavioural markers of domestic abuse, including coercive control, indirect presentation, and injuries inconsistent with explanation
  • Asking sensitively, in private, using routine or targeted enquiry as appropriate to the setting
  • Assessing immediate safety, including whether the patient (and any children) can safely return home
  • Documenting accurately, factually, and contemporaneously, in a way that could support future legal proceedings without editorialising
  • Referring to specialist services — IDVA support, social care where children are involved, mental health services where appropriate, and the police only with the patient's consent unless there is an overriding duty to act
  • Providing information about national and local support services, discreetly and without pressure

What Is Outside the Doctor's Remit

  • Acting as an investigator — a doctor's role is not to determine, beyond reasonable doubt, whether abuse occurred, gather forensic evidence as a police officer would, or cross-examine a patient's account for inconsistencies in the way a legal process might
  • Confronting the suspected perpetrator directly, which can escalate danger to the patient without achieving any protective benefit
  • Promising specific legal or criminal justice outcomes — a doctor cannot guarantee that a perpetrator will be prosecuted, that an order will be granted, or that a report will lead to any particular result
  • Making unilateral decisions about a capacitous adult's relationship — a patient with capacity retains the right to remain in a relationship a clinician considers unsafe, and the doctor's role is to inform, support, and document, not to compel a particular choice
  • Breaching confidentiality routinely — information should only be shared without consent in the narrow circumstances described above, and any such decision must be clearly justified and documented

This distinction — recognise, document, refer, rather than investigate, confront, or decide — is one of the most consistently rewarded pieces of professional judgement across UKMLA safeguarding content generally, and it aligns directly with the standards set out in the professional duties every doctor is expected to meet, which frame safeguarding action as part of a doctor's wider duty of candour and care rather than a separate specialism bolted onto clinical practice.

Bringing It Together: A Worked Domestic Abuse Scenario

A 29-year-old woman attends the emergency department for the fourth time this year, on this occasion with a wrist injury she says happened when she "tripped on the stairs." Her partner, who has accompanied her, answers most of your questions before she can, and becomes visibly irritated when you ask him to wait outside while you examine her wrist. Once alone, you ask gently how things are at home. She hesitates, then says her partner "gets angry sometimes" and that she has to "check with him" before making plans with friends. She has no visible bruising beyond the wrist injury, and the account of the fall is plausible on its own.

This scenario is designed to test several skills at once. First, targeted enquiry was triggered appropriately by the pattern of frequent attendance and the partner's controlling behaviour in the consultation, not by any single dramatic red flag. Second, the disclosure describes coercive control — restricted social contact, a partner's anger used as a controlling mechanism — rather than a single violent incident, and this still meets the threshold for a domestic abuse safeguarding response even without visible bruising elsewhere. Third, the correct next steps are to thank her for disclosing, assess her immediate safety, ask about any children in the household, discuss IDVA referral and the National Domestic Abuse Helpline, and document the conversation factually and in her own words — not to challenge her account of the stair fall, confront the partner, or insist she leave the relationship immediately. A well-prepared candidate should be able to walk through each of these steps fluently, in a CPSA station with an actor or in the reasoning behind an AKT answer option.

Frequently Asked Questions About Domestic Abuse and the UKMLA

What is the difference between routine enquiry and targeted enquiry for domestic abuse?

Routine enquiry means asking every patient (or every patient in a defined high-risk group, such as antenatal attendees) a standard question about safety at home, regardless of any specific suspicion. Targeted enquiry means asking directly because something in the presentation — an inconsistent injury, a controlling partner, a pattern of frequent attendance — has raised clinical suspicion. Both approaches require the same core conditions: privacy, no suspected perpetrator present, and a calm, non-judgemental manner.

Is coercive control considered a form of domestic abuse if there is no physical violence?

Yes. Coercive control is a recognised and legally defined pattern of controlling, threatening, or manipulative behaviour within an intimate or family relationship, and it has been a specific criminal offence in England and Wales since 2015, entirely separate from any requirement to prove physical assault. Clinically, a patient can be experiencing serious, high-risk abuse with no visible injury at all, which is why UKMLA scenarios increasingly test recognition of controlling behaviour patterns rather than bruising alone.

Why is documentation so important in domestic abuse cases?

Clinical records made at the time of a disclosure or examination may later be used as evidence in criminal proceedings, family court proceedings, or an inquest, sometimes months or years after the consultation itself. Accurate, factual, non-judgemental, and contemporaneous documentation — using the patient's own words, precise descriptions of any injury, and a clear record of who was present — protects the patient's later access to justice and protects the clinician's own professional position.

What is an IDVA and when should a patient be referred to one?

An Independent Domestic Violence Advisor (IDVA) is a trained specialist who provides safety planning, emotional support, and practical help navigating the criminal justice system, housing, and civil protection orders for people experiencing domestic abuse, particularly those at higher risk. Once a doctor has recognised a domestic abuse concern, ensured immediate safety, and documented the consultation, referral to or discussion of IDVA support is typically the single most important next step, rather than a doctor attempting to manage the situation entirely alone.

Should a doctor call the police if a patient discloses domestic abuse but does not want them involved?

Generally, no — a capacitous adult's wishes about police involvement should be respected, and the correct approach is to offer information, support, and referral pathways such as an IDVA or the National Domestic Abuse Helpline. The exception is where there is an immediate and serious risk to life, where children in the household may be at risk of harm, or where the patient lacks the capacity to make an informed decision about their own safety — in these narrower circumstances, escalation may be required regardless of the patient's initial preference.

How should a doctor respond if the suspected abusive partner is present in the consultation?

The priority is to find a legitimate, low-suspicion reason to speak to the patient alone — for example, an intimate examination, a private discussion of results, or routine observations taken separately — rather than confronting the partner directly or asking about domestic abuse with them present. Confronting a suspected perpetrator, or asking sensitive questions within their earshot, can significantly increase risk to the patient once they leave the clinical setting.

Does a doctor need to investigate whether abuse actually occurred before referring a patient for support?

No. A doctor's role is to recognise the clinical and behavioural indicators of possible domestic abuse, ask sensitively, assess immediate safety, document accurately, and refer to specialist services — not to establish proof, gather forensic evidence, or determine guilt, which are functions of the police and courts, not of clinical practice. Referral to an IDVA or specialist safeguarding team should follow reasonable clinical suspicion, not a certainty that abuse has definitely taken place.

Conclusion: Preparing Confidently for Domestic Abuse Questions in the UKMLA

Domestic abuse UKMLA content rewards candidates who can combine clinical pattern recognition with genuine communication skill and clear professional boundaries. Practise recognising indirect presentations, rehearse the language of sensitive, private enquiry, understand coercive control as a distinct and serious form of abuse in its own right, internalise the standard expected of contemporaneous, non-judgemental documentation, and know exactly which specialist services — IDVA support and national helplines chief among them — exist to take a disclosure forward once you have made it safely.
