Anaesthesia UKMLA questions do not expect candidates to think like an anaesthetist — they expect a foundation doctor's working knowledge of what happens to a patient before, during, and after an operation. This guide sets out exactly that: the pre-operative assessment basics every junior doctor must apply on a surgical ward, the conceptual difference between local, regional, and general anaesthesia, the common perioperative complications a foundation doctor is called to assess out of hours, and the safety systems (fasting rules, the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist, venous thromboembolism prophylaxis) that keep patients safe around the time of surgery. Both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) are tested on this content, because it sits at the interface of medicine and surgery — exactly where foundation doctors spend much of their working lives.

Perioperative medicine is a deliberately broad UKMLA content area. It is not filed under "anaesthetics" as a specialty in the way cardiology or respiratory medicine are; instead, it is woven through surgical presentations, prescribing safety, and patient safety questions precisely because every foundation doctor — regardless of eventual specialty — will spend time covering surgical wards, clerking patients before theatre, and being the first call when something goes wrong afterwards. This guide is structured to mirror that reality.

Quick Answer: What Does Anaesthesia UKMLA Content Actually Test?

Anaesthesia UKMLA questions test a foundation doctor's ability to assess a patient's fitness for surgery, manage their regular medications safely around the operation, recognise which broad category of anaesthesia (local, regional, or general) is being described, and identify and initially manage common postoperative complications such as hypotension, delirium, nausea and vomiting, and venous thromboembolism (VTE). It also tests familiarity with the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist and basic fasting rules before an anaesthetic.

Crucially, the exam does not test anaesthetic drug dosing calculations, airway device selection, or regional block technique — that content belongs to anaesthetic specialty training, not the generalist AKT. Where the UKMLA does test anaesthesia-adjacent pharmacology, it tends to overlap with the separate pharmacology and prescribing safety content area, particularly around opioid analgesia, antiemetics, and anticoagulant management. Candidates who try to over-prepare technical anaesthetic knowledge often under-prepare the practical, ward-based judgement questions that make up the bulk of real anaesthesia UKMLA marks.

The remainder of this guide works through each testable component in the order a patient would actually experience it: assessment before surgery, the anaesthetic itself at a conceptual level, and then the complications and safety systems that follow.

Pre-Operative Assessment: What a Foundation Doctor Must Check

Pre-operative assessment is the single highest-yield component of anaesthesia UKMLA questions — arguably the anaesthesia UKMLA topic most likely to appear as a standalone AKT item — because foundation doctors are frequently the ones clerking patients on a pre-assessment or surgical admissions unit. The goal of pre-operative assessment is to identify and, where possible, optimise anything that increases the risk of anaesthesia or surgery before the patient reaches theatre.

A structured pre-operative assessment covers: a full medical history (with particular attention to cardiorespiratory disease, diabetes, and previous anaesthetic problems), a drug history (including over-the-counter medicines, herbal remedies, recreational substance use, and allergies), a focused examination (airway, cardiovascular, and respiratory systems), and baseline investigations tailored to the patient's comorbidities and the magnitude of surgery planned. A history of previous anaesthetic complications — such as malignant hyperthermia in a family member, difficult intubation, or severe postoperative nausea and vomiting — must always be documented and flagged clearly, since these substantially change anaesthetic planning even though the foundation doctor will not be the one making that plan. Where a patient's history raises a suspicion of substance misuse or an unclear overdose risk, the assessment overlaps with the site's separate toxicology and poisoning content, since withdrawal risk and drug interactions both need to be anticipated before anaesthesia is planned.

ASA Grading: The Standard Fitness Classification

The American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) Physical Status Classification is the standard system used across UK anaesthetic practice to summarise a patient's baseline fitness before surgery. It is a simple, ordinal 1–6 scale, and UKMLA questions commonly present a short clinical vignette and ask the candidate to assign the correct ASA grade. Higher ASA grades correlate strongly with increased perioperative morbidity and mortality, and the grade itself often informs decisions about where a patient should be cared for after surgery (ward, high-dependency unit, or intensive care).

ASA GradeDefinitionExample
ASA IA normal healthy patientFit, non-smoking young adult with no systemic disease
ASA IIA patient with mild systemic disease, no functional limitationWell-controlled hypertension or type 2 diabetes; mild obesity; current smoker with no other disease
ASA IIIA patient with severe systemic disease that limits function but is not immediately life-threateningPoorly controlled diabetes with complications; stable angina; moderate COPD; BMI ≥40
ASA IVA patient with severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to lifeUnstable angina, recent (<3 months) myocardial infarction, severe valvular dysfunction, sepsis
ASA VA moribund patient not expected to survive without the operationRuptured abdominal aortic aneurysm; massive trauma with multi-organ dysfunction
ASA VIA declared brain-dead patient whose organs are being removed for donationOrgan retrieval surgery

An "E" suffix (e.g. ASA IIIE) denotes emergency surgery, which independently increases risk regardless of the underlying ASA grade. Foundation doctors should note that ASA grading is a global assessment of comorbidity, not a measure of the difficulty of the operation itself — a fit ASA I patient can still face a technically difficult, high-risk operation, and a frail ASA IV patient may undergo a comparatively minor procedure. Both dimensions (patient fitness and surgical magnitude) are considered together when planning perioperative care.

Fasting Guidelines Before Anaesthesia

Fasting rules exist to reduce the risk of pulmonary aspiration of gastric contents during induction of anaesthesia, when protective airway reflexes are temporarily lost. The standard UK guidance, often summarised as the "2-4-6" rule, is one of the most frequently tested individual facts within anaesthesia UKMLA questions because it is precise, easily examined, and directly relevant to ward-level prescribing and theatre-list planning.

Intake typeMinimum fasting time before elective anaesthesia
Clear fluids (water, black tea/coffee, ice lollies)2 hours
Breast milk4 hours
Formula milk, other milk, and a light meal6 hours

In practice, most UK hospitals allow clear fluids up to 2 hours before surgery to reduce thirst and dehydration, while solids and non-clear fluids are stopped at least 6 hours beforehand. Foundation doctors are frequently asked to write "nil by mouth" (NBM) instructions on a drug chart and must therefore understand these timings well enough to counsel patients and avoid unnecessarily prolonged, harmful fasting — a recognised cause of dehydration, hypoglycaemia, and patient dissatisfaction, particularly in children, older patients, and people with diabetes. Emergency surgery is different: patients are frequently not adequately fasted, and this risk is instead managed anaesthetically (for example, with a rapid sequence induction) rather than by delaying urgent surgery.

Medication Management Before Surgery

Deciding which regular medications to continue, adjust, or stop before an operation is one of the most practically important tasks a foundation doctor performs on a surgical pre-assessment clinic or admissions ward, and it is tested heavily in anaesthesia UKMLA scenarios — this is arguably the single most exam-relevant anaesthesia UKMLA prescribing skill of all. The general principle is straightforward even though the individual drug rules require memorisation: continue medications whose sudden withdrawal is dangerous, and pause medications whose continuation increases bleeding, metabolic, or interaction risk during surgery.

MedicationTypical perioperative actionReason
WarfarinStop around 5 days before major surgery; check INR; bridge with LMWH if high thrombotic risk (e.g. mechanical heart valve)Reduce bleeding risk while covering thrombotic risk during the withdrawal window
DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran)Stop 24–72 hours before, depending on agent, renal function, and bleeding risk of surgeryShorter half-life than warfarin; bridging usually unnecessary

ACE inhibitors / ARBsFrequently omitted on the morning of surgeryCombined with anaesthetic vasodilation, they can cause pronounced intraoperative hypotension

Two points are worth stressing for exam purposes. First, the guiding logic (bleeding risk versus withdrawal risk versus metabolic risk) is more examinable than any single cut-off number, since exact stop-times for individual anticoagulants are periodically revised by local trusts and national guidance. Second, patients with diabetes require particularly careful perioperative planning, since both prolonged fasting and disrupted medication regimens can rapidly destabilise glucose control — this is one of the areas where anaesthesia UKMLA content deliberately overlaps with the broader prescribing safety curriculum, since a poorly timed insulin dose written on the drug chart is a classic prescribing error scenario. Foundation doctors should always verify current local policy and, where any doubt exists, discuss anticoagulant or diabetes medication changes with the anaesthetic or surgical team rather than acting alone — a habit that also reduces the kind of avoidable prescribing error covered in the site's dedicated patient safety guide.

Both UK medical students and IMGs should note that while the underlying pharmacological principles are internationally consistent, exact perioperative drug protocols (fasting-before-stopping windows, bridging thresholds, specific agents preferred) can differ between the UK and other health systems. IMGs who trained or practised elsewhere should specifically revise UK-based perioperative drug management rather than assuming their home-country protocol applies unchanged, since the AKT tests UK clinical practice.

Local, Regional, and General Anaesthesia: The Conceptual Basics

This anaesthesia UKMLA section tests recognition of the broad category of anaesthesia being used in a clinical scenario, not the technical performance of that anaesthetic. A foundation doctor needs to understand what each type is, roughly when it is used, and what its principal risks are — enough to interpret a patient's anaesthetic record, anticipate likely complications, and communicate sensibly with the anaesthetic team, rather than enough to administer any of them.

Local Anaesthesia

Local anaesthesia is the injection or application of an anaesthetic agent (such as lidocaine or bupivacaine) directly into a small, defined area of tissue to block sensation in that specific location while the patient remains fully conscious throughout. It is used for minor procedures — suturing a wound, excising a skin lesion, or draining a small abscess — and is something foundation doctors themselves administer regularly, unlike regional or general anaesthesia. The principal risks are local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST), which occurs if the agent enters the bloodstream in excessive quantity, and simple allergic reactions. LAST presents with perioral tingling, tinnitus, and a metallic taste progressing, in severe cases, to seizures and cardiovascular collapse; management includes stopping the injection immediately, supportive resuscitation, and intravenous lipid emulsion in severe cases. Maximum safe doses (typically quoted per kilogram of body weight, and lower with adrenaline-free preparations) are a commonly tested numerical fact.

Regional Anaesthesia

Regional anaesthesia numbs a larger region of the body by blocking nerve transmission at a point proximal to the surgical site, while the patient may remain awake, sedated, or occasionally have this combined with a general anaesthetic. The two forms most relevant to foundation doctors are neuraxial techniques (spinal and epidural anaesthesia) and peripheral nerve blocks (for example, a femoral nerve block for a hip fracture). Spinal anaesthesia involves a single injection of local anaesthetic into the cerebrospinal fluid in the subarachnoid space, producing a dense, relatively short-lived block — commonly used for caesarean section and lower-limb orthopaedic surgery. Epidural anaesthesia involves a catheter placed in the epidural space, allowing continuous or repeated dosing, and is widely used for labour analgesia and for prolonged postoperative pain control after major abdominal or thoracic surgery.

Foundation doctors are frequently the first call for complications of regional anaesthesia on the ward, and this is a genuinely high-yield area of anaesthesia UKMLA content. Key complications to recognise include:

  • Post-dural puncture headache — a characteristic headache that is significantly worse when upright and improves when lying flat, caused by cerebrospinal fluid leakage after a spinal or inadvertent dural puncture during an epidural attempt; managed conservatively with fluids, analgesia, and caffeine, or with an epidural blood patch if severe or persistent.
  • Epidural haematoma — a rare but neurosurgical emergency presenting with worsening back pain, progressive leg weakness, or new sphincter disturbance after an epidural or spinal, particularly in a patient who is anticoagulated; requires urgent MRI and neurosurgical review, since delayed decompression risks permanent paralysis.
  • High or total spinal block — local anaesthetic spreading higher than intended, causing progressive hypotension, bradycardia, and, in the most severe cases, respiratory compromise or loss of consciousness; requires immediate escalation to anaesthetics.
  • Urinary retention — a common, usually benign consequence of a neuraxial block temporarily affecting bladder sensation and detrusor function; managed with catheterisation until the block wears off.

General Anaesthesia

General anaesthesia is a reversible, drug-induced state of unconsciousness in which the patient has no awareness of, and typically no memory of, the surgical procedure. It has three widely recognised conceptual components — hypnosis (unconsciousness), analgesia (pain control), and muscle relaxation (where surgically required) — often described together as the "triad of anaesthesia." Some regimens also add a fourth component, control of the autonomic stress response to surgery. Foundation doctors do not need to know induction agents, muscle relaxants, or ventilator settings in detail for the AKT, but should understand the practical sequence: induction (the patient is rendered unconscious, usually with an intravenous agent), maintenance (usually with an inhaled volatile agent or continued intravenous infusion, plus ongoing analgesia and, if required, muscle relaxation), and emergence (anaesthetic agents are stopped and the patient regains consciousness and protective airway reflexes).

The key general-anaesthesia risks a foundation doctor should recognise on the ward afterwards include airway complications during emergence (such as laryngospasm), aspiration if fasting rules were not followed or in an emergency setting, malignant hyperthermia (a rare but life-threatening hypermetabolic reaction to certain anaesthetic agents in genetically susceptible individuals, presenting with rapidly rising temperature, muscle rigidity, and tachycardia, managed with dantrolene and immediate cessation of the triggering agent), and the residual sedative and respiratory-depressant effects that make careful postoperative monitoring essential. Patients who have had a general anaesthetic — particularly older patients, those with pre-existing cognitive impairment, and those undergoing longer operations — are also at higher risk of postoperative delirium, covered in detail below.

Choosing between these three broad categories is a joint decision between the anaesthetist, surgeon, and patient, weighing the nature and duration of surgery, patient comorbidity and preference, and anaesthetic risk — but a foundation doctor's job is to understand the choice that was made well enough to anticipate its likely complications on the ward, not to make that choice.

Common Postoperative Complications: What a Foundation Doctor Is Called to Assess

The majority of "urgent" bleeps a foundation doctor receives on a surgical ward relate to a small number of recurring postoperative problems, and this is where anaesthesia UKMLA content most directly maps onto day-to-day clinical practice. These questions test the ability to systematically assess each presentation, generate a sensible differential diagnosis, and identify which findings mandate immediate escalation rather than simple observation.

Postoperative Hypotension

Postoperative hypotension is a fall in blood pressure below the patient's normal baseline (or an absolute systolic blood pressure that triggers a ward early warning score) occurring in the hours to days following surgery. It is one of the most common reasons a foundation doctor is called to a surgical ward and always deserves a structured assessment rather than a reflexive fluid bolus, since the underlying cause dictates the correct treatment.

A useful way to think about postoperative hypotension is by broad mechanism:

  • Hypovolaemia — from ongoing bleeding (concealed or revealed), inadequate fluid replacement, or third-space losses; look for tachycardia, cool peripheries, reduced urine output, falling haemoglobin, and check the drain output and wound for signs of active bleeding.
  • Sepsis — from an evolving surgical site infection, anastomotic leak, or unrelated infective source; look for fever (or hypothermia in severe sepsis), tachycardia, raised or very low white cell count, and rising lactate.
  • Cardiac causes — myocardial infarction, arrhythmia, or fluid overload precipitating heart failure, particularly in patients with existing cardiac disease; an ECG and troponin are usually warranted if this is suspected.
  • Residual anaesthetic or analgesic effect — including the vasodilatory effect of an epidural or spinal block, or opioid-related hypotension; check the epidural infusion rate and sensory block level if relevant.
  • Anaphylaxis — rare, but should be considered if hypotension is accompanied by rash, wheeze, or angioedema shortly after a new drug or blood product.

Initial assessment always follows an ABCDE approach: check airway and breathing, assess perfusion and heart rate, examine the wound and drains, review the fluid balance chart and most recent bloods, and look specifically for concealed haemorrhage (which can be substantial in abdominal or pelvic surgery without any external sign). A fluid challenge (typically 250–500 mL of crystalloid) with reassessment of response is a reasonable initial step in a haemodynamically unstable but not critically unwell patient, but persistent or recurrent hypotension, especially with a falling haemoglobin or rising lactate, requires urgent senior surgical and/or critical care review — this is not a problem to manage repeatedly at foundation-doctor level alone.

Postoperative Confusion and Delirium

Postoperative delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness that develops within days of surgery and is extremely common, particularly in older patients, those with pre-existing cognitive impairment, and after emergency or prolonged operations. It is a genuine medical emergency in the sense that it always has an underlying, often treatable, cause and carries a real risk of missed serious pathology if simply attributed to "just being confused after the anaesthetic."

Delirium can be hyperactive (agitated, restless, sometimes aggressive — more likely to be recognised) or hypoactive (withdrawn, drowsy, quiet — more likely to be missed, and associated with a worse prognosis if unrecognised). A validated screening tool such as the 4AT is commonly used on UK wards to formally identify delirium. The UKMLA expects candidates to generate a broad differential for new postoperative confusion rather than defaulting immediately to "anaesthetic effect," which is rarely the correct final answer in an exam vignette. Common and important causes include:

  • Hypoxia (check oxygen saturations and arterial blood gas)
  • Infection, especially urinary tract infection or an evolving surgical site or chest infection
  • Metabolic derangement (hyponatraemia, hypoglycaemia, uraemia, hypercalcaemia)
  • Drugs, particularly opioids, benzodiazepines, and anticholinergics
  • Alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal in a patient with an unrecognised dependence
  • Urinary retention or severe constipation ("faecal impaction")
  • Pain that is poorly controlled or, conversely, over-sedation from analgesia
  • Unfamiliar environment and sleep deprivation, compounding an underlying vulnerability

Management centres on identifying and treating the reversible cause, alongside supportive, non-pharmacological measures: reorientation, ensuring the patient has their glasses and hearing aids, minimising ward moves, promoting normal sleep, and involving family or carers where possible. Sedative medication is reserved for patients who are a genuine risk to themselves or others and cannot be managed with reassurance and de-escalation alone, and antipsychotics are used cautiously and at the lowest effective dose given their own risk profile in older patients. Restraint and unnecessary sedation are actively avoided wherever possible, consistent with the same dignity-and-safety principles that run through the site's broader patient safety content.

Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV) is extremely common, affecting a substantial proportion of surgical patients, and is one of the most frequently tested "everyday" complications in anaesthesia UKMLA questions because it directly tests antiemetic pharmacology alongside basic risk-factor recognition. Well-established risk factors are often summarised using the Apfel score, which counts four independent predictors: female sex, a history of motion sickness or previous PONV, non-smoking status, and the use of postoperative opioids — the more of these present, the higher the predicted risk, and the more strongly prophylactic antiemetics are indicated before surgery even begins.

Beyond drug treatment, non-pharmacological measures reduce both the incidence and severity of PONV: minimising perioperative opioid use through multimodal analgesia, adequate but not excessive intravenous fluids, and avoiding unnecessary prolonged fasting. Persistent vomiting after surgery should never be assumed to be routine PONV without considering other causes, particularly bowel obstruction or ileus after abdominal surgery, raised intracranial pressure after neurosurgery, or an adverse drug reaction — a foundation doctor's job is to recognise when "just PONV" stops being a safe assumption and escalate accordingly.

Venous Thromboembolism Prophylaxis in Surgical Patients

No anaesthesia UKMLA revision guide is complete without a firm grasp of venous thromboembolism (VTE) prevention. VTE — encompassing deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE) — is one of the most preventable causes of death and morbidity in hospitalised surgical patients, and VTE risk assessment is now a mandatory, formally documented part of every surgical admission in the NHS. This makes it one of the most consistently examinable parts of anaesthesia UKMLA and broader perioperative content, since it combines risk assessment, prescribing, and safety-netting in a single, well-defined pathway.

UK practice follows the NICE guideline NG89 on venous thromboembolism in over 16s, which sets out a structured approach: every patient admitted to hospital should have an individualised assessment of VTE risk and bleeding risk within 24 hours of admission, using a validated tool, and this assessment should be revisited whenever the clinical situation changes. Foundation doctors are frequently the ones completing this assessment on admission and prescribing the resulting prophylaxis, making it directly relevant day-to-day ward practice as well as exam content.

Mechanical Prophylaxis

Mechanical measures reduce venous stasis without any bleeding risk, and are used in essentially all surgical patients unless contraindicated (for example, by severe peripheral arterial disease). They include correctly fitted anti-embolism stockings (thigh- or knee-length) and intermittent pneumatic compression devices, typically applied before surgery and continued until the patient is fully mobile.

Pharmacological Prophylaxis

Pharmacological prophylaxis, usually a low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) such as enoxaparin or dalteparin, is added for patients at moderate-to-high VTE risk once bleeding risk has been assessed as acceptable. It is typically started some hours after surgery (rather than immediately before or during, to reduce intraoperative and immediate postoperative bleeding risk) and continued for the duration of admission or longer. For patients undergoing major cancer surgery in the abdomen or pelvis, NICE NG89 recommends extending pharmacological prophylaxis for a total of 28 days after surgery, reflecting the substantially elevated and prolonged VTE risk in this group. Fondaparinux is a recognised alternative where heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) is a concern, and DOACs are increasingly used for extended prophylaxis after certain orthopaedic procedures such as hip and knee replacement.

Contraindications to pharmacological prophylaxis include active or high risk of major bleeding, a platelet count that is significantly reduced, and known or suspected HIT — in these situations, mechanical prophylaxis alone, escalated where possible, is used instead, and the decision is reviewed regularly as the clinical picture evolves. Early mobilisation after surgery is a simple but genuinely effective adjunct to both mechanical and pharmacological prophylaxis and should always be encouraged unless specifically contraindicated.

Recognising a possible DVT or PE on the ward is a distinct but related skill. A DVT typically presents with unilateral calf swelling, warmth, tenderness, and pitting oedema, usually several days after surgery, and is assessed with a Wells score followed by Doppler ultrasound. A PE presents with sudden breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, and occasionally haemoptysis or collapse in a haemodynamically significant case, and is assessed with a Wells score for PE, D-dimer where appropriate, and CT pulmonary angiography. Both are managed with therapeutic anticoagulation once confirmed (or empirically, while awaiting confirmation, in a high pre-test probability case who is otherwise stable), and a massive PE causing haemodynamic compromise is an emergency requiring senior escalation and consideration of thrombolysis. Foundation doctors should have a low threshold for suspecting VTE in any surgical patient with new unexplained tachycardia, hypoxia, or leg swelling, particularly if prophylaxis was interrupted or inadequate.

The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist and Practical Safety Principles

Patient safety around the time of surgery depends as much on systems and checklists as it does on individual clinical knowledge, and this is deliberately tested within anaesthesia UKMLA content — arguably the most exam-relevant safety system of the whole topic — because foundation doctors are present in theatre and on surgical wards where these systems operate daily. The single most important of these systems is the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist, adopted across NHS operating theatres since 2009 and shown in large observational studies to meaningfully reduce surgical complications and mortality.

The checklist is completed at three fixed points during every operation, and UKMLA questions frequently test which specific check happens at which stage:

StageWhenKey checks
Sign InBefore induction of anaesthesiaPatient identity, procedure and site confirmed; consent verified; allergies checked; anaesthetic equipment and pulse oximeter checked; site marked
Time OutBefore skin incision, with the whole team presentTeam introductions; procedure, site, and patient re-confirmed aloud; antibiotic prophylaxis given (or deliberately withheld and documented); critical steps and anticipated problems discussed; imaging displayed if needed
Sign OutBefore the patient leaves the operating theatreProcedure performed is confirmed and recorded; instrument, swab, and needle counts confirmed correct; specimens correctly labelled; equipment problems noted; postoperative care plan communicated to the team taking over

The checklist works because it forces active verbal confirmation from every team member rather than passive assumption, and it deliberately targets the most common and most preventable causes of surgical harm: wrong-site or wrong-patient surgery, missed antibiotic or VTE prophylaxis, retained instruments or swabs, and failures of communication at handover between the surgical and recovery or ward teams. Foundation doctors are often the team member documenting these checks, and may be asked to prompt for a step that has been missed — a small but genuinely safety-critical role that is directly examinable.

Beyond the checklist itself, several broader practical safety principles recur throughout anaesthesia UKMLA content and mirror the wider patient safety and prescribing error curriculum: always confirm patient identity and allergy status independently before any drug or blood product is given; never assume a medication has been correctly withheld or continued without checking the actual drug chart; document clearly and contemporaneously, particularly around consent, fasting status, and any deviation from the checklist; and escalate early rather than repeatedly managing an unstable postoperative patient alone. These habits generalise well beyond the operating theatre and are consistently rewarded across UKMLA scenarios, not only ones explicitly labelled as surgical or anaesthetic.

Consent itself deserves a brief mention in this context, since it is formally verified at the Sign In stage of the checklist. Valid consent requires that the patient has capacity, has been given adequate information about the procedure (including material risks a reasonable patient would want to know, following the Montgomery principle), and consents voluntarily. Foundation doctors are frequently asked to confirm rather than take consent for procedures they are not performing, and should never complete a consent form for an operation they cannot themselves adequately explain.

Bringing It Together: A Foundation Doctor's Perioperative Checklist

Anaesthesia UKMLA scenarios often present as a single patient journey rather than isolated facts, so it is worth summarising the practical sequence a foundation doctor follows from clerking to discharge. Before surgery: take a thorough history including previous anaesthetic problems, assign or confirm the ASA grade, check and document fasting status, review the drug chart against the standard perioperative medication rules, and complete the VTE risk assessment. During the perioperative period: understand (without needing to perform) which broad category of anaesthesia is planned, and anticipate the complications specific to that category. After surgery: assess new hypotension, confusion, nausea and vomiting, or leg swelling systematically rather than reflexively, using ABCDE and a structured differential diagnosis, and escalate early when a red flag is present.

This sequence recurs constantly across surgical firms and is exactly why anaesthesia UKMLA content is tested alongside, rather than separately from, core surgical presentations and trauma and orthopaedics content — a hip fracture patient, for example, requires pre-operative optimisation, an anaesthetic decision (commonly a spinal or general anaesthetic), VTE prophylaxis, and postoperative delirium screening, drawing on almost every section of this guide simultaneously. Recognising these overlaps, rather than treating each topic as an isolated fact set, is what genuinely improves performance on integrated AKT scenarios.

IMGs in particular should use question banks that include UK-specific perioperative scenarios rather than relying solely on general international anaesthesia references, since exact fasting rules, VTE prophylaxis duration, and drug-management protocols are drawn directly from UK national guidance such as NICE NG89. Building a broader structured revision plan around this topic, rather than reading it as a one-off, pays off because perioperative scenarios recur across the Applied Knowledge Test in slightly different clinical dressing each time. UK medical students, meanwhile, often underestimate how frequently this content appears precisely because it feels "borrowed" from surgical placements rather than a dedicated teaching block — treating it as a distinct, revisable topic rather than incidental ward knowledge is a worthwhile exam strategy in itself.

Frequently Asked Questions About Anaesthesia UKMLA Content

The following anaesthesia UKMLA questions are phrased as candidates and IMGs commonly search for them, each answered as a self-contained summary.

Do foundation doctors need to know anaesthetic drug doses for the UKMLA?

No. Anaesthesia UKMLA questions focus on pre-operative assessment, recognition of anaesthesia types, and management of common perioperative complications rather than specific induction agent doses, muscle relaxant selection, or airway device technique. Detailed anaesthetic pharmacology and technique belong to anaesthetic specialty training, not the generalist Applied Knowledge Test. Candidates should prioritise ASA grading, fasting rules, perioperative medication management, and postoperative complication recognition instead.

What is the difference between local, regional, and general anaesthesia?

Local anaesthesia numbs a small, specific area while the patient stays fully conscious, and is often administered by non-anaesthetists for minor procedures. Regional anaesthesia numbs a larger region of the body, such as the lower limbs or abdomen, via a nerve block or neuraxial technique like a spinal or epidural, with the patient awake or sedated. General anaesthesia produces reversible unconsciousness with loss of awareness of the procedure, typically combining hypnosis, analgesia, and, where needed, muscle relaxation. The choice between them depends on the type of surgery, patient comorbidity, and patient preference, and is made jointly by the anaesthetist and surgical team.

What is the standard preoperative fasting rule in the UK?

The standard UK rule, often summarised as "2-4-6," requires a minimum of 2 hours of fasting from clear fluids, 4 hours from breast milk, and 6 hours from formula milk or a light meal before elective anaesthesia. These minimum times reduce the risk of pulmonary aspiration during induction, when protective airway reflexes are temporarily lost. Emergency surgery may proceed without full fasting, with the increased aspiration risk instead managed anaesthetically, such as with a rapid sequence induction.

How is ASA grade used in perioperative decision-making?

The ASA Physical Status Classification grades a patient's baseline systemic health from ASA I (a normal healthy patient) to ASA VI (a declared brain-dead organ donor), with an "E" suffix added for emergency surgery. A higher ASA grade is associated with increased perioperative risk and often influences decisions about postoperative monitoring level, such as whether a patient needs high-dependency or intensive care rather than routine ward care. It reflects overall patient fitness rather than the technical complexity of the operation itself, so both factors are considered together when planning anaesthetic and surgical care.

Why is new confusion after surgery never just put down to the anaesthetic?

Postoperative delirium always has an underlying, often reversible cause, and attributing new confusion solely to "the anaesthetic wearing off" risks missing serious pathology such as hypoxia, infection, metabolic derangement, or urinary retention. A systematic assessment — checking oxygen saturations, screening for infection, reviewing recent blood results, and checking the drug chart for sedating medications — is required in every case. Non-pharmacological measures such as reorientation and ensuring the patient has glasses and hearing aids are first-line management, with sedative medication reserved for patients who pose a genuine safety risk.

What are the main risk factors for postoperative nausea and vomiting?

The Apfel score identifies four independent risk factors for postoperative nausea and vomiting: female sex, a history of motion sickness or previous postoperative nausea and vomiting, non-smoking status, and planned postoperative opioid use. Patients with several of these risk factors are typically given prophylactic antiemetics, such as ondansetron or dexamethasone, before the anaesthetic even begins. Persistent vomiting after surgery should always prompt consideration of other causes, including bowel obstruction or ileus, rather than being assumed to be routine postoperative nausea and vomiting.

How long is VTE prophylaxis continued after major cancer surgery?

Under NICE guideline NG89, patients undergoing major abdominal or pelvic cancer surgery are typically prescribed extended pharmacological VTE prophylaxis, usually a low molecular weight heparin, for a total of 28 days after surgery. This reflects the substantially elevated and prolonged risk of venous thromboembolism in this specific surgical group compared with lower-risk elective procedures. Mechanical prophylaxis and early mobilisation are continued alongside pharmacological prophylaxis throughout the admission regardless of the planned duration of drug treatment.

What happens at each stage of the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist?

The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist has three stages: Sign In, completed before induction of anaesthesia, confirming patient identity, procedure, site, consent, and allergies; Time Out, completed before skin incision with the whole team present, confirming the procedure aloud and antibiotic prophylaxis administration; and Sign Out, completed before the patient leaves theatre, confirming the procedure performed, instrument and swab counts, and specimen labelling. Each stage requires active verbal confirmation from the surgical team rather than passive assumption. The checklist has been shown to reduce surgical complications and mortality since its NHS-wide adoption in 2009.

Conclusion

Anaesthesia UKMLA questions reward foundation-doctor-level judgement rather than specialist anaesthetic technique: accurate ASA grading, correct application of fasting rules, safe perioperative medication management, and confident recognition of local, regional, and general anaesthesia at a conceptual level. Just as importantly, they test the systematic assessment of common postoperative complications — hypotension, delirium, nausea and vomiting, and venous thromboembolism — alongside the safety systems, particularly the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist and NICE-guided VTE prophylaxis, that keep surgical patients safe. Mastering this ward-level perioperative knowledge, rather than deeper anaesthetic pharmacology, is what actually earns marks in the AKT.
