Nutrition and Metabolic Disorders in the UKMLA: What to Revise

Nutrition UKMLA questions turn up far more often than most candidates expect, threading through gastroenterology, endocrinology, care of the elderly, and general internal medicine stations on the Applied Knowledge Test. Rather than being confined to a single specialty block, malnutrition, obesity, vitamin deficiency, and inherited metabolic disease appear as background detail in surgical, psychiatric, and paediatric scenarios alike, which is exactly why so many candidates under-revise the topic. This guide brings together the core nutrition and metabolic medicine content the UKMLA expects you to know, built around real NICE guidance, so that both UK medical students and IMGs sitting the AKT can revise it as one coherent block rather than scattered fragments.

If you are still mapping out your overall revision plan, it is worth first reading our UKMLA syllabus and content map guide to see exactly where nutrition and metabolic medicine sits alongside the other clinical presentations you are expected to know.

Quick Answer: What Does Nutrition UKMLA Content Actually Cover?

Nutrition UKMLA content covers five core areas that recur across AKT questions: malnutrition screening using tools such as the Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST), refeeding syndrome recognition and prevention, obesity assessment and management along the lifestyle-to-surgical pathway, classic vitamin and mineral deficiency syndromes, and inherited metabolic conditions that can present acutely. These topics are drawn directly from NICE clinical guidance, particularly NICE CG32 on nutrition support in adults and NICE CG189 on obesity identification, assessment and management, both of which remain live, referenced guidelines that the exam expects you to be familiar with in principle.

Unlike single-organ specialties, nutrition-related questions are frequently embedded inside another presentation — an elderly patient admitted with a fall who turns out to have a low MUST score and unintentional weight loss, or a post-operative patient developing confusion and ophthalmoplegia after days of intravenous dextrose with no thiamine cover. Recognising these patterns, rather than memorising isolated facts, is what actually earns marks in the AKT.

Malnutrition Screening for the UKMLA

Malnutrition is under-recognised in UK hospitals and community settings, and the UKMLA expects candidates to know both how it is screened for and why early identification matters. Malnutrition is formally defined as a state of deficiency in energy, protein, and other nutrients that causes measurable adverse effects on body composition, function, and clinical outcome.

The MUST Score Concept

The Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST) is the screening instrument most frequently referenced in UK clinical practice and, by extension, in UKMLA scenarios. It is a five-step tool developed by the British Association for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition (BAPEN) that produces an overall risk score from three components:

  • Body mass index (BMI) score — 0 points for BMI over 20 kg/m² (over 18.5 in some versions), 1 point for BMI 18.5–20 kg/m², 2 points for BMI under 18.5 kg/m².
  • Unplanned weight loss score — based on percentage weight loss in the past 3–6 months: 0 points for less than 5%, 1 point for 5–10%, 2 points for more than 10%.
  • Acute disease effect score — 2 points if the patient is acutely unwell and there has been, or is likely to be, no nutritional intake for more than 5 days.

The three component scores are added together to give an overall risk category: score 0 is low risk, score 1 is medium risk, and score 2 or more is high risk of malnutrition. For AKT purposes, the exact scoring thresholds matter less than the underlying logic: MUST identifies patients who are already underweight, who are losing weight rapidly, or who are unlikely to eat adequately for several days — and each of those three routes independently raises risk.

Why Screening Matters Clinically

Malnutrition is associated with longer hospital stays, higher rates of pressure ulcers and infection, delayed wound healing, and increased mortality. NICE CG32 explicitly recommends that all hospital inpatients be screened on admission and weekly thereafter, and that care home and community patients be screened at first contact and periodically after that. Consequently, a UKMLA stem describing an elderly patient with unintentional weight loss, low oral intake, and a low BMI is testing whether you recognise the need for formal nutritional screening and referral to a dietitian, not simply whether you can spot that the patient looks thin.

High-risk patients identified by MUST should have a care plan that may include: dietary advice and food fortification, oral nutritional supplements, referral to a dietitian, and — where oral intake cannot meet requirements — enteral or parenteral nutrition. This escalation pathway from oral to enteral to parenteral support is a recurring theme across nutrition UKMLA questions and mirrors the structure of NICE's own guidance.

Routes of Nutritional Support: Oral, Enteral, and Parenteral

Once a patient has been screened and identified as needing nutritional support, the UKMLA expects candidates to understand the escalation logic between the three routes of feeding, since choosing the correct route — not just recognising that support is needed — is frequently the actual marked decision in an AKT stem. Oral nutritional support (dietary advice, food fortification, and oral nutritional supplements such as high-calorie drinks) is first-line whenever the gut is functioning and the patient can swallow safely. Enteral tube feeding (nasogastric, nasojejunal, or a percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy for longer-term needs) is used when oral intake cannot meet requirements but the gastrointestinal tract remains functional — for example, in dysphagia following a stroke, or in patients unable to protect their airway safely enough to swallow. Parenteral nutrition, delivered intravenously via a central or peripheral line, is reserved for patients whose gastrointestinal tract is not functional or accessible, such as prolonged ileus, short bowel syndrome, or severe inflammatory bowel disease with a non-functioning gut.

NICE CG32 is explicit that the enteral route should always be preferred over parenteral nutrition where the gut can be used at all, since enteral feeding preserves gut mucosal integrity, carries a lower infection risk, and is considerably cheaper — the guiding principle often summarised as "if the gut works, use it." Parenteral nutrition carries specific risks that are worth knowing for the AKT: line-related bloodstream infection, hepatic dysfunction with prolonged use, and the same refeeding risk discussed in the next section, since parenteral feeding can reintroduce calories just as abruptly as enteral or oral feeding if not started cautiously.

Refeeding Syndrome: A Classic High-Yield Exam Topic

Refeeding syndrome is one of the most consistently tested nutrition topics in the UKMLA AKT, precisely because it is dangerous, preventable, and tests a candidate's ability to anticipate a complication before it happens rather than simply treat it once established.

What Refeeding Syndrome Is

Refeeding syndrome is the constellation of metabolic and electrolyte disturbances that occurs when nutrition — enteral, parenteral, or even oral — is reintroduced too quickly in a patient who has been starved or severely malnourished. During prolonged starvation, the body shifts from carbohydrate to fat and protein metabolism, insulin secretion falls, and intracellular phosphate, potassium, and magnesium become depleted even though serum levels may appear deceptively normal. When carbohydrate is reintroduced, insulin secretion surges again, driving glucose, phosphate, potassium, and magnesium rapidly into cells and causing a sudden fall in their serum concentrations.

Recognising Who Is at Risk

NICE CG32 sets out criteria for identifying patients at high risk of refeeding problems. A patient is considered at high risk if they have one or more of the following:

  • BMI less than 16 kg/m²
  • Unintentional weight loss of more than 15% in the past 3–6 months
  • Little or no nutritional intake for more than 10 days
  • Low levels of potassium, phosphate, or magnesium prior to feeding

Alternatively, a patient is considered high risk if they have two or more of the following: BMI less than 18.5 kg/m², unintentional weight loss of more than 10% in the past 3–6 months, little or no nutritional intake for more than 5 days, or a history of alcohol misuse, or drugs including insulin, chemotherapy, antacids, or diuretics. A UKMLA stem describing a patient with anorexia nervosa, chronic alcohol excess, prolonged post-operative fasting, or a homeless patient admitted after several days without food should immediately raise the possibility of refeeding risk in your differential for how to manage nutritional support.

The Classic Biochemical Picture

The hallmark biochemical finding in refeeding syndrome is hypophosphataemia, often accompanied by hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia. Clinically, this combination can cause cardiac arrhythmias, cardiac failure, respiratory failure due to diaphragmatic weakness, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, and confusion. Thiamine deficiency frequently coexists, since thiamine stores are also depleted by prolonged starvation and rapidly consumed once carbohydrate metabolism resumes — which is why thiamine replacement is given alongside refeeding, not as an afterthought.

Prevention Is the Exam Point

The single most important concept for the AKT is that refeeding syndrome is managed by preventing it, not by waiting for electrolytes to fall and correcting them afterwards. NICE recommends starting nutritional support in high-risk patients at no more than 10 kcal/kg/day, increasing slowly over 4–7 days to meet full needs if clinical and biochemical monitoring is satisfactory. For patients at extreme risk (for example, BMI under 14 kg/m² or negligible intake for more than 15 days), starting as low as 5 kcal/kg/day may be appropriate under specialist supervision.

Alongside cautious caloric introduction, guidance recommends:

  • Giving oral thiamine and vitamin B co-strong (or IV Pabrinex where indicated) before and during the first 10 days of feeding.
  • Providing balanced multivitamin and trace element supplementation throughout refeeding.
  • Correcting potassium, phosphate, and magnesium before feeding starts where levels are already low, and monitoring them daily for the first week.
  • Restricting sodium and fluid where cardiac failure risk is high, since refeeding can also precipitate fluid overload.

This "start low, go slow, replace electrolytes and thiamine pre-emptively" pattern is the answer the AKT is almost always looking for when a stem hints at refeeding risk — a much higher-yield fact to hold onto than the precise numeric thresholds themselves, though those are worth knowing too.

Obesity Assessment and Management

Obesity questions in the UKMLA test conceptual understanding of the assessment and stepped management pathway described in NICE CG189, rather than detailed pharmacology of any single agent.

BMI Categories and Classification

Body mass index remains the standard screening measure for adiposity in adults, calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in metres squared. The widely used categories are:

NICE CG189 also highlights that BMI thresholds should be interpreted with caution in some population groups. For people of South Asian, Chinese, other Asian, Middle Eastern, Black African, and African-Caribbean family backgrounds, health risks from excess adiposity are recognised at lower BMI values, and a lower threshold — around 27.5 kg/m² rather than 30 kg/m² — is often used to trigger intervention or referral. Waist circumference is used as an adjunct measure, particularly in patients with a BMI under 35 kg/m², since central adiposity carries independent metabolic risk.

Lifestyle Management

First-line management for all patients with overweight or obesity is lifestyle intervention: dietary modification to create an energy deficit, increased physical activity, and behavioural strategies to support long-term adherence. NICE recommends multicomponent interventions combining dietary advice, physical activity, and behaviour change support, ideally delivered through structured weight management programmes rather than advice alone, since combined approaches show better long-term outcomes than any single element in isolation.

Pharmacological Options at a Conceptual Level

Where lifestyle measures alone have not achieved sufficient weight loss and BMI thresholds are met (generally BMI 30 kg/m² or above, or 27 kg/m² or above with a weight-related comorbidity such as type 2 diabetes), pharmacological therapy may be considered as an adjunct to, not a replacement for, lifestyle change. For the UKMLA, the conceptual point to hold onto is that anti-obesity medications work through appetite suppression or reduced nutrient absorption, that they are prescribed alongside continued dietary and activity support, and that they are reviewed for effectiveness after a defined trial period, with treatment stopped if insufficient weight loss is achieved. Newer incretin-based agents used for weight management (the same drug class as some type 2 diabetes therapies covered in our endocrinology and diabetes revision guide) illustrate how metabolic and weight-management pharmacology increasingly overlap — a connection the AKT likes to test across specialty boundaries.

Bariatric Surgery Thresholds

Bariatric surgery is considered for patients who meet specific NICE criteria: BMI of 40 kg/m² or more, or BMI of 35–39.9 kg/m² with a significant comorbidity that could improve with weight loss (such as type 2 diabetes or hypertension), where non-surgical measures have failed to achieve or maintain clinically beneficial weight loss for at least 6 months, and the patient is fit for anaesthesia and surgery. NICE also recommends surgery as a first-line option (rather than after a trial of non-surgical measures) for adults with a BMI of 35 kg/m² or more and recent-onset type 2 diabetes, reflecting strong evidence that early surgical intervention can induce diabetes remission. Common procedures include gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy, and adjustable gastric banding, each carrying a different balance of weight-loss efficacy, reversibility, and nutritional consequence — post-bariatric patients require lifelong micronutrient monitoring and supplementation, since malabsorptive procedures in particular predispose to iron, B12, folate, and fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies.

Both Audiences: What This Means for UK Students and IMGs

For UK medical students, obesity questions often appear embedded in primary care or pre-operative assessment scenarios, testing whether you know when to refer for structured weight management versus specialist bariatric assessment. For IMGs, particularly those coming from health systems where bariatric surgery criteria or first-line pharmacological pathways differ, it is worth explicitly memorising the NICE-specific BMI thresholds above, since the UKMLA tests UK guideline-concordant practice rather than practice patterns from other jurisdictions.

Eating Disorders and Nutritional Risk

Eating disorders — anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder — sit at the intersection of nutrition and mental health content on the UKMLA, and are among the highest-risk populations for both malnutrition and refeeding syndrome. Patients with severe anorexia nervosa frequently meet several of the NICE high-risk refeeding criteria simultaneously (very low BMI, prolonged minimal intake, pre-existing electrolyte depletion), so a stem describing a young patient admitted with anorexia nervosa and a very low BMI should immediately trigger consideration of a cautious, specialist-supervised refeeding plan rather than standard full-calorie nutritional support. Refeeding in this population is usually managed jointly between eating disorder psychiatry services and dietetics, reflecting the same principle that applies more broadly whenever a nutritional risk overlaps with a psychiatric or substance-use disorder, a theme also covered from the addiction-medicine angle in our substance misuse and addiction revision guide, since chronic alcohol misuse independently predisposes to both malnutrition and thiamine deficiency.

Vitamin and Mineral Deficiencies: Classic Clinical Signs

Deficiency syndromes are a gift to question writers because each one has a small number of pathognomonic or highly characteristic clinical features that can be tested with a short, elegant stem. Learning the syndromes as recognisable patterns, rather than as isolated facts, is the most efficient way to revise this material.

Vitamin B12 and Folate Deficiency

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) and folate deficiency both cause macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia, but only B12 deficiency causes neurological disease, which is the key discriminating feature tested in the AKT. B12 deficiency causes subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord, affecting the dorsal columns (loss of vibration and proprioception) and corticospinal tracts (spasticity, upgoing plantars), sometimes with peripheral neuropathy causing glove-and-stocking sensory loss. Causes include pernicious anaemia (autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells producing intrinsic factor), terminal ileal disease or resection (Crohn's disease), strict veganism without supplementation, and metformin use. Folate deficiency, by contrast, causes megaloblastic anaemia without the neurological features, and common causes include poor dietary intake, alcohol excess, pregnancy (increased demand), and certain drugs such as methotrexate and phenytoin.

An important exam trap: folate should never be given alone when B12 deficiency has not been excluded, because folate replacement can correct the megaloblastic anaemia while allowing subacute combined degeneration to progress unchecked, since the anaemia was masking rather than reflecting the neurological process. Always replace B12 first, or simultaneously, when both are deficient.

Vitamin D Deficiency

Vitamin D deficiency causes rickets in children (bowing of the legs, widened wrists, costochondral swelling — "rachitic rosary," delayed fontanelle closure) and osteomalacia in adults (bone pain, proximal myopathy causing a waddling gait, increased fracture risk, and a classic biochemical picture of low or low-normal calcium, low phosphate, and markedly raised alkaline phosphatase). Risk factors include limited sun exposure, darker skin pigmentation, malabsorption (including post-bariatric surgery and coeliac disease), chronic kidney disease (impaired 1-alpha-hydroxylation), and strict vegan diets. Treatment is with vitamin D replacement (colecalciferol or ergocalciferol), with calcium co-supplementation where dietary calcium intake is also inadequate.

Iron Deficiency

Iron deficiency is the most common cause of microcytic anaemia worldwide and, in adult men and post-menopausal women, must always prompt investigation for occult gastrointestinal blood loss until proven otherwise — a principle also emphasised in our gastroenterology AKT revision guide, since iron-deficiency anaemia is frequently the presenting clue to colorectal or upper GI malignancy. Clinical features beyond fatigue and pallor include koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), angular stomatitis, glossitis, and — rarely — Plummer-Vinson syndrome (dysphagia, oesophageal web, and iron-deficiency anaemia). Ferritin is the most useful test but is an acute-phase reactant, so it can appear falsely normal or elevated in the presence of inflammation or infection, a common examiner trap.

Thiamine Deficiency and Wernicke's Encephalopathy

Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency is a UKMLA favourite because of its dramatic, reversible-if-caught-early presentation. Wernicke's encephalopathy classically presents with a triad of confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia (or nystagmus) — though the AKT expects you to know that all three features are present together in only a minority of real cases, so a stem describing even one or two of these features in an at-risk patient should raise suspicion. Risk factors include chronic alcohol excess, hyperemesis gravidarum, prolonged fasting or starvation, bariatric surgery, and malignancy with poor oral intake.

If untreated, Wernicke's encephalopathy can progress to Korsakoff syndrome, characterised by profound anterograde amnesia, confabulation, and retrograde memory loss, which is typically irreversible. This is precisely why thiamine (as IV Pabrinex) must be given before or alongside glucose in any at-risk patient — giving glucose first can precipitously consume remaining thiamine stores and precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy, the same underlying principle tested in refeeding syndrome prevention above.

Zinc, Magnesium, and Other Trace Element Deficiencies

Beyond the major deficiency syndromes above, the AKT occasionally tests trace element deficiencies, usually in the context of malabsorption or prolonged parenteral nutrition without adequate supplementation. Zinc deficiency causes a characteristic perioral and acral dermatitis, diarrhoea, and impaired wound healing, and is classically associated with acrodermatitis enteropathica in infants (an inherited defect of zinc absorption) or with prolonged unsupplemented parenteral nutrition in adults. Magnesium deficiency causes neuromuscular irritability (tremor, tetany, seizures) and is notable for causing a secondary hypocalcaemia that will not correct until magnesium is replaced — a frequently tested biochemical trap, since clinicians who simply replace calcium without checking magnesium will find the hypocalcaemia persists. Copper deficiency, though rare, can cause a sideroblastic-type anaemia and neutropenia, and is worth knowing about in patients who have had excessive zinc supplementation, since zinc and copper compete for absorption.

Food Allergy, Intolerance, and Nutritional Overlap

Food-related presentations sit at the boundary between nutrition and immunology on the UKMLA, and it is worth being clear on the distinction the exam expects: a true IgE-mediated food allergy (to peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, or eggs, for example) causes rapid-onset symptoms — urticaria, angioedema, or anaphylaxis — through mast cell degranulation, whereas food intolerance (such as lactose intolerance from acquired or primary lactase deficiency) causes delayed, dose-dependent gastrointestinal symptoms without an immune mechanism. Coeliac disease is a distinct, non-IgE-mediated immune response to gluten that causes villous atrophy and secondary malabsorption of iron, folate, and fat-soluble vitamins — which is why unexplained iron-deficiency anaemia or osteomalacia in a young adult should always prompt coeliac serology. For the fuller immunological mechanisms behind true allergic disease, including anaphylaxis management, see our companion immunology and allergy revision guide, which complements the nutritional malabsorption angle covered here.

Inherited Metabolic Conditions Relevant to Acute Presentations

Inherited metabolic disorders are individually rare but collectively important for the UKMLA because they frequently present acutely and are treatable or preventable if recognised promptly — precisely the kind of "don't miss" diagnosis the exam favours.

Phenylketonuria (PKU)

Phenylketonuria results from deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase, the enzyme that converts phenylalanine to tyrosine. Untreated, phenylalanine accumulates and causes severe, irreversible intellectual disability, seizures, and a characteristic musty odour. PKU is identified through the UK newborn bloodspot ("heel-prick") screening programme, allowing treatment — a lifelong low-phenylalanine diet — to begin before irreversible neurological damage occurs. The key exam point is that PKU is a screening success story: early dietary treatment allows normal neurodevelopment, whereas late diagnosis does not reverse damage already done.

Medium-Chain Acyl-CoA Dehydrogenase (MCAD) Deficiency

MCAD deficiency is a fatty acid oxidation disorder, also detected on newborn screening, in which the body cannot break down medium-chain fatty acids for energy during fasting. Affected infants and children are at risk of a life-threatening hypoketotic hypoglycaemic crisis triggered by prolonged fasting or intercurrent illness — a presentation the AKT may test as an infant who becomes acutely unwell, lethargic, and hypoglycaemic after a period of reduced feeding, such as during a viral illness. Management centres on avoiding prolonged fasting and providing emergency glucose during illness.

Glycogen Storage Disorders

Glycogen storage disorders, of which Von Gierke disease (type I) is the classic example, impair the ability to mobilise glucose from glycogen stores, causing fasting hypoglycaemia, lactic acidosis, hepatomegaly, and growth delay in affected children. The conceptual link back to the rest of this guide is that, like MCAD deficiency, the underlying vulnerability is an inability to tolerate normal fasting intervals — the same physiological stress that malnutrition screening and refeeding precautions are designed to manage safely in a different context.

Wilson's Disease

Wilson's disease is a disorder of copper metabolism causing pathological copper accumulation in the liver, brain, and cornea. It classically presents in adolescents or young adults with a combination of liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis) and neuropsychiatric features (tremor, dysarthria, personality change), with Kayser-Fleischer rings visible on slit-lamp examination of the cornea being the classic sign. Diagnosis involves low serum caeruloplasmin and raised 24-hour urinary copper excretion, and treatment is with copper-chelating agents such as penicillamine or trientine, plus zinc to reduce further copper absorption. Any UKMLA stem combining unexplained liver disease with movement disorder or psychiatric change in a young patient should prompt consideration of Wilson's disease.

Hereditary Haemochromatosis

Hereditary haemochromatosis is an autosomal recessive disorder of iron metabolism (most commonly due to HFE gene mutations) causing excessive intestinal iron absorption and progressive iron deposition in the liver, pancreas, heart, joints, and skin. The classic (though now less commonly seen at presentation, due to earlier detection) triad is bronze skin pigmentation, diabetes mellitus ("bronze diabetes"), and cirrhosis, though arthropathy (particularly of the second and third metacarpophalangeal joints) and cardiomyopathy are also tested features. Diagnosis is via elevated ferritin and transferrin saturation, confirmed with genetic testing, and first-line treatment is regular venesection to deplete iron stores.

Metabolic Syndrome and Cardiometabolic Risk

Metabolic syndrome is the clustering of central obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia, and hypertension that together substantially raise cardiovascular and type 2 diabetes risk, and it is a useful unifying concept for tying together the obesity and vitamin/mineral content above into a single cardiometabolic risk picture the AKT likes to test. Commonly used diagnostic criteria require central obesity (raised waist circumference, with ethnicity-specific thresholds) plus at least two of: raised triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol, raised blood pressure, and raised fasting glucose or established type 2 diabetes. Recognising metabolic syndrome matters clinically because it identifies patients who benefit most from early, intensive lifestyle intervention — weight loss of even 5–10% of body weight measurably improves insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and lipid profile, reinforcing why lifestyle management remains the foundation of the obesity pathway described earlier rather than merely a box-ticking exercise before pharmacological therapy.

Nutrition in Older Adults and Frailty

Malnutrition risk rises sharply with age, frailty, and multimorbidity, which is why nutrition and care-of-the-elderly content overlap so closely in the UKMLA — a connection also explored in more depth in our care of the elderly and frailty revision guide. Reduced appetite, dental problems, swallowing difficulty, polypharmacy, social isolation, cognitive impairment, and depression all compound to place older adults at disproportionate malnutrition risk, and a low MUST score in an elderly inpatient should trigger the same structured escalation pathway — dietary fortification, supplements, dietitian referral — described earlier in this guide, alongside a broader search for reversible contributing causes such as poorly fitting dentures or undiagnosed depression.

How to Revise Nutrition and Metabolic Medicine for the AKT

Nutrition UKMLA revision is most efficient when organised around the clinical decision each topic is really testing, rather than as a long list of isolated facts. A practical approach that works well alongside the broader strategies in our UKMLA preparation guide is to build a short set of "trigger patterns" from this guide: a low MUST score or unintentional weight loss should trigger a structured nutritional care plan; a very low BMI or prolonged starvation should trigger refeeding precautions and pre-emptive thiamine; a BMI at a NICE threshold should trigger the correct next step on the lifestyle-to-surgical pathway; and a combination of anaemia type with a specific neurological, dermatological, or hepatic sign should trigger the correct vitamin or mineral deficiency, or inherited metabolic condition.

Because this content overlaps so heavily with other specialties, it is worth revising it alongside — not instead of — related topics: the endocrine and diabetes overlap covered above, the gastrointestinal malabsorption causes of iron and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, and the frailty and polypharmacy context in elderly patients. Candidates new to the exam format should also make sure they understand how AKT questions are structured before drilling content in isolation; our introduction to the UKMLA is a good starting point if you have not yet read it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the MUST score used for in nutrition screening?

The Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST) is used to identify adults at risk of malnutrition by combining a BMI score, an unplanned weight loss score, and an acute disease effect score into one overall risk category of low, medium, or high risk. It is recommended by NICE for use on hospital admission, weekly during admission, and at first contact in community and care home settings, guiding referral to dietetic support and structured nutritional care plans.

What are the classic biochemical abnormalities in refeeding syndrome?

The hallmark abnormality in refeeding syndrome is hypophosphataemia, usually accompanied by hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia, as insulin release following reintroduction of carbohydrate drives these electrolytes rapidly into cells. These shifts can cause cardiac arrhythmia, cardiac failure, respiratory failure, and rhabdomyolysis, which is why NICE recommends cautious, slow reintroduction of calories with pre-emptive electrolyte and thiamine replacement in high-risk patients rather than waiting for abnormalities to appear.

At what BMI does NICE recommend considering bariatric surgery?

NICE recommends considering bariatric surgery for adults with a BMI of 40 kg/m² or more, or a BMI of 35–39.9 kg/m² with a significant weight-related comorbidity such as type 2 diabetes, once non-surgical measures have failed to achieve sustained benefit for at least 6 months. Surgery may also be offered first-line, without a prior non-surgical trial, for adults with a BMI of 35 kg/m² or more and recently diagnosed type 2 diabetes.

Why must thiamine be given before glucose in malnourished or alcohol-dependent patients?

Thiamine is a required cofactor for carbohydrate metabolism, and malnourished or alcohol-dependent patients typically have depleted thiamine stores. Giving glucose before thiamine can rapidly consume the little thiamine that remains, precipitating or worsening Wernicke's encephalopathy, which presents with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia or nystagmus. IV thiamine (as Pabrinex) should therefore be given before or alongside any glucose-containing fluids in at-risk patients.

What is the difference between vitamin B12 deficiency and folate deficiency?

Both vitamin B12 and folate deficiency cause macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia, but only B12 deficiency causes neurological disease, specifically subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord affecting the dorsal columns and corticospinal tracts. Because folate replacement can mask the anaemia of B12 deficiency while neurological damage progresses, B12 status should always be checked and corrected before or alongside folate replacement.

Which inherited metabolic conditions are detected by UK newborn screening?

UK newborn bloodspot screening detects several inherited metabolic conditions relevant to the UKMLA, including phenylketonuria (PKU) and medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase (MCAD) deficiency, alongside other conditions such as congenital hypothyroidism and cystic fibrosis. Early detection allows treatment — a low-phenylalanine diet in PKU, or avoidance of prolonged fasting in MCAD deficiency — to begin before irreversible harm occurs, which is the central teaching point examiners return to.

Do IMGs need to know UK-specific nutrition guidelines for the UKMLA, or general international principles?

The UKMLA tests UK guideline-concordant practice specifically, so IMGs should learn the NICE-specific thresholds referenced throughout this guide — such as MUST risk categories, refeeding risk criteria, and NICE obesity BMI thresholds — rather than relying on equivalent guidance from their home country, since numerical cut-offs and first-line management pathways can differ between health systems.

Conclusion

Nutrition UKMLA questions reward candidates who understand the underlying physiology — why refeeding is dangerous, why thiamine precedes glucose, why B12 must be corrected before folate — rather than those who have simply memorised isolated facts in a vacuum. Anchor your revision around the MUST screening pathway, the refeeding syndrome prevention protocol, the NICE obesity BMI thresholds, and the classic deficiency syndromes summarised in the table above, and you will be well placed to answer nutrition and metabolic medicine questions wherever they surface across the AKT, whether embedded in gastroenterology, care of the elderly, or general medicine stems.
