What is the UKMLA? The UK Medical Licensing Assessment (UKMLA) is the General Medical Council's unified national examination that every doctor must pass before being granted a licence to practise medicine in the United Kingdom. Introduced in its current form in 2024, the UKMLA replaced a fragmented system in which UK medical school finals and the separate PLAB examination for international medical graduates (IMGs) operated to different standards and used different formats, creating an inconsistent entry threshold to the UK medical workforce. The UKMLA brings all routes to GMC registration under a single, evidence-based standard, ensuring that every newly licensed doctor — regardless of where they trained — can demonstrate the same core level of clinical knowledge, skills, and professional behaviour.

The assessment is designed around the principle that the standard for safe, independent practice as a Foundation Year 1 doctor in the UK should be identical whether you graduated from a UK medical school, a European university, or an institution in South Asia, West Africa, or the Middle East. This is both a quality assurance measure and an equity measure: patients anywhere in the NHS should be able to trust that every doctor seeing them has met the same independently verified standard.

This guide covers everything you need to know about the UKMLA — its history, purpose, structure, who must sit it, how it differs from previous assessments, what happens if you fail, key statistics, and how it fits within the broader landscape of UK medical education. Whether you are a current medical student planning your final year or an IMG exploring your pathway to UK practice, this is the reference you need.

Why Was the UKMLA Introduced? The History Behind the Assessment

Before the UKMLA, the UK had a two-track entry system. UK graduates completed their medical school's own summative assessments — typically a combination of written papers and an OSCE — which varied significantly in format, standard, and rigour between different universities. There was no single common national examination, meaning the threshold for graduation (and provisional GMC registration) was effectively set by each medical school independently. The PLAB examination, introduced in the 1990s, served as the separate licensing hurdle for IMGs, but it operated on a different content framework and had no formal alignment with what UK medical schools were testing.

This dual system attracted increasing scrutiny. Reports from medical education bodies and inquiries following patient safety incidents highlighted the risks of an inconsistent entry standard. The GMC's own research showed that the knowledge and competencies assessed by different medical schools' final examinations, and by PLAB, did not always map to the same clinical competency framework. The Medical Schools Council (MSC) and the GMC collaborated over several years to design a solution: a single national licensing assessment that would be taken by all UK graduates and, in a parallel form, by all IMGs.

Pilot testing of the UKMLA began with a small number of medical schools in 2022 and 2023. From the 2024–25 academic year, the UKMLA became the mandatory licensing assessment for all UK medical students graduating from that year onward. Simultaneously, the PLAB examination was formally aligned with the MLA Content Map standard from August 2024, meaning IMGs now take a PLAB that is built on the same blueprint as the UKMLA taken by UK graduates. The formal rebrand of PLAB to MLA-equivalent pathways (with PLAB 1 equating to the AKT and PLAB 2 equating to the CPSA) completed the structural unification.

Who Needs to Sit the UKMLA?

Understanding who must take the UKMLA requires distinguishing between two groups: UK medical graduates and IMGs.

UK medical students graduating from a GMC-approved UK medical school from the 2024–25 academic year onwards must pass the UKMLA as a condition of their degree and a prerequisite for provisional GMC registration. This is now a standard part of the medical education curriculum in all UK medical schools. The assessment is embedded in the final year of the programme, and students sit both the AKT and the CPSA as organised by their institution in partnership with the GMC and, for the AKT, the Medical Schools Council.

International Medical Graduates (IMGs) — doctors who obtained their primary medical qualification outside the UK — must pass both components of the UKMLA (via the PLAB route) as part of the GMC registration process. For IMGs, this means booking and sitting PLAB 1 (equivalent to the AKT) and PLAB 2 (equivalent to the CPSA) through the GMC's booking system. Unlike UK students, IMGs pay the full exam fees directly to the GMC and must meet additional prerequisites including English language proficiency and an acceptable internship pattern.

Some groups are exempt from UKMLA. Doctors applying for specialist or GP registration who have already achieved a recognised qualification (such as Membership of the Royal Colleges of Physicians) follow a specialist registration route and do not take the UKMLA. EEA doctors with certain qualifications obtained before specific transition dates may also benefit from grandfather provisions. The GMC website publishes up-to-date eligibility guidance for all routes.

The Two Components of the UKMLA Explained

The UKMLA is a two-stage assessment comprising the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA). Both must be passed to satisfy the UKMLA requirement; there is no exemption from either component based on performance in the other.

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT)

The AKT is a computer-based written examination consisting of 200 Single Best Answer (SBA) questions delivered over three hours (180 minutes). Questions are written by panels of practising clinicians and medical educators, mapped to the MLA Content Map, and reviewed through a rigorous quality assurance process before use. The AKT tests the application of clinical knowledge to realistic patient scenarios — candidates are presented with a brief clinical vignette and must select the single most appropriate answer from five options.

For UK medical students, the AKT is administered at the student's medical school or a designated Pearson VUE centre. For IMGs, the AKT is sat through the PLAB 1 route at Pearson VUE test centres in over 180 countries worldwide, allowing candidates to sit the exam without travelling to the UK. The pass mark is determined for each sitting using criterion-referenced standard setting (the Angoff method), so it varies slightly between sittings, typically falling in the range of 53–68% of raw items correct.

The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA)

The CPSA is a face-to-face Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) comprising 18 stations of eight minutes each. It takes place at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester for IMGs (via PLAB 2), and at the student's medical school for UK undergraduates. The CPSA tests clinical examination skills, communication with patients and colleagues, clinical reasoning in real time, practical clinical procedures, and professional behaviours including ethical decision-making and patient safety awareness.

At each station, candidates interact with a trained simulated patient and are assessed by an examiner using a structured marking scheme combining a global rating and a detailed checklist. The CPSA cannot be taken before passing the AKT, and no credit is given for partial completion — both components must be passed independently.

What Does the UKMLA Assess? The MLA Content Map

The content of both UKMLA components is defined by the MLA Content Map, a document published and maintained by the GMC that sets out the core knowledge, skills, and behaviours required for safe, independent practice as a Foundation Year 1 doctor in the UK. The Content Map organises assessable material into four main pillars: clinical presentations, clinical conditions, professional behaviours, and practical skills.

The September 2026 update to the MLA Content Map significantly expanded the scope of assessable material. The list of core clinical conditions grew from approximately 311 to 430, with substantial additions in women's health, genetics, transgender health, and social determinants of health. The mapping grid that previously restricted which conditions could appear in which specialty contexts was removed, meaning any condition can now be tested in any clinical setting. This makes broad, systematic preparation across all specialties more important than ever.

The Content Map is publicly available on the GMC website and should be the primary reference document for any candidate preparing for the UKMLA. It is distinct from a traditional curriculum: it does not prescribe how knowledge should be taught, only what standard must be demonstrated in the assessment. This means different medical schools and different preparation resources may take different approaches to covering the same content, but all must ultimately assess against the same map.

For more detail on the MLA Content Map's structure and how to use it for revision, see our UKMLA syllabus guide.

How the UKMLA Differs from Previous Assessments

For UK medical graduates, the UKMLA represents a significant structural change from the pre-2024 system. Previously, a student at one UK medical school might sit a final-year written paper with 120 questions written entirely by that school's faculty, covering topics selected according to the school's own curriculum emphasis. A student at another school might sit a different paper with different question styles, different topic weighting, and different pass standards. There was no requirement for scores or standards to be comparable across institutions.

The UKMLA changes this fundamentally. Every UK medical student now sits the same AKT paper on the same questions (with some variation between sittings for security), marked against the same standard. The playing field is levelled in a way that has not existed before in UK medical education. Medical schools retain their autonomy over how they teach — the UKMLA is an end-point assessment, not a curriculum prescription — but the bar they must prepare their students to clear is now uniform and externally set.

For IMGs, the change is equally profound. Pre-2024 PLAB was built on a different content framework from UK Finals, meaning the two pathways to GMC registration did not formally test the same things in the same way. PLAB 1 questions tended toward recall of clinical facts, while UK Finals increasingly moved toward applied reasoning. The MLA-aligned PLAB now tests applied reasoning from the same Content Map as the UK undergraduate AKT, creating genuine equivalence between the two routes for the first time.

The UKMLA Timeline: From Sitting the Exam to GMC Registration

Understanding the full timeline from beginning your UKMLA journey to obtaining GMC registration is essential for career planning. The sequence of steps differs between UK students and IMGs, but both involve multiple stages that require careful coordination.

Timeline for UK Medical Students

UK students typically sit the AKT in the spring of their final year, with results issued within four to six weeks. The CPSA follows in the final weeks of the academic year. Results are combined with the medical school's own assessments, and successful graduates receive both their degree and confirmation of UKMLA completion. Provisional GMC registration applications can then be submitted, typically completing in time for the start of the Foundation Programme in August.

Timeline for IMGs

For IMGs the timeline is less fixed. After confirming eligibility (including English language proficiency and internship requirements), candidates book and sit PLAB 1 / AKT — which can be done at test centres worldwide. AKT results are typically released within three to four weeks of sitting. Candidates then book PLAB 2 / CPSA in Manchester; booking availability varies, and popular dates may be weeks or months away. After passing the CPSA, candidates compile their full GMC registration application, which can take two to four months to process depending on document verification requirements. The full IMG journey from first PLAB 1 attempt to active GMC registration typically takes twelve to twenty-four months.

What Happens If You Fail the UKMLA?

Failing one or both components of the UKMLA does not mark the end of your UK medical career pathway. Both UK students and IMGs can re-sit the assessment.

For UK medical students, re-sit arrangements vary by medical school. Most schools provide at least one further opportunity within the same academic cycle and may offer additional support, including supervised revision, targeted feedback, and access to pastoral services. Students who fail to achieve a pass after the maximum number of school-permitted attempts may be asked to intercalate or may be referred to a fitness to practise process if there are concerns beyond academic performance alone. The specific re-sit policy is determined by each medical school in consultation with the GMC.

For IMGs, there is no cap on the number of attempts. Each re-sit requires booking and paying for a new sitting (£283 for AKT, £1,036 for CPSA). The GMC provides result feedback that shows performance by domain, helping candidates identify weaker areas for targeted re-preparation. Many IMGs make multiple attempts before passing; this is normal and should not be discouraging. The important principle is to analyse feedback carefully, adjust your preparation strategy, and address identified gaps systematically before re-sitting.

Detailed information on re-sit rules, appeals, and the feedback process is available in our appeals and re-sits guide.

The UKMLA in the Context of UK Medical Education

The UKMLA does not exist in isolation — it is one element of a broader UK medical education and training system that includes undergraduate training, the Foundation Programme, specialty training, and ongoing professional development throughout a medical career. Understanding where the UKMLA fits within this system helps candidates understand its purpose and what it does (and does not) assess.

The UKMLA sets the threshold for entry to the medical workforce. It answers the question: can this person safely practise as a newly qualified Foundation Year 1 doctor? It does not assess specialist knowledge, advanced procedural skills, or the competencies acquired during specialty training — those are tested by the Royal Colleges' specialty examinations (MRCP, MRCS, MRCGP, and others). The UKMLA is explicitly positioned as an entry-level, Foundation-Programme-threshold assessment, benchmarked against what a competent, recently graduated doctor should know and be able to do in their first clinical post.

This positioning has implications for preparation. Candidates should not try to pass the UKMLA by studying at the level of a specialist — they should aim for solid, broad competency across all areas of the MLA Content Map at the level expected of a Foundation doctor. Depth in one area does not compensate for gaps in another; the examination is designed to detect unsafe practice across all domains, not reward extraordinary performance in a single specialty.

Frequently Asked Questions About What Is UKMLA

Is the UKMLA replacing PLAB entirely?

Not exactly. The PLAB examination continues to exist as the route through which IMGs access the UKMLA pathway. From August 2024, PLAB 1 was aligned with the MLA AKT standard and PLAB 2 was aligned with the MLA CPSA standard. So IMGs still book a test labelled PLAB, but the content and standard are now identical to the UKMLA taken by UK graduates. The PLAB brand has not been abolished, but it now functions as the IMG delivery vehicle for what is effectively the same national licensing assessment. See our detailed UKMLA vs PLAB comparison for more on this distinction.

At what stage of medical school do UK students sit the UKMLA?

The UKMLA is a final-year assessment for UK medical students. Most students sit the AKT in the spring of their fifth year (or sixth year for six-year programmes) and the CPSA in the final weeks of the programme. The exact timing varies by medical school, as institutions integrate the UKMLA into their own assessment calendar. Students should check with their medical school's academic office for the specific dates applicable to their cohort.

Can I sit the UKMLA AKT outside the UK?

Yes, if you are an IMG sitting via the PLAB 1 route. Pearson VUE operates over 180 test centres worldwide, and IMGs can sit the AKT at any of these centres. UK medical students, however, sit the AKT as administered by their medical school or at designated centres within the UK. The AKT is the same examination regardless of where it is sat; only the logistics of access differ by candidate group.

What is the Medical Schools Council's role in the UKMLA?

The Medical Schools Council (MSC) works in partnership with the GMC to deliver the AKT for UK medical students. The MSC coordinates the AKT administration across all UK medical schools, handles the technical delivery of the computer-based examination, and manages the aggregated data from medical school sittings. The GMC remains responsible for the content, standard-setting, and overall quality assurance of the UKMLA. For IMGs, the MSC has no direct role; the GMC manages PLAB administration directly through its own team and the Pearson VUE network.

What does provisional GMC registration mean for UK graduates?

UK medical graduates initially receive provisional registration, which allows them to work in approved Foundation Programme posts under supervision. Provisional registration converts to full registration after satisfactory completion of the Foundation Year 1 programme, at which point doctors can work in more autonomous clinical roles. IMGs who pass both PLAB components and meet all other requirements receive full registration directly, without a provisional period, provided they already hold an acceptable internship qualification from their home country.

How long are UKMLA results valid?

AKT results are valid for five years from the date of passing. If a candidate passes the AKT but does not complete the CPSA and full registration within five years, they may need to re-sit the AKT. CPSA results do not have a standalone published validity period under the MLA framework; candidates should check current GMC guidance for any updates. For UK medical students, the UKMLA results are tied to the specific graduation cohort and do not require independent validity management.

Conclusion

The UKMLA is the most significant change to UK medical licensing in a generation. By creating a single, uniform national standard for all doctors entering the UK medical workforce — regardless of whether they trained in Edinburgh, Lagos, Mumbai, or Manila — the GMC has built a more consistent, transparent, and equitable licensing system. For UK medical students, it represents a new feature of their final year with the institutional support of their medical school behind them. For IMGs, it represents a clearly defined, rigorous, but achievable pathway to practising in one of the world's most respected healthcare systems.

Success in the UKMLA requires systematic, content-map-led preparation, with a particular focus on applied clinical reasoning rather than rote memorisation. Early engagement with the official GMC guidance, the MLA Content Map, and quality question banks gives candidates the best possible foundation for passing on their first attempt and beginning their UK medical career on the strongest footing.
