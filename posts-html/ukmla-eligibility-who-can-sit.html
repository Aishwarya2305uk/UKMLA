UKMLA eligibility determines who can sit the UK Medical Licensing Assessment, and understanding the rules fully is the essential first step whether you are a UK medical student nearing graduation or an international medical graduate (IMG) planning to practise in the United Kingdom. Getting eligibility wrong wastes time and money, so this guide covers every requirement in detail — from accepted qualifications and English language scores to the verification process, special cases, and how to check whether your specific situation qualifies.

The UKMLA consists of two components: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), a written single-best-answer examination, and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), an OSCE-style practical exam. Both must be passed to obtain a licence to practise. The eligibility rules differ slightly depending on your route — UK medical school student or IMG — so both pathways are addressed separately below.

Since 2024, the GMC has been transitioning from the legacy PLAB route to the unified UKMLA for all candidates. IMGs who started the PLAB process before January 2025 may complete their existing attempts, but new IMG applicants registering from 2025 onwards enter the UKMLA route by default. If you are unsure which route applies to you, contact the GMC directly via your GMC Online account.

UK Medical Students: UKMLA Eligibility Requirements

If you are enrolled at a UK medical school recognised by the GMC, your eligibility for the UKMLA is primarily managed by your medical school rather than directly by the GMC. Your institution is responsible for entering you into the AKT and CPSA at the appropriate points in your programme, typically in your penultimate and final clinical years.

To be eligible as a UK medical student, you must be enrolled in a GMC-approved undergraduate or graduate-entry medical degree programme. You do not need to apply directly to the GMC to sit the UKMLA — your university coordinates entries on your behalf. However, you must maintain satisfactory academic progress within your programme and comply with your medical school's fitness to practise requirements.

The AKT is normally sat during Year 4 or Year 5 (or equivalent for graduate-entry programmes), while the CPSA is typically sat during the final year. Your medical school will communicate exact internal deadlines and sitting windows. If you have concerns about timing, speak with your medical school's student support or undergraduate office well in advance.

Students with reasonable adjustments or disabilities must apply through their medical school's disability services team, which will liaise with the GMC to ensure appropriate accommodations are in place. Do not leave reasonable adjustment requests to the last minute — applications should ideally be submitted at least three months before your expected sitting date.

International Medical Graduates: UKMLA Eligibility Overview

For IMGs, UKMLA eligibility requires meeting four distinct criteria: holding an acceptable primary medical qualification (PMQ), having that qualification independently verified, demonstrating English language proficiency to the GMC standard, and making declarations about good health and good character. All four criteria must be met before you can book the AKT.

IMGs apply directly to the GMC via GMC Online. Unlike UK medical students, IMGs manage the entire process themselves, including verification, English language testing, and exam booking. This means planning and timeline management fall entirely on your shoulders — which is why building a realistic timeline is critical (see the section on timing below).

Once you have passed both the AKT and CPSA, you will then need to complete a period of satisfactory supervised practice (provisionally registered) before full GMC registration is granted. The UKMLA pass alone does not grant you the right to work in the UK; it is a prerequisite for registration.

Accepted Primary Medical Qualifications

Your medical degree must come from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) and assessed as acceptable by the GMC. The GMC does not publish a simple country-by-country approved list, but in practice degrees from the following regions are routinely accepted when the school appears on the WDOMS and meets GMC criteria:

  • South Asia: India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal
  • Middle East and North Africa: Egypt, Sudan, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Jordan, Lebanon
  • Sub-Saharan Africa: Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, South Africa, Zimbabwe
  • Southeast Asia: Philippines, Malaysia
  • Europe: EEA graduates may qualify via mutual recognition depending on when they qualified
  • The Americas: Graduates of Caribbean, US, and Latin American schools are assessed individually
  • Australia and New Zealand: Routinely accepted

Degrees from schools not on the WDOMS, or schools flagged by the GMC as not meeting baseline standards, will not be accepted. If you completed your degree at a school that has since been removed from the WDOMS, contact the GMC for individual assessment — outcomes vary on a case-by-case basis.

The MyIntealth Verification Process: Step by Step

Before 2025, qualification verification was handled through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials). EPIC has now been retired and replaced by MyIntealth, a new platform developed by Intealth — the parent organisation of ECFMG. All new IMG applicants must use MyIntealth; EPIC portfolios that were already established may still be referenced for GMC purposes, but new credentials must go through MyIntealth.

The MyIntealth verification process involves primary-source verification, meaning Intealth contacts your medical school directly to confirm your qualification — you cannot self-certify. Here is the step-by-step process:

  1. Create a MyIntealth account at the MyIntealth website. You will need a valid email address and a government-issued ID.
  2. Enter your personal and educational details, including your full name as it appears on your degree certificate, your date of birth, and details of your medical school and graduation year.
  3. Upload your documents, including a certified copy of your medical degree certificate, your transcript, and your postgraduate qualifications if relevant.
  4. Select the GMC as a receiving organisation so that verification results are sent directly to the GMC on your behalf.
  5. Pay the verification fee. Fees vary depending on the number of credentials being verified.
  6. Wait for primary-source verification. Intealth contacts your medical school directly. This can take 4 to 12 weeks for most applicants, though delays of 3 to 6 months can occur if your medical school is slow to respond.
  7. Monitor your MyIntealth dashboard under "My Cases" to track progress. Intealth sends email notifications as each credential is verified.
  8. GMC receives results automatically. Once verification is complete, the GMC is notified and you can proceed to the next steps of your application.

Common issues that cause delays include name discrepancies between your degree and your passport, medical schools that have merged or changed names since you graduated, and lost or damaged original records. If you anticipate any of these issues, contact MyIntealth support early and explain your situation — they handle such cases regularly.

English Language Requirements for UKMLA Eligibility

English language proficiency is a non-negotiable component of UKMLA eligibility for IMGs. The GMC accepts two tests: IELTS Academic and OET (Occupational English Test) Medicine. Both must have been taken within two years of your GMC application date to be valid.

IELTS Academic Requirements

For GMC registration purposes, you must achieve an overall IELTS Academic score of 7.5 with no individual component below 7.0. This means you need at least 7.0 in each of Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. You cannot average out a low component with a high one — all four must meet the minimum.

OET Medicine Requirements

For OET, you must achieve Grade B (equivalent to a score of 350 or above) in all four sub-tests: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. As with IELTS, all components must be passed — a Grade B in three components but a Grade C in one is not sufficient.

In practice, most IMG candidates find OET more accessible because all prompts and content are medical in nature. The Writing sub-test requires writing a referral letter, which doctors practise regularly, while the Speaking sub-test uses medical consultation roleplay. IELTS Writing and Speaking use general topics that can feel less intuitive for clinically focused candidates.

For the UK Foundation Programme (UKFP), the English language requirements are higher: IELTS 7.5 in every skill (not just an overall 7.5 with a minimum of 7.0), or OET score of 400 or above in every sub-test. If you are aiming for foundation posts, plan your English testing accordingly.

There are exemptions from English language testing for candidates who qualified from a medical school in a country where English is the primary language of instruction, or who have practised as a doctor in a majority English-speaking country for a sustained period. The GMC assesses exemption applications individually; contact the GMC before assuming you qualify for an exemption.

Exemptions and Alternative Routes to UKMLA

Not all candidates need to sit both components of the UKMLA. Certain exemptions and alternative routes exist, though they are narrow in scope.

EEA Graduates Post-Brexit

Before Brexit, EEA-qualified doctors could register with the GMC via mutual recognition of their European medical qualifications without sitting a licensing exam. This changed on 1 January 2021. EEA doctors who obtained GMC registration before 31 December 2020 were not affected. However, EEA doctors who did not register before that date must now go through the full UKMLA process as IMGs, including qualification verification and English language testing (if applicable).

Specialist Doctors with Postgraduate Qualifications

Certain overseas-qualified specialists can apply for specialist or GP registration via the Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) or Certificate of Eligibility for General Practice Registration (CEGPR) routes. These routes are separate from the UKMLA and assess equivalence to a UK CCT (Certificate of Completion of Training). CESR/CEGPR applicants do not sit the UKMLA; they instead submit a portfolio of evidence. This route is complex and requires substantial documentation of specialist training and experience.

Doctors Returning to Practice

Doctors who were previously registered with the GMC but allowed their registration to lapse may be able to restore their registration without sitting the full UKMLA, depending on how long they have been out of practice and their circumstances. The GMC assesses these applications individually. If you have been out of clinical practice for five or more years, you will likely need to demonstrate currency through a returner's programme.

Special Cases and Conditional Eligibility

Some candidates fall into edge cases that the standard eligibility framework does not clearly address. Here is a guide to the most common special situations:

  • Dual-qualified doctors: If you hold qualifications from two countries, the GMC will assess the qualification that you are primarily using to support your application. In most cases, using the higher-recognised qualification is advisable.
  • Doctors with a history of fitness to practise proceedings: Any previous sanctions, conditions, or erasure from a medical register in any country must be disclosed. The GMC will assess these cases individually; disclosure does not automatically bar registration but failure to disclose will.
  • Medical graduates who have not yet practised clinically: Some IMGs complete their degree and then move into research or other fields for several years. The GMC may request evidence of how you have maintained clinical currency. While there is no formal expiry on a medical qualification, very long gaps between graduation and application may prompt additional questions.
  • Foundation Year 1 (FY1) and Internship equivalence: Some countries require a post-graduate internship year before full licensure. The GMC considers the internship year as part of the overall qualification — you do not need to have completed a UK FY1 before applying to sit the UKMLA, but you may need it before commencing clinical practice in the UK depending on the role.
  • International Foundation Programme (IFP): The IFP is a route designed specifically for IMGs to complete a foundation-equivalent year in the UK. Eligibility for the IFP requires GMC provisional registration, which in turn requires passing the AKT and CPSA. Check our full eligibility guide for IFP-specific requirements.

How to Check If Your Qualification Is Accepted

The fastest way to check whether your medical school is recognised is to search the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) at wdoms.org. Enter your school's name, country, and the year you graduated. If your school appears in the directory and was listed during the years of your study, that is a strong indicator your qualification will be accepted.

However, listing on the WDOMS is necessary but not always sufficient — the GMC carries out its own assessment of qualifications from certain countries and may apply additional scrutiny. For countries where the GMC has specific concerns about standards, they may require additional documentation or refuse certain qualifications even if the school is on the WDOMS.

If you are uncertain, the best approach is to contact the GMC directly via their online enquiry form. Provide the name of your medical school, your country, and your graduation year. The GMC can give you a preliminary indication of whether your qualification is likely to be accepted, though formal assessment only happens once you submit a full application.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Eligibility

Can EU or EEA graduates still apply for GMC registration without sitting the UKMLA?

No — since 1 January 2021, post-Brexit rules mean that EEA-qualified doctors who did not register with the GMC before 31 December 2020 must go through the full UKMLA process, including qualification verification and English language testing. The mutual recognition route that previously allowed EEA graduates to bypass a licensing exam is no longer available for new applicants. If you graduated in an EEA country and hold a valid EEA medical licence, you will still need to verify your qualification through MyIntealth and meet the English language requirements unless you qualify for an exemption.

Is there an age limit for sitting the UKMLA?

There is no upper or lower age limit for sitting the UKMLA or for GMC registration. Doctors of any age can apply, provided they meet all eligibility criteria. Practically speaking, you must have completed your medical degree, which typically means candidates are at least in their mid-twenties. The GMC does assess health and good character, and extremely mature applicants who have been out of practice for many years may be asked for additional evidence of clinical currency, but age itself is not a disqualifying factor.

What happens if my medical qualification is not accepted?

If the GMC does not accept your primary medical qualification, you cannot register via the UKMLA route. Your options in that situation are limited. You could pursue further medical training to obtain a recognised qualification — for example, completing an additional year of study or a recognised conversion programme. Alternatively, you might explore whether a postgraduate specialist qualification you hold could support a CESR application, though this requires substantial evidence of specialist competence. The GMC will explain the reasons for rejection in writing, and you can appeal the decision or seek legal advice if you believe the rejection was incorrect.

Do I need to complete an English language test if I trained in a country where English is widely spoken?

Exemption from English language testing is granted by the GMC on a case-by-case basis. Qualifying countries include those where English is an official language and the primary medium of medical education — such as Australia, New Zealand, Canada, the USA, and some Caribbean nations. Having attended an English-medium school in a non-English-speaking country, or having practised in English in a clinical setting, does not automatically qualify you for an exemption. You must apply for the exemption via the GMC and provide supporting evidence. If in doubt, take the test rather than applying for an exemption — it avoids delays if the exemption is not granted.

Can I sit PLAB 1 or PLAB 2 while my MyIntealth verification is still in progress?

No. The GMC will not process your application or allow you to book any UKMLA or PLAB examination until your primary medical qualification has been verified through MyIntealth and the results have been received by the GMC. This is why starting the verification process as early as possible is so important — delays in verification directly delay your ability to book an exam sitting. Some candidates start the MyIntealth process 12 to 18 months before they intend to sit the exam to build in a buffer for unexpected delays.

What is the difference between provisional and full GMC registration?

Passing the UKMLA (both AKT and CPSA) grants you eligibility for provisional GMC registration, not full registration. With provisional registration, you can work in approved Foundation Year 1 posts or equivalent supervised posts in the UK. Full registration is granted after you satisfactorily complete your provisional registration period — typically a one-year Foundation Programme. Full registration is required before you can work unsupervised as a doctor in the UK. IMGs entering via the International Foundation Programme (IFP) follow a similar pathway.

What does good character mean in the context of UKMLA eligibility?

Good character for GMC registration purposes means that you do not have any undisclosed criminal convictions, cautions, or regulatory proceedings that would call into question your suitability to practise medicine. You must disclose all cautions and convictions, including spent convictions, when applying to the GMC — this is different from standard employment disclosure rules. You must also disclose any previous fitness to practise proceedings from any medical regulatory body in any country, any periods of suspension or erasure from a medical register, and any refusal of medical registration in another country. Non-disclosure is treated very seriously and can result in your application being refused or your registration being revoked if the non-disclosure is discovered later.

Conclusion

Understanding UKMLA eligibility in full is the essential foundation of your UK medical licensing journey. Whether you are a UK medical student whose school manages the process or an IMG navigating qualification verification, English language testing, and GMC applications independently, knowing exactly what is required — and in what order — saves time and prevents costly mistakes.

The key takeaways are: start your MyIntealth verification as early as possible, choose between IELTS and OET based on your strengths, confirm your qualification is on the WDOMS, and be completely transparent in all declarations you make to the GMC.
