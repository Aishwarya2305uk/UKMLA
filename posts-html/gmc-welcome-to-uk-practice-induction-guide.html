GMC Welcome to UK Practice is a free half-day workshop the General Medical Council runs specifically for internationally qualified doctors who are new to working in the NHS, covering the ethical scenarios most likely to catch an international medical graduate (IMG) off guard in UK clinical practice — consent, confidentiality, raising concerns, safeguarding children and young people, and prescribing. It is optional, it is not an exam, and it carries no pass or fail outcome, yet it consistently ranks among the most practically useful sessions a newly registered IMG can attend in their first months in the UK. This guide explains, in plain terms, what the workshop actually covers, why it is worth making time for even though nothing requires you to attend, how to book a place through GMC Online, and what wider induction support your employing NHS trust or deanery should also be offering alongside it.

For doctors who have just cleared GMC registration, or who are counting down the weeks until their UKMLA results convert into a licence to practise, the temptation is to treat registration as the finish line and NHS induction as a formality to get through on the first day of a new job. That is a mistake worth avoiding. The transition from training and working as a doctor in one health system to practising medicine inside the NHS involves more than a change of employer — it involves a different regulatory culture, different patient expectations, different escalation pathways, and, in many cases, a different default answer to questions an IMG assumed were universal across medicine everywhere. Welcome to UK Practice exists precisely to close that gap before it becomes a clinical incident or a fitness-to-practise concern, and this guide sets out exactly how to use it.

What Is GMC Welcome to UK Practice? The Quick Answer

GMC Welcome to UK Practice is a free, half-day interactive workshop run by the General Medical Council for doctors who qualified outside the UK and are new to working in the NHS, delivered both online and face-to-face, and built around real-life ethical scenarios rather than lectures. It is aimed at doctors who are registered with the GMC, or who are shortly due to register, and who have started — or are about to start — working in UK clinical practice. The workshop is not mandatory, does not affect registration status, and cannot be failed; it exists purely to help doctors apply GMC ethical guidance confidently in a UK clinical setting from day one, rather than learning those lessons the hard way through a difficult patient interaction or an avoidable complaint.

Doctors can book a place themselves through their GMC Online account once they qualify, or their employing NHS trust or deanery can support and encourage attendance as part of a structured induction programme. Either route is legitimate — what matters is that the workshop actually gets attended, ideally within the first weeks of starting UK practice rather than deferred indefinitely once the pressures of a new job take over. The GMC's own dedicated workshop information page is the authoritative, up-to-date source for current session dates, formats, and booking access, and should always be checked directly before finalising any attendance plans, since scheduling details are reviewed and refreshed periodically.

Who Welcome to UK Practice Is For

The workshop is targeted deliberately at a specific stage of an IMG's journey, and understanding exactly who it is designed for helps explain why it is worth prioritising rather than treating as optional background noise.

Doctors Who Have Just Registered With the GMC

The most common attendee is a doctor who has recently completed the full GMC registration requirements for international doctors — eligibility, primary source verification, English language evidence, UKMLA success, and a processed registration application — and is now either starting their first UK clinical post or has been in post for a matter of weeks. At this stage, the theory of UK medical ethics has usually been studied for exam purposes, particularly for the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment, but has not yet been tested against a real patient, a real ward, and a real multidisciplinary team under UK working conditions. Welcome to UK Practice is designed to sit in exactly that gap.

Doctors Who Are Soon to Register

A doctor does not need to hold full registration on the day they attend; the workshop is equally aimed at doctors who are shortly due to register and are already preparing for their first UK post, since much of the content — consent conventions, safeguarding expectations, how raising a concern actually works inside an NHS organisation — is just as useful to absorb before starting work as during the first week of it. Doctors preparing for this stage of the journey may also find it useful to read the site's broader guide to the route from UKMLA success to an NHS job, since workshop attendance fits naturally alongside the practical steps of securing and starting a first UK post. It is also worth being realistic about timing: Welcome to UK Practice attendance is planned around the same registration timeline as the rest of the pathway, not treated as a separate task to fit in whenever convenient.

Doctors New to the UK Regardless of Prior Experience

Seniority in a doctor's home health system does not exempt them from benefiting from the workshop. A consultant-grade specialist arriving to work in the NHS faces exactly the same gap between "what UK ethical guidance says on paper" and "how it plays out in a UK ward, GP surgery, or emergency department" as a newly qualified doctor does. The workshop's scenario-based format is designed around this reality — it assumes clinical competence and instead focuses purely on the UK-specific regulatory and ethical framework that surrounds that competence.

What the Workshop Actually Covers

Welcome to UK Practice is built around interactive, real-life ethical scenarios rather than a traditional lecture format, using case studies to bring GMC ethical guidance to life in situations an IMG is genuinely likely to encounter in their first months of UK clinical practice. The scenario-based structure exists specifically because ethical guidance read in the abstract, weeks or months before starting work, rarely transfers cleanly into a fast-moving clinical decision — working through realistic cases in a facilitated group setting closes that gap far more effectively than reading a policy document alone.

Consent

UK consent practice places significant weight on shared decision-making and on a patient's right to make what a clinician might consider an unwise decision, provided the patient has capacity. IMGs arriving from health systems with a more paternalistic or family-mediated approach to consent — where, for example, a family member is routinely consulted before a patient, or where a doctor's recommendation is expected to be followed rather than debated — often find this the single biggest adjustment of their early UK practice. The workshop works through consent scenarios explicitly to surface this difference before it becomes a real clinical friction point.

Confidentiality

Patient confidentiality in the UK carries specific legal and professional weight, including tightly defined circumstances in which information can be shared with family members, employers, or other third parties without explicit patient consent. Doctors from systems with looser or more informal confidentiality norms — where, for instance, discussing a patient's condition with an accompanying relative by default is unremarkable — benefit from working through confidentiality scenarios that make the UK boundary explicit and memorable.

Raising Concerns

The GMC places a professional duty on every doctor to raise concerns about patient safety, and UK healthcare culture generally expects this duty to be acted on through defined organisational channels rather than absorbed silently. For IMGs arriving from systems with a steeper hierarchy, where junior doctors are discouraged — explicitly or culturally — from challenging senior colleagues, this can be one of the hardest adjustments to internalise, precisely because it runs against years of prior professional conditioning. The workshop's raising-concerns scenarios are designed to make this expectation concrete: what a concern actually looks like in practice, and which internal route it should travel through.

Care for Children and Young People

Consent, confidentiality, and safeguarding all carry additional layers of complexity when the patient is a child or young person, including UK-specific frameworks such as Gillick competence for assessing whether a young person can consent to their own treatment. These frameworks differ meaningfully from the age-based or purely parental-consent models used in many other health systems, and the workshop dedicates specific scenario time to this area given how frequently it arises across GP, paediatric, and emergency settings alike.

Prescribing

Prescribing scenarios in the workshop typically explore the GMC's expectations around safe, responsible prescribing practice — including where a doctor's personal or cultural expectations about medication use (for example, patient expectations of antibiotics for viral illness, or differing regional norms around opioid or benzodiazepine prescribing) may not align with UK prescribing guidance and antimicrobial stewardship expectations. This is a practical, everyday area where a scenario-based discussion tends to be far more useful than reading formulary guidance alone.

Beyond these core ethical scenario areas, the workshop also gives attendees practical guidance on interacting and communicating with patients and colleagues in a UK clinical setting, and explores more broadly how UK health services differ structurally from health systems elsewhere — differences that shape not just ethics but day-to-day working relationships, referral pathways, and expectations of documentation.

What Actually Happens on the Day

A typical Welcome to UK Practice session runs as a facilitated group discussion built around a small number of detailed case studies, not a slideshow of GMC rules read aloud. Understanding the format in advance helps a first-time attendee get more out of the half-day, since the workshop rewards active participation rather than passive listening.

Sessions are generally facilitated by GMC staff or trained associates with direct clinical or educational background, and group sizes are kept deliberately manageable so that every attendee has a genuine opportunity to contribute rather than sitting through a one-way presentation. A typical session structure looks roughly like this:

  • A short introduction framing why the workshop exists and what it is not — not an assessment, not a GMC compliance check, and not a re-test of clinical knowledge.
  • A series of case-study scenarios, each covering one of the core ethical themes (consent, confidentiality, raising concerns, safeguarding, prescribing), presented as a realistic situation with no single obviously "correct" answer.
  • Small-group or whole-group discussion of how attendees would respond, followed by facilitator input connecting the discussion back to specific GMC guidance.
  • Open discussion time, where attendees are encouraged to raise their own real or anticipated situations, drawing on the shared experience of a room full of doctors navigating the same transition.
  • A closing summary of key takeaways and signposting to further GMC resources and guidance documents for anyone who wants to explore a particular theme in more depth afterwards.

Because the format depends on genuine discussion, attendees who come prepared to share their own perspective — including, where relevant, how a similar situation would typically be handled in the health system they trained in — tend to get considerably more value from the session than attendees who treat it as a passive lecture to sit through. The GMC has published anonymised example case studies illustrating the kind of scenario used, which is a useful way to get a feel for the workshop's style before attending.

Why It Is Worth Attending, Even Though It's Optional

Because Welcome to UK Practice carries no registration consequence for skipping it, it is easy for a newly arrived doctor, already juggling a new job, new accommodation, and often a new country, to deprioritise it indefinitely. Three reasons make that a false economy.

  • The scenarios map directly onto real early-career risk areas. Consent, confidentiality, raising concerns, and safeguarding are precisely the areas where an avoidable misstep by an otherwise clinically excellent doctor can escalate into a complaint or, in serious cases, a referral that touches on how GMC fitness-to-practise concerns are actually handled. Attending the workshop is one of the most effective, low-cost ways to reduce that risk before it materialises.
  • Independent evaluation has found it changes real-world behaviour, not just awareness. The GMC's own published evaluation reported that doctors showed measurably improved awareness and understanding of key ethical issues immediately after attending, and — more tellingly — within three months of the workshop, a substantial majority reported having actually changed something about how they worked as a direct result. That is a materially different outcome from most one-off training sessions, which tend to raise awareness without changing behaviour.
  • It offers peer connection that is genuinely hard to replicate elsewhere. Face-to-face sessions in particular bring together doctors who have all very recently made the same transition from another health system into UK practice, creating a rare space to compare notes, normalise the adjustment period, and build a small informal support network — something that matters more than it might sound, given how isolating the first months of practising medicine in an unfamiliar system can be.

The workshop has, over more than a decade of delivery, become a well-established and widely attended part of the UK's induction landscape for IMGs, run in all four UK nations and attended by IMGs in the thousands over its lifetime. Exact current attendance figures are best confirmed directly on the GMC's own website rather than quoted from any third-party source, since the programme has continued to run and expand since its original figures were first published.

How to Book a Place

Doctors book Welcome to UK Practice through their GMC Online account, which is the same account used throughout the registration journey for tracking EPIC verification, exam results, and the registration application itself — the same GMC Online account behind the UKMLA exam booking process earlier in your journey. In practice, doctors who qualified outside the UK typically receive a direct email from the GMC once their registration has been granted, inviting them to view available workshop dates and book a place through GMC Online — so keeping the email address linked to a GMC Online account current and checked regularly matters just as much during this stage as it did earlier in the registration process.

Steps to Book Directly

  • Log in to GMC Online using the same credentials created earlier in the registration journey.
  • Locate the Welcome to UK Practice booking section, where currently available online and face-to-face session dates are listed.
  • Choose a format — face-to-face where a convenient location and date exist, or online if travel or shift patterns make an in-person session impractical.
  • Confirm the booking and add the session to a personal calendar well in advance, since face-to-face dates in particular can fill given the workshop's popularity.

Booking Through an Employer or Deanery

Doctors do not have to arrange attendance independently. NHS trusts, deaneries, and other employing organisations are actively encouraged by the GMC to offer Welcome to UK Practice as a standard part of their own international medical graduate induction programme, and many organisations block-book sessions or coordinate attendance for cohorts of newly arrived IMGs as a matter of course. If an employer has not proactively mentioned the workshop, it is entirely reasonable — and, per the checklist below, advisable — for a doctor to ask their induction lead, education supervisor, or international recruitment team directly whether organised attendance is available before booking independently.

Choosing Between Online and Face-to-Face

Both formats cover the same core ethical scenario content, so neither is a lesser option content-wise. The practical difference lies in delivery: face-to-face sessions offer deeper immersion in group discussion and the peer-connection benefit described above, while online sessions offer scheduling flexibility that particularly suits doctors already juggling shift patterns, on-call commitments, or geographic distance from a face-to-face venue. Doctors with a genuine choice between the two, and no strong scheduling constraint, may find the face-to-face format slightly more valuable for the peer-network reason alone — but an online session attended promptly is unambiguously better than a face-to-face session deferred indefinitely.

The Wider Induction Landscape: What Else to Expect (and Ask For)

Welcome to UK Practice is one component of a broader, minimum-standard induction that every NHS employer should be offering IMGs, not a substitute for the rest of it. GMC and NHS guidance on supporting international medical graduates — most notably the "Welcoming and Valuing International Medical Graduates" guidance developed for NHS organisations — sets out several distinct strands of induction that should sit alongside the ethics workshop, each addressing a different practical gap in an IMG's transition.

Welcome and Pastoral Induction

This strand covers the human, non-clinical side of relocating to work in the UK: orientation to the local area, help navigating practical logistics such as housing, banking, and registering with a GP, and a named point of contact for pastoral support during the early weeks. It is easy for a busy employing organisation to under-invest in this strand precisely because it is not clinical, but its absence is consistently reported by IMGs as one of the hardest parts of the transition, disproportionate to how little it typically costs an organisation to provide well.

Professional Practice Induction

This covers the clinical-culture adjustment described throughout this guide: escalation pathways, documentation standards and expectations, ward-round conventions, how multidisciplinary teams operate in a UK setting, and local departmental protocols that may differ meaningfully from a doctor's prior training environment. Welcome to UK Practice contributes directly to this strand through its ethical scenario content, but a genuinely complete professional practice induction goes further, covering department-specific and organisation-specific procedures the national workshop cannot address.

IT and Electronic Patient Record Systems Induction

Every NHS trust runs its own electronic patient record (EPR) system, and competent, safe use of that system — prescribing, documenting, requesting investigations, handing over care — is a genuine patient-safety issue, not merely an administrative inconvenience. A doctor who has never used a UK EPR system before their first shift is operating at a real disadvantage, and this strand of induction should be delivered, and ideally supervised in practice, before a doctor is expected to work unsupported.

Language and Communication Induction

Passing the English language requirement for registration — whether through an IELTS or OET route — demonstrates a strong baseline of English proficiency, but it does not automatically cover UK-specific medical terminology conventions, regional accents and dialects a doctor will encounter from patients, or the softer communication norms UK patients often expect (for example, the degree of directness or indirectness considered appropriate when delivering difficult news). A dedicated language and communication induction strand bridges this gap specifically, and is distinct from — and additional to — the English test itself.

Guidance and Support for Supervisors of IMGs

The final strand is not aimed at the IMG directly but at the educational and clinical supervisors responsible for them, equipping supervisors to recognise the specific adjustment challenges IMGs face and to provide meaningfully tailored support rather than treating an IMG's early performance or communication style as equivalent to that of a UK-trained doctor at the same career stage. A well-briefed supervisor is one of the single biggest positive influences on how smoothly an IMG's first year in UK practice goes, and it is entirely reasonable for an IMG to ask, early on, whether their supervisor has received this kind of guidance.

Checklist: What to Ask Your Employer or Deanery For

Not every organisation offers every strand of induction proactively or at the same standard, and it is entirely appropriate — expected, even — for an IMG to ask directly rather than assume support will simply appear. The following checklist is worth raising explicitly with an induction lead, education supervisor, or HR/international recruitment contact in the first week of a new UK post, or ideally before starting:

  • Confirm whether GMC Welcome to UK Practice attendance is arranged for you, or whether you need to book independently through GMC Online.
  • Ask for a named pastoral contact for practical, non-clinical support during the first few months.
  • Request a structured professional practice induction covering local escalation pathways, documentation expectations, and department-specific protocols, not just a generic trust-wide welcome talk.
  • Confirm supervised practice time on the trust's electronic patient record system before being expected to use it unsupported on a live shift.
  • Ask whether your named clinical or educational supervisor has received training or guidance specific to supporting IMGs.
  • Clarify who to approach, and through which route, if you want to raise a patient safety concern — this should be explained proactively, not left for a doctor to discover only when a concern actually arises.
  • Ask whether a language and communication induction session is offered, particularly around UK-specific clinical terminology and communication norms, separate from the English test itself.
  • Request contact details for other recently arrived IMGs at the same organisation, if a formal peer-support or buddy scheme exists.

If an employer cannot confirm most of these on request, that is itself useful information — it does not mean the job or organisation is unsafe, but it does mean an IMG may need to be more proactive about seeking out equivalent support independently, starting with booking Welcome to UK Practice directly through GMC Online rather than waiting for it to be offered.

It is easy for a newly arrived IMG to lose track of which piece of support covers which gap, particularly when a first UK post arrives with a flurry of separate welcome emails, e-learning links, and one-off sessions. Treating the two as complementary, rather than assuming one implies the other, is the safest way for an IMG to audit what they have actually been given.

How This Fits Into the Wider Registration and Career Pathway

Welcome to UK Practice sits at a specific point in a much longer journey, and it is worth briefly placing it in that context. Before reaching this stage, most IMGs will already have worked through the different types of GMC registration available to them, decided on their English language testing route, and passed both components of the UKMLA — a process covered in detail in the site's guide to UKMLA preparation for IMGs and in the site's broader exam preparation resources. Once registration is granted and a first UK post is secured, the ethics and induction content covered in this guide becomes directly relevant, typically within the same window as starting work and completing employer-led induction.

It is also worth understanding, honestly, that the workshop is not a substitute for ongoing professional vigilance. UK ethical guidance continues to evolve, GMC guidance documents are updated periodically, and no single half-day session — however well designed — can pre-empt every scenario a doctor will encounter across a full career in UK practice. Attending Welcome to UK Practice is best understood as a strong, evidence-backed head start rather than a one-off box to tick and never revisit; many of the ethical principles it introduces are worth returning to, informally, whenever a genuinely unfamiliar situation arises in day-to-day practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is GMC Welcome to UK Practice mandatory for IMGs?

No. Welcome to UK Practice is entirely optional and attending, or not attending, has no bearing on GMC registration status. It is strongly recommended as good practice and is often built into an employer's own induction programme, but there is no regulatory requirement to attend, and there is no pass, fail, or assessed outcome involved.

How long does the Welcome to UK Practice workshop last?

It is a half-day workshop, delivered as a single interactive session either online or face-to-face. The exact session length can vary slightly by format and location, so the current schedule for a specific date should always be confirmed at the point of booking through GMC Online.

Do I need to already be GMC registered to attend?

Not necessarily. The workshop is aimed at doctors who are registered with the GMC or who are soon to be registered and are preparing to start UK clinical practice. Doctors in the final stages of registration, with a confirmed or imminent start date for a UK post, are a normal part of the intended audience, not an exception.

How do I book a place on Welcome to UK Practice?

Bookings are made through a doctor's GMC Online account, where available online and face-to-face session dates are listed once registration has been granted or is imminent. Many doctors receive a direct email invitation from the GMC at this stage; alternatively, an employing NHS trust or deanery may arrange attendance as part of its own induction programme, in which case a doctor should check with their induction lead before booking independently.

Is Welcome to UK Practice available online, or only in person?

Both. The workshop runs as face-to-face sessions across all four UK nations and as online sessions for doctors who cannot attend in person. Both formats cover the same core ethical scenario content; the choice is mainly a matter of scheduling convenience versus the additional peer-connection benefit of an in-person session.

What topics does the workshop cover?

The workshop uses real-life, interactive scenarios covering consent, confidentiality, raising concerns, care for children and young people, and prescribing, alongside broader guidance on communicating with patients and colleagues in a UK setting and how UK health services differ structurally from other countries' systems. It is built around applying GMC ethical guidance to situations an IMG is realistically likely to face in their first months of UK practice.

Does attending Welcome to UK Practice help with fitness-to-practise risk?

Indirectly, yes. The scenarios covered — consent, confidentiality, raising concerns, safeguarding, and prescribing — are precisely the areas where an avoidable misunderstanding of UK norms can escalate into a complaint or, in more serious cases, a matter that touches on fitness-to-practise processes. Attending will not guarantee anything, but independent evaluation has found it measurably improves doctors' understanding of these issues and changes real working behaviour within months of attendance.

What if my employer has never mentioned Welcome to UK Practice?

This is common and does not mean anything is wrong with the job or the employer, but it does mean a doctor should not assume attendance will happen automatically. The most reliable option is to check directly for a booking invitation inside a GMC Online account, since eligible doctors are typically contacted directly by the GMC once registration is granted, rather than always relying on an employer to raise it first. Asking an induction lead or education supervisor directly is also a reasonable, low-friction way to confirm whether organised attendance already exists.

Can experienced or senior doctors skip the workshop since it seems aimed at newly qualified doctors?

The workshop is not aimed only at newly qualified doctors; it is aimed at anyone new to UK practice, regardless of seniority or years of prior clinical experience elsewhere. A consultant-grade specialist arriving from another health system faces the same UK-specific ethical and cultural adjustment as a more junior doctor, since clinical seniority does not automatically transfer familiarity with UK-specific consent, confidentiality, and escalation norms.

Conclusion

GMC Welcome to UK Practice is a small time investment — a single free half-day session — set against what it protects: a smoother, safer, more confident start to practising medicine in an unfamiliar regulatory and clinical culture. It will not replace a properly structured employer induction, and it should not be treated as one, but as one well-evidenced strand within a wider induction landscape that every newly registered or soon-to-register IMG is entitled to expect, and entitled to ask for directly if it is not offered proactively. Booking a place through GMC Online, and raising the induction checklist above with an employer or deanery early, are two of the most practical steps a newly registered IMG can take in their first weeks of UK practice.

Doctors who are earlier in the journey — still weighing up eligibility, deciding between IELTS and OET, or preparing for the UKMLA itself — will find that this guide sits at the far end of a much longer pathway covered in depth elsewhere on this site. Reading ahead to GMC Welcome to UK Practice and the wider induction landscape described here, even before registration is complete, can help frame exam and registration preparation as one continuous process rather than a series of disconnected milestones, with UK clinical practice itself as the ultimate destination rather than an afterthought.
