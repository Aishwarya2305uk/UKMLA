Effective PLAB 2 preparation is what separates candidates who pass the GMC's clinical assessment on their first attempt from those who face the expense and stress of a resit. PLAB 2 — formally the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) in the UKMLA framework — is a high-stakes OSCE held exclusively in Manchester that tests not just clinical knowledge but communication, empathy, professionalism, and safe practice across a circuit of 16 clinical stations. With a pass rate of approximately 70-75%, one in four candidates fails on their first attempt, and most of those failures come down to communication skills and preparation method rather than lack of clinical knowledge.

This guide covers every dimension of PLAB 2 preparation: the exam's circuit structure, types of stations, marking methodology, preparation timelines, recommended resources, logistics in Manchester, and the most common mistakes to avoid. Whether you have three months or twelve weeks, this is the preparation framework that works.

What PLAB 2 Assesses: The OSCE Format and MLA Content Map

PLAB 2 is an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) held at the GMC's clinical assessment centre in Manchester. There are currently two PLAB 2 test centres, both located in Manchester city centre. The exam circuit consists of 16 scored stations plus at least two rest stations, making for a total examination time of just over three hours.

Each station lasts 8 minutes, with 90 seconds between stations to read the next station brief. The brief is posted on the door of the station and gives you the clinical scenario you are about to enter. You must read it quickly but carefully — candidates who misread the brief and enter the wrong type of station (for example, treating a counselling station as a history-taking station) lose marks immediately.

The content is drawn from the MLA Content Map, the same blueprint used for the AKT. Stations are drawn from all clinical domains: acute medicine, primary care, surgery, obstetrics and gynaecology, paediatrics, psychiatry, and more. The GMC does not publish a specific station list, but analysis of candidate experiences shows that history-taking, communication and counselling, examination, and breaking bad news are the most frequently occurring station types.

How PLAB 2 Scoring Works

Understanding the marking system is fundamental to PLAB 2 preparation because it tells you where to invest your effort. Each station is assessed across three domains:

  1. Data gathering / Technical skills: How effectively you gathered clinical information (history, examination findings) or performed the technical procedure.
  2. Clinical management: Your ability to synthesise information, form a differential diagnosis, and propose an appropriate management plan including safety-netting.
  3. Interpersonal skills: Your communication, empathy, rapport, respect, and professionalism in your interaction with the patient (actor).

The UKMLA CPSA uses borderline regression methodology for standard-setting. Rather than a fixed pass mark, the pass mark is calculated statistically based on the distribution of scores across candidates and examiner global ratings. This means the pass mark can vary slightly between sittings. To pass, you must typically pass at least 10 out of 16 stations AND achieve an overall total score above the statistically determined threshold.

Crucially, interpersonal skills carry disproportionate weight in the examiner's global rating. Research consistently shows that the global impression an examiner forms of a candidate is heavily influenced by their communication quality, even when technical data collection is good. This is why communication-focused preparation is the highest-leverage investment you can make.

The ICE Framework in History-Taking Stations

The ICE framework — Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations — is non-negotiable in PLAB 2 history-taking stations. Every history you take must include all three components to score fully on the interpersonal skills domain. ICE is not just a box-ticking exercise; it represents genuine patient-centred practice and scores marks across multiple domains simultaneously.

  • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing your symptoms?" — Acknowledges the patient's own thinking and builds rapport.
  • Concerns: "Is there anything in particular you're worried about?" — Opens the door to the patient's underlying anxiety (often the key issue in the station).
  • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" — Clarifies the patient's goals for the consultation.

In breaking bad news stations, a modified approach is used: Warn first ("I have some difficult news to share"), pause, deliver the news clearly, then use ICE to explore the patient's response. Never rush through ICE — take your time, allow silence, and respond to what the patient says rather than moving immediately to your next planned question.

History-Taking Stations: A Detailed Approach

History-taking stations follow a consistent structure, and memorising this structure until it becomes automatic frees your cognitive bandwidth to actually listen to and respond to the patient. The SOCRATES framework is the backbone for symptom inquiry, supplemented by systematic review of systems, relevant past medical history, drug history, family history, and social history.

Begin every history station with an open question: "Tell me what's brought you in today." Allow the patient (actor) to speak for at least 30 seconds without interruption. This gives you information, shows patient-centred practice, and gives you a moment to calm your nerves. Then systematically use SOCRATES to explore the presenting complaint, followed by targeted systems review relevant to the likely differentials.

Always ask about red flag symptoms relevant to the presenting complaint. Examiners notice if you miss red flags — for example, failing to ask about weight loss, night sweats, or haemoptysis in a patient presenting with chronic cough is a significant omission that suggests poor clinical reasoning. Practise identifying the three or four most important red flags for the twenty most common presenting complaints before your exam.

Communication, Counselling, and Breaking Bad News

Communication stations are where PLAB 2 is won or lost. The technical content of what you say matters less than how you say it. Examiners assess empathy, clarity, pacing, non-verbal cues (in face-to-face stations), and whether you genuinely engage with the patient's emotional responses rather than bulldozing through a scripted explanation.

A useful structure for any explanation or counselling station is: Establish what the patient already knows, Fill in gaps with clear, jargon-free information (use chunk-and-check — give a piece of information, pause, check understanding), Address emotions as they arise, Provide a safety net and next steps, and Summarise.

Phrases that score well in communication stations include: "I can see that's very upsetting, and I'm sorry you're going through this"; "Before I explain the treatment, can I check what you already understand about your condition?"; "That's a really important question — let me explain"; "Is there anything you're particularly worried about that we haven't discussed?"; and "I want to make sure I've explained that clearly — can you tell me back what you understand we've discussed?"

Phrases that lose marks include jargon without explanation ("You have a PE and we need anticoagulation"), failing to acknowledge patient emotions, rushing through explanations without checking understanding, and inappropriate use of technical language ("Your ECG shows ST elevation with reciprocal changes in the inferior leads") without translating into lay terms.

Practical Procedure and Examination Stations

For examination stations, use a systematic approach every time. The examiner expects to see a standard UK clinical examination framework — wash hands, introduce yourself, gain consent, expose appropriately, and follow the standard sequence (inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation for cardiovascular and respiratory; inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation for abdominal). Do not skip any step even if you think there is nothing to find — the structure itself scores marks.

For procedure stations involving manikins, talk through what you are doing as you do it. "I'm going to clean the skin with an alcohol swab in a circular motion and allow it to dry" — narrating your actions demonstrates safety awareness and clinical reasoning even when the technical execution is being assessed. Candidates who perform silently often score lower than those who speak throughout, even if their technique is equivalent.

PLAB 2 Preparation Timeline

12-Week Preparation Plan

  • Weeks 1-2: Read PLAB 2 preparation textbook (Saeed's or 1000 PLAB 2 OSCE Cases), review station types and marking criteria, begin practising station openings and closings aloud.
  • Weeks 3-6: Daily practice with a partner — ideally two to three stations per day minimum. Focus on history-taking and communication. Record yourself and watch back to identify verbal and non-verbal habits.
  • Weeks 7-9: Expand to all station types including examinations and procedures. Practice full 16-station mock circuits. Join a preparation course if budget allows.
  • Weeks 10-11: Full mock exams under exam conditions (8 minutes per station, 90-second transitions). Debrief every station. Focus on eliminating remaining habits that lose marks.
  • Week 12: Light revision, confidence-building, and logistics preparation (Manchester accommodation, route to test centre).

8-Week Plan

Compress weeks 1-2 and 3-6 into weeks 1-4 with more intensive daily practice. This requires at least three to four hours of active practice per day and is only feasible if you are not working clinically during preparation. Mock circuits from week 5.

Preparation Courses and Resources

PLAB 2 preparation courses provide structured practice with simulated patients and experienced feedback. When choosing a course, look for: small group sizes (so you get adequate practice time per person), experienced OSCE examiners or PLAB 2 passers as tutors, structured feedback on communication skills specifically, and full mock circuits as part of the course rather than just station practice.

Recommended resources include: Saeed's 1000 PLAB 2 OSCE Cases (the standard reference text), clinical examination guides (Talley and O'Connor or Macleod's), the GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance (particularly for ethics stations), and online case banks from established PLAB 2 providers. TalkingCases.ai and similar platforms offer digital practice scenarios that supplement face-to-face preparation but should not replace it — PLAB 2 is ultimately a human interaction test.

Manchester Logistics: What to Know Before You Arrive

PLAB 2 takes place at two GMC-operated assessment centres in Manchester. Both are in the city centre, accessible by public transport from Manchester Piccadilly station. Allow at least 30 minutes extra for travel on exam day — do not arrive with zero margin. Test centre locations and reporting times are confirmed in your admission documentation from the GMC.

Accommodation: Book in Manchester city centre for the night before your exam. Avoid travelling from another city on the morning of the exam — the anxiety and fatigue of long-distance travel before a three-hour OSCE is a significant disadvantage. Many PLAB 2 candidates stay in the same hotels near the test centres and form informal revision groups the evening before — this peer practice can be valuable but avoid late-night cramming that compromises sleep.

What to wear: Dress professionally and appropriately for a clinical setting. Business casual or smart professional attire is appropriate — a collared shirt or blouse, trousers or skirt, and clean shoes. For examination stations, you may be examined by the simulated patient — ensure your clothing allows easy demonstration of examination positioning. Avoid dangling jewellery that could interfere with procedures. Ties should be tucked away or absent (infection control awareness is noted by examiners).

After PLAB 2: Results and What Happens Next

PLAB 2 results are released approximately eight to twelve weeks after your sitting date via GMC Online. You will receive an email notification when results are available. If you pass, you will receive a pass certificate and instructions for proceeding to GMC registration. If you fail, you will receive a detailed feedback report highlighting performance by domain across stations — read this carefully before planning your resit strategy.

After passing PLAB 2, you can apply for provisional GMC registration, which allows you to work in approved Foundation-level posts in the UK. This application is made via GMC Online and typically takes two to four weeks to process. See our results and scoring page for a full breakdown of what comes after passing both PLAB components.

Common PLAB 2 Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

  • Treating it as a knowledge test: The biggest mistake. Examiners know you know the clinical content. They are assessing whether you can communicate and practise safely. Prioritise communication practice over reading clinical textbooks in your final weeks of preparation.
  • Scripting responses: Candidates who memorise scripts sound robotic and fail to respond to what the patient actually says. Practice frameworks, not scripts.
  • Forgetting to safety-net: Every station should end with a safety-netting statement — what the patient should do if things get worse, when to come back, or who to contact. Missing this is a frequent, avoidable mark-loser.
  • Not managing time: Eight minutes passes very quickly. Practice pacing your consultations so that you reach a natural conclusion with 30-60 seconds to spare for summary and safety-netting, rather than being cut off mid-history.
  • Ignoring the station brief: The 90-second reading time before each station is precious. Use it fully. Identify the station type, the key clinical scenario, and the likely focus area before you open the door.

Frequently Asked Questions About PLAB 2 Preparation

How many stations are in PLAB 2 and how long does the exam take?

PLAB 2 consists of 16 scored clinical stations plus at least two rest stations, giving a total of 18 or more stations in the circuit. Each station lasts 8 minutes, with 90 seconds between stations. Including rest stations, the total examination time is approximately three hours and ten to fifteen minutes. You must maintain focus and professionalism throughout the entire circuit — examiner fatigue or candidate fatigue late in the circuit is a real phenomenon, and maintaining energy and communication quality throughout is part of what PLAB 2 tests.

What is the pass rate for PLAB 2?

The pass rate for PLAB 2 hovers around 70 to 75%. This means approximately one in four candidates fails on their first attempt. The most common reasons for failure are poor communication skills rather than lack of clinical knowledge, poor time management within stations, and failure to use a patient-centred approach. Candidates who invest heavily in communication practice — particularly with a partner role-playing as the patient — perform significantly better than those who focus primarily on reading clinical content.

Can I prepare for PLAB 2 on my own without a course?

It is possible but significantly harder to prepare effectively without face-to-face practice with a partner. PLAB 2 tests human interaction skills that cannot be adequately practised by reading or watching videos alone. At minimum, find a preparation partner — ideally another PLAB 2 candidate — and practise stations together with feedback. If your budget allows, a structured course with experienced tutors and simulated patients provides feedback that is difficult to replicate through self-study. Many successful candidates use a hybrid approach: self-study from textbooks and case banks plus a focused one-week or two-week intensive course in the final preparation phase.

How soon after passing PLAB 1 should I sit PLAB 2?

You must sit PLAB 2 within two years of passing PLAB 1. Beyond this technical requirement, most candidates benefit from a preparation period of three to four months between PLAB 1 and PLAB 2. Sitting too soon — within weeks of PLAB 1 without dedicated OSCE preparation — is the most common cause of first-attempt PLAB 2 failure. Book your PLAB 2 slot as soon as you receive your PLAB 1 pass result (given the four-to-six-month waiting time for slots), and use the time between booking and your sitting for structured preparation.

What happens if I fail PLAB 2?

If you fail PLAB 2, you will receive a detailed feedback report highlighting your performance by domain across stations. Review this report carefully before planning your resit. Identify whether your failures were consistent (suggesting a systematic issue with your approach) or station-specific (suggesting gaps in particular clinical areas). Most candidates who fail PLAB 2 do so due to communication and interpersonal skills deficits rather than clinical knowledge gaps — adjust your preparation accordingly. You can rebook PLAB 2 for the next available slot. The same two-year validity window applies to the resit — as long as your PLAB 1 pass has not expired, you can resit. See our resits and appeals guide for full information on attempt limits and resit strategy.

Conclusion

Successful PLAB 2 preparation is built on communication practice, structured clinical technique, and Manchester logistics. The candidates who pass on their first attempt are not necessarily the most clinically knowledgeable — they are the ones who have practised their consultations until patient-centred communication feels natural rather than scripted, who know how to manage eight minutes without rushing, and who arrive in Manchester rested and ready. Use the preparation timeline in this guide, prioritise face-to-face practice with a partner or course, and treat every practice station as if it were the real exam.
