Quick Answer: What Are the Key Early Pregnancy Complications?

Early pregnancy complications UKMLA topics centre on four core diagnoses that occur before 13 weeks of gestation: miscarriage (spontaneous loss of an intrauterine pregnancy, subclassified as threatened, inevitable, incomplete, complete, or missed), ectopic pregnancy (implantation outside the uterine cavity, most commonly in the fallopian tube), hyperemesis gravidarum (severe, dehydrating nausea and vomiting that exceeds normal pregnancy sickness), and molar pregnancy (a form of gestational trophoblastic disease arising from abnormal fertilisation). All four typically present with some combination of vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, and a positive pregnancy test in the first trimester, which is exactly why the AKT tests them together as a differential diagnosis cluster rather than in isolation.

The single most important learning point across all four conditions is this: any woman of reproductive age with a positive pregnancy test, abdominal pain, and/or vaginal bleeding must have ectopic pregnancy actively excluded before any other diagnosis is entertained, because it is the only one of the four that can kill within hours through catastrophic intra-abdominal haemorrhage. The table further down in this guide sets out the miscarriage classifications side by side, but the ectopic "red flag" principle sits above all of them in clinical priority.

Miscarriage: Classification and Management

Miscarriage is defined as the spontaneous loss of a pregnancy before 24 weeks of gestation, though the vast majority of miscarriages — around 80% — occur in the first trimester, before 13 weeks. It is thought to affect around one in five to one in six recognised pregnancies, making it one of the most common experiences in early pregnancy care, and something every foundation doctor will encounter repeatedly regardless of specialty. Because miscarriage is so common and so often emotionally significant for the patient, the tone taken when discussing it — both in clinical practice and in how this guide is written — matters as much as the clinical facts themselves.

The UKMLA content map groups miscarriage under early pregnancy loss, and questions typically test two things: the ability to classify the type of miscarriage from a clinical vignette (history, examination findings, and ultrasound description), and the ability to select the appropriate management pathway (expectant, medical, or surgical) based on that classification and the patient's preferences and clinical stability. Getting comfortable with this classification is one of the most efficient ways to bank marks on early pregnancy complications UKMLA stations, because the same five-way split reappears in slightly different clinical dress across dozens of past questions.

It is also worth situating miscarriage within the wider picture of reproductive healthcare that the UKMLA expects candidates to understand holistically. A woman who has just experienced a miscarriage will often, once she has recovered physically and emotionally, want to discuss future fertility plans and contraceptive choices, and being able to signpost sensitively to that conversation — covered in detail in the site's dedicated guide to contraceptive method counselling and shared decision-making — is part of delivering genuinely patient-centred early pregnancy care, not a separate, unrelated topic.

The Five Classic Types of Miscarriage

Threatened miscarriage describes vaginal bleeding, often light and painless or with mild cramping, in a woman with a confirmed intrauterine pregnancy, where the cervical os remains closed on examination and the fetus (or fetal pole) is still viable on ultrasound with a detectable fetal heartbeat. Around half of threatened miscarriages will progress to a completed pregnancy loss, but the other half continue successfully to term, which is why reassurance combined with safety-netting is the right approach rather than immediate intervention.

Inevitable miscarriage occurs when the cervical os is open, often with heavier bleeding and more significant cramping pain, but products of conception have not yet been passed. The pregnancy is not viable at this stage and progression to incomplete or complete miscarriage is expected, usually within hours to a few days.

Incomplete miscarriage describes the situation where some but not all products of conception have been passed, typically with an open cervical os, ongoing bleeding, and retained tissue visible on transvaginal ultrasound (often described as heterogeneous material within the endometrial cavity, sometimes with an endometrial thickness greater than 15 mm used as a rough threshold for considering intervention). Bleeding can be heavy and, uncommonly, can cause haemodynamic compromise if retained tissue is substantial.

Complete miscarriage means all products of conception have been fully passed, the cervical os is closed, bleeding has usually settled to light spotting or stopped altogether, and the uterus is empty on ultrasound. Clinically this can be difficult to distinguish from an ectopic pregnancy with a spontaneously resolving pregnancy of unknown location, which is why a clear history of passing recognisable products of conception, together with falling serum beta-hCG, is reassuring.

Missed miscarriage (also called a delayed or silent miscarriage) is diagnosed when the fetus has died or the pregnancy has stopped developing, but the products of conception have not been passed and the cervical os remains closed. The woman may have had no bleeding at all and may only discover the loss at a routine dating or booking scan, which can be a particularly distressing way to receive the news because there were often no preceding symptoms. Ultrasound findings that confirm a missed miscarriage include an empty gestational sac with a mean sac diameter of 25 mm or more (an "anembryonic pregnancy" or "blighted ovum"), or a fetal pole with a crown-rump length of 7 mm or more with no visible fetal heartbeat. Because early pregnancy dating can be uncertain, UK Early Pregnancy Assessment Units follow a strict rescan protocol — a repeat scan at least 7 days later — before confirming non-viability if the findings are borderline, precisely to avoid the tragedy of intervening on a pregnancy that was simply less advanced than assumed.

Expectant, Medical, and Surgical Management

NICE guidance, set out in NICE guideline NG126 on ectopic pregnancy and miscarriage, recommends that expectant management should be offered as the first-line option for 7 to 14 days in women without risk factors for haemorrhage or infection, since the majority of miscarriages will complete spontaneously within this window without any further intervention. This approach avoids the risks associated with surgery or medication and is often the option many women prefer once the diagnosis has been explained sensitively and they understand what to expect in terms of bleeding and pain.

Medical management uses vaginal (or oral) misoprostol, a prostaglandin analogue that causes the cervix to soften and the uterus to contract, expelling the retained tissue. It is typically offered when expectant management has failed, is declined, or is clinically inappropriate (for example, if bleeding has already been heavy). Women should be warned to expect increased bleeding and cramping pain over the following days and should be given clear guidance on when to seek urgent review, including if bleeding soaks more than two sanitary pads an hour for two consecutive hours.

Surgical management involves either manual vacuum aspiration under local anaesthetic (increasingly offered as an outpatient procedure and preferred where available, since it avoids general anaesthesia) or evacuation of retained products of conception (ERPC) under general anaesthetic in theatre. Surgical management is indicated when there is heavy or persistent bleeding, haemodynamic instability, evidence of infected retained tissue, or when the woman has a strong preference for a definitive, faster resolution rather than waiting for expectant or medical management to work. Anti-D prophylaxis should be given to all rhesus-negative women undergoing surgical management of miscarriage, and is also considered for medical management beyond 12 weeks — this is a detail the AKT likes to test because it is easy to overlook amid the emotional weight of the consultation.

Whichever pathway is chosen, the decision should always be shared with the woman rather than dictated to her: all three options are clinically reasonable in an uncomplicated miscarriage, and NICE NG126 is explicit that patient preference should guide the choice wherever there is no urgent clinical indication favouring one approach. This is also tested indirectly on the AKT, where "correct" single-best-answers for stable, uncomplicated cases are often "offer expectant management first" rather than jumping straight to intervention.

Recurrent Miscarriage

Recurrent miscarriage is defined as three or more consecutive first-trimester miscarriages and warrants formal investigation, including testing for antiphospholipid syndrome (lupus anticoagulant and anticardiolipin antibodies), parental karyotyping, and pelvic ultrasound to assess for uterine structural abnormalities. Antiphospholipid syndrome is treated in future pregnancies with low-dose aspirin and prophylactic low-molecular-weight heparin, which significantly improves live birth rates. While recurrent miscarriage sits slightly outside the core "single episode" scenarios more commonly tested, the AKT does occasionally include it as a distractor or a follow-up question after an initial miscarriage vignette, so it is worth being aware of the referral threshold even if the detailed work-up is more relevant to specialist practice than day-one foundation competence.

Ectopic Pregnancy: The Emergency That Must Not Be Missed

Ectopic pregnancy is a pregnancy implanted outside the endometrial cavity, most commonly (in around 95% of cases) within the fallopian tube, though it can rarely occur in the ovary, cervix, or abdominal cavity, or within a caesarean section scar. It is the single most dangerous diagnosis in this entire cluster of early pregnancy complications because a ruptured ectopic pregnancy causes rapid intra-abdominal haemorrhage and can lead to hypovolaemic shock and death within a matter of hours if it is not recognised and treated promptly. This is precisely why every AKT candidate must internalise the "red flag" principle: in any woman of reproductive age presenting with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, or collapse, a pregnancy test must be performed and ectopic pregnancy must be actively excluded before any other diagnosis is settled on.

Risk Factors and Classic Presentation

The strongest risk factor for ectopic pregnancy is a previous ectopic pregnancy, which increases the risk of recurrence roughly tenfold. Other significant risk factors include previous pelvic inflammatory disease or salpingitis (through tubal scarring), previous tubal surgery (including sterilisation), the use of an intrauterine device or intrauterine system at the time of conception, in vitro fertilisation (IVF) and other assisted conception techniques, and smoking. However, it is important to note clinically and for exam purposes that many women who present with a ruptured ectopic pregnancy have no identifiable risk factors at all, so the absence of risk factors must never be used to lower clinical suspicion.

The classic presentation is a woman of reproductive age with a short period of amenorrhoea (typically 6 to 8 weeks since her last menstrual period), lower abdominal or pelvic pain (often unilateral, though this is not universal), and vaginal bleeding that is usually darker and lighter than a normal period ("prune juice" in colour and less in volume). Shoulder tip pain is a crucial finding that indicates diaphragmatic irritation from free blood in the peritoneal cavity and should raise strong suspicion of intraperitoneal bleeding. Dizziness, syncope, or a feeling of "impending doom" can indicate significant haemorrhage and haemodynamic compromise. On examination, cervical excitation (pain on moving the cervix during a bimanual examination) and adnexal tenderness are classic findings, though a formal bimanual examination should be deferred or performed with caution if rupture is suspected, since it can theoretically worsen bleeding.

The catastrophic presentation — a ruptured ectopic pregnancy — presents with sudden, severe abdominal pain, signs of hypovolaemic shock (tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, collapse), and a rigid, tender abdomen with signs of peritonism. This is a genuine surgical emergency requiring immediate resuscitation and emergency laparoscopic or open surgery, and recognising this presentation instantly — without waiting for confirmatory investigations to return — is one of the highest-yield, highest-stakes learning points across the entire UKMLA curriculum. The Role of Serial Beta-hCG and Transvaginal Ultrasound

When a woman presents with a positive pregnancy test and no pregnancy is visible on transvaginal ultrasound, this is termed a "pregnancy of unknown location" (PUL), and this is where serial serum beta-hCG measurement becomes central to the diagnostic pathway. In a normal, viable intrauterine pregnancy, beta-hCG should roughly double every 48 hours in early gestation. A rise of less than 63% over 48 hours, or a plateauing or falling level, is suggestive of either a failing intrauterine pregnancy or an ectopic pregnancy, and warrants close monitoring with repeat scanning and beta-hCG measurement, generally in a dedicated Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit (EPAU) rather than through routine primary care follow-up.

A transvaginal ultrasound scan is the first-line imaging investigation once a positive pregnancy test is confirmed, and it should reliably identify an intrauterine gestational sac once beta-hCG reaches what is often called the "discriminatory zone" — commonly cited as approximately 1,500 to 2,000 IU/L, though local protocols vary and this threshold should be interpreted alongside clinical findings rather than in isolation. If beta-hCG is above this level and no intrauterine pregnancy is seen, an ectopic pregnancy must be strongly suspected until proven otherwise, even in the absence of a visible adnexal mass, since the ectopic itself is not always directly visualised on ultrasound. Free fluid in the pouch of Douglas is a supportive but non-specific finding that can indicate haemoperitoneum.

It is worth emphasising for both audiences that a woman with a suspected ectopic pregnancy who is haemodynamically unstable should never be kept waiting for serial bloods or a delayed scan — clinical instability itself is the indication for immediate senior obstetric and gynaecology input and emergency theatre, regardless of what any single beta-hCG value shows. This is one of the clearest examples in the whole curriculum of a situation where "wait and repeat the test" is the wrong answer, and it is a distinction the AKT tests deliberately by including vignettes with subtly different degrees of haemodynamic stability.

Methotrexate vs Surgical Management

Once an ectopic pregnancy is confirmed (or strongly suspected) in a haemodynamically stable woman, there are three broad management options: expectant, medical (methotrexate), and surgical, and the choice depends on beta-hCG level, ultrasound findings (including the size of the ectopic mass and presence of fetal heart activity), pain, and the woman's ongoing fertility wishes.

Expectant management may be appropriate for a small number of women with a low and falling beta-hCG level (typically below around 1,000 to 1,500 IU/L, though this is unit-dependent), minimal symptoms, and a small ectopic mass with no visible heartbeat, monitored closely with serial beta-hCG measurement until it falls to a non-pregnant level.

Methotrexate, a folate antagonist that stops rapidly dividing trophoblastic tissue from proliferating, is offered to haemodynamically stable women who meet strict criteria: a beta-hCG typically below around 1,500 IU/L (again, local thresholds vary, though some units extend this to 5,000 IU/L in favourable cases), no significant pain, no fetal heartbeat visible on ultrasound, and an ectopic mass generally smaller than 35 mm with no evidence of rupture or significant free fluid. Methotrexate is given as a single intramuscular dose based on body surface area, and the woman must be counselled that she needs to avoid another pregnancy for at least 3 months afterwards, avoid alcohol and NSAIDs during treatment, and must be able to reliably attend follow-up appointments for repeat beta-hCG monitoring, since a proportion of women will require a second dose or will still need surgical intervention if levels do not fall as expected.

Surgical management, most commonly salpingectomy (removal of the affected fallopian tube) via laparoscopy, is the definitive treatment for women who are haemodynamically unstable, have significant pain, have a large ectopic mass, have a visible fetal heartbeat, have a high beta-hCG level outside the criteria for methotrexate, or who simply prefer a single definitive procedure over weeks of monitoring. Salpingotomy (removing the ectopic pregnancy while preserving the tube) may be considered in women with risk factors for infertility, such as damage to or absence of the contralateral tube, though this carries a higher risk of persistent trophoblastic tissue requiring further treatment and should be discussed carefully as part of shared decision-making about future fertility.

Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Hyperemesis gravidarum is severe, persistent nausea and vomiting in pregnancy that leads to weight loss (typically defined as more than 5% of pre-pregnancy body weight), dehydration, and electrolyte imbalance, and it is distinguished from the much more common "normal" nausea and vomiting of pregnancy (often called morning sickness, though it frequently occurs throughout the day) primarily by its severity and functional impact rather than by any single symptom being unique to it. It affects roughly 1 in 100 pregnancies and typically presents and peaks between 8 and 12 weeks of gestation, usually resolving by 16 to 20 weeks, though a minority of women experience symptoms throughout pregnancy.

Recognising Hyperemesis Gravidarum vs Normal Pregnancy Sickness

Ordinary nausea and vomiting of pregnancy is common, affecting up to 80% of pregnant women to some degree, and while unpleasant, it does not typically cause significant dehydration, ketonuria, or weight loss, and women are generally able to maintain adequate oral intake of food and fluids even if uncomfortable. Hyperemesis gravidarum, by contrast, is diagnosed when vomiting is so frequent and severe that the woman cannot keep down food or fluids, leading to measurable dehydration, weight loss, and often ketones in the urine. The validated PUQE score (Pregnancy-Unique Quantification of Emesis) is used in UK practice to grade severity objectively and guide the decision between community and hospital management, scoring the number of vomiting and retching episodes and hours of nausea over the preceding 24 hours.

Risk factors for hyperemesis gravidarum include multiple pregnancy (twins or higher-order multiples), molar pregnancy (discussed below — the two conditions are linked through markedly elevated beta-hCG levels), a previous history of hyperemesis gravidarum in an earlier pregnancy, and a family history of the condition. It is important to actively consider and exclude molar pregnancy and multiple pregnancy in any woman presenting with unusually severe hyperemesis, since both are associated with disproportionately high beta-hCG levels that drive more severe symptoms.

Investigation and Management

Initial assessment should include urinalysis for ketones (a marker of starvation and dehydration), urea and electrolytes (to identify hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia, or metabolic derangement from prolonged vomiting), and an ultrasound scan to confirm gestation, exclude multiple pregnancy, and exclude molar pregnancy. Thyroid function may also be checked, since hyperemesis gravidarum is associated with a transient, self-limiting biochemical hyperthyroidism related to the structural similarity between beta-hCG and TSH, which should not be treated as primary thyroid disease.

Mild cases can often be managed in the community with reassurance, dietary advice (small, frequent meals; avoiding triggers), and first-line antiemetics such as cyclizine or promethazine, or the combination of doxylamine and pyridoxine, all of which have a good safety profile in pregnancy. Ondansetron and metoclopramide are used as second-line options when first-line antiemetics fail, though metoclopramide should be limited to a maximum of 5 days of use due to the risk of extrapyramidal side effects, and ondansetron carries a small, likely modest increased risk of orofacial clefting when used in the first trimester, information that should be discussed as part of shared decision-making.

Women with significant dehydration, ketonuria, or an inability to tolerate oral fluids require admission for intravenous fluid rehydration (typically normal saline with added potassium as required, avoiding dextrose-containing fluids initially due to a rare risk of Wernicke's encephalopathy if thiamine stores are depleted), antiemetics, and thromboprophylaxis with low-molecular-weight heparin, since pregnancy itself and reduced mobility from prolonged vomiting both increase venous thromboembolism risk. Thiamine supplementation (oral or intravenous) should be considered in women with prolonged vomiting, precisely to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy, which is a rare but serious and preventable complication of hyperemesis gravidarum that the AKT does test as a "do not miss" complication. Corticosteroids are occasionally used as a later-line option in refractory hyperemesis under specialist guidance.

Psychological impact deserves explicit mention here too, because it is easy for a purely biomedical account of hyperemesis gravidarum to understate how disabling the condition can be. Women admitted repeatedly with hyperemesis gravidarum can experience significant anxiety, low mood, and, in severe or prolonged cases, a real impact on their ability to work or care for other children, and some go on to make difficult decisions about future pregnancies as a direct result of how unwell they were. A compassionate clinician acknowledges this explicitly rather than treating hyperemesis as merely an inconvenient but ultimately trivial variant of normal pregnancy sickness — a distinction the CPSA increasingly rewards through its emphasis on empathetic, patient-centred communication, not just correct pharmacological management.

Molar Pregnancy Basics

Molar pregnancy is a form of gestational trophoblastic disease that arises from abnormal fertilisation, resulting in excessive, disorganised trophoblastic tissue growth instead of, or alongside, normal fetal development. A complete mole occurs when an empty egg (with no maternal genetic material) is fertilised, most commonly by a single sperm that then duplicates its own chromosomes, resulting in a pregnancy that is entirely paternal in origin with no fetal tissue at all. A partial mole occurs when a normal egg is fertilised by two sperm (or by one sperm that duplicates), resulting in triploid tissue that contains some abnormal fetal or fetal-like tissue alongside the abnormal trophoblastic proliferation.

Classic presenting features include vaginal bleeding in the first trimester (often the presenting complaint, similar to a threatened miscarriage), a uterus that is unusually large for the stated dates, hyperemesis gravidarum out of proportion to gestation (driven by the very high beta-hCG levels molar tissue produces), and, in a minority of complete moles, early-onset pre-eclampsia before 20 weeks — a presentation that should always prompt consideration of molar pregnancy, since typical pre-eclampsia does not occur this early. Ultrasound classically shows a "snowstorm" or "bunch of grapes" appearance of mixed echogenicity within the uterine cavity in a complete mole, reflecting the swollen, hydropic villi, though partial moles can be harder to distinguish sonographically and are often only confirmed on histology after evacuation.

Molar pregnancy is one of the less commonly tested early pregnancy complications UKMLA topics compared with miscarriage and ectopic pregnancy, but it still appears often enough in past papers that it cannot safely be skipped during revision. Diagnosis is ultimately confirmed by histological examination of tissue obtained via surgical evacuation of the uterus, which is both diagnostic and therapeutic. Following evacuation, women are registered with a specialist UK Gestational Trophoblastic Disease Centre for structured beta-hCG follow-up, because a small proportion of molar pregnancies (more so complete than partial moles) progress to persistent gestational trophoblastic disease or, rarely, choriocarcinoma, a malignant condition requiring chemotherapy. Women are advised to avoid pregnancy during this follow-up monitoring period (commonly for at least 6 to 12 months, depending on how quickly beta-hCG normalises) specifically because a new pregnancy would make it impossible to distinguish rising hCG from a new gestation versus recurrent trophoblastic disease. Anti-D prophylaxis is also required for rhesus-negative women following evacuation of a molar pregnancy.

Although molar pregnancy is rarer than miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, or hyperemesis gravidarum, it is tested precisely because it links several concepts already covered in this guide — abnormal beta-hCG kinetics, disproportionate hyperemesis, and unusual first-trimester bleeding — making it a favourite way for the AKT to test whether a candidate can synthesise a slightly atypical presentation rather than pattern-match a textbook case. Because gestational trophoblastic disease sits at the boundary between early pregnancy complications and gynaecological oncology, it is also worth reading alongside the site's dedicated guide to gynaecological cancer recognition and referral pathways, which covers choriocarcinoma and other malignant trophoblastic outcomes in more depth than is appropriate here.

Bringing It Together: The Differential in Practice

In real clinical practice, and in AKT vignettes, the starting point is almost always the same: a woman of reproductive age with a positive pregnancy test presents with abdominal pain and/or vaginal bleeding in the first trimester. The clinician's job is to work through this differential systematically, always keeping the life-threatening possibility at the front of the mind. Haemodynamic status is assessed first and takes priority over any laboratory or imaging result — an unstable woman goes straight to resuscitation and emergency surgical review, full stop, regardless of what a beta-hCG level might otherwise suggest.

If the woman is stable, a transvaginal ultrasound and serum beta-hCG guide the next step: a confirmed intrauterine pregnancy with an open or closed cervical os and matching clinical findings points toward one of the miscarriage subtypes; no intrauterine pregnancy visible with a beta-hCG above the discriminatory zone points strongly toward ectopic pregnancy; and a uterus that is unusually large for dates with a very high beta-hCG and a characteristic ultrasound appearance points toward molar pregnancy. Hyperemesis gravidarum, meanwhile, is largely a clinical diagnosis of exclusion made on the severity of vomiting and its metabolic consequences, though it should always prompt a scan to exclude the two conditions (multiple pregnancy and molar pregnancy) most strongly associated with disproportionately severe symptoms.

Throughout every one of these presentations, communication matters as much as the clinical algorithm. Whether the outcome is a threatened miscarriage that goes on to a healthy pregnancy, a confirmed pregnancy loss, or an ectopic pregnancy requiring emergency surgery, women deserve clear, honest, and compassionate explanations, delivered without euphemism but also without unnecessary bluntness. This is not a "soft skill" separate from clinical competence on the AKT — CPSA-style stations increasingly test exactly this kind of sensitive communication, and it is a defining feature of good practice in early pregnancy care in the NHS.

For revision purposes, it helps to build a simple mental checklist for every early pregnancy complications UKMLA vignette you encounter: first, assess and state haemodynamic stability explicitly; second, confirm pregnancy status and gestation; third, decide whether ectopic pregnancy has been adequately excluded; and only then move on to classifying miscarriage subtype, considering hyperemesis severity, or thinking about molar pregnancy. Working through vignettes in this fixed order, every single time, prevents the common exam error of jumping straight to a plausible-sounding diagnosis before the genuinely dangerous alternative has been ruled out.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important thing to rule out in early pregnancy complications?

Ectopic pregnancy must always be actively excluded in any woman of reproductive age presenting with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, or collapse alongside a positive pregnancy test. It is the only early pregnancy complication that can cause rapid, life-threatening haemorrhage if the fallopian tube ruptures, so clinical suspicion should remain high even in the absence of typical risk factors.

What is the difference between a missed miscarriage and a threatened miscarriage?

A threatened miscarriage has a closed cervical os and a viable, ongoing pregnancy with a visible fetal heartbeat on ultrasound, meaning the pregnancy may still continue successfully. A missed miscarriage also has a closed cervical os, but the pregnancy has already stopped developing or the fetus has died, confirmed by an empty gestational sac 25 mm or larger, or a fetal pole 7 mm or larger with no heartbeat.

How is ectopic pregnancy diagnosed before rupture occurs?

Ectopic pregnancy is suspected when a transvaginal ultrasound shows no intrauterine pregnancy despite a beta-hCG level above the discriminatory zone (commonly around 1,500 to 2,000 IU/L), or when serial beta-hCG measurements taken 48 hours apart fail to rise appropriately (a normal pregnancy roughly doubles hCG every 48 hours). Clinical findings such as unilateral pelvic pain, shoulder tip pain, and cervical excitation support the diagnosis, and any haemodynamic instability warrants immediate emergency surgical assessment regardless of test results.

When is methotrexate used instead of surgery for ectopic pregnancy?

Methotrexate is offered to haemodynamically stable women with a relatively low beta-hCG level, no visible fetal heartbeat, a small ectopic mass, and no signs of rupture, provided they can reliably attend follow-up appointments for repeat blood tests. Surgery (usually laparoscopic salpingectomy) is required instead when a woman is unstable, has significant pain, a large ectopic mass, a visible fetal heartbeat, or a beta-hCG level outside the criteria considered safe for medical management.

How do you tell the difference between hyperemesis gravidarum and normal morning sickness?

Normal pregnancy sickness is common and unpleasant but does not usually cause significant dehydration, weight loss, or ketones in the urine, and most women can still manage some oral intake. Hyperemesis gravidarum is diagnosed when vomiting is severe enough to cause weight loss of more than around 5% of pre-pregnancy body weight, dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, and ketonuria, often requiring hospital admission for intravenous fluids and antiemetics.

Can you get pregnant again straight after a molar pregnancy?

No — women are advised to avoid becoming pregnant for a specified follow-up period, commonly 6 to 12 months, while their beta-hCG levels are monitored by a specialist Gestational Trophoblastic Disease Centre. This is because a new pregnancy would make rising hCG impossible to interpret, since it could reflect either the new pregnancy or persistent or recurrent trophoblastic disease.

Do all miscarriages need surgery?

No — expectant management, allowing the miscarriage to complete naturally over 7 to 14 days, is recommended as a reasonable first-line option for most women without risk factors for heavy bleeding or infection. Medical management with misoprostol and surgical management (manual vacuum aspiration or evacuation of retained products of conception) are both available as alternatives, and the final choice should reflect the woman's clinical situation and personal preference.

Conclusion

Early pregnancy complications UKMLA questions reward candidates who can hold two things in mind simultaneously: a clear, structured differential — miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, hyperemesis gravidarum, and molar pregnancy — and an unwavering instinct to exclude the life-threatening possibility first. Master the miscarriage classification and management pathways, know the ectopic pregnancy red flags cold, understand how beta-hCG and transvaginal ultrasound guide the diagnostic pathway, and recognise when hyperemesis or an unusually large-for-dates uterus should prompt you to think beyond the obvious diagnosis.
