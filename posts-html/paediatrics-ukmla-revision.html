Paediatrics for the UKMLA is one of the highest-yield topic areas in both the AKT and the CPSA, reflecting the GMC's expectation that every newly licensed doctor can assess an unwell child, recognise red flags, apply the UK childhood immunisation schedule, identify safeguarding concerns, and manage common paediatric emergencies. The breadth of paediatrics — from developmental milestones in infants to sepsis thresholds in teenagers — means that structured revision across all subsections of this specialty is essential for exam success.

Why Paediatrics Is High-Yield in the UKMLA

Children present in every clinical setting — emergency departments, general practice, acute medical wards, and community services. The UKMLA blueprint reflects this, with paediatric questions embedded across multiple domains including child health, safeguarding, pharmacology, and emergency medicine. The CPSA specifically tests the ability to perform a structured paediatric assessment, apply NICE traffic light tools for fever, recognise features of safeguarding concern, and counsel parents about vaccinations and developmental concerns. Candidates who dismiss paediatrics as "specialist" territory do so at significant cost to their exam score.

Developmental Milestones

Developmental milestones are specific skills or behaviours that most children demonstrate by a given age. Understanding what is normal versus what is delayed is tested in the AKT through clinical scenarios and directly in the CPSA through history-taking. Milestones span four domains: gross motor, fine motor, language and communication, and social and emotional development.

Gross Motor Milestones

By six weeks, a baby shows head lag when pulled to sit but can briefly lift the head in prone position. By four months, there is good head control with minimal lag and the infant can hold the head up in the prone position. By six months, the infant can sit with support. By nine months, the infant sits unsupported and may pull to stand. By twelve months, most infants are walking with support; by fifteen months, the majority walk independently. Walking independently by eighteen months is the outer limit of normal. Running typically begins by eighteen to twenty-four months. Climbing stairs (two feet per step with support) begins at around twenty-four months, and alternating feet on stairs at around thirty-six months. Hopping on one foot develops around four years. Riding a bicycle without stabilisers is typically achieved around five to six years.

Fine Motor Milestones

By three months, the infant grasps a rattle placed in the hand. By six months, palmar grasp is present and objects are transferred hand to hand. By nine months, a pincer grip (inferior — between thumb and lateral index finger) develops. By twelve months, a fine pincer grip (thumb tip to index finger tip) allows picking up small objects such as raisins. By eighteen months, a child can build a tower of three bricks. By twenty-four months, a tower of six to seven bricks and circular scribble. By three years, copying a circle and building a bridge with three bricks. By four years, copying a cross. By five years, copying a triangle and writing their own name.

Language Milestones

By six weeks, a social smile appears. By six months, babbling (consonant-vowel syllables such as "bababa"). By nine to twelve months, mama and dada with meaning, comprehension of "no," waving bye-bye. By twelve months, two to three words with meaning. By eighteen months, at least six to ten words. By twenty-four months, two-word phrases (noun-verb combinations — "want milk"). By thirty-six months, three-word sentences, can give their own name, and speech is understandable to strangers approximately 75% of the time. By four years, speech is fully intelligible to strangers; the child can give their full name and age. By five years, complex sentences and narrative storytelling. Referral for speech and language assessment is warranted if a child has no words by eighteen months, no two-word phrases by twenty-four months, or if speech is not intelligible to strangers by three years.

Social and Emotional Milestones

Social smile by six weeks. Stranger anxiety begins around eight months and peaks around twelve to eighteen months. Parallel play (playing alongside, not with, other children) is typical from eighteen months to three years. Cooperative or interactive play emerges from around three years. By four years, children understand rules, take turns, and play group games. Theory of mind — understanding that others have beliefs, desires, and intentions different from one's own — typically develops around four years and is assessed using false-belief tasks. Absence or delay of theory of mind is associated with autism spectrum disorder.

Growth and Nutrition in Children

Growth monitoring in UK children uses WHO growth charts (six weeks to two years) and UK 1990 reference charts (two years and above) plotted in personal child health records (the Red Book). Key principles: birth weight regained by ten to fourteen days; expected to double birth weight by five months and treble by twelve months. Head circumference, length/height, and weight should all be plotted on the centile chart at each health visitor contact. Crossing two or more major centile lines (from the set of 0.4th, 2nd, 9th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 91st, 98th, 99.6th centiles) downward is concerning and warrants investigation.

Failure to thrive (FTT) — now more commonly termed faltering growth — is defined as weight that has crossed downward through two or more major centile lines, or weight persistently below the 2nd centile. Causes are broadly divided into organic (cardiac disease, coeliac disease, cystic fibrosis, renal tubular acidosis, chronic infection, endocrine disorders) and non-organic (inadequate caloric intake, feeding difficulties, psychosocial deprivation). In practice, most cases are multifactorial. Management requires dietitian involvement, consideration of organic workup, and social assessment if neglect is suspected.

BMI in children is plotted on age- and sex-specific centile charts rather than using adult cut-offs. Overweight is defined as BMI between the 91st and 98th centile; obese is above the 98th centile. Underweight is below the 2nd centile. Management of childhood obesity follows NICE guidance including lifestyle modification (dietary change, increased physical activity, reduced screen time) and family-based interventions; pharmacotherapy and bariatric surgery are rarely used in children.

Common Childhood Infections

Bronchiolitis

Bronchiolitis is the most common serious respiratory infection in infants under two years and peaks in winter. It is caused by respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) in the vast majority of cases. Clinical features: coryzal prodrome followed by tachypnoea, subcostal and intercostal recession, fine end-inspiratory crackles and wheeze, poor feeding, and oxygen desaturation. Diagnosis is clinical. Management is supportive: ensure adequate oxygenation (target SpO2 above 92%), maintain hydration (nasogastric feeding or IV fluids if oral intake is inadequate), and nurse in a slightly head-up position. Bronchodilators, corticosteroids, and antibiotics are not routinely recommended (NICE NG9). Criteria for hospital admission: SpO2 below 92%, respiratory rate above 70 in infants, severe recession, apnoeas, poor feeding, or age below six weeks. Nirsevimab (Beyfortus — a long-acting monoclonal antibody against RSV F protein) has been introduced in the UK for passive immunisation of all infants born in or entering their first RSV season.

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Croup is viral laryngotracheobronchitis caused most commonly by parainfluenza virus, typically affecting children aged six months to three years. Presents with a barking seal-like cough, inspiratory stridor, and variable degrees of respiratory distress. The Westley Croup Score assesses severity based on stridor, retractions, air entry, cyanosis, and level of consciousness. Mild croup (Westley 0 to 2): reassurance and oral dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg single dose. Moderate croup (Westley 3 to 5): oral or IM dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg, consider nebulised adrenaline (0.4 mL/kg of 1:1000 solution, maximum 5 mL). Severe croup (Westley 6 or above): nebulised adrenaline is the immediate treatment, dexamethasone, and senior airway support. Adrenaline has a short duration of action (up to two hours), so patients receiving it require observation for at least three to four hours for rebound stridor. A child with stridor at rest requires hospital admission.

Epiglottitis

Epiglottitis is a life-threatening bacterial infection of the epiglottis, classically caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (now rare following Hib vaccination but still occurs). Presents with acute onset of high fever, drooling (inability to swallow secretions), muffled "hot-potato" voice, tripod position (sitting forward with neck extended and mouth open), and rapid deterioration. Critically, do NOT examine the throat, do NOT use a tongue depressor, and do NOT attempt IV access in a distressed child with suspected epiglottitis — any distress can precipitate complete airway obstruction. Call for senior help immediately (anaesthetics and ENT). Management is in theatre with an experienced team: gaseous induction of anaesthesia, direct laryngoscopy, intubation, and then IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone). The "cherry-red epiglottis" can be seen on laryngoscopy. On lateral neck X-ray, the "thumb sign" (swollen epiglottis resembling a thumb) is characteristic.

Scarlet Fever

Scarlet fever is caused by Group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes) producing erythrogenic toxins. It presents in school-aged children with fever, sore throat, and a characteristic fine sandpaper-like erythematous rash starting on the trunk and spreading to the limbs, sparing the perioral area (circumoral pallor). The tongue shows a white coating initially, then a strawberry tongue (prominent red papillae). Treatment is oral penicillin V or amoxicillin for ten days. Scarlet fever is a notifiable disease — the local health protection team must be informed.

Meningococcal Disease

Meningococcal disease (septicaemia with or without meningitis) caused by Neisseria meningitidis presents with fever, headache, vomiting, photophobia, neck stiffness, and — most importantly — a non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash. The rash indicates vasculitic skin involvement (meningococcaemia) and is associated with a high mortality. Immediate management: IV or IM benzylpenicillin (if no penicillin allergy) or ceftriaxone before hospital transfer. In hospital: blood cultures, LP (unless contraindicated), IV ceftriaxone, dexamethasone (reduces neurological sequelae of bacterial meningitis), and supportive care. Contacts of confirmed meningococcal disease receive chemoprophylaxis with ciprofloxacin or rifampicin. Note: LP is contraindicated if there are signs of raised intracranial pressure (papilloedema, focal neurological signs, reduced GCS, deteriorating consciousness), a coagulopathy, or haemodynamic instability — in these cases, give antibiotics and perform CT head first.

Asthma in Children

Asthma is the most common chronic condition of childhood in the UK. NICE guidance on paediatric asthma follows a stepwise approach similar to adults but with age-specific considerations. For children under five years: if wheeze is present, a trial of inhaled salbutamol (SABA via spacer and mask) is given. If recurrent wheeze occurs, a low-dose ICS trial (beclometasone 200 micrograms daily) is offered for eight weeks and continued only if there is a clear response. MART (Maintenance And Reliever Therapy — a single ICS-LABA inhaler used both as daily maintenance and as an as-needed reliever, such as Symbicort MART) is appropriate for children from twelve years of age and above in the UK, but is under evaluation for younger age groups. For children over five: stepwise management mirrors the adult pathway but with age-appropriate inhaler devices (spacer and mask for under three years, spacer and mouthpiece for three to five years, then dry powder or MDI without spacer for older children once technique is confirmed).

Acute severe asthma in children requires urgent assessment using PEFR (in those who can perform it — typically from age five to six years), oxygen saturation, heart rate, and respiratory rate. A PEFR below 33% predicted is life-threatening. Oxygen to maintain SpO2 above 94%, salbutamol nebulisers (driven by oxygen), ipratropium nebulisers every twenty minutes, IV/IM or oral prednisolone, and IV magnesium sulphate (single dose 40 mg/kg, max 2g over twenty minutes) for severe/life-threatening asthma not responding to initial treatment. Transfer to PICU if no improvement or deteriorating.

NICE Traffic Light System for Fever in Children

NICE NG143 provides a structured approach to the assessment of fever in children under five. The traffic light system identifies children at low (green), intermediate (amber), or high risk (red) of serious illness. Red features require immediate assessment by a senior clinician: cyanosis, SpO2 below 90%, abnormal skin colour (mottled, ashen, or blue), absent normal skin colour, non-blanching rash, bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, focal neurological signs, RR above 60, moderate or severe subcostal recession, or an ill appearance. Amber features include pallor, tachycardia above age-specific thresholds, RR above 50 (age 6 to 12 months) or above 40 (age 12 to 24 months), not responding normally to social cues, reduced activity, fever above 39°C in two- to three-month-olds, or rigors. Age-specific tachycardia thresholds: above 160 bpm in under one year, above 150 bpm in one to two years, above 140 bpm in two to five years.

Paediatric Sepsis — NICE NG51 Thresholds

Sepsis thresholds in children differ from adults. Age-specific vital sign thresholds must be applied. In newborns (under one month): tachycardia above 180 bpm or bradycardia below 100 bpm, RR above 60, temperature above 38°C or below 36°C. In infants (one to three months): similar thresholds with additional consideration of prolonged capillary refill time (above two seconds). In older children: tachycardia and tachypnoea thresholds decrease progressively with age. The Paediatric Early Warning Score (PEWS) is used in most hospitals. If paediatric sepsis is suspected, the Sepsis 6 bundle is applied with age-appropriate fluid boluses (10 mL/kg crystalloid in most paediatric sepsis, not the 500 mL used in adults) and weight-based antibiotic dosing.

Safeguarding Children

NICE NG76 — When to Suspect Child Maltreatment

All healthcare professionals have a statutory duty to act on concerns about child maltreatment. NICE NG76 provides detailed guidance on which clinical features should raise concern. Physical abuse features: bruising in a non-mobile infant (any bruise in a baby not yet walking independently is highly concerning), bruising on the torso, back, abdomen, arms, buttocks, ears, or face in a child of any age, bruising in recognisable patterns of implements (linear, looped, circular), human bite marks, lacerations on areas not typically injured in play, burns in unusual distributions or with sharply demarcated borders suggesting deliberate immersion, unexplained fractures particularly in infants (posterior rib fractures are highly specific for non-accidental injury — caused by squeezing of the chest; metaphyseal corner fractures — "bucket handle fractures" — are caused by traction or twisting forces). Retinal haemorrhages (particularly multilayered, extending to the periphery) in infants are strongly associated with abusive head trauma (shaken baby syndrome / abusive head trauma).

Emotional abuse includes persistent emotional ill-treatment causing severe and persistent adverse effects on the child's emotional development. Features include: a child describing being made to feel worthless, unloved, or frightened, persistent low self-esteem or self-blame, and unusually compliant or fearful behaviour around parents or carers. Sexual abuse features: age-inappropriate sexual knowledge, sexualised behaviour or play, disclosure of abuse (always believe and record the child's words, do not ask leading questions), physical examination findings (rarely present, and the absence of physical findings does not exclude abuse). Neglect: persistent failure to meet a child's basic physical or psychological needs — features include faltering growth, poor hygiene, inadequate clothing for weather, dental caries, or untreated medical conditions.

Female genital mutilation (FGM) is illegal in the UK. If a girl under 18 has undergone or is at risk of FGM, there is a mandatory reporting duty for regulated health and social care professionals — report to the police. Child sexual exploitation (CSE) involves sexual abuse in the context of an exploitative relationship. It may not be immediately recognised as abuse by the young person and may involve grooming. Referral to Children's Social Care is mandatory if CSE is suspected. A child protection conference is convened when a multi-agency assessment concludes that a child has suffered or is at risk of significant harm — the outcome may be placement on the child protection register under one of the abuse categories.

Neonatal Conditions

Neonatal jaundice is extremely common — approximately 60% of term and 80% of preterm neonates develop visible jaundice in the first week of life. Physiological jaundice appears after 24 hours and resolves within two weeks. Jaundice appearing within 24 hours of birth is always pathological — most commonly due to haemolytic disease of the newborn (rhesus or ABO incompatibility). Jaundice persisting beyond two weeks in a term neonate or three weeks in a preterm neonate is prolonged and requires investigation for conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinaemia — biliary atresia is the most important cause to exclude as it requires Kasai portoenterostomy by eight weeks of life for the best outcome. Breastmilk jaundice is a common and benign cause of prolonged unconjugated jaundice in healthy, thriving breastfed infants. Management of neonatal jaundice uses phototherapy and exchange transfusion based on age-specific bilirubin thresholds.

Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS, formerly hyaline membrane disease) affects preterm neonates due to surfactant deficiency. Management includes antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone) to the mother if preterm delivery is anticipated, exogenous surfactant therapy to the neonate, and respiratory support (CPAP or mechanical ventilation). Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC) is a life-threatening condition predominantly in preterm neonates involving intestinal inflammation and necrosis. Features: abdominal distension, bloody stools, bilious vomiting, feed intolerance, and systemic instability. X-ray shows pneumatosis intestinalis (gas in the bowel wall) — pathognomonic. Treatment is bowel rest, IV antibiotics, and surgical resection if perforation occurs.

The UK newborn blood spot screening programme (heel-prick test) screens for: phenylketonuria, congenital hypothyroidism, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, MCAD deficiency, maple syrup urine disease, isovaleric acidaemia, glutaric aciduria type 1, homocystinuria, and more recently, spinal muscular atrophy (SMA). Congenital hypothyroidism is screened by measuring TSH — early treatment with levothyroxine is essential to prevent intellectual disability.

High-Yield Paediatric Presentations

Intussusception

Intussusception occurs when a proximal segment of bowel telescopes into the adjacent distal segment, most commonly at the ileocaecal junction. It typically affects infants aged three months to two years and presents with intermittent, colicky abdominal pain causing the infant to draw up the legs and cry, vomiting (initially non-bilious then bilious), a sausage-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant, and eventually "redcurrant jelly" stools (blood and mucus). Diagnosis is confirmed on ultrasound (doughnut sign or pseudokidney sign). Treatment is pneumatic or hydrostatic air/water enema reduction under radiological guidance. If unsuccessful or if there is peritonitis, surgical reduction is performed.

Pyloric Stenosis

Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis presents between two and eight weeks of age (peak at four to six weeks) with projectile non-bilious vomiting after feeds. The baby remains hungry after vomiting. Prolonged vomiting causes hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis (loss of hydrochloric acid in vomit, with paradoxical aciduria). An olive-shaped mass may be palpable in the right upper quadrant. Ultrasound confirms pyloric wall thickness above 4 mm and pyloric channel length above 16 mm. Treatment is surgical — Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy, after correcting the metabolic abnormality with IV fluid and electrolyte replacement.

Henoch-Schonlein Purpura

Henoch-Schonlein Purpura (HSP — now termed IgA vasculitis) is the most common systemic vasculitis of childhood, typically affecting children aged two to eleven years. The classic tetrad is: palpable purpuric rash on the buttocks and lower limbs (symmetrical, non-thrombocytopaenic), arthralgia (large joints — knees and ankles), colicky abdominal pain (can cause intussusception or GI bleeding), and renal involvement (haematuria, proteinuria — HSP nephritis). Investigations: urinalysis for blood and protein at presentation and at follow-up (renal disease can appear weeks later). Most cases are self-limiting. Management is supportive: analgesia for joint and abdominal pain, and monitoring for renal complications. Steroids are used for severe abdominal pain or nephritis.

Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki disease is an acute self-limiting vasculitis predominantly affecting children under five years, particularly East Asian children. It is the most common cause of acquired heart disease in children in the developed world (coronary artery aneurysms). Diagnostic criteria require fever for five or more days plus four of five features: bilateral non-exudative conjunctival injection, strawberry tongue and cracked lips (changes in oropharynx), cervical lymphadenopathy (usually unilateral), polymorphous rash, and changes of the extremities (erythema and oedema of hands and feet acutely, with periungual desquamation in the subacute phase). Treatment: high-dose aspirin (anti-inflammatory during acute phase) plus IV immunoglobulin (IVIG — single dose reduces the risk of coronary aneurysm formation). Echocardiogram at diagnosis and follow-up. Note: aspirin is otherwise contraindicated in children under 16 due to the risk of Reye's syndrome, but is specifically indicated in Kawasaki disease.

Febrile Convulsions

Febrile convulsions (febrile seizures) occur in children between six months and five years and are the most common type of seizure in childhood, affecting 2 to 5% of children. They are triggered by a rapid rise in temperature rather than the absolute temperature. Simple febrile convulsions are generalised, last less than fifteen minutes, occur once in a twenty-four-hour period, and have complete recovery. Complex febrile convulsions are focal, last more than fifteen minutes, or recur within twenty-four hours — these require further investigation (EEG, MRI, consider LP to exclude meningitis). Management of an acute febrile convulsion: ABC, time the seizure, place in recovery position if possible, loosen clothing, do not restrain. If seizure lasts more than five minutes: administer midazolam buccal or diazepam rectal. Parents should be reassured that febrile convulsions are not associated with lasting neurological damage in simple cases, and that the risk of developing epilepsy is slightly increased but remains low (approximately 2 to 3%, compared to 0.5% in the general population).

Frequently Asked Questions About Paediatrics in the UKMLA

What developmental milestones are most tested in the AKT?

The most commonly tested milestones are the outer limits of normal — ages beyond which development should be investigated. Key red flags: no smile by ten weeks, not sitting unsupported by nine months, not walking by eighteen months, no single words by twelve months, no two-word phrases by twenty-four months. The AKT often presents a child at a given age and asks whether their development is normal or whether referral is indicated.

How do I differentiate croup from epiglottitis?

Croup presents with a gradual onset, barking cough, mild-to-moderate respiratory distress, and no drooling. The child is alert and distressed but not ill-appearing. Epiglottitis presents with sudden onset, high fever, severe toxicity, drooling, inability to swallow, no barking cough (voice is muffled), and the child sits in the characteristic tripod position. Any child with suspected epiglottitis should not have their throat examined and should go immediately to theatre for anaesthetic airway management.

Which findings on a CXR suggest pneumonia versus bronchiolitis in an infant?

Bronchiolitis shows hyperinflation (flattened diaphragms, increased AP diameter) and peri-bronchial cuffing (thickening of bronchial walls) — a bilateral, diffuse pattern. Pneumonia shows lobar or segmental consolidation — an area of increased density (white) in a specific lobe. CXR is not routinely required for bronchiolitis unless there are atypical features or failure to respond to management. Conversely, CXR is useful in diagnosing pneumonia, although clinical diagnosis is often sufficient in children with characteristic features.

What is the first-line investigation for intussusception?

Ultrasound of the abdomen is the first-line investigation for suspected intussusception. It has high sensitivity and specificity and avoids radiation exposure. Plain abdominal X-ray may show signs suggestive of bowel obstruction but is less specific. Pneumatic or hydrostatic enema (performed under fluoroscopic or ultrasound guidance) is both diagnostic and therapeutic — it confirms and reduces the intussusception in approximately 80 to 90% of cases.

When is aspirin appropriate in children?

Aspirin is generally contraindicated in children and young people under sixteen due to the risk of Reye's syndrome (a rare but serious condition causing liver failure and encephalopathy following aspirin use in viral illnesses). However, aspirin is specifically indicated in two paediatric conditions: Kawasaki disease (high dose anti-inflammatory during the acute phase, then low-dose antiplatelet), and certain thromboembolic conditions under specialist guidance.

Conclusion

Paediatrics is a broad, high-yield domain that repays systematic revision across developmental milestones, immunisations, common infections, safeguarding, and emergency presentations. Mastering the NICE traffic light fever tool, knowing the UK vaccination schedule, and being confident with safeguarding recognition will consistently gain marks across the AKT and CPSA.
