Substance misuse UKMLA questions turn up far more often than candidates expect, because alcohol- and drug-related presentations cut across almost every rotation in UK medicine — the acute medical take, the emergency department, primary care, surgical wards managing unplanned withdrawal, and psychiatry liaison. A trainee who can recognise alcohol withdrawal early, prescribe a sensible benzodiazepine-based regimen, protect against Wernicke's encephalopathy, and respond to an opioid overdose without hesitation is demonstrating exactly the kind of "safe to practise on day one" competence that the Applied Knowledge Test is designed to certify. This guide pulls together the addiction medicine content most likely to appear in the UKMLA: alcohol withdrawal and delirium tremens, opioid dependence and substitution therapy, overdose recognition and response, alcohol screening tools, smoking cessation, and the professional, non-stigmatising communication style the GMC expects from every doctor treating substance use disorders.

This topic sits alongside — but is distinct from — the broader psychiatric curriculum. If you are looking for mood disorders, psychosis, the Mental Health Act, or general psychiatric history-taking, see the companion psychiatry revision guide; this article focuses specifically on substance use disorders, their acute complications, and addiction medicine as its own examinable domain. Because withdrawal states and overdoses frequently present as medical emergencies, there is deliberate overlap with acute care topics — anyone revising this area alongside critical care fundamentals and toxicology and poisoning will find the material reinforces itself well.

Quick Answer: What the UKMLA Tests on Substance Misuse

Substance misuse UKMLA content clusters around five recurring themes: recognising and staging alcohol withdrawal (including when it progresses to delirium tremens), safely prescribing a reducing benzodiazepine regimen alongside thiamine to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy, understanding opioid substitution therapy at a conceptual level, responding correctly to suspected overdose, and applying validated screening tools such as AUDIT and CAGE in a primary care or admission context. Questions are usually scenario-based single-best-answer items: a patient two to three days post-admission develops tremor, agitation, and hallucinations, and candidates must select the next best step in management from a list that mixes correct and superficially plausible distractors.

Because these presentations occur on general wards as often as in specialist addiction services, the exam treats substance misuse as a patient-safety and acute-medicine topic as much as a psychiatric one — which is why it also connects naturally to prescribing safety principles and to how the exam tests avoidable prescribing errors more broadly. A doctor who under-doses benzodiazepines in withdrawal, or who gives glucose before thiamine in a malnourished patient, is committing exactly the kind of preventable error the GMC wants the UKMLA to screen out before registration.

Alcohol Withdrawal: Recognition and Staged Management

Why Withdrawal Happens and Who Is at Risk

Chronic heavy alcohol use causes sustained potentiation of inhibitory GABA-A receptor signalling and compensatory upregulation of excitatory glutamate (NMDA) receptor activity. When alcohol intake stops or drops sharply, this adaptation is suddenly unmasked: GABA-mediated inhibition falls away while glutamate-driven excitation persists unchecked, producing a state of central nervous system hyperexcitability. This is the physiological basis for tremor, autonomic overactivity, seizures, and, in severe cases, delirium tremens. Any patient admitted to hospital who drinks heavily and regularly is at risk of unplanned withdrawal the moment their usual alcohol intake is interrupted by admission itself — this is one of the most commonly missed acute presentations on general wards, because the trigger (hospitalisation) is the same event that removes the patient's usual alcohol supply.

Risk factors for a more severe withdrawal course include a history of previous withdrawal seizures or delirium tremens (the single strongest predictor of recurrence), long duration and high volume of daily drinking, concurrent use of other sedatives, poor nutritional state, and coexisting acute illness or infection. Every patient admitted with a history of regular heavy drinking should have a structured alcohol history taken on admission, including typical daily units, time of last drink, and any prior withdrawal complications, so that a preventive regimen can be started before symptoms escalate rather than reactively after they do.

The Time Course and Staged Symptoms

Alcohol withdrawal follows a fairly predictable timeline, and recognising which stage a patient is in is a frequently tested discriminator.

  • 6–12 hours after the last drink: mild autonomic symptoms begin — tremor, anxiety, sweating, headache, palpitations, nausea, and insomnia.
  • 12–24 hours: symptoms can intensify and some patients develop alcoholic hallucinosis — visual, auditory, or tactile hallucinations occurring with a clear sensorium (the patient remains oriented and aware the hallucinations are not real). This distinguishes hallucinosis from delirium tremens, where confusion is the defining feature.
  • 24–48 hours: peak risk period for generalised tonic-clonic withdrawal seizures, which are typically single or brief clusters rather than status epilepticus.
  • 48–96 hours (typically 2–3 days): the window in which delirium tremens develops if it is going to, representing the most dangerous phase of the withdrawal syndrome.

Delirium tremens is a medical emergency and is fatal in a meaningful proportion of untreated cases, principally from cardiovascular collapse, arrhythmia, or hyperthermia. It is characterised by the triad of global confusion, marked autonomic hyperactivity (tachycardia, hypertension, profuse sweating, fever), and vivid hallucinations (classically visual — small animals or insects, "Lilliputian" hallucinations) accompanied by agitation and tremor. The key distinguishing feature from simple hallucinosis is the presence of disorientation and clouded consciousness. Any patient developing new confusion during the 48–96 hour window post-admission in the context of known heavy drinking should be assessed for delirium tremens without delay.

The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment Concept

The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, revised (CIWA-Ar), is the structured scoring tool used across UK hospitals to standardise both the assessment and the treatment of alcohol withdrawal. It scores ten domains — nausea and vomiting, tremor, paroxysmal sweats, anxiety, agitation, tactile disturbances, auditory disturbances, visual disturbances, headache, and orientation/clouding of sensorium — each on a severity scale, producing a total score that drives a symptom-triggered dosing protocol rather than a fixed dosing schedule. A low score (typically under 8–10) usually needs no additional sedation beyond a baseline regimen; moderate scores (roughly 10–15) call for additional benzodiazepine doses; high scores (above 15–20) indicate a high risk of progression to seizures or delirium tremens and warrant urgent senior review and more aggressive treatment. The exam value of CIWA-Ar is less about memorising exact score cut-offs and more about understanding the underlying principle: withdrawal severity should be reassessed regularly and objectively, and treatment should be titrated to the patient's actual symptoms rather than given as a rigid one-size-fits-all schedule.

Benzodiazepine-Based Treatment Regimens

Long-acting benzodiazepines are the mainstay of alcohol withdrawal treatment because cross-tolerance between alcohol and benzodiazepines allows them to substitute for alcohol's GABAergic effect, smoothing the transition and preventing seizures and delirium tremens. Chlordiazepoxide is the benzodiazepine used most widely in UK inpatient practice for this purpose, typically given as a reducing regimen over several days — starting at a moderate dose and tapering progressively as symptoms settle, often guided by a symptom-triggered protocol informed by CIWA-Ar-style scoring. In patients with significant hepatic impairment, chlordiazepoxide's long half-life and active metabolites can accumulate dangerously, so a shorter-acting benzodiazepine without active metabolites, such as lorazepam, is preferred in this group, since it is metabolised by glucuronidation rather than oxidative hepatic pathways and is therefore safer in liver disease.

Community-based ("home") detoxification using a fixed reducing course of chlordiazepoxide is appropriate for patients with mild-to-moderate dependence, a stable home environment, and no history of seizures or delirium tremens. Inpatient, medically supervised detoxification is required for patients with a history of withdrawal seizures or delirium tremens, significant comorbidities, pregnancy, poor social support, or very high daily alcohol intake. Recognising which setting is appropriate for a given clinical vignette is a common exam discriminator between otherwise similar answer options.

Wernicke's Encephalopathy and Thiamine Prophylaxis

Chronic alcohol excess causes thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency through a combination of poor dietary intake, impaired intestinal absorption, and reduced hepatic storage. Thiamine is an essential cofactor for enzymes in cerebral glucose metabolism, and its depletion can precipitate Wernicke's encephalopathy, classically presenting with the triad of confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia (typically nystagmus or a lateral rectus/conjugate gaze palsy) — though the exam frequently tests the fact that all three features are present together in only a minority of real cases, so a high index of suspicion is needed even when the triad is incomplete. Left untreated, Wernicke's encephalopathy can progress to Korsakoff syndrome, an often-irreversible amnestic state characterised by profound anterograde amnesia, confabulation, and disorientation, with relatively preserved other cognitive functions.

The critical, frequently examined safety point is this: thiamine must be given before or alongside glucose, never after, in any malnourished or alcohol-dependent patient. Administering glucose first consumes the patient's already-depleted thiamine reserves in glycolysis, and can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy acutely. This single sequencing error is one of the most commonly tested "avoidable harm" scenarios in this topic area, and it dovetails directly with the exam's broader interest in safe prescribing sequences where the order of administration, not just the choice of drug, determines patient safety. IV thiamine (as part of a vitamin B-complex preparation) is given prophylactically to all patients at risk of Wernicke's encephalopathy on admission, and at treatment doses if the syndrome is suspected or confirmed, typically continued for several days before switching to oral maintenance thiamine.

Opioid Dependence and Opioid Substitution Therapy

Recognising Opioid Dependence and Withdrawal

Opioid dependence is defined by tolerance (needing progressively higher doses for the same effect), a characteristic withdrawal syndrome on cessation, and continued use despite harmful consequences, alongside a strong subjective desire or compulsion to use. Opioid withdrawal is intensely unpleasant but, unlike alcohol withdrawal, rarely life-threatening in an otherwise healthy adult. Symptoms include intense craving, dysphoria, lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, yawning, piloerection ("goose bumps" — the origin of the phrase "cold turkey"), mydriasis, abdominal cramps, diarrhoea, nausea and vomiting, muscle aches, and marked anxiety and insomnia. Onset and duration depend on the half-life of the opioid used: withdrawal from short-acting opioids like heroin typically begins within 6–12 hours and peaks at 48–72 hours, while withdrawal from longer-acting opioids like methadone has a later onset and a more protracted course.

Opioid Substitution Therapy: Methadone and Buprenorphine

Opioid substitution therapy (OST) replaces an illicit, short-acting opioid with a long-acting, medically supervised alternative, stabilising the patient, reducing the risks associated with illicit use (overdose, injecting-related infection, criminal activity), and providing a platform from which recovery-oriented treatment can proceed. The two agents used in UK practice are methadone and buprenorphine, and the exam expects candidates to understand their basic pharmacological distinction at a conceptual level rather than to prescribe exact doses.

Methadone is a full mu-opioid receptor agonist with a long and variable half-life, allowing once-daily dosing that suppresses withdrawal and craving. Because it is a full agonist, it carries a real risk of respiratory depression, particularly during the initial titration period before tolerance to the substitute has been established — most methadone-related deaths in treatment occur in this early induction phase, which is why initiation is done cautiously and under close supervision.

Buprenorphine is a partial mu-opioid receptor agonist with very high receptor affinity and a "ceiling effect" — beyond a certain dose, further increases produce progressively less additional opioid effect, including less respiratory depression. This gives buprenorphine a wider safety margin than methadone, particularly in overdose, and makes it an attractive choice where safety in less controlled settings is a priority. Its high receptor affinity also means it can precipitate acute withdrawal if given too soon after another opioid, since it can displace a full agonist from the receptor while providing only partial activation — patients must therefore be in a mild-to-moderate withdrawal state (not still intoxicated) before their first buprenorphine dose.

Choice between the two depends on patient preference, treatment history, comorbidities, and risk profile, and is a decision made jointly with specialist drug treatment services rather than something expected of the AKT candidate to determine unaided — the exam tests recognition of these agents' basic mechanisms and safety profiles, not detailed dose titration. NICE's guidance on drug misuse in over-16s covers both the psychosocial and pharmacological dimensions of this treatment pathway in detail for anyone wanting to go beyond the conceptual level tested here.

Why Substitution Therapy Matters Beyond Symptom Control

OST is not simply about making withdrawal more comfortable — it measurably reduces mortality, reduces blood-borne virus transmission associated with injecting drug use, reduces criminal activity, and improves engagement with wider health and social care. Candidates should understand OST as harm-reduction and public-health intervention as much as an individual treatment, since questions sometimes test the rationale for offering OST to a patient who has not yet expressed a desire to stop using opioids entirely — engagement and stabilisation are valid goals in their own right, not just a precursor to abstinence.

Recognising and Responding to Overdose Without Judgement

Opioid Overdose

Opioid overdose presents with the classic triad of respiratory depression, pinpoint (miotic) pupils, and reduced consciousness. Additional features include bradycardia, hypotension, and hypothermia. Respiratory depression is the feature that kills — hypoventilation leads to hypoxia, hypercapnia, and ultimately respiratory arrest and cardiac arrest if untreated. Any patient found unresponsive with slow or shallow breathing and pinpoint pupils should be assumed to have an opioid overdose until proven otherwise, and airway and breathing take absolute priority in the initial response, exactly as with any other cause of reduced consciousness.

Naloxone is a competitive opioid receptor antagonist that rapidly reverses opioid-induced respiratory depression and is the definitive treatment for suspected opioid overdose. It can be given intravenously, intramuscularly, or intranasally, and its effect is often dramatic and rapid. A crucial safety point tested regularly: naloxone's duration of action is typically shorter than that of many opioids (particularly long-acting ones like methadone), so a patient who initially responds well can relapse into respiratory depression once the naloxone wears off — meaning ongoing monitoring, and often repeat dosing or an infusion, is required even after an apparently successful initial reversal, and patients should not simply be discharged immediately after a single dose. Naloxone can also precipitate acute, unpleasant opioid withdrawal in a dependent patient; this is an expected and acceptable trade-off in a life-threatening overdose, but the dose should be carefully titrated to restore adequate breathing rather than aiming for full alertness, to avoid unnecessarily severe precipitated withdrawal.

Alcohol Overdose (Acute Alcohol Toxicity)

Severe acute alcohol intoxication can cause respiratory depression, aspiration risk from vomiting in a reduced conscious level, profound hypoglycaemia (particularly in those with depleted glycogen stores), hypothermia, and, at very high blood alcohol concentrations, coma and death from brainstem depression. Management is supportive: airway protection (often positioning in the recovery position if consciousness is only mildly reduced, or more advanced airway management if severely reduced), monitoring of blood glucose and correction of hypoglycaemia, warming, and monitoring for aspiration and cardiovascular instability. As above, if thiamine is indicated, it must be given before or with any glucose administered.

The Professional, Non-Judgemental Response

Every UKMLA scenario involving overdose or acute intoxication also tests professional attitude, not just clinical knowledge. Good Medical Practice requires doctors to treat all patients with dignity and without discrimination, and this applies with particular force to patients presenting with self-inflicted or substance-related harm, where stigmatising attitudes among healthcare staff are a well-documented and persistent problem. The correct exam answer to "how should the doctor respond" in an overdose or intoxication scenario is never one that delays treatment, criticises the patient's choices, or conditions care on the patient's willingness to engage with future treatment — clinical care is given on the same urgent, non-judgemental basis as for any other medical emergency, with harm-reduction conversations and treatment engagement addressed separately, later, and only once the patient is medically stable and receptive.

Screening Tools for Alcohol Use

CAGE Questionnaire

CAGE is a rapid four-question screening tool useful in time-pressured settings such as a busy GP surgery or an admissions clerking. The mnemonic stands for: have you ever felt you should Cut down on your drinking; have people Annoyed you by criticising your drinking; have you ever felt Guilty about your drinking; have you ever had a drink first thing in the morning to steady your nerves or get rid of a hangover (an Eye-opener). Two or more positive answers suggest a significant likelihood of alcohol dependence and should prompt further, more detailed assessment. CAGE is quick but relatively insensitive to lower levels of harmful (non-dependent) drinking, which is precisely why AUDIT exists alongside it.

AUDIT and AUDIT-C

The Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test (AUDIT) is a ten-item, WHO-developed questionnaire covering consumption, dependence symptoms, and alcohol-related harm, producing a score out of 40. It is more sensitive than CAGE for detecting the full spectrum of alcohol use disorders, including hazardous and harmful drinking that falls short of dependence, and is the tool recommended in UK primary care and hospital settings for structured alcohol screening. A shortened version, AUDIT-C (the first three consumption-focused questions only, scored out of 12), is commonly used as a rapid initial screen — a positive AUDIT-C score prompts completion of the full ten-item AUDIT for a more detailed picture. Broadly, higher AUDIT scores map onto increasing risk categories from low-risk drinking through hazardous and harmful drinking to likely dependence, and the tool is designed to guide the intensity of the intervention offered — from brief advice at the lower end to referral for specialist assessment and possible medically assisted withdrawal at the upper end.

Choosing Between the Tools in Practice

Exam questions sometimes ask which tool is "most appropriate" for a given scenario. The general principle: CAGE is faster and better suited to opportunistic screening or a very brief consultation, while AUDIT (or AUDIT-C as a first pass) is preferred where a more complete picture of consumption and risk is needed, such as new patient health checks, pre-operative assessment, or structured alcohol care in hospital. Neither tool replaces a full clinical history and examination once a screen is positive — they exist to identify who needs that fuller assessment, not to make a diagnosis on their own.

Smoking Cessation: A High-Yield Public-Health Topic

Why Smoking Cessation Is Tested So Often

Smoking cessation sits at the intersection of substance misuse, pharmacology, and public health, and is one of the most consistently high-yield, commonly under-revised topics candidates encounter. Every patient contact — not just those in a smoking cessation clinic — is considered an opportunity for "very brief advice," and the GMC-aligned curriculum expects every doctor to be comfortable initiating this conversation and prescribing or recommending appropriate pharmacological support.

Pharmacological Options

Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) — patches, gum, lozenges, inhalators, and sprays — reduces withdrawal symptoms by supplying nicotine without the other harmful constituents of tobacco smoke, and combining a long-acting patch with a short-acting form (gum or lozenge) for breakthrough cravings is more effective than either alone. Varenicline is a partial agonist at the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor: it reduces craving and withdrawal by providing partial receptor stimulation while also blocking nicotine from cigarettes from producing its usual reward, reducing the satisfaction of a lapse. Bupropion, originally an antidepressant, also supports smoking cessation, though it carries a lower seizure threshold and is contraindicated in patients with a history of seizures or eating disorders. All three approaches roughly double quit rates compared with willpower alone, and combining behavioural support with pharmacotherapy is more effective than either in isolation.

Brief Intervention and the "Ask, Advise, Act" Model

UK practice favours a simple, time-efficient structure: ask about smoking status at every appropriate contact, advise clearly and personally on the health benefits of stopping, and act by offering referral to a stop smoking service and/or pharmacotherapy for those who are interested. This brief approach is deliberately low-friction precisely because it is meant to be deliverable by any doctor in any specialty within a normal consultation, not just by smoking cessation specialists — a fact the exam likes to test by placing a smoking-status question inside an unrelated clinical vignette (for example, a pre-operative assessment or an unrelated acute admission) to check whether candidates recognise the opportunity for brief intervention regardless of the presenting complaint.

Special Populations

Smoking cessation in pregnancy is a particular priority given the well-established risks of smoking to fetal growth and outcome; NRT can be used in pregnancy following a risk-benefit discussion, since the risks of continued smoking generally outweigh the risks of nicotine replacement itself, whereas varenicline and bupropion are generally avoided in pregnancy. Patients admitted to hospital represent another key opportunity, since a hospital stay is often smoke-free by necessity and can be used as a "teachable moment" to initiate NRT and refer for ongoing support on discharge.

Vaping and E-Cigarettes as a Cessation Tool

E-cigarettes have become one of the most commonly used quit aids in the UK, and current guidance recognises them as a reasonable option to support smoking cessation, on the basis that vaping carries substantially lower harm than continuing to smoke combustible tobacco, even though it is not entirely risk-free and is not recommended for people who have never smoked. The exam-relevant point is nuanced: a doctor should not actively discourage a patient who is using vaping successfully to stay off cigarettes, but should also not present vaping as risk-free, and should encourage an eventual plan to stop vaping too once smoking has been reliably stopped. This distinction — harm reduction versus zero-risk — comes up as a discriminator in questions that offer "advise the patient to stop vaping immediately" as a plausible but incorrect distractor.

Stimulants and Novel Psychoactive Substances

Cocaine and Amphetamine Toxicity

Substance misuse UKMLA scenarios are not limited to alcohol and opioids — stimulant toxicity is a recurring theme, particularly cocaine, which acts by blocking the reuptake of dopamine, noradrenaline, and serotonin at nerve terminals, producing intense sympathetic overactivity. The classic presentation is a triad of tachycardia, hypertension, and agitation, sometimes with chest pain from cocaine-induced coronary vasospasm — a genuinely important exam distractor, because the standard first-line treatment for an acute coronary syndrome (a beta-blocker) is relatively contraindicated in cocaine-associated chest pain, since blocking beta-receptors while leaving alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed can worsen coronary spasm. Benzodiazepines, rather than beta-blockers, are the first-line treatment for cocaine-related chest pain and agitation, since they reduce the central sympathetic drive directly. Amphetamines (including methamphetamine) produce a broadly similar sympathomimetic toxidrome — tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, and agitation or psychosis — with management again centred on benzodiazepines, cooling, and supportive care rather than beta-blockade.

Novel Psychoactive Substances ("Legal Highs")

Novel psychoactive substances (NPS) — synthetic cannabinoids, synthetic cathinones, and related compounds sold under constantly changing brand names — present a particular clinical challenge because their exact composition is frequently unknown even to the person who has taken them, and routine hospital toxicology screens often do not detect them. The exam-relevant principle is that management is symptom- and supportive-care-based rather than substance-specific: treat the physiological derangement in front of you (agitation, hyperthermia, tachycardia, seizures) using the same general toxicology and critical care principles covered elsewhere in this guide, rather than waiting for a specific identification of the substance involved. A history of NPS use should also prompt safety-netting around unpredictable effects and the possibility of co-ingestion with other substances.

Communicating With Patients Who Have Substance Use Disorders

Good Medical Practice and Non-Stigmatising Care

The GMC's Good Medical Practice sets out the professional standards expected of every UK doctor, including treating patients as individuals and respecting their dignity, working in partnership with patients, and not allowing personal views about a patient's lifestyle to affect the care that is offered. Substance use disorders are chronic relapsing health conditions, not moral failings, and exam scenarios reward answers that reflect this framing: using person-first, non-judgemental language (a "person who uses drugs" rather than a stigmatising label), avoiding assumptions about honesty or motivation, and involving the patient collaboratively in decisions about their own care wherever possible.

Confidentiality and Trust

Patients with substance use disorders may be reluctant to disclose their use for fear of judgement, legal consequences, or loss of custody of children, and building trust through consistent, confidential, non-punitive care is essential to accurate history-taking and safe management. Confidentiality is maintained on the same basis as for any other health condition, with the same limited exceptions (such as a genuine and serious risk of harm to the patient or others) applying no differently than they would for any other diagnosis.

Motivational Interviewing Principles

Rather than confrontation or moralising, a motivational, patient-centred communication style — exploring the patient's own ambivalence about change, reflecting back what they say, and supporting self-efficacy — is consistently associated with better engagement and outcomes. Candidates are not expected to conduct a full motivational interview in an exam vignette, but should recognise that the "correct" communication answer in a substance misuse scenario is almost always the one that avoids confrontation, judgement, or ultimatums, and instead offers information, support, and choice.

Involving the Wider Team

Substance misuse care is rarely delivered by one clinician alone. Specialist drug and alcohol services, psychiatry liaison, social work, and — where relevant — safeguarding teams (for patients who are parents or carers) often need to be involved. Recognising when to refer to specialist addiction services, rather than attempting to manage complex dependence unsupported in a generalist setting, is itself a patient-safety competency the exam tests.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between alcoholic hallucinosis and delirium tremens?

Alcoholic hallucinosis involves hallucinations (usually visual, auditory, or tactile) occurring while the patient remains alert, oriented, and aware the experiences are not real, typically appearing 12–24 hours after the last drink. Delirium tremens, by contrast, involves genuine confusion and disorientation alongside hallucinations and marked autonomic instability, usually emerging 48–96 hours after the last drink, and is a medical emergency rather than a self-limiting symptom.

Why must thiamine be given before glucose in alcohol-dependent patients?

Thiamine is an essential cofactor for glucose metabolism, and chronic alcohol use commonly depletes the body's thiamine stores. Giving glucose to a thiamine-deficient patient before replacing thiamine can consume the little remaining thiamine in glycolysis and precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy, so thiamine should always be given before or at the same time as glucose in this patient group, never after.

What is the main pharmacological difference between methadone and buprenorphine?

Methadone is a full mu-opioid receptor agonist with no ceiling on its opioid effect, meaning higher doses keep producing more respiratory depression; buprenorphine is a partial agonist with a ceiling effect, meaning its respiratory depressant effect plateaus at higher doses, giving it a wider safety margin. Buprenorphine's high receptor affinity can also precipitate acute withdrawal if started too soon after another opioid, so patients must be in mild-to-moderate withdrawal before their first dose.

Why might a patient relapse into respiratory depression after naloxone reverses an opioid overdose?

Naloxone's duration of action is often shorter than that of the opioid causing the overdose, particularly long-acting opioids such as methadone, so its effect can wear off before the opioid has been cleared from the body. This means patients need ongoing monitoring — and sometimes repeat doses or an infusion — even after they initially appear to have responded well, rather than being discharged immediately after a single dose.

What does a positive CAGE questionnaire mean?

A score of two or more positive answers out of the four CAGE questions (Cut down, Annoyed, Guilty, Eye-opener) suggests a significant likelihood of alcohol dependence and should prompt a fuller clinical assessment, such as the more detailed AUDIT questionnaire, rather than being treated as a diagnosis on its own.

Is nicotine replacement therapy safe in pregnancy?

Nicotine replacement therapy can be used in pregnancy after a risk-benefit discussion, because the harms of continued smoking to the pregnancy generally outweigh the risks associated with nicotine replacement itself. Varenicline and bupropion, by contrast, are generally avoided in pregnancy, making NRT the preferred pharmacological option for pregnant patients who want support to stop smoking.

How should a doctor respond to a patient who has overdosed on drugs or alcohol?

The immediate priority is exactly the same as for any other medical emergency: airway, breathing, circulation, and specific treatment such as naloxone for suspected opioid overdose, delivered urgently and without judgement. Conversations about substance use, treatment options, or behaviour change should happen later, once the patient is medically stable, and should never delay or be made conditional on the emergency treatment itself.

Conclusion

Substance misuse UKMLA questions reward candidates who can think in stages — recognising where a patient sits on the alcohol withdrawal timeline, sequencing thiamine correctly relative to glucose, understanding the basic safety trade-offs between methadone and buprenorphine, and responding to overdose with urgency rather than judgement. These are patient-safety skills as much as psychiatric knowledge, and they are tested precisely because they come up on general wards and in emergency departments, not only in specialist addiction clinics. Revise the alcohol withdrawal timeline and CIWA-Ar principle, the thiamine-before-glucose rule, the methadone-versus-buprenorphine safety distinction, naloxone's limitations, CAGE and AUDIT, and the GMC's expectation of dignified, non-stigmatising care, and this topic becomes one of the more reliably scorable areas of the exam.
