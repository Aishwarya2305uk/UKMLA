The UKMLA for Ethiopian doctors route runs through three stages: verifying your Ethiopian MD degree and internship, passing the two-part UK Medical Licensing Assessment (the AKT and the CPSA), and completing GMC registration with a licence to practise. Doctors who graduated from an Ethiopian medical school such as Addis Ababa University, Jimma University, the University of Gondar, or another institution listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools are eligible to apply, provided their qualification and internship are verified and they meet the GMC's English language standard. This guide sets out exactly what Ethiopian MD graduates need at every step, current fees, a realistic timeline, and the mistakes that most often slow the process down.

Key takeaways

  • Ethiopian doctors with a verifiable MD degree from a recognised medical school can apply for UKMLA and GMC registration; the degree title "MD" is treated as a primary medical qualification, not a postgraduate doctorate.
  • Budget for the AKT (£283), the CPSA (£1,036), registration fees (£26 provisional or £481 full), an English test (roughly £180-£230), and annual retention (£481 with a licence, £172 without) — all GMC figures effective 1 April 2026, always confirm on gmc-uk.org.
  • Primary source verification through MyIntealth (the platform that replaced EPIC) is compulsory and should be started as early as possible, since Ethiopian university verification can take several weeks.
  • Completion of the compulsory Ethiopian internship and any service obligation is required before the GMC will consider your primary qualification fully awarded.
  • A realistic end-to-end timeline is 12 to 24 months, and the CPSA currently runs at UK test centres, so travel and visa planning matter as much as exam preparation.
  • Ethiopian applicants face the same core GMC checklist as doctors from Kenya, Sudan and other East African countries, with country-specific differences in the verifying authority and document formats.

Why Ethiopian MD graduates are a growing part of the UKMLA cohort

Ethiopia trains a large number of doctors every year through a fast-expanding network of public and private medical schools, and a meaningful share of those graduates look outward for postgraduate training, better-resourced systems, or NHS careers. The UKMLA for Ethiopian doctors pathway exists precisely for this group: international medical graduates (IMGs) whose primary qualification was not awarded in the UK, Ireland, or a small number of exempted jurisdictions, and who must therefore sit the UK Medical Licensing Assessment before the GMC will grant registration with a licence to practise.

This is the same route already used by doctors from Kenya and other East African countries, and the general shape of the process is shared across the region. What differs for Ethiopian applicants is the domestic regulatory picture: the name and structure of the licensing authority, the specific wording used on Ethiopian degree certificates and transcripts, and the internship and service arrangements tied to the MD qualification. Understanding those local details before you start reduces avoidable delay later.

Ethiopia's medical degree and regulatory landscape

Ethiopian medical schools award a Doctor of Medicine (MD) degree as the primary qualification, typically after a programme lasting around six years including pre-clinical and clinical training, rather than the "MBBS" title used in the UK, India, and many Commonwealth systems. This is a naming convention, not a difference in seniority: the Ethiopian MD is the entry-level medical qualification, equivalent in function to an MBBS elsewhere, and it is this primary degree — not any later postgraduate award — that the GMC assesses for UKMLA eligibility.

Major Ethiopian medical schools include the College of Health Sciences at Addis Ababa University, Jimma University, the University of Gondar, and a growing number of newer public and private institutions. For GMC purposes, what matters is not the reputation of the school but whether it is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, the global reference the GMC and MyIntealth use to confirm that a medical qualification is genuine and eligible for verification.

On the regulatory side, Ethiopia's system has evolved over the past decade. Health professional registration and licensing has historically sat with the Food, Medicine and Healthcare Administration and Control Authority (FMHACA/EFMHACA), established under Council of Ministers Regulation 189/2010, with a mandate covering the competence, ethics and continuing professional development of health professionals. More recent reforms have reorganised parts of this mandate under the Ethiopian Food and Drug Authority (EFDA), established under Proclamation No. 1263/2021, alongside health professional licensing functions run through the Ministry of Health's eHPEL online licensing portal. Because Ethiopian regulatory structures have been in flux, always verify with your medical school and the Ministry of Health which body currently issues your practising licence and certificate of good standing before you rely on any single name.

Hedge: the GMC does not publish country-specific guidance naming Ethiopia's regulator by name in the way it sometimes does for larger source countries, so treat the above as background context rather than a GMC-confirmed requirement, and confirm current expectations directly with the GMC's primary source verification guidance.

Ethiopia's expanding medical education system

Ethiopia has expanded its medical training capacity substantially over the past two decades, moving from a handful of established schools to a much wider network of public and private institutions across the country. This expansion was driven partly by chronic doctor shortages relative to population size and partly by a deliberate national policy push to scale up health workforce training. The practical effect for anyone researching the UKMLA for Ethiopian doctors route is that medical school reputations and resources vary considerably, and applicants from newer institutions should take particular care to confirm World Directory of Medical Schools listing and to expect closer scrutiny during verification.

Addis Ababa University's College of Health Sciences, home to the historic Tikur Anbessa (Black Lion) Specialized Hospital, remains the country's flagship medical school and training hospital, with a long-established curriculum and international academic partnerships. Jimma University, in Ethiopia's Oromia region, was an early pioneer of community-based medical education in the country and has produced large numbers of graduates who have gone on to postgraduate training abroad. The University of Gondar, in Amhara region, similarly has decades of teaching history and a strong reputation in public health and tropical medicine training, reflecting Ethiopia's particular disease burden. Beyond these three, universities such as Hawassa, Mekelle, Bahir Dar, Haramaya and several newer institutions now also run MD programmes, alongside a small number of private medical colleges.

Regardless of which of these schools you graduated from, the GMC's assessment criteria do not change: your qualification must be genuine, verifiable, and drawn from an institution recognised in the World Directory of Medical Schools. If you trained at a newer or less internationally known Ethiopian institution, it is worth checking the directory listing yourself before committing time to MyIntealth verification, since a missing listing (rare, but possible for very new colleges) would need to be resolved with the school and directory administrators first.

Ethiopian internship and service requirements

Ethiopian medical training typically includes a compulsory internship year built into or following the MD programme, during which graduates rotate through major clinical specialties under supervision before their degree is considered fully conferred and they are eligible for a practising licence. Many public medical schools in Ethiopia also attach a subsequent multi-year general practitioner service obligation, often in underserved regions, as a condition tied to state-subsidised training places.

For GMC purposes, what matters is that your internship is completed and that your medical school or licensing body can confirm your primary qualification was fully and validly awarded — including any internship component that is a formal requirement for the degree to take effect. If your internship is still in progress, most doctors wait until it is finished before starting formal GMC registration, since an unverifiable or incomplete qualification will stall your application at the primary source verification stage.

The multi-year service obligation attached to some Ethiopian training pathways is a domestic employment matter between you and the Ethiopian authorities or your sponsoring institution, not a GMC eligibility requirement — but it can affect your practical ability to leave the country or obtain a certificate of good standing, so plan around it realistically.

Clinical experience: does it help your application?

Many Ethiopian doctors accumulate several years of clinical experience — often within the compulsory service obligation described above, and sometimes in additional roles afterward — before beginning the GMC registration process. This experience is genuinely valuable for CPSA preparation, since confident, well-practised clinical skills translate directly into stronger consultation performance, but it is worth being clear about what it does and does not do for your GMC application itself.

The GMC does not currently offer a formal exemption from the AKT or CPSA on the basis of years of clinical experience alone; there is no accepted substitute for passing the UKMLA if your qualification is not otherwise exempting. What experience does affect is your competitiveness for NHS jobs once you are registered, since UK employers weigh clinical experience heavily when shortlisting IMG doctors for foundation-equivalent or more senior posts. Ethiopian doctors with several years of general practice, internal medicine, surgery, obstetrics, or emergency medicine experience are often well placed for mid-level NHS roles once registered, rather than needing to start at the most junior level.

If you have postgraduate qualifications from Ethiopia or elsewhere — for example, a residency in a recognised specialty — these should be included in your GMC application evidence and CV, since some may also require separate primary source verification if used to support specialist registration routes later in your career, though that is a separate process from initial UKMLA-based registration.

Full eligibility checklist for Ethiopian doctors

1. A recognised primary medical qualification

Your Ethiopian MD must come from a medical school listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools. Nearly all established Ethiopian universities qualify, but newer or private colleges should be checked individually.

2. Completed internship

Your internship year must be finished and confirmable by your medical school, consistent with how your qualification was originally awarded.

3. Primary source verification via MyIntealth

This is the single most important — and most frequently delayed — step. See the dedicated section below for full detail, and see the general explainer on how GMC EPIC/MyIntealth verification works for the mechanics that apply to every IMG, Ethiopian doctors included.

4. English language proficiency

Since Ethiopian medical training is conducted primarily in English at the university level but is not on the GMC's short list of exempted qualifications, most Ethiopian applicants will need to sit IELTS Academic or the OET and meet the GMC's current minimum scores across all four components.

5. Passing the UKMLA (AKT and CPSA)

Unless you hold an accepted exempting qualification, both components are mandatory. See the dedicated section below.

6. Certificate of good standing / evidence of good standing

Where required, this is obtained from the Ethiopian licensing authority confirming your practising history and disciplinary record.

7. Identity and criminal record documentation

Passport, identity verification, and — depending on current GMC requirements — a certificate of criminal record from Ethiopia and any other country you have lived or worked in for an extended period.

Documents checklist

  • MD degree certificate and full academic transcript
  • Internship completion certificate
  • Medical school confirmation letter (for MyIntealth verification)
  • Valid passport and identity documents
  • English test certificate (IELTS Academic or OET) within the GMC's validity window
  • Certificate/letter of good standing from your Ethiopian licensing authority, where applicable
  • Evidence of any postgraduate training or clinical experience since qualifying
  • Criminal record certificate(s) as currently required by the GMC

Primary source verification: MyIntealth for Ethiopian graduates

Before the GMC will register any international medical graduate, your primary medical degree must be independently verified directly with the awarding institution. This used to run through EPIC (the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials), operated by ECFMG. EPIC has now been retired and replaced by MyIntealth, a newer integrated platform from Intealth, ECFMG's parent organisation. Verifications already completed under EPIC have been migrated across, so returning applicants do not need to redo prior verification.

For Ethiopian doctors, this step means MyIntealth will contact your Ethiopian medical school's records office to confirm your degree, dates of study, and (where applicable) your internship completion. Response times from Ethiopian university administration offices can vary considerably, and this is consistently the step that adds the most unplanned delay to an otherwise well-prepared application. Start this process as early as possible — ideally before you book the AKT — and follow up proactively with your medical school's alumni or registrar's office if you do not hear back within a few weeks.

Full mechanics of this process, including how to create an account, which documents to upload, and how long verification typically takes, are covered in the site's dedicated GMC EPIC/MyIntealth verification explainer, which applies equally to Ethiopian, Kenyan, Sudanese and other IMG applicants.

Understanding the AKT and CPSA

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT)

The AKT is a computer-based, multiple-choice examination covering the breadth of UK clinical practice: presenting complaints, diagnosis, investigations, management, and professional and ethical scenarios. It tests applied clinical knowledge rather than raw recall, so questions are typically framed as realistic patient scenarios. Ethiopian graduates whose undergraduate training emphasised the country's own disease burden — including a heavier weighting toward infectious disease, maternal health, and resource-limited settings — should expect the AKT to lean more heavily toward UK primary care presentations, NHS referral pathways, prescribing conventions, and UK-specific guidelines (NICE, in particular) than their home curriculum did.

The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA)

The CPSA is the practical, station-based component: a circuit of simulated consultations assessing history-taking, examination technique, clinical reasoning, communication, and professionalism with role-played patients. It is broadly similar in structure to objective structured clinical examinations (OSCEs) most Ethiopian graduates will already have sat during their MD training, but the CPSA specifically tests UK clinical communication norms, including how much you explain to patients, how you discuss risk and consent, and how you structure a UK-style consultation.

How the AKT content map differs from Ethiopian licensing exams

Ethiopia introduced its own national Health Professionals' Licensing Examination for new graduates in 2019, a competency assessment required before Ethiopian bachelor-level and, in practice, MD-level health professionals can be licensed domestically. While this exam and the AKT share the broad goal of confirming clinical competence, they are built against different blueprints. The Ethiopian licensing exam is calibrated to Ethiopia's own disease burden, referral structures, and drug formulary, whereas the AKT is calibrated specifically to UK primary and secondary care, the NHS referral system, and UK prescribing guidance. Passing the Ethiopian licensing exam is a useful sign of general competence, but it is not a substitute for AKT preparation, and candidates who assume the two exams test the same priorities are often surprised by how UK-centric AKT scenarios are.

Preparation tips for Ethiopian candidates

  • Study UK-specific guidelines directly — NICE pathways, BNF prescribing conventions, and UK referral thresholds differ in places from protocols used in Ethiopian teaching hospitals.
  • Practise CPSA-style consultations in English with a study partner, focusing on structured history-taking and explicit safety-netting, since UK examiners mark communication style as closely as clinical content.
  • Use question banks aligned specifically to the UKMLA content map rather than generic international MCQ banks, so your revision matches the actual blueprint.
  • Join or start a study group with other Ethiopian or East African doctors preparing for the same sitting — shared past-experience notes on station types are genuinely useful, provided you do not rely on unofficial "leaked" content.
  • Book your CPSA travel and English test early, since availability at UK test centres can be tighter than AKT slots, particularly around popular sitting windows.

Funding the process: practical financial planning

The combined cost of the UKMLA route — exam fees, English test, registration fees, travel, and accommodation for the CPSA — represents a very significant sum relative to average incomes in Ethiopia, and financial planning deserves the same attention as exam preparation. Very few Ethiopian doctors can self-fund the entire journey from a single salary at Ethiopian public sector pay scales, so most combine several strategies: personal savings built up over one to two years, family or community financial support, and in some cases NHS employer-sponsored routes that cover part of the cost of relocation once a job offer is in hand.

Some NHS trusts running international recruitment programmes will cover or advance certain costs — such as visa fees, the Immigration Health Surcharge, or travel to the UK — as part of a formal job offer, though this varies by trust and is not something to assume without a written offer confirming exactly what is and is not covered. It is worth researching any given trust's international recruitment terms carefully and comparing several offers if you have them, since sponsorship packages differ considerably in generosity and conditions (including minimum service commitments).

Because CPSA travel is a one-off, front-loaded cost, many candidates choose to sit the AKT first, bank the pass, and then spend a further period saving specifically for CPSA travel and fees, rather than attempting to fund both stages simultaneously. Whatever approach you take, build a realistic personal budget covering every line item in the fee table above, plus a contingency margin for exchange rate movement and unplanned resits, before committing to a sitting date.

Step-by-step registration process for Ethiopian doctors

  1. Confirm your medical school is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools and gather your degree certificate, transcript, and internship completion evidence.
  2. Start MyIntealth primary source verification as early as possible — this is usually the longest lead-time item, so begin it before booking any exam.
  3. Book and sit an approved English test (IELTS Academic or OET), aiming for a result well within its validity window relative to your planned registration date.
  4. Register for a GMC online account and begin your application, uploading identity and qualification evidence as required.
  5. Book and sit the AKT at an approved test centre — increasingly available in several African cities, though availability should always be checked directly on gmc-uk.org.
  6. Book travel and sit the CPSA at a UK test centre, allowing sufficient buffer time for visa processing if you need a visit visa to attend.
  7. Apply for provisional or full GMC registration once your AKT and CPSA results, verification, and English test are all in place.
  8. Secure an NHS job offer and apply for a Health and Care Worker visa, then relocate and complete final onboarding, including occupational health checks and right-to-work verification.

For the immigration side of this sequence, see the site's UK visa guide for international doctors, and for what happens once you land, the practical relocation guide for IMG doctors covers housing, banking, and settling in.

Realistic timeline

Most Ethiopian doctors should plan for 12 to 24 months from the point they begin document verification to holding full GMC registration with a licence to practise, though motivated candidates with strong English scores already in hand and rapid MyIntealth turnaround have completed the journey faster. Typical phases:

  • Months 1-4: MyIntealth verification submitted and progressing; English test booked and sat.
  • Months 3-8: AKT preparation and booking; sitting the AKT.
  • Months 6-12: CPSA preparation, visa arrangements for UK travel, sitting the CPSA.
  • Months 9-15: GMC registration application submitted once all components are complete.
  • Months 12-24: Job search, Health and Care Worker visa application, relocation and NHS induction.

Delays most commonly arise from slow university responses during verification, CPSA booking bottlenecks, and visa processing times — all of which are worth building slack into your plan for rather than assuming the fastest-case timeline.

CPSA travel and visa considerations

Because the CPSA currently runs at test centres in the UK, Ethiopian candidates need to plan an international trip specifically for this exam unless or until international CPSA circuits expand to cover East Africa. This typically means applying for a UK visit visa, booking flights and accommodation around your confirmed CPSA date, and allowing enough lead time for visa processing, which can take several weeks depending on demand and document completeness.

Candidates should also plan finances carefully: airfare from Addis Ababa to the UK, accommodation for the exam period, and the CPSA fee itself represent a significant combined cost, so many Ethiopian doctors save specifically for this stage or coordinate travel with a study partner sitting the same circuit to share costs. Always check the GMC's current list of approved test venues and any changes to international testing availability directly on gmc-uk.org before booking non-refundable travel.

Life after registration: the NHS career path

Passing the UKMLA and gaining GMC registration is the gateway, not the destination. Once registered, Ethiopian doctors typically enter the NHS through one of several routes depending on experience: newly registered doctors with limited postgraduate experience often enter at foundation-equivalent trust grade posts, while those with several years of Ethiopian clinical experience — particularly in specialties such as internal medicine, general surgery, obstetrics and gynaecology, paediatrics, or emergency medicine — are frequently competitive for more senior trust grade or specialty training posts, subject to the same competitive processes UK-trained doctors go through.

Many Ethiopian doctors use their first NHS post to build UK-specific clinical experience and references before applying into formal specialty training programmes, which have their own separate application cycles, competency requirements, and sometimes additional exams (such as membership exams for the relevant Royal College). This next stage is a substantial topic in its own right and sits outside the scope of the initial UKMLA registration process covered here, but it is worth researching in parallel with your registration journey so that your first NHS job choice supports your longer-term specialty ambitions rather than closing off options.

Settling into life in the UK — registering with a GP, opening a bank account, arranging accommodation, and understanding your employment rights and NHS pension arrangements — is covered in detail in the site's practical relocation guide for IMG doctors, which is worth reading well before your planned arrival date rather than after you land.

Building a competitive NHS application as an Ethiopian doctor

GMC registration opens the door to NHS employment, but it does not by itself guarantee a specific post or specialty track — that requires a deliberate application strategy on top of your exam results. Most Ethiopian doctors entering the NHS for the first time begin either through the Foundation Programme, if they qualify as an early-career applicant without an accepted internship equivalent, or through a trust-grade or locally employed doctor post if their Ethiopian clinical experience already meets what NHS recruiters are looking for at a more senior junior level.

A strong first NHS application typically combines a clear, honestly presented CV covering your Ethiopian training and any postgraduate experience, a well-prepared interview that speaks confidently to UK clinical guidelines and NHS-specific processes (not just clinical knowledge in the abstract), and, where possible, informal contact with doctors already working in your target trust or specialty. Ethiopian doctors sometimes underplay strong clinical experience gained in resource-constrained settings, but NHS recruiters increasingly value the breadth of presentations and independent decision-making that many Ethiopian training environments provide — frame this experience as a genuine strength in interviews and applications rather than something to minimise.

Once in an NHS post, most Ethiopian doctors use their first one to two years to build UK-specific references, sit any postgraduate exams relevant to their target specialty, and research specialty training application cycles in detail before committing to a formal application. This staged approach, rather than applying to competitive specialty training immediately on arrival, tends to produce stronger outcomes and a smoother transition into UK practice generally.

Documents checklist for Ethiopian applicants

Gather these documents as early as possible, ideally before you formally begin your MyIntealth application: your original MD degree certificate and official academic transcripts from your medical school; evidence of internship and any compulsory service completion; a valid passport; passport-style photographs meeting GMC specifications; your English test certificate once sat (IELTS Academic or OET); and, where available, a certificate of good standing or licence-to-practise confirmation from Ethiopia's relevant health regulatory authority. If any of these documents use a name or date format that differs slightly across records (a common issue with transliterated names), resolve discrepancies with your university and passport office before submitting your verification request, since mismatches are a frequent cause of delay.

Common mistakes Ethiopian applicants make

  • Starting MyIntealth verification too late. This is the single biggest cause of delay; begin it months before you plan to book the AKT.
  • Underestimating the English test's UK-specific vocabulary demands, particularly OET's healthcare-specific format, without dedicated practice.
  • Booking non-refundable CPSA travel before confirming visa approval, risking wasted cost if the visa is delayed or refused.
  • Treating AKT preparation as a repeat of Ethiopian licensing exam revision, rather than studying specifically against the UKMLA content map and UK guidelines.
  • Not verifying internship completion documentation in advance, leading to unexpected verification queries partway through the process.
  • Overlooking the assistance available for displaced doctors. Ethiopian doctors who cannot access standard documentation because of conflict or displacement elsewhere in the region should check the GMC's assistance programme for refugee and displaced doctors rather than assuming the standard route is their only option.
  • Not researching neighbouring-country routes for comparison. Frequently asked questions

Can Ethiopian MD graduates sit the UKMLA?

Yes. Doctors who hold a Doctor of Medicine (MD) degree from an Ethiopian medical school recognised by the World Directory of Medical Schools can apply for GMC registration and are required to pass the UKMLA, made up of the AKT and the CPSA, unless they hold an exempting qualification.

Is Ethiopia's MD degree treated the same as an MBBS for GMC purposes?

The GMC does not require a specific degree title. What matters is that the primary medical qualification is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools and can be verified through MyIntealth. Ethiopia's MD is a first, undergraduate-entry medical degree and is assessed on that basis, not as a postgraduate doctorate.

Do I need to be registered with EFDA before applying to the GMC?

The GMC does not list registration with Ethiopia's Food and Drug Authority as a mandatory precondition, but holding a current licence to practise in Ethiopia strengthens your application, supports your certificate of good standing, and is generally expected by NHS employers and visa sponsors.

How much does the UKMLA cost for Ethiopian doctors in total?

Budget for the AKT (£283), the CPSA (£1,036), the provisional registration application fee (£26 if applicable) or full registration fee (£481), plus an English test (roughly £180-£230) and travel to a CPSA test centre. These are GMC figures effective from 1 April 2026 and should always be confirmed on gmc-uk.org before you pay.

Where do Ethiopian doctors sit the CPSA?

The CPSA is currently held at UK test centres. Ethiopian candidates should check the GMC's current list of venues and booking windows, since international CPSA circuits open and close periodically and are not guaranteed to run in every part of Africa.

What English language test do Ethiopian applicants need?

Most Ethiopian graduates will need to prove English proficiency through IELTS Academic or OET, since medical training in Ethiopia is not accepted by the GMC as automatic proof of English competence in the way a small number of exempted countries are. Confirm current accepted scores on gmc-uk.org.

How long does the process take from Ethiopia to GMC registration?

Realistically 12 to 24 months from starting document verification to receiving full registration with a licence to practise, depending on English test scheduling, AKT and CPSA booking availability, internship completion, and visa processing timelines.

What is MyIntealth and why do I need it?

MyIntealth is the platform (successor to EPIC) that carries out primary source verification of your Ethiopian medical degree directly with your awarding university. The GMC will not proceed with registration until this verification is complete, so Ethiopian applicants should start it early.

Do I need to complete my Ethiopian internship before applying?

Yes. GMC registration requires evidence that you have completed the internship and any compulsory service period tied to your primary medical qualification, since this is part of what makes your degree a recognised, licence-eligible qualification in Ethiopia.

Can Ethiopian doctors get help if fleeing conflict or unable to access documents?

Doctors who cannot obtain standard documentation because of displacement or conflict may be able to use the GMC's assistance programme for refugee and displaced doctors, which offers alternative evidence routes. This is a specialist pathway and eligibility is assessed case by case.

How does the Ethiopian route compare with neighbouring countries?

The core UKMLA process is identical for every country, but local details differ, such as the name of the licensing authority, degree titles, and internship length. Kenyan, Sudanese and other East African doctors face broadly the same GMC checklist with country-specific variations in verification and documentation.

What is the most common mistake Ethiopian applicants make?

Leaving MyIntealth verification until late in the process. Because Ethiopian university records departments can take weeks to respond to verification requests, starting this step months before you plan to book the AKT avoids the single biggest cause of delay.
