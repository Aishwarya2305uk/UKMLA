UKMLA resits are a reality that many candidates face — and understanding the rules, limits, and how to rebuild effectively after a setback is just as important as knowing how to prepare for the first attempt. Whether you have just received a fail result for the AKT, the CPSA, PLAB 1, or PLAB 2, the most important thing to know is that a single fail is not the end of your UK medical licensing journey. Most candidates who fail once go on to pass on a subsequent attempt when they change their approach. This guide covers everything you need to know: attempt limits, validity windows, mitigating circumstances, resit strategy, and the psychological dimension of bouncing back.

The GMC distinguishes between UK medical students and IMGs in how resit rules are structured. UK medical students' resit rules are largely managed at the medical school level within GMC-set parameters, while IMGs apply resit attempts directly through GMC Online. Both pathways are covered in detail below.

How Many Attempts Are Allowed? Complete Attempt Limit Table

The GMC sets maximum attempt limits for each component of the UKMLA. For IMGs taking the legacy PLAB route (still available to candidates who started before the UKMLA transition), the limits are well-established. For the full UKMLA pathway, the GMC has confirmed that up to four attempts at each component are permitted, with a fifth attempt possible in exceptional circumstances.

If you have already attempted PLAB 1 or PLAB 2 multiple times, clarify with the GMC how your previous attempts will be counted against your UKMLA attempt limit before booking your next sitting.

What Happens When You Reach Your Attempt Limit

Reaching the standard attempt limit does not automatically mean your medical career in the UK is over, but the options become significantly more restricted. For IMGs who have exhausted four PLAB 1 attempts, a fifth attempt may be permitted if you complete additional clinical training or supervised practice that the GMC considers appropriate and relevant. The GMC must approve the training before you sit a further attempt — you cannot simply complete any clinical work and assume it will be accepted.

For candidates who have exhausted all permitted attempts, the realistic alternatives include: the CESR/CEGPR route if you have postgraduate specialist qualifications and substantial specialist experience; exploring registration with another country's medical authority and then applying for specialist recognition; or, in rare cases, retraining through a recognised programme to obtain a new qualifying degree. Candidates in this position should seek advice from the GMC directly and may benefit from professional legal or regulatory advice.

For UK medical students, reaching a school's internal attempt limit will typically trigger a formal academic review, which may result in required additional training, programme extension, or in extreme cases, withdrawal from the programme. These processes are managed by individual medical schools according to their own regulations and the GMC's overarching fitness to practise and academic progress standards.

Validity Periods and Timing Windows

Understanding validity periods is essential for UKMLA resit planning because running out of validity on a passed component means starting again from scratch — at significant cost and delay.

  • AKT validity: Three years (36 months). The CPSA must be passed within this window.
  • PLAB 1 validity (for legacy pathway candidates): Two years (24 months). PLAB 2 must be sat and passed within this window.
  • English language test validity: Two years from the date of the test. If your test expires before you complete your GMC application and registration, you must resit the English language test.

The most dangerous scenario is accumulating multiple CPSA/PLAB 2 resits that push you towards the three-year AKT/PLAB 1 validity boundary. If you have failed your CPSA twice and are now in your third year after the AKT, you must prioritise your CPSA resit above all else to avoid the AKT expiring. If the AKT expires while you are still attempting the CPSA, you lose your AKT pass and must resit from the beginning.

Mitigating Circumstances: What They Are and How to Apply

Mitigating circumstances are genuine external factors beyond your control that affected your ability to perform to your usual standard in the exam. Examples include serious illness or injury in the period immediately before or during the exam, bereavement of a close family member, significant family emergencies, or traumatic events.

Mitigating circumstances do not guarantee a pass — they allow the relevant body (your medical school for UK students, or the GMC for IMGs) to consider whether the attempt should be treated differently. Depending on the outcome, this might mean the attempt does not count towards your attempt limit, or that you are given priority booking for the next sitting.

Mitigating Circumstances Application Process (Step by Step)

  1. Act immediately: Contact your medical school (UK students) or the GMC (IMGs) as soon as you know you are affected by a mitigating circumstance. Do not wait until results day.
  2. Complete the formal application form: Obtain the mitigating circumstances form from your medical school or GMC Online. Fill it out fully and accurately.
  3. Gather supporting evidence: Evidence must be objective, from an appropriate third party, and specifically relate to the timing of the examination. See the section below on acceptable evidence.
  4. Submit before the deadline: For UK medical students, schools typically require mitigating circumstances applications within a specific window after the exam (often within 7-14 days of the sitting). For IMGs applying to the GMC, submit as soon as possible.
  5. Await the outcome: The reviewing panel will consider your application and evidence and issue a decision. If your application is successful, you will be informed of how the attempt will be treated.
  6. Appeal if unsuccessful: If you believe your application was incorrectly refused, you have the right to appeal. See our appeals guide for information on the appeal process.

What Counts as Acceptable Evidence

  • Medical certificate or letter from a doctor confirming illness and its timing
  • Death certificate or funeral director's documentation for bereavement
  • Police report or official documentation for traumatic events
  • Hospital attendance or admission records
  • Letter from a GP confirming an ongoing health condition that flared up
  • Employer or supervisor letter for work-related emergencies (though these are assessed with more scrutiny)

Self-certification (i.e., your own written account without supporting documentation) is rarely accepted. Personal statements about exam anxiety, financial stress, or general life pressures are not considered mitigating circumstances in the formal sense, though some medical schools have wellbeing support pathways that may be relevant for such situations.

Root Cause Analysis: Why Did You Fail?

The most important step before beginning a resit preparation is identifying the root cause of the failure. Candidates who fail and simply do "more of the same" preparation rarely improve significantly. Failing a medical licensing exam typically falls into one of three categories:

  1. Knowledge gaps: You did not know enough of the clinical content. Identified by consistently getting questions wrong in specific domains, or by finding that you had not covered certain areas of the syllabus.
  2. Technique and exam strategy issues: You knew the content but struggled to apply it under exam conditions — misreading questions, overthinking, poor time management, changing correct answers, or misidentifying what the question was asking.
  3. Exam anxiety and psychological factors: Your preparation was adequate but performance on the day was affected by anxiety, sleep deprivation, physical illness, or other psychological factors.

Each of these requires a different response. For knowledge gaps, the solution is more structured content review with emphasis on weak domains. For technique issues, the solution is more timed practice under realistic exam conditions with deliberate reflection on process. For anxiety, the solution may involve mindfulness, counselling, or anxiety management strategies alongside continued preparation.

Use your result feedback report — which the GMC provides to failing candidates — as your primary diagnostic tool. This report identifies performance by domain and, for the CPSA/PLAB 2, by station type. Read it carefully and objectively before building your resit plan.

AKT Resit Strategy

If you have failed the AKT, a structured resit strategy should address the specific domains where you underperformed, not just general revision. Use the feedback report to identify your two or three weakest domains and make these the priority for your resit preparation.

Common improvements that make the difference in AKT resits include: switching from passive reading to active question-based learning (doing questions, reviewing explanations, noting patterns); using a spaced repetition system for high-yield facts in weak domains; practising under strictly timed conditions to build exam pace; and deliberately practising the technique of reading the lead-in question first before the clinical vignette, which reduces time wasted and improves focus.

Also examine your previous preparation method critically. Did you rely on a single question bank? Did you use questions that were genuinely UKMLA-standard? Did you practise under exam conditions or just casually doing questions while relaxed? The resit is an opportunity to correct preparation method as much as content knowledge.

CPSA / PLAB 2 Resit Strategy

PLAB 2 resits require an honest assessment of your communication skills. Review your feedback report and identify whether failures were concentrated in the interpersonal skills domain — if so, communication practice is the central priority. If failures were in data gathering, focus on systematising your history-taking and examination approach. If failures were in clinical management, focus on clinical decision-making and management planning.

For communication-based failures, the most effective resit preparation involves a significant increase in face-to-face practice with a partner, ideally with specific feedback on identified behaviours (such as not using ICE, rushing through explanations, or failing to safety-net). A structured PLAB 2 preparation course in the lead-up to your resit is often the single most valuable investment for a candidate who failed primarily on communication.

The Psychological Impact of Failing and How to Manage It

Failing a medical licensing exam is genuinely hard. Many candidates experience a period of shock, embarrassment, self-doubt, and grief following a fail result — these are normal responses to a significant setback in a high-stakes professional context. Acknowledging these feelings rather than suppressing them is an important part of recovering effectively.

Practical strategies for managing the psychological impact include: giving yourself a short (but time-limited) period to process the result before beginning resit planning; talking to trusted friends, family, or a mentor who understands the medical training context; using the BMA's wellbeing support services or your medical school's student support team; and reframing the fail as diagnostic information rather than a verdict on your worth as a doctor or person.

The most damaging response to a fail is immediate all-day studying driven by panic, without taking time to do root cause analysis or plan a structured resit strategy. This leads to burnout, reinforced poor habits, and often another fail. Give yourself time to regroup, then plan carefully.

Financial Planning for Resits

Resits have a real financial cost that candidates should plan for honestly. The exam fees alone add up: PLAB 1 is £255 per sitting and PLAB 2 is significantly more expensive. Add MyIntealth verification costs, English language test resits if needed, travel and accommodation for Manchester (for PLAB 2), preparation course fees, and lost income from time spent studying rather than working.

If you are an IMG who is not yet working in the UK, the cumulative cost of multiple resit attempts can be several thousand pounds. Plan your financial runway realistically — if you are on attempt three of four for PLAB 1, ensure you have the financial resources to sustain the preparation period without financial pressure that could affect your exam performance.

Using the UKMLA Feedback Report to Guide Resit Prep

The GMC provides a performance feedback report to all candidates who fail the AKT or CPSA. This report is one of the most valuable resources available to a resitting candidate and should be the foundation of your resit preparation plan. For the AKT, the report breaks down performance by clinical domain and question type. For the CPSA, it breaks down performance by station type and assessment domain (data gathering, clinical management, interpersonal skills).

Create a structured analysis of your feedback report by categorising your weaker areas, estimating the size of the gap between your score and the pass mark in each domain, and using this to allocate proportional preparation time in your resit plan. Domains where you scored very close to the pass mark need less attention than domains where you scored well below it.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA Resits

Is there a waiting period between attempts?

Yes. There is typically a minimum waiting period between PLAB 1 / AKT attempts, which in practice means waiting for the next scheduled quarterly sitting. You cannot sit the same exam component twice in rapid succession. For PLAB 2 / CPSA, the waiting period depends on the next available slot in Manchester, which is commonly four to six months. Check the GMC guidance for any specific minimum waiting period rules that apply to your situation, as these can be updated.

Do mitigating circumstances automatically mean my attempt doesn't count?

No. Submitting a mitigating circumstances application does not guarantee that the attempt will not count towards your limit. The reviewing panel must assess your application and the supporting evidence. Some applications are successful in having the attempt discounted; others result in alternative arrangements (such as a free resit or priority booking) rather than the attempt being removed from the count. The outcome depends on the nature and severity of the mitigating circumstances and the quality of the evidence provided.

Can I resit PLAB 1 while my appeal is ongoing?

This depends on the timing and the nature of the appeal. In most cases, appeals do not automatically prevent you from booking a resit, but sitting a resit while an appeal is pending can affect the appeal process — for example, if the appeal is about whether your previous attempt should count. Contact the GMC before booking a resit if you have an active appeal, and seek advice on the implications of sitting before the appeal is resolved.

What happens to my attempt count during the UKMLA transition?

The GMC has confirmed that previous PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 attempts will count towards the corresponding UKMLA AKT and CPSA attempt limits. If you have already sat PLAB 1 three times and are now registering under the UKMLA system, you have one remaining standard attempt at the AKT. The exact mechanics of how attempts are counted during the transition period should be confirmed directly with the GMC, as the rules continue to be refined during the transition.

When should I consider not resitting?

This is a deeply personal question. Consider seeking external advice — from a medical career adviser, a mentor, or the BMA — if you have reached or are approaching your attempt limit, if you are experiencing significant psychological distress that is not improving with support, or if the cumulative financial and emotional cost of repeated resit attempts is unsustainable. Alternative pathways do exist, including the CESR route for experienced specialists, and there are medical careers outside the UK clinical licensing framework that your medical training qualifies you for. A fail is not the end of your medical career — but it may signal a need to reconsider which pathway is right for you at this time.

Conclusion

UKMLA resits are challenging but manageable with the right approach. The key is honest root cause analysis, a structured resit strategy based on your feedback report, appropriate management of the psychological impact, and careful attention to attempt limits and validity windows. Most candidates who fail once and apply the lessons learned go on to pass.
