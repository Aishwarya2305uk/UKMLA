UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors describes a well-defined, increasingly popular pathway to UK medical registration for graduates of Bangladeshi medical schools, and it follows the same core structure the GMC applies to every international medical graduate: degree recognition, credential verification, English language testing, two structured examinations, and a formal registration application. None of these steps is conceptually difficult on its own, but together they involve enough administrative detail, cost, and waiting time that many first-time applicants from Bangladesh underestimate how long the full journey actually takes.

This guide walks through the entire UKMLA pathway specifically as it applies to doctors trained in Bangladesh — from confirming that an MBBS degree from a medical college recognised by the Bangladesh Medical and Dental Council (BM&DC) satisfies GMC eligibility rules, through primary source verification, booking exams, and budgeting in taka for a pathway priced in pounds sterling. It also covers where Bangladeshi candidates can sit the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), why the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) must be taken in Manchester, and the realistic timeline and common mistakes that shape how quickly a Bangladeshi candidate actually reaches registration.

Bangladesh is one of the fastest-growing source countries for internationally trained doctors joining the UK medical register, and interest in the route has grown considerably in recent years as more Bangladeshi graduates look beyond traditional destinations. Consequently, the volume of practical, first-hand experience with the UKMLA process is still smaller than for more established diaspora routes, which makes a clear, accurate guide to eligibility, cost, and sequencing especially valuable for Bangladeshi applicants planning their journey.

The UKMLA Route From Bangladesh: A Quick Overview

Before examining each stage in detail, it helps to see the whole pathway from BM&DC registration to NHS practice at a glance. UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors follows a defined seven-stage route, and the sequence for a Bangladeshi-trained doctor generally runs as follows:

  1. Confirm eligibility: verify that your MBBS degree from a BM&DC-recognised Bangladeshi medical college is accepted by the GMC, and that you hold full, unrestricted BM&DC registration following your compulsory internship year.
  2. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your medical qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials).
  3. Sit and pass an approved English language test — IELTS Academic or OET Medicine — unless you hold an accepted exemption.
  4. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT (the PLAB 1 equivalent for IMGs), sat at a Pearson VUE test centre.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA (the PLAB 2 equivalent), which can only be sat in person at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.
  6. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise once both assessments are passed and all supporting documents are verified.
  7. Apply for NHS posts — typically clinical fellow roles, trust-grade positions, or Foundation Year 2-equivalent posts — and begin practising in the UK.

Each stage carries its own processing time, cost, and typical delay, all covered in the sections below. Understanding the full sequence early prevents the single most common planning mistake made by Bangladeshi candidates: assuming that passing the two exams is the hardest part, when in practice credential verification and document logistics frequently take longer than exam preparation itself. Many candidates who are used to the relatively centralised, self-contained structure of Bangladeshi postgraduate processes are surprised by how much of the UKMLA pathway depends on external parties responding promptly — medical college registrars, the BM&DC head office in Dhaka, and GMC caseworkers — rather than on their own study effort alone. Treating the administrative track and the exam preparation track as two separate projects, run in parallel rather than one after the other, is therefore the single biggest determinant of how quickly a Bangladeshi candidate reaches registration.

BM&DC Recognition and UKMLA Eligibility for Bangladeshi Doctors

The starting point for any Bangladeshi doctor exploring the UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors pathway is confirming basic eligibility. The GMC requires that your primary medical qualification comes from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools and that your degree is recognised by the relevant national regulator — in Bangladesh's case, the Bangladesh Medical and Dental Council (BM&DC). Graduates of BM&DC-accredited institutions such as Dhaka Medical College, Chittagong Medical College, and Sir Salimullah Medical College generally satisfy this first eligibility hurdle without difficulty, provided the college was properly accredited at the time of graduation.

Beyond the medical college itself, the GMC also expects evidence that you completed the mandatory one-year internship required by the BM&DC and that you hold full, unrestricted BM&DC registration rather than provisional or temporary registration. A Certificate of Good Standing confirming your registration status and disciplinary history is typically required as part of your application, and this must be requested directly from the BM&DC in Dhaka — a step many candidates leave too late, since the council can take several weeks to process such requests, particularly outside standard office hours in the working week.

Full detail of who is permitted to sit the exam, including specific rules for degrees awarded outside standard timeframes or through non-standard routes, is set out in the site's guide to UKMLA eligibility requirements. It is worth reading this in full before committing time and money to the process, since eligibility is assessed on a case-by-case basis for some qualification types. Comparing the Bangladeshi route against a broadly similar South Asian pathway is also useful context here — the requirements facing Sri Lankan doctors pursuing the same route follow an almost identical structure, even though the specific verification body and typical timelines differ.

Primary Source Verification via EPIC

Once basic eligibility is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of your Bangladeshi medical degree, meaning your qualification is confirmed directly with your medical college rather than accepted on the strength of a certificate alone. This is handled through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials), the platform used globally for this purpose. Bangladeshi medical colleges vary considerably in how quickly they respond to EPIC verification requests; well-established government colleges with dedicated academic affairs offices tend to respond within four to eight weeks, whereas others can take three months or longer, particularly during examination periods or national holidays.

Full detail on how primary source verification works, what documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting evidence is available directly from the regulator: see the GMC's primary source verification requirements for international medical graduates. Although the GMC's own worked examples on that page tend to reference Indian institutions, the underlying EPIC process is identical for every IMG source country, including Bangladesh; the detailed walk-through in the site's guide to GMC registration and EPIC verification is written around an Indian example but applies in exactly the same way to a Bangladeshi applicant's own EPIC submission. Candidates are strongly advised to read both resources before initiating their EPIC application, since incomplete submissions are one of the most common causes of delay.

What Counts as BM&DC Recognition in Practice

It is worth being precise about what "BM&DC-recognised" actually means for GMC purposes, because the two regulators assess slightly different things. BM&DC registration confirms that you are entitled to practise medicine within the Bangladeshi health system; on its own, it does not transfer any rights or recognition in the UK. The GMC instead looks at three separate elements: whether your medical college appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant graduation year, whether the degree programme met minimum duration and curriculum standards, and whether you can produce primary evidence — degree certificate, full transcripts, and internship completion documentation — that stands up to independent verification. A doctor can hold entirely valid BM&DC registration and still face delays at the GMC stage if, for example, a college's WDOMS listing lapsed for a period, or academic transcripts are incomplete. Checking your medical college's current WDOMS status before you begin is a five-minute task that can save weeks of confusion later. It is also worth noting that Bangladeshi qualifications are sometimes confused with degrees from elsewhere in South Asia during preliminary desk checks, so applicants should be precise and consistent about naming their exact college and graduation year on every form submitted.

Documents Bangladeshi Candidates Should Gather Early

Beyond the primary degree certificate itself, most Bangladeshi applicants need a consistent set of supporting documents, and gathering them in parallel rather than sequentially saves considerable time:

  • MBBS degree certificate and full academic transcripts, year by year, from your medical college.
  • Internship completion certificate confirming you completed the mandatory one-year BM&DC-supervised internship.
  • BM&DC Certificate of Good Standing, confirming your registration status and disciplinary record, requested directly from the BM&DC head office in Dhaka.
  • International passport, valid well beyond your expected registration date.
  • Evidence of any name changes — marriage certificates or statutory declarations — where your degree name and passport name differ, which is a common source of query for Bangladeshi applicants whose documents record names in slightly varying transliterations.
  • Certified translations of any document not already issued in English, from a recognised professional translation service.

Requesting the BM&DC Certificate of Good Standing early is particularly important, since this document is issued by a separate national body from your medical college and follows its own, often slower, administrative timeline. Building in a buffer of several extra weeks for this single document alone is a sensible default for most Bangladeshi applicants.

How to Apply for the UKMLA From Bangladesh: Step by Step

With eligibility confirmed, the practical application process behind UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors follows six sequential steps. Each is described below in the order it should typically be actioned.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

Registration begins with creating a GMC Online account at the GMC's official website. This account becomes your central hub for the entire process — you will use it to track your EPIC verification status, book both the AKT and CPSA, submit your English language evidence, and eventually complete your registration application. Use a personal email address you will retain long-term, not a college or hospital email that may be deactivated after you leave a post, since GMC Online correspondence can span well over a year for most Bangladeshi candidates.

Step 2: Begin EPIC Verification Early

As covered above, EPIC verification of your MBBS degree is usually the single longest step in the entire pathway. Start this as early as possible — ideally before you have finalised your English test date or exam bookings — because it runs in parallel with other preparation and does not require your active involvement once submitted, beyond following up with your medical college if there are delays. Keep a written record of every communication with your college's registrar, since this becomes useful evidence if you need to escalate a delayed response.

Step 3: Gather and Submit English Language Evidence

In parallel with EPIC verification, sit your English language test (IELTS Academic or OET Medicine — full detail in the dedicated section below) and upload your certificate to GMC Online once results are issued. Many Bangladeshi candidates assume that strong academic English from school and college reduces the testing requirement; in practice, formal evidence is still required in almost all cases, so it is worth booking a test slot early rather than waiting to find out whether an exemption applies.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once your GMC Online account is active, you can book the AKT through the Pearson VUE booking system linked from your account. Booking windows typically open several months ahead of each testing period, and popular dates fill quickly, so book as soon as a suitable slot appears rather than waiting until the last moment. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines exactly what conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas are examinable, alongside a structured question bank — see the guidance on choosing a good AKT question bank for a comparison of the main preparation options available to Bangladeshi candidates.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once you have passed the AKT. Because the CPSA is only available in Manchester, Bangladeshi candidates need to plan a UK trip around this stage, factoring in visa arrangements, flights, and several days of accommodation for preparation immediately before the assessment. Many candidates combine the trip with a short period of UK clinical observation or shadowing beforehand, which helps with the specific communication and consultation style the CPSA examines.

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and your EPIC verification, English language evidence, and any outstanding documents (Certificate of Good Standing, internship completion certificate, identity documents) are all confirmed, you can submit your full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage is available in the site's GMC registration process guide, which covers document checklists, processing times, and what to do if your application is queried.

UKMLA AKT Test Centres for Bangladeshi Candidates

One of the most practical advantages of UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors, compared with some other overseas licensing routes, is that the AKT can often be sat regionally rather than requiring a long-haul trip purely for the exam. The AKT is delivered through the Pearson VUE global network of test centres, and candidates should check current centre locations and availability directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal, since options in and around South Asia change between sitting windows. For a full walk-through of how to find, compare, and book a suitable centre, see the site's dedicated AKT test centres guide, which explains the booking workflow and what to check before confirming a date.

This is a significant contrast with the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in Bangladesh, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — it is held exclusively at the GMC's purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. This means every Bangladeshi candidate must, at minimum, make one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway, even if the AKT is passed entirely without leaving South Asia. Budgeting for this single mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visitor visa, accommodation, and living costs for the preparation and exam period — should be built into financial planning from the very start of the process, not left until after the AKT is passed.

English Language Requirements for Bangladeshi Doctors

English language evidence is one of the most consistently underestimated requirements within UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors applications. Medical education in Bangladesh is delivered substantially in English from the pre-clinical years onward, which leads many Bangladeshi doctors to assume that formal English testing will not apply to them. In practice, the GMC requires documentary evidence of English language proficiency from the great majority of international medical graduates regardless of the language of their medical training, and Bangladeshi degrees are not automatically exempt. Candidates should plan for formal testing as a standard part of the pathway rather than hoping for a waiver.

Two tests are accepted: IELTS Academic, where the GMC requires an overall score of 7.0, with no individual component (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the GMC requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. OET is often more intuitive for practising doctors because its scenarios are built entirely around healthcare communication — referral letters, doctor-patient consultations, and clinical case notes — rather than general academic topics. IELTS Academic, by contrast, draws on a broader range of subject matter and has more widely available preparation material and test centres across Bangladesh.

Both test certificates have a defined validity period from the date of the test, so timing matters: sitting the test too early relative to your overall pathway risks the certificate expiring before you submit your registration application, which would require a costly and time-consuming re-sit. Full detail on accepted scores, accepted test providers, and how evidence is submitted is available directly from the regulator. Although the site's own detailed walk-through of the English language requirement, the English language requirement for the UKMLA, is written with an Indian example, the underlying scoring bands, test formats, and submission process are identical for a Bangladeshi applicant. It is worth reading both this guide and the official GMC page in full, since the specific evidentiary requirements are updated from time to time and always take precedence over general summaries.

Life and Career for Bangladeshi Doctors in the NHS After Registration

Completing UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors is only the midpoint of the journey, not the destination. Once GMC registration is secured, Bangladeshi doctors join a growing and increasingly visible community within the NHS. Although the diaspora is not yet as large as those from India or Nigeria, Bangladesh has climbed steadily up the list of source countries for internationally trained doctors on the UK medical register, and Bangladeshi doctor associations and informal peer networks are now active in most major NHS regions, particularly in areas with established Bangladeshi communities such as East London, Birmingham, and parts of the North West.

Career progression after registration generally follows one of several routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post (the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs, offering FY2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place), a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK experience and portfolio evidence are in place, or locum work through NHS staff banks and agencies for those who want flexibility while building their UK clinical track record. Many Bangladeshi doctors who enter through clinical fellow posts subsequently progress into core or specialty training within two to three years, particularly in specialties experiencing significant workforce shortages such as general internal medicine, psychiatry, and emergency medicine, where competition ratios for IMG applicants tend to be more favourable.

It is worth noting that the broad shape of this journey — degree recognition, credential verification, English testing, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam, then registration — mirrors the equivalent routes for doctors from other major South Asian source countries. The pathway followed by Pakistani doctors pursuing UK registration uses an almost identical sequence, and the much larger and longer-established route followed by doctors from India shares the same structural skeleton, even though the specific verification bodies, typical processing times, and diaspora scale differ meaningfully between all three countries.

Day-to-day life for newly registered Bangladeshi doctors in the NHS typically involves an adjustment period covering both clinical and cultural dimensions. Clinically, the biggest early adjustments tend to be around NHS documentation standards (structured handover formats such as SBAR), a strong institutional emphasis on multidisciplinary team working, and a more codified approach to consent and capacity than many candidates are accustomed to from Bangladeshi practice. Socially, Bangladeshi doctors moving to the UK benefit from established Bangladeshi community networks, particularly in London and the South East, alongside a growing number of dedicated professional associations offering mentorship to newly arrived doctors. Salary progression follows the standard NHS pay scale for the relevant grade, and while it can take time to reach specialty training, most Bangladeshi doctors who remain committed to the pathway report that the structured nature of UK training — clear competency frameworks, protected training time, and defined progression points — compares favourably with less standardised systems elsewhere.

Common Mistakes Bangladeshi Doctors Make on This Route

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among Bangladeshi candidates pursuing UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors as their route to GMC registration. Most of these mistakes are not clinical — they are planning and administrative errors that have nothing to do with medical knowledge, yet they are responsible for a disproportionate share of the delays and extra costs Bangladeshi candidates experience. Being aware of them in advance, before committing money to a specific exam date or travel booking, can save months of delay and hundreds of pounds in wasted fees.

  • Starting EPIC verification too late. Because EPIC processing time depends heavily on your medical college's administrative speed rather than your own effort, this step should be initiated before — not after — you begin AKT preparation. Candidates who wait until they are ready to book the AKT before starting EPIC often lose two to three months they could have used productively.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The £1,036 exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Flights from Dhaka or Chittagong, several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, food, and — for many candidates — a short UK preparation course before the exam, can together add £1,000–£2,000 on top of the fee. Candidates who budget only for the headline fee frequently find themselves scrambling for additional funds close to their exam date.
  • Not accounting for taka exchange rate volatility. Because all UKMLA and GMC fees are charged in pounds sterling, a Bangladeshi candidate who calculates their taka budget many months before actually paying can find the real cost has risen substantially by the time payment is due. Build a buffer of at least 15–20% above your initial taka budget calculation, and monitor exchange rates in the weeks leading up to each payment rather than assuming an old estimate still holds.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. Both IELTS and OET certificates are valid for a defined period from the test date. Candidates who sit their English test early in the process — before EPIC verification or AKT preparation is complete — sometimes find their certificate has expired by the time they are ready to submit their full registration application, forcing a costly re-sit. Sequence your English test so its validity period comfortably covers your expected registration application date.
  • Treating document translation and certification casually. Any document not already issued in English requires a certified professional translation, not an informal translation from a bilingual colleague. Underestimating the time this takes, or submitting translations that do not meet the GMC's certification standard, is a common cause of application delay.
  • Assuming AKT and CPSA preparation can be compressed into the final weeks. Both assessments draw on a specific, defined content base — the official MLA content map for the AKT and a structured OSCE circuit for the CPSA — and candidates who begin serious preparation only a few weeks before their booked date typically underperform relative to their actual clinical knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a Bangladeshi MBBS degree accepted by the GMC?

Degrees from BM&DC-accredited Bangladeshi medical colleges, such as Dhaka Medical College, Chittagong Medical College, and Sir Salimullah Medical College, generally satisfy the GMC's basic eligibility requirement, provided the college was properly accredited at the time you graduated and your qualification appears on the World Directory of Medical Schools. However, acceptance also depends on completing primary source verification through EPIC and holding full, unrestricted BM&DC registration following your internship year. Always confirm your specific situation against the current guidance on UKMLA eligibility requirements rather than assuming eligibility automatically.

Where can Bangladeshi doctors sit the UKMLA AKT?

The AKT is delivered through the Pearson VUE global network of test centres, and candidates should check current locations and availability directly through their GMC Online account or the Pearson VUE booking portal, since options can change between testing windows. The site's AKT test centres guide explains how to search for and compare suitable centres before booking a date.

Do I have to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, this step cannot be avoided. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. There is no option to sit the CPSA remotely or anywhere within Bangladesh, so every Bangladeshi candidate must budget for at least one trip to the UK to complete the pathway.

How much does the whole UKMLA process cost in taka?

The core GMC and exam fees — AKT, CPSA, and initial registration — total approximately £1,800, which converts to roughly ৳280,000 at broadly representative and approximate exchange rates, though this figure moves with the market. Once English testing, EPIC verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget is closer to £3,300–£4,800. Because the taka has fluctuated against the pound in recent years, candidates should recheck current rates and current GMC fee levels close to the time of each payment rather than relying on an estimate made months in advance.

Do Bangladeshi doctors need to take an English language test if their degree was taught in English?

In almost all cases, yes. Bangladeshi medical education being conducted substantially in English does not automatically exempt a candidate from the GMC's formal English language evidence requirement. Most Bangladeshi candidates need to submit a valid IELTS Academic score (7.0 overall, no component below 7.0) or OET Medicine result (Grade B minimum in all four components) as part of their application.

What is the realistic timeline from graduating in Bangladesh to working in the NHS?

Most candidates should plan for 18 to 30 months from starting EPIC verification to beginning their first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. This accounts for BM&DC document processing, EPIC verification delays, English test preparation, exam preparation for both assessments, and the GMC registration application itself. Candidates who need to re-sit either exam, or who face slower-than-average verification, should expect this timeline to extend.

How does the Bangladeshi route compare to other South Asian IMG routes?

The overall structure — degree recognition, primary source verification, English testing, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam in the UK, then registration — is broadly the same across South Asian source countries, including the routes taken by Sri Lankan doctors and Pakistani doctors. What differs between countries is the specific verification body, typical EPIC processing times, and the size and structure of the existing diaspora network available to support new arrivals. Candidates weighing up degree recognition more broadly may also find it useful to compare how the GMC treats Bangladeshi qualifications against the question of whether an Indian MBBS is considered valid in the UK, since the underlying recognition principles are shared across the region.

Can a Bangladeshi doctor with several years of post-internship experience skip the AKT?

No. There is no experience-based exemption from either the AKT or the CPSA. Regardless of how many years a Bangladeshi doctor has practised following internship — whether in general practice, a teaching hospital, or a specialist unit — both components of the UKMLA must be passed to gain full GMC registration with a licence to practise. Substantial post-qualification clinical experience is genuinely valuable preparation for the CPSA in particular, since it builds the clinical reasoning and consultation fluency the assessment examines, but it does not substitute for sitting the exams themselves.

Conclusion

The UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors is a clear, well-structured pathway that rewards early planning around credential verification, realistic budgeting in taka, and careful sequencing of English testing against exam dates. Candidates who start EPIC verification early, prepare systematically against the official content map and a reliable AKT question bank, and plan properly for the mandatory Manchester trip consistently move through the pathway faster and with fewer costly setbacks. Comparing notes with candidates following the equally structured Nigerian doctors' route can also be useful, since both countries' diaspora communities have learned similar lessons about sequencing and budgeting even though the specific regulators differ.
