NHS job interview preparation is the single stage of the whole UK medical career pathway that candidates most often leave until the last minute, despite it being the moment that actually determines whether months of exam revision and paperwork convert into a real job offer. Passing the UKMLA and clearing GMC registration proves that a candidate is safe and competent to practise; the interview is where a panel decides whether that candidate is the right fit for their specific department, team, and patient population. Treating the interview as a formality that will somehow take care of itself is one of the most common and most avoidable mistakes newly registered doctors make, whether they trained in the UK or overseas.

This guide sets out exactly what to expect from a typical NHS clinical interview panel, how to prepare competency-based answers using the STAR technique, which NHS values frameworks panels routinely draw on, how to structure a clinical scenario answer so that it demonstrates safe, escalatable reasoning, and what international medical graduates (IMGs) specifically need to do differently to prepare, including the growing number of interviews now conducted entirely by video. It is written for both UK medical graduates attending their first Foundation Programme or Trust Grade interview and IMGs attending their first NHS interview from overseas, since the two audiences share far more preparation ground than either group typically assumes.

None of what follows requires innate talent or a naturally polished interview manner. Structured preparation — understanding the format, rehearsing answers against a known framework, and researching the specific trust and post — reliably outperforms raw confidence or clinical knowledge alone. Candidates who prepare systematically for their NHS job interview preparation consistently report feeling less anxious and performing more fluently than those who walk in hoping their knowledge will simply "come out" under pressure.

NHS Job Interview Preparation: The Quick Answer

NHS job interview preparation means being ready for three distinct question types in a single panel interview: values-based questions assessing fit with the NHS Constitution and trust values, competency-based behavioural questions best answered using the STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) technique, and clinical scenario questions that test safe, structured clinical reasoning. A well-prepared candidate researches the specific trust and post in advance, rehearses two or three STAR examples per common competency theme, practises a consistent structure for clinical scenarios that always includes safety-netting and escalation, and — for IMGs in particular — demonstrates working familiarity with UK-specific frameworks such as Good Medical Practice and NICE guidance even before starting their first UK post.

Most NHS interviews last between twenty and forty-five minutes and are conducted by a panel of two to four people, typically a consultant or senior clinician from the department, a representative from HR or medical staffing, and sometimes a nurse or allied health professional to assess multidisciplinary awareness. Structured preparation across all three question types listed above, rather than concentrating effort only on clinical knowledge, is what most reliably converts an interview invitation into a job offer.

Understanding the Structure of an NHS Clinical Interview Panel

NHS recruitment, even for entry-level Trust Grade and Foundation Programme posts, follows a considerably more standardised format than many candidates expect based on interview norms elsewhere. Understanding this structure in advance is the first and most important part of any serious NHS job interview preparation plan, since it allows a candidate to allocate preparation time proportionally rather than over-preparing for one question type at the expense of the others.

Who Sits on the Panel

A typical NHS interview panel includes a consultant or senior clinician from the specialty or department recruiting for the post, a manager or representative from HR or medical staffing whose role is partly to assess professionalism and values alignment, and — increasingly common, particularly for larger trusts and more senior posts — a nurse, pharmacist, or other allied health professional whose presence signals how seriously the trust takes multidisciplinary working as a selection criterion. Some panels also include a lay or patient representative, especially for posts with significant patient-facing responsibility. Knowing in advance who is likely to be present helps a candidate calibrate their answers: a clinical scenario answer should satisfy the consultant's expectation of safe reasoning, while a values-based answer should speak as directly to the HR panel member as to the clinician.

The Three Question Categories

Almost every NHS clinical interview draws questions from three overlapping categories, usually in a roughly even split across the interview's time allocation.

  • Values-based questions, assessing alignment with the NHS Constitution and the employing trust's own stated values — for example, "Tell us about a time you put a patient's needs before your own convenience" or "What does compassion mean to you in a clinical context?"
  • Competency-based (behavioural) questions, typically phrased as "Tell me about a time when..." or "Give an example of...", probing communication, teamwork, conflict resolution, and handling pressure — these are the questions the STAR technique, covered in detail below, is specifically designed to answer.
  • Clinical scenario or situational judgement-style questions, presenting a hypothetical clinical situation and asking the candidate to talk through their reasoning and management, testing safe prioritisation and appropriate escalation rather than encyclopaedic textbook knowledge.

A fourth, shorter category appears in most interviews too: practical and logistical questions covering availability to start and, for IMGs, visa or right-to-work status. These are usually brief and factual, but candidates should still prepare concise, confident answers rather than treating them as an afterthought, since hesitation on straightforward logistical questions can unsettle a candidate's composure heading into the more substantive parts of the interview.

How the Format Differs Across Post Types

The exact balance of these question categories shifts depending on the type of post. Foundation Programme interviews, where they still occur locally rather than through the centralised national allocation process, tend to weight values-based and situational judgement questions heavily, mirroring the style of the Situational Judgement Test candidates may already be familiar with from medical school. Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow interviews, the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs following the pathway from UKMLA success to a first NHS post, typically place somewhat more weight on clinical scenario questions, since the panel is assessing whether a candidate can safely manage a specific caseload from day one. Specialty training interviews, further along the career pathway, often add a portfolio or evidence-based discussion component. Regardless of post type, the underlying preparation approach — STAR for competency questions, a consistent safety-first structure for clinical scenarios, and genuine familiarity with NHS values — remains the same.

Consequently, before attending any interview, a candidate should establish which post type they are applying for and adjust their preparation balance accordingly, rather than applying a single generic template to every interview they attend during a job search.

The STAR Technique for Competency-Based Questions

The STAR technique — Situation, Task, Action, Result — is the standard framework NHS interview panels expect candidates to use when answering competency-based behavioural questions, and it is, without exaggeration, the single most useful preparation tool available for this part of an NHS interview. STAR gives an answer a clear beginning, middle, and end, preventing the two most common failure modes panels report: answers that ramble without ever reaching a concrete outcome, and answers that describe a general approach ("I always try to communicate well") rather than a specific real example.

Breaking Down Each Element

  • Situation — briefly set the scene: where you were working, what your role was, and the context of the event. This should take no more than two or three sentences; panels consistently mark down answers that spend too long on background at the expense of the action and result.
  • Task — state clearly what needed to be achieved or what problem you were specifically responsible for addressing. This distinguishes your individual responsibility from the situation around you.
  • Action — describe, in specific and personal terms, what you actually did. This is the part of the answer that should receive the most time and detail, and it should be phrased in the first person ("I contacted the registrar and explained...") rather than the collective ("we decided..."), since panels are assessing your individual contribution, not a team's.
  • Result — close with what happened as a direct consequence of your action, ideally including a measurable or observable outcome, and — where genuinely relevant — a brief reflection on what you learned or would do differently. A result without any reflection can feel unfinished; a reflection without a concrete result can feel vague, so aim to include both.

Preparing STAR Examples in Advance

The most effective way to prepare using STAR is not to memorise word-for-word scripts, which tend to sound rehearsed and can fall apart under a follow-up question, but to prepare a bank of five to eight genuine examples from clinical placements, prior employment, or relevant non-clinical experience, each mapped loosely to a common competency theme. A single well-chosen example can often be adapted to answer more than one question, so the goal is breadth of underlying experience rather than a separate rehearsed answer for every conceivable question.

Candidates should draft a brief written outline — a few bullet points per example, not a full script — covering the Situation, Task, Action, and Result for each of these six themes, and then practise saying each one aloud several times before the interview. Rehearsing aloud, ideally with another person asking follow-up questions, exposes gaps and vague phrasing far more effectively than reading a written answer silently. This same discipline of preparing structured, evidence-based answers in advance rather than composing them under pressure is equally relevant at the earlier NHS CV and application stage, where competency-based questions on the structured application form draw on exactly the same bank of examples a candidate should later bring to interview.

Common STAR Mistakes to Avoid

Several recurring errors weaken otherwise strong candidates' STAR answers. Firstly, spending too long describing the Situation and Task and rushing the Action and Result — aim to spend roughly 60 to 70 per cent of the answer's length on Action and Result combined. Secondly, describing what "the team" did rather than what the candidate personally did, which makes it impossible for a panel to assess individual competency. Thirdly, choosing an example where the outcome was poor without offering any genuine reflection. Finally, reusing the same example for multiple answers within a single interview — panels notice this quickly, and it suggests a narrow range of experience even when that is not actually the case.

NHS Values and the NHS Constitution: What Panels Are Really Assessing

Values-based questions are frequently underestimated by candidates who assume interviews are primarily a test of clinical knowledge. In reality, NHS recruitment places substantial weight on whether a candidate's personal values genuinely align with the values the NHS has formally committed to, and panels are trained to probe beyond a rehearsed, generic answer to assess whether that alignment is authentic.

The NHS Constitution's Values

The NHS Constitution for England sets out seven core values that underpin how NHS staff are expected to behave and make decisions: working together for patients, respect and dignity, commitment to quality of care, compassion, improving lives, everyone counts, and — a value added in later revisions — a fair and consistent approach for all. Anyone serious about NHS job interview preparation should read the official NHS Constitution for England directly at least once before any interview, since panels sometimes ask candidates to name or explain specific values, and a candidate who can speak to them specifically performs noticeably better than one who offers only a vague paraphrase.

  • Working together for patients — placing patient interests above professional or organisational self-interest, and collaborating across disciplines and organisations.
  • Respect and dignity — valuing every individual, whether patient, carer, or colleague, and treating them without discrimination.
  • Commitment to quality of care — striving to get the basics of quality and safety right every time, alongside continuous improvement.
  • Compassion — responding with humanity and kindness, understanding a patient's physical, mental, and emotional needs.
  • Improving lives — striving to improve health outcomes and people's wider wellbeing, seeking out and applying research and innovation.
  • Everyone counts — using resources fairly and effectively for the whole community, not just individual patients, and ensuring nobody is discriminated against or excluded.

Beyond the national Constitution, most individual NHS trusts also publish their own set of local values — common examples include variations on "care, compassion, respect, excellence, and teamwork" — usually displayed on the trust's own website. A well-prepared candidate should look up the specific values of the trust they are interviewing with and, where genuinely possible, reference them by name, since this signals real research and interest in that particular employer rather than a generic answer that could apply to any NHS trust.

How to Answer Values-Based Questions Authentically

The strongest values-based answers combine a brief acknowledgement of the relevant value with a specific, genuine example — in effect, a shortened STAR structure applied to a values theme rather than a pure competency theme. For example, when asked "What does compassion mean to you as a doctor?", a strong answer briefly defines compassion in a clinical context and then illustrates it with a short, real example of a moment when the candidate consciously prioritised a patient's emotional needs alongside their clinical ones. Panels are trained to notice generic, textbook-sounding answers that could have been given by anyone, and to value answers that feel personal and specific. Consequently, candidates should resist giving the answer they believe the panel "wants to hear" in the abstract, and instead draw on a genuine memory, however small, that illustrates the value in practice.

Clinical Scenario Questions: Structuring a Safe, Confident Answer

Clinical scenario questions are where many otherwise strong candidates lose marks in their NHS job interview preparation, not because their clinical knowledge is inadequate, but because their answer lacks a clear, safety-first structure that a panel can follow and assess. Unlike an exam question with a single correct answer, a clinical scenario interview question is deliberately open-ended, and panels are assessing the process of reasoning at least as much as the final answer reached.

The Typical Question Format

Clinical scenario questions are usually presented as a brief hypothetical case — for example, "You are the junior doctor covering a ward overnight and a nurse calls you about a patient who has become acutely confused. Talk me through how you would approach this." — followed by the panel probing further as the answer develops, often introducing an additional complication partway through to see how the candidate adapts. The scenario is rarely designed to have a single "correct" diagnosis; instead, it reveals whether the candidate approaches an undifferentiated clinical problem safely and systematically, with appropriate awareness of their own limits.

A Safety-First Structure for Any Clinical Scenario

Regardless of the specific scenario presented, a consistent underlying structure reliably produces a strong answer, and rehearsing this structure in advance — rather than trying to guess every possible scenario a panel might ask — is a far more efficient use of preparation time.

  • Start with an immediate safety assessment. Before discussing a differential diagnosis, state that you would first ensure the patient is not in immediate danger — checking or asking about airway, breathing, circulation, and level of consciousness as appropriate to the scenario. This signals that patient safety, not diagnostic curiosity, is the first priority.
  • Gather focused information. Describe a concise, relevant history and examination approach rather than reciting an exhaustive textbook list, showing that you can prioritise the most clinically useful information first.
  • State a reasonable differential, not just one diagnosis. Offer two or three plausible explanations rather than committing immediately to a single cause, demonstrating open, safe clinical reasoning rather than premature closure.
  • Describe initial safe management. Explain the immediate steps you would take, being explicit about which interventions you are confident to perform yourself and which require senior input.
  • Escalate appropriately and explicitly. This is the single most important element panels listen for. State clearly and specifically when and to whom you would escalate — for example, "I would contact the medical registrar on call at this point, because..." — rather than leaving escalation vague or implied. Candidates who explicitly name the escalation trigger and the appropriate senior colleague consistently score higher than those who describe only what they would do themselves.
  • Communicate clearly with the team and the patient. Close by describing how you would document the encounter, hand over relevant information to colleagues, and explain the situation to the patient and, where appropriate, their family, in plain, reassuring language.

This sequence — safety first, focused assessment, reasonable differential, safe initial management, explicit and appropriately timed escalation, clear communication — works as a reusable mental template across almost any clinical scenario a panel might present, whether the case involves an acutely unwell patient, a medication error, a difficult conversation, or an ethical dilemma. Practising this structure with two or three sample scenarios beforehand, ideally aloud with a colleague or mentor providing follow-up questions, builds the fluency needed to apply it confidently to a completely unfamiliar scenario on the day.

Why Escalation Matters More Than a "Correct" Diagnosis

A recurring theme in feedback from NHS interview panels is that candidates, particularly those who trained in systems with a more hierarchical clinical culture, sometimes hesitate to mention escalation, worrying that doing so will make them look less capable. In fact, the opposite is true: NHS clinical governance places enormous emphasis on knowing the limits of one's own competence and escalating early rather than late, and panels specifically listen for a candidate who demonstrates this instinct unprompted. A junior doctor who says "I would manage this myself" when a scenario clearly warrants senior input is assessed as a greater safety risk than one who says "I would attempt X myself, but I would call my senior colleague at this point because..." Demonstrating comfort with escalation is, in effect, demonstrating clinical maturity, not a lack of confidence.

NHS Job Interview Preparation for IMGs: UK-Specific Awareness

International medical graduates face an additional layer of preparation beyond STAR and clinical scenario technique: demonstrating credible, working familiarity with UK-specific systems, guidance, and terminology, even before having worked a single shift in the NHS. Panels do not expect an IMG candidate to have memorised every NHS process in detail, but they do expect evidence of genuine effort to understand the system being joined, since this is one of the clearest available proxies for how quickly that candidate will adapt safely once in post.

Demonstrating Awareness of NICE Guidelines

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) produces the clinical guidelines that underpin most NHS treatment decisions, and referencing NICE by name during a clinical scenario answer — for example, "NICE guidance on this presentation would typically recommend..." — signals that a candidate already frames their clinical reasoning in UK-specific terms rather than in the guidelines of their country of training. IMG candidates do not need to memorise specific NICE guideline numbers, but should be comfortable naming NICE as the relevant UK guidance body for common presentations in their intended specialty, and should be prepared to say, honestly, that they would confirm exact current guidance where a detail is genuinely uncertain — a habit that itself reflects good, safe practice.

Good Medical Practice and Professional Standards

The GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance sets out the professional standards every UK-licensed doctor is expected to meet, covering domains such as knowledge, skills and performance; safety and quality; communication, partnership and teamwork; and maintaining trust. IMG candidates should be familiar with the broad structure of Good Medical Practice and should be comfortable referencing its language — duty of candour, raising concerns, professional boundaries — naturally within values-based and clinical governance answers. A candidate who can say, for instance, "this reflects the duty of candour set out in Good Medical Practice" during a patient safety scenario demonstrates a level of professional fluency that stands out against one who describes the same concept only in generic terms.

NHS Hierarchy and Terminology

NHS clinical hierarchy and terminology differ meaningfully from many other health systems, and misusing these terms at interview is a common, avoidable stumble for IMG candidates. Understanding the grade structure — Foundation Year 1 and 2, core trainee, registrar, specialty and associate specialist (SAS) doctor, and consultant — and using the correct term for the senior colleague one would escalate to (typically "the registrar on call" or "my consultant," rather than a generic "the senior doctor") makes an escalation answer sound immediately more credible.

  • Multidisciplinary team (MDT) — the expectation that clinical decisions, particularly complex ones, are made collaboratively with nursing staff, pharmacists, physiotherapists, and other allied health professionals, not unilaterally by the doctor alone.
  • SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) — the standard structured handover and escalation communication format used across the NHS, worth referencing by name in a communication-themed answer.
  • Datix — the incident-reporting system used by most trusts to formally log patient safety concerns and near-misses; referencing formal incident reporting by name, even generically, signals awareness of NHS clinical governance culture.
  • Clinical governance — the overarching framework of accountability, audit, and continuous quality improvement that underpins everyday NHS clinical decision-making.

IMG candidates preparing for their NHS job interview preparation should weave two or three of these terms naturally into clinical scenario and values-based answers, since doing so consistently and accurately does more to reassure a panel of UK-readiness than almost any other single preparation step. Candidates who have already read the site's guidance on the wider UKMLA-to-NHS job pathway and on UKMLA preparation specifically tailored for IMGs will already have encountered much of this vocabulary in context, which makes it considerably easier to recall and use naturally under interview pressure rather than needing to learn it from scratch in the days before an interview.

Researching the Specific Trust and Post

Beyond system-level UK awareness, sound NHS job interview preparation includes researching the specific trust and department — its size, patient population, any recent Care Quality Commission (CQC) inspection rating, and its stated local values — since panels routinely ask "why do you want to work here specifically?" and a generic answer that could apply to any trust is easy for an experienced panel to spot. A candidate who can reference a genuine detail about the department, even something as simple as a particular subspecialty interest it is known for, demonstrates a level of preparation that a purely clinical answer cannot.

Preparing for Video and Remote NHS Interviews

Video interviews have become an increasingly common feature of NHS recruitment, particularly for overseas candidates for whom travelling to the UK purely for an in-person interview is impractical or prohibitively expensive. This makes remote-specific readiness a genuine part of NHS job interview preparation in its own right, not an afterthought bolted onto in-person advice. While the underlying content of a remote interview — values-based, competency-based, and clinical scenario questions, assessed against the same STAR and safety-first structures described above — does not change, several additional practical considerations specifically affect remote and video interview performance.

Technical Preparation

  • Test the platform in advance. Confirm which video platform the trust will use, install and test it several days beforehand rather than minutes before the interview, and check camera, microphone, and internet connection stability under realistic conditions.
  • Have a backup plan for connectivity issues. Know in advance how to quickly switch to a mobile hotspot or contact the recruitment team by phone if the connection drops during the interview, and keep that contact number readily accessible rather than buried in an old email.
  • Consider time zone carefully. Candidates interviewing from countries several hours ahead of or behind the UK should double- and triple-check the interview time in their own local time zone well in advance, since a time zone error is one of the most common and entirely avoidable causes of a missed remote interview.

Environment and Presentation

A quiet, well-lit space with a tidy background, camera positioned at eye level, and a stable surface for any notes all contribute to a professional first impression that is just as relevant on video as in person. Candidates should dress exactly as they would for an in-person interview, and should keep a printed copy of key notes, such as their prepared STAR examples, nearby but out of camera view, to glance at briefly if needed without appearing to read from a script.

Communicating Effectively on Camera

Video introduces subtle communication challenges that do not exist in an in-person interview. Slight delays in audio transmission mean candidates should pause briefly before answering, both to avoid talking over a panellist and to allow the panel to finish a question fully before responding. Looking at the camera lens itself, rather than at the panellists' faces on screen, better simulates eye contact from the panel's perspective, even though it feels initially unnatural. Speaking slightly more slowly and clearly than feels necessary in person also helps compensate for any audio compression or minor connection lag — a particularly useful habit for candidates whose first language is not English.

Following Up After a Remote Interview

Practical logistics also differ slightly after a remote interview: candidates should confirm with the recruitment coordinator how and when they will be informed of the outcome, since this is sometimes communicated by phone rather than the video platform itself, and should ensure their contact details are correct and monitored in the days immediately following the interview. Given that many IMGs are still overseas at this stage, being reachable promptly can meaningfully affect how quickly the subsequent steps — covered in detail in the guide to the UKMLA-to-NHS job pathway, including visa sponsorship and pre-employment checks — can begin.

Building a Complete NHS Job Interview Preparation Plan

Bringing all of the above together, a structured NHS job interview preparation plan in the one to two weeks before an interview should cover research, rehearsal, and logistics in roughly equal measure, rather than concentrating entirely on clinical revision at the expense of the other two.

One to Two Weeks Before

  • Research the specific trust: its size, patient population, recent CQC rating, and published local values.
  • Re-read the job description and person specification closely, noting every competency and value explicitly mentioned, since panels often draw questions directly from this document.
  • Draft five to eight STAR examples covering the common competency themes — communication, teamwork, conflict, pressure, patient safety, leadership — from genuine clinical or relevant non-clinical experience.
  • Read the NHS Constitution for England in full at least once, noting the seven core values and how they might map to personal examples.

Two to Three Days Before

  • Rehearse STAR examples aloud, ideally with a colleague, mentor, or fellow candidate asking realistic follow-up questions.
  • Practise the safety-first clinical scenario structure with two or three sample cases relevant to the specific specialty applied for.
  • For IMG candidates, review key NHS-specific terminology and confirm familiarity with the broad structure of Good Medical Practice and the role of NICE guidance.
  • For remote interviews, test the video platform, camera, microphone, and internet connection under realistic conditions.

The Day Before and Day Of

  • Confirm interview time, format, and — for video interviews — time zone, well in advance rather than the morning of the interview.
  • Prepare two or three thoughtful questions to ask the panel at the end, since almost every NHS interview closes with an invitation for the candidate to ask questions, and having none prepared can undercut an otherwise strong performance.
  • Get adequate rest rather than last-minute cramming; a well-rested candidate consistently communicates more clearly than one who has stayed up rehearsing until the early hours.

Candidates who have already worked through the site's guidance on writing an NHS CV and application will find much of this interview preparation builds directly on groundwork already laid at the application stage, since the same competency themes, the same trust research, and often the very same STAR examples carry through from application form to interview. Preparation is genuinely cumulative across the whole NHS job-search process, rather than something that resets at each new stage.

Common Mistakes at NHS Interviews and How to Avoid Them

Several recurring mistakes appear consistently across NHS interview feedback, regardless of specialty or candidate background, and most are entirely avoidable with the preparation approach set out above.

  • Giving generic, textbook-sounding answers to values-based questions instead of a specific, personal example — panels consistently rate authenticity and specificity above a technically correct but impersonal definition.
  • Rushing straight to a differential diagnosis in a clinical scenario without first stating a safety assessment, which can make a candidate appear to prioritise diagnostic interest over immediate patient safety.
  • Failing to mention escalation explicitly, particularly among candidates worried that doing so signals a lack of confidence, when in fact the opposite is true — explicit, appropriately timed escalation is one of the strongest positive signals a panel listens for.
  • Describing team actions rather than individual actions in STAR answers, making it impossible for the panel to assess the candidate's own specific contribution.
  • Arriving with no prepared questions for the panel at the end of the interview, which can read as a lack of genuine interest in the specific post.
  • For IMGs specifically, avoiding UK-specific terminology entirely out of uncertainty about correct usage, rather than using it naturally and correcting course if a term is used slightly imprecisely — panels generally respond far better to confident, approximately correct use of terms like NICE, MDT, or clinical governance than to a candidate who avoids UK-specific vocabulary altogether.
  • Under-preparing for remote interview logistics, including time zone errors and untested video platforms, which can undermine an otherwise well-prepared candidate before the substantive interview even begins.

Avoiding these mistakes will not, on its own, guarantee a job offer, since panels also compare candidates against one another and against the specific requirements of the post. However, systematically eliminating these avoidable errors reliably improves interview performance more than any last-minute clinical revision, since most candidates who reach interview stage already possess adequate clinical knowledge — what separates strong performances from weak ones is overwhelmingly a matter of structure, preparation, and communication.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the STAR technique in an NHS job interview?

The STAR technique — Situation, Task, Action, Result — is a structured method for answering competency-based interview questions by briefly setting the scene, stating the specific responsibility involved, describing in detail what the candidate personally did, and closing with a concrete outcome. NHS panels expect competency questions such as "tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult situation" to be answered in this structure, since it allows a clear, comparable assessment of a candidate's individual actions rather than a vague general description.

What questions are typically asked in an NHS clinical interview?

NHS clinical interviews typically combine three question types: values-based questions assessing alignment with the NHS Constitution and trust values, competency-based behavioural questions best answered using STAR, and clinical scenario questions testing safe clinical reasoning and appropriate escalation. Most interviews also include a short section on practical logistics, such as availability to start and, for international candidates, visa status.

How should I answer a clinical scenario question at an NHS interview?

A strong clinical scenario answer starts with an immediate safety assessment, gathers focused relevant information, offers a reasonable differential rather than a single premature diagnosis, describes safe initial management, escalates explicitly to the appropriate senior colleague at the right point, and closes with clear communication to the team and patient. Panels are assessing the safety and structure of the reasoning process at least as much as the final clinical answer reached.

What NHS values are interview panels looking for?

Interview panels commonly assess alignment with the seven values set out in the NHS Constitution for England — working together for patients, respect and dignity, commitment to quality of care, compassion, improving lives, everyone counts, and a fair and consistent approach for all — as well as the individual trust's own local values, which are usually published on the trust's website and worth researching before any interview.

How can IMGs prepare for NHS interviews before arriving in the UK?

IMGs can prepare by reading the NHS Constitution and the GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance, familiarising themselves with UK-specific terminology such as MDT, SBAR, and clinical governance, and practising referencing NICE guidance naturally within clinical scenario answers. Demonstrating this working awareness even before having worked a single UK shift signals genuine preparation and materially improves an IMG candidate's performance relative to one who relies on clinical knowledge alone.

Are NHS job interviews conducted online or in person?

Both formats are common, and video interviews have become increasingly frequent, particularly for overseas candidates for whom in-person attendance is impractical. The underlying content and structure of the interview do not change for a remote format, but candidates should additionally test their video platform, camera, and internet connection in advance, confirm the interview time in their own local time zone, and prepare a quiet, well-lit, professional environment.

What should I ask the panel at the end of an NHS interview?

Most NHS interviews close with an invitation for the candidate to ask questions, and arriving with two or three genuine, well-researched questions — for example, about the department's induction process, typical caseload, or opportunities for professional development — demonstrates real interest in the specific post. Candidates should avoid asking questions whose answers are already published on the trust's website or in the job description, since this can undercut the impression of thorough preparation built up over the rest of the interview.

How long does an NHS clinical interview usually last?

Most NHS clinical interviews last between twenty and forty-five minutes, depending on the seniority of the post, and are conducted by a panel of two to four people typically including a consultant or senior clinician and an HR or medical staffing representative. Specialty training interviews, which often add a portfolio or evidence-based discussion component, tend to run towards the longer end of this range.

Conclusion

Thorough NHS job interview preparation is what converts an interview invitation into an actual job offer, and it rewards structured preparation far more reliably than raw clinical knowledge or natural confidence alone. Understanding the panel's likely composition and the three core question types, rehearsing genuine STAR examples for common competency themes, practising a consistent safety-first structure for clinical scenarios, and — for IMGs specifically — demonstrating credible familiarity with UK-specific values, guidance, and terminology before ever setting foot on a UK ward together account for the overwhelming majority of the difference between a strong and a weak interview performance.
