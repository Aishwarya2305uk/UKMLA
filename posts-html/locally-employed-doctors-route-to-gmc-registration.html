Locally employed doctors GMC registration status is often assumed to be a fixed destination rather than a starting point — many doctors take up a Trust grade post, settle into NHS life, and only later start asking how, or whether, they can ever move beyond it. In fact, a Locally Employed Doctor (LED) post is one of the most common ways an international medical graduate (IMG) begins their NHS career, and it is very rarely the end of the story. This guide is written specifically for doctors who have already cleared the earlier hurdles — GMC registration is done, a licence to practise is in hand, and a substantive or fixed-term NHS job is already underway outside the formal, nationally numbered training programme. The question this article answers is the one that comes next: how does a doctor already working as an LED progress from where they are now to full registration on the Specialist Register or the GP Register?

This is a deliberately later-career question than the one covered in the site's companion guide on whether experienced specialists and GPs need to sit the UKMLA at all. That article is about the entry gate — whether a senior doctor's existing qualifications exempt them from the exam before they are registered. This article assumes registration is already settled and looks at what happens years into an NHS career, once a doctor is working clinically but sitting outside a numbered training programme, and wants to know the realistic route to the same specialist or GP recognition that a doctor who went through formal UK training would eventually reach. The two questions are related but distinct, and confusing them leads a lot of doctors to look for the wrong information at the wrong stage of their career.

Because the process described here — long known as the Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) and its GP equivalent, the Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration (CEGPR), and now branded by the GMC as the Portfolio Pathway — is evidence-heavy, individually assessed, and subject to periodic review by the GMC and the relevant medical royal colleges, this guide deliberately avoids inventing a fixed checklist of exactly what every applicant must submit. Instead, it explains clearly what an LED post actually is, why so many IMGs begin their NHS careers this way, what the Portfolio Pathway (CESR/CEGPR) is for, broadly how the process differs from progressing through a numbered training programme, and where to find the GMC's own current, authoritative guidance before starting to build a portfolio.

Quick Answer: How Do Locally Employed Doctors Reach Full Registration?

Locally employed doctors GMC registration progression typically runs through what the GMC now calls the Portfolio Pathway — the current name for the route previously known as the Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) and, for GPs, the Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration (CEGPR). Both certificate names are still in active, correct use, and the GMC itself describes the pathway as "Portfolio pathway (previously CESR and CEGPR)" on its own site. It is a GMC process, run with input from the relevant medical royal college, that lets an experienced doctor demonstrate they already meet the same requirements as someone who completed a UK specialty or GP training programme. Rather than re-entering formal training from the start, a doctor applies directly to the GMC with a portfolio of evidence drawn from their training, qualifications, and clinical experience, including experience gained outside the UK and within Trust grade or clinical fellow posts.

This is a genuinely different route from the standard, numbered training programme (the CT1/ST1-and-onward pathway most doctors picture when they think of "specialty training"), and it is important to be realistic about what that difference means in practice. A CESR or CEGPR application is self-directed rather than programme-led: there is no training programme director tracking progress, no automatic annual review confirming a trainee is on schedule, and no fixed curriculum timetable dictating what to do next. The doctor themselves, usually alongside informal mentorship from consultant colleagues and sometimes a structured support scheme run by their Trust or royal college, has to identify gaps against the relevant curriculum, gather the right evidence, and judge when a portfolio is genuinely strong enough to submit. It is also, in most cases, a longer and more demanding undertaking than many doctors expect going in, precisely because assembling credible evidence against an entire specialty curriculum takes considerably more time than most people budget for.

None of this means the LED route is a dead end — far from it. Thousands of doctors currently on the GMC's Specialist Register and GP Register reached it via CESR or CEGPR rather than a numbered training programme, and the route is a long-established, entirely legitimate part of UK medical regulation, not an informal workaround. What it does mean is that a doctor working as an LED should treat the transition to full registration as a distinct project requiring its own planning, evidence-gathering discipline, and realistic timeline — not something that happens automatically the longer they stay in post. The sections below explain what an LED role actually involves, why so many doctors — especially IMGs — start their NHS career this way, what CESR and CEGPR require in broad terms, and how to start building a credible portfolio.

What Is a Locally Employed Doctor (LED) Post?

A Locally Employed Doctor is a doctor working in a substantive or fixed-term NHS post that sits outside the formal, nationally numbered specialty or GP training programme. LED posts are also commonly called Trust grade posts, clinical fellow posts, or, collectively with a wider group of experienced non-training-grade doctors, Staff, Associate Specialist and Specialty (SAS) doctor or SAS-track posts, depending on seniority and how a particular Trust structures its workforce. Whatever the exact job title, the defining feature is the same: the doctor is directly employed and managed by an individual NHS Trust for a specific post, rather than holding a National Training Number allocated through a centrally coordinated recruitment round such as Oriel.

How an LED Post Differs From a Formal Training Programme Post

The practical difference between an LED post and a formal training programme post is structural, not just a matter of terminology. A doctor in a numbered training programme post — for example, an ST3 general surgery trainee or a GPST2 GP trainee — holds a National Training Number, follows a curriculum set by the relevant royal college, is tracked through the Annual Review of Competence Progression (ARCP) process, rotates through a pre-arranged sequence of placements designed to build competence systematically, and is guaranteed, provided each ARCP outcome is satisfactory, progression towards a Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT) at the end.

An LED post typically has none of these built-in structures. The doctor is recruited by a single department for a single post, usually without a guaranteed rotation into other departments afterwards, without a curriculum-mapped assessment framework attached to the job itself, and without an ARCP-style external check confirming that a year in post has actually moved them closer to specialist standard. This is not a criticism of LED posts — many offer excellent clinical exposure, valuable responsibility, and genuine autonomy — but it does mean that progression towards full registration has to be actively organised by the doctor themselves rather than delivered automatically by the structure of the job. A doctor could, in principle, spend several clinically busy and genuinely useful years in a sequence of LED posts without accumulating the kind of curriculum-mapped, well-evidenced portfolio that a CESR or CEGPR application requires, simply because nobody was tracking that requirement on their behalf.

Common Titles: Trust Grade, Clinical Fellow, SAS Doctor

Terminology in this area is genuinely inconsistent between Trusts, which adds to the confusion doctors in these roles often feel about their own status — a doctor can be an LED for years without that status changing which register they sit on. "Trust grade doctor" is a long-standing generic term for any doctor employed directly by a Trust outside the national training numbers system. "Clinical fellow" is frequently used for posts that combine clinical duties with a specific additional focus — research, medical education, quality improvement, or a particular sub-specialty interest — and is common in teaching hospitals recruiting experienced IMGs into a defined, often fixed-term, role. "SAS doctor" (Staff, Associate Specialist and Specialty doctor) is the more formal workforce category for experienced, non-training-grade doctors who have chosen, or ended up in, a career pathway outside formal specialty training, and who may hold either a substantive (permanent) or fixed-term contract.

What all three share is the point that matters most for the purposes of this guide: none of them, by themselves, confer specialist or GP registration, and none of them come with an automatic route onto the Specialist Register or GP Register simply by virtue of years served. Time spent in any of these roles is valuable — often extremely valuable — as evidence within a future CESR or CEGPR application, but it has to be actively converted into that evidence rather than assumed to count on its own. NHS Employers, which represents Trusts on workforce matters, uses "LED" as a deliberately broad catch-all for any doctor employed on a locally agreed rather than nationally negotiated contract, distinguishing this group from Staff, Associate Specialist and Specialty (SAS) doctors, who hold nationally agreed terms despite also sitting outside formal training — a distinction worth knowing if a doctor is ever unsure which category their own contract actually falls into.

Why So Many IMGs Start Their NHS Career as Locally Employed Doctors

It is worth being direct about why the LED route is so common among IMGs specifically, rather than treating it as an unusual or second-choice option. For a substantial proportion of doctors who complete GMC registration after passing the UKMLA — including those who cleared registration via one of the recognised routes to satisfying the English language requirement without sitting a separate test — an LED post is the fastest and most realistic way into paid NHS clinical work, and the natural next stage covered in the site's guide to finding an NHS job after passing the UKMLA, for several structural reasons that have little to do with clinical ability.

Faster Entry Than Competing for a Numbered Training Post

Numbered training programme posts are recruited through centrally coordinated, competitive national rounds — Oriel for most specialties, with separate processes for GP training — that run on fixed annual or twice-yearly cycles, have defined application windows, and are genuinely competitive, particularly at the more popular specialties and more sought-after regions. An IMG who has only just secured GMC registration — a process that itself has its own timeline, covered in the site's separate guide to what determines how quickly a registration application is processed — and who may also be managing visa timing pressures, often cannot afford to wait for the next national recruitment cycle to open, be shortlisted, interview, and then wait again for a post to actually start. LED posts, by contrast, are advertised and recruited by individual Trusts on a rolling basis throughout the year, closer to the pace of any other NHS vacancy, which makes them a far faster route into a first UK clinical post and, often, into visa sponsorship.

Recognising Experience That Does Not Map Neatly Onto UK Training Levels

Many IMGs arrive in the UK with years of postgraduate clinical experience gained overseas — sometimes at a genuinely senior level — that does not map cleanly onto the UK's numbered training structure. The foundation year vs specialty training decision covered in more detail elsewhere on this site illustrates this well: an IMG with substantial prior specialist experience is often not well served by re-entering a two-year Foundation Programme designed for brand-new graduates, but may also not yet have the specific, recognised evidence a Royal College needs to grant direct entry to CT1/ST1-level training. An LED post at an appropriate seniority level — often explicitly recruited to reflect a doctor's prior specialist background — lets that experience continue to be used productively in the NHS while a longer-term registration route, whether that is eventually a numbered training application or CESR/CEGPR, is worked out.

A Practical Stepping Stone While Building UK-Specific Evidence

Regardless of how experienced a doctor already is, UK medical regulation places real weight on UK-specific evidence: UK clinical governance structures, UK-specific consultation and documentation conventions, references from consultants who have directly observed a doctor's UK practice, and, ultimately, either curriculum-mapped training-programme evidence or an equivalent CESR/CEGPR-standard portfolio. An LED post is frequently the practical way a doctor accumulates exactly this kind of UK-specific evidence — supervised UK clinical experience, UK consultant references, and exposure to NHS systems — while deciding whether their longer-term route to full registration should run through a numbered training application, direct CESR/CEGPR application, or some combination of both over time.

It is worth adding a note of realism here, in keeping with this site's general approach to hedging wherever individual circumstances vary: not every doctor who starts as an LED intends to, or needs to, pursue CESR or CEGPR. Some LEDs successfully apply for numbered training posts once they have built a stronger UK-specific portfolio and application; others stay in SAS or LED roles by genuine preference, valuing the flexibility and work-life balance those roles can offer over the structured but demanding progression of formal training or a CESR/CEGPR portfolio project. This guide is written for the specific group who do want to progress towards full specialist or GP registration without re-entering formal training from scratch, since that is the route with the most genuine confusion and the least accessible plain-English explanation.

CESR and CEGPR Explained: The Route to Full Registration Without Restarting Training

The Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration is the certificate awarded through the GMC's mechanism for granting entry to the Specialist Register to a doctor who has not completed a UK-approved specialty training programme, but who can demonstrate that their training, qualifications, and experience meet the requirements a doctor who has completed such a programme would also meet. The GP equivalent, the Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration, works on the same underlying principle for entry to the GP Register. Since November 2023 the GMC has marketed the application route leading to both certificates under a single, simplified name — the Portfolio Pathway — though CESR and CEGPR remain the correct names for the certificates themselves and are still widely used, including by the GMC's own royal college partners. Both are formally GMC processes, but the GMC works with the relevant medical royal college for each specialty, since the college holds the detailed curriculum and clinical expertise needed to judge whether a given portfolio genuinely demonstrates the required standard.

What CESR and CEGPR Are Actually Assessing

CESR and CEGPR are not a shortcut or a lesser alternative to specialty training — they are an equivalence assessment. The GMC and the relevant royal college are asking a single underlying question: does this doctor's combined training, qualifications, and clinical experience, wherever in the world it was gained, demonstrate that they have already reached the same standard expected of a doctor who completed the full UK specialty or GP training programme and its curriculum? Because the assessment is against the same curriculum standard used for programme-based trainees, rather than a separate or reduced standard, a successful CESR or CEGPR award carries the same Specialist Register or GP Register entry, and the same standing, as one gained through formal training.

This equivalence principle is also why the evidence a CESR or CEGPR application needs is inherently broader in source than a training-programme application. Experience gained outside the UK — years spent in specialist or GP practice in another country, postgraduate qualifications from another jurisdiction, structured fellowships, published research, audit and quality improvement work — can all form legitimate parts of the evidence base, alongside UK-based experience such as time in LED, Trust grade, clinical fellow, or SAS posts. The GMC does not require every year of relevant experience to have been gained in the UK; it requires that the combined evidence, wherever gained, credibly demonstrates the same standard the curriculum expects.

How CESR/CEGPR Differs From Progressing Through Formal Training

The practical differences between a CESR/CEGPR application and progression through a numbered training programme are significant, and doctors moving from one mental model to the other often underestimate them. A numbered training programme is structured and externally paced: the curriculum, rotations, assessments, and ARCP timeline are set by the training programme, and a trainee's task is largely to meet each stage's requirements as they come. A CESR or CEGPR application is self-directed and self-paced: the doctor has to obtain and study the relevant curriculum themselves, work out which parts of it their existing experience already evidences well, identify genuine gaps, and often actively seek out additional clinical exposure, courses, or opportunities specifically to close those gaps, all without an external programme structure prompting each step.

This self-directed nature is precisely why CESR and CEGPR applications are widely — and accurately — described as evidence-heavy and time-consuming to prepare. Where a training-programme trainee's evidence accumulates as a natural by-product of following the curriculum, a CESR/CEGPR applicant has to retrospectively assemble, organise, and often supplement evidence that was not originally gathered with a future application in mind. Doctors who have spent years in LED or Trust grade posts sometimes discover, once they start seriously preparing a portfolio, that they have plenty of relevant clinical experience but comparatively little of it formally documented in the specific way the curriculum-mapping exercise requires — a gap that takes real time to close retrospectively, and one of the most common reasons CESR/CEGPR preparation takes considerably longer than doctors initially expect.

There is also a meaningful difference in how long the overall process realistically takes. A numbered training programme has a defined, published duration for each specialty. CESR and CEGPR timelines are far more variable, since they depend on how much of the applicant's existing experience already maps cleanly onto the curriculum, how quickly gaps can be closed, how long the portfolio itself takes to assemble and quality-check, and the GMC and college's own processing time once a completed application is submitted. Doctors researching this route should treat "how long will this take me" as a genuinely individual question rather than looking for a single fixed answer, and should build in realistic time for at least one round of addressing feedback if the college requests further evidence, which is a routine, not unusual, part of the process for many applicants.

What the GMC Says About Timescales

One piece of concrete, checkable timing detail is worth stating plainly, since it helps set realistic expectations without straying into invented specifics: the GMC's own published guidance states that once a Portfolio Pathway (CESR/CEGPR) application is opened, a doctor has 24 months to submit it, and that a submitted application then typically takes 6 to 12 months to process before it is sent to the relevant royal college or faculty for evaluation. These figures describe the application-processing stage only — they do not include the time a doctor spends beforehand mapping their experience against the curriculum and assembling their evidence, which, as covered above, is highly individual and often the longer part of the overall project in practice.

Where to Find the GMC's Authoritative Current Guidance

Because CESR/CEGPR (Portfolio Pathway) evidence requirements, specialty-specific curricula, and application procedures are reviewed and updated periodically by the GMC and the individual royal colleges, this guide deliberately does not attempt to reproduce a fixed evidence checklist that could quickly become outdated. The single most reliable source for current requirements is the GMC's own specialist application guidance, which sets out the process, the specialty-specific application guides published with each royal college, and the current portfolio requirements in detail. Doctors seriously considering this route should treat the GMC's Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration or Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration application guidance as the starting point for their own planning, since the specialty-specific guide relevant to their own field will set out exactly which curriculum areas need to be evidenced and in what format, rather than relying on general descriptions from third-party sources, however well-intentioned.

It is also worth checking directly with the relevant medical royal college for a doctor's own specialty, since colleges often publish additional supporting guidance, sample portfolios, or structured support schemes for doctors preparing a Portfolio Pathway application — detail that goes beyond what the GMC's own pages cover and that changes college by college. Doctors who are still earlier in their journey, and have not yet secured GMC registration in the first place, should start instead with the site's guide to the full GMC registration process, since CESR and CEGPR are only relevant once standard registration is already in place.

Building a CESR/CEGPR Portfolio While Working as an LED

Although this guide deliberately avoids inventing a specific, fixed evidence checklist — that detail genuinely belongs to the GMC's own current specialist application guides and the relevant royal college's specialty-specific guidance — there is useful, honest general advice to give about how doctors in LED posts typically approach building towards a CESR or CEGPR application, without overstating certainty about details that vary by specialty and change over time.

Start Mapping Experience Against the Curriculum Early

The single most consistently repeated piece of practical advice from doctors who have been through a successful CESR or CEGPR application is to start mapping existing and ongoing experience against the relevant curriculum as early as possible, rather than waiting until a portfolio needs to be assembled all at once. An LED doctor who obtains their specialty's curriculum early in their post, and periodically checks their accumulating clinical experience, courses, audits, and other activity against it, arrives at the eventual application stage with a portfolio that has been organised as they went along, rather than one that has to be reconstructed retrospectively from years of scattered records. This single habit is widely credited with turning what would otherwise be a daunting, multi-month reconstruction project into a considerably more manageable, ongoing one.

Keep Structured Records From the Start of an LED Post

Because CESR and CEGPR portfolios rely heavily on documented evidence rather than simply asserted experience, doctors in LED posts benefit from keeping structured records from early in their employment rather than trying to reconstruct a career history later. This generally means retaining copies of job descriptions and contracts for each post, keeping a running log of procedures, clinical activity, and case mix where relevant to the specialty, requesting and filing consultant references and appraisal documentation at the time they are given rather than months or years later, and keeping evidence of any courses, qualifications, audits, quality improvement projects, teaching, or research undertaken alongside clinical duties. None of this needs to be curriculum-perfect from day one — the exercise of periodically checking it against the curriculum, described above, is what turns a general career record into CESR/CEGPR-ready evidence.

Seek Structured Support Rather Than Navigating Alone

Because CESR and CEGPR applications are self-directed rather than programme-led, doctors preparing one benefit disproportionately from actively seeking structured support rather than assuming they must navigate the process entirely alone. This can include mentorship from consultant colleagues who have supervised the doctor's work directly and can speak credibly to specific curriculum areas, formal or informal support schemes run by some Trusts or royal colleges specifically for SAS and LED doctors preparing CESR/CEGPR applications, and honest, early conversations with a clinical or educational supervisor about which parts of the curriculum current clinical duties evidence well and which parts may need to be deliberately sought out through additional exposure, secondments, or courses. Doctors who treat the process as something to work through collaboratively with informed colleagues, rather than as a solitary paperwork exercise completed in isolation, generally report a smoother — if still demanding — path to a submission-ready portfolio.

Be Realistic About Time and Effort Required

It is worth stating plainly, in keeping with this site's general approach to honest expectation-setting, that a genuinely thorough CESR or CEGPR portfolio takes sustained effort over an extended period for the great majority of applicants — this is a realistic multi-year undertaking for most doctors, not a project that can be compressed into a few intense weeks alongside a full clinical workload. Doctors who underestimate this, and start seriously assembling evidence only shortly before they hope to submit, often find themselves needing far more time than expected to locate historical references, reconstruct activity logs, or obtain missing documentation from previous employers or overseas institutions. Building the habits described above — early curriculum mapping, ongoing structured record-keeping, and active use of available support — from as early as possible in an LED career is consistently the difference between a manageable, steady process and a stressful, rushed one.

Common Pitfalls for LEDs Considering the CESR/CEGPR Route

Beyond the general advice above, a handful of recurring mistakes are worth flagging explicitly, since they account for a disproportionate share of the delay and frustration doctors report when preparing a CESR or CEGPR application from an LED background.

The first is treating years of general clinical experience as automatically sufficient, without ever checking it against the actual curriculum document. Plenty of very experienced, clinically excellent LED doctors discover late in the process that their experience, while genuinely strong, has gaps against specific curriculum domains simply because their day-to-day role never happened to cover certain areas — sub-specialty exposure, particular procedures, specific leadership or teaching competencies — that the curriculum expects evidence for. Checking early, as covered above, is the direct fix for this.

The second is under-documenting experience at the time it happens, on the assumption that it can always be reconstructed later. References, logbook entries, and appraisal records are far easier to obtain contemporaneously than years afterwards, particularly if a previous employer, supervisor, or overseas institution is difficult to reach by the time an application is being prepared.

The third is attempting the entire process without seeking any structured support, whether from a mentor, supervisor, Trust-run scheme, or the relevant royal college's own guidance materials. Given how self-directed the CESR/CEGPR process is by design, doctors who try to navigate it in complete isolation tend to take considerably longer and submit weaker first applications than those who actively seek input along the way.

The fourth, more strategic pitfall is failing to plan for the possibility that a first application may come back with a request for further evidence rather than an outright award. This is a routine part of the process for a meaningful proportion of applicants across specialties, not a sign of a fundamentally flawed application, and doctors who anticipate it — by keeping their underlying evidence organised and their professional network of referees warm — tend to respond to such requests far more quickly and with far less disruption than those who treat any request for more evidence as an unexpected setback.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a Locally Employed Doctor and a doctor in formal training?

A Locally Employed Doctor (LED) is recruited and employed directly by an individual NHS Trust into a specific post, without holding a National Training Number, a curriculum-mapped rotation sequence, or an Annual Review of Competence Progression (ARCP). A doctor in formal training holds a National Training Number allocated through a national recruitment process such as Oriel, follows a royal-college curriculum, and is tracked through ARCP towards a Certificate of Completion of Training.

Can a locally employed doctor become a consultant or GP partner in the UK?

Yes. An LED can reach full Specialist Register or GP Register status either by later securing a place in a numbered training programme, or by applying directly for a Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) or Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration (CEGPR) once they can evidence that their training and experience meet the required curriculum standard. Both routes lead to exactly the same register entry and professional standing.

Is CESR the same as specialty training?

No. CESR is an equivalence assessment against the same curriculum standard used for specialty training, but the process itself is different: it is self-directed rather than programme-led, draws on evidence from any relevant clinical setting rather than only UK training-programme placements, and has no fixed, published duration in the way a numbered training programme does.

Does experience gained outside the UK count towards a CESR or CEGPR application?

Yes, in principle. CESR and CEGPR are assessed on the combined strength of a doctor's training, qualifications, and experience wherever in the world it was gained, provided it can be evidenced to a standard the relevant royal college accepts as meeting the curriculum requirement. Exactly how overseas experience should be presented and evidenced is set out in the specialty-specific guidance published alongside the GMC's specialist application guides, since expectations vary by specialty.

How long does it take to prepare a CESR or CEGPR portfolio while working as an LED?

There is no single fixed answer for the preparation stage, since it depends heavily on how much of a doctor's existing experience already maps onto the relevant curriculum, how quickly any gaps can be closed, and how much time can realistically be dedicated to portfolio preparation alongside clinical duties — most doctors who have been through the process describe it as a sustained, multi-year undertaking. Once an application is actually submitted, the GMC's own published guidance states that a doctor has 24 months from opening an application to submit it, and that processing a submitted application typically takes a further 6 to 12 months before it is sent to the relevant royal college for evaluation.

Should a locally employed doctor apply for numbered training instead of CESR/CEGPR?

It depends on the individual doctor's circumstances, and many doctors genuinely could pursue either route. A doctor earlier in their career, or whose existing experience does not yet map well onto the specialty curriculum, may find a numbered training application the more structured and ultimately faster route. A doctor with substantial, well-evidenced relevant experience, including from before moving to the UK, may find CESR or CEGPR the more direct route to recognition, since it avoids repeating training already effectively completed elsewhere.

Where can a locally employed doctor find the GMC's official guidance on CESR and CEGPR?

The GMC publishes its specialist application guidance, including specialty-specific application guides developed with each royal college, directly on its own website. Doctors preparing to apply should treat this — rather than general third-party descriptions, however accurate they aim to be — as the authoritative, current source for exactly what evidence their own specialty's application requires.

Conclusion

For a doctor already working in the NHS as a Locally Employed Doctor, locally employed doctors GMC registration progression to full specialist or GP recognition is a realistic, well-established goal, not an informal workaround or a lesser route than formal training. The Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration and its GP equivalent, the Certificate of Eligibility for GP Registration, allow an experienced doctor to demonstrate — through a self-directed, evidence-heavy portfolio built from UK and overseas training, qualifications, and clinical experience alike — that they already meet the same standard as a doctor who completed a numbered UK training programme, leading to exactly the same register entry and professional standing. The route demands early planning, disciplined record-keeping, and realistic patience with a genuinely longer and more self-managed process than formal training, but it is a proven path that thousands of doctors on the Specialist and GP Registers have already taken. Doctors earlier in their journey who have not yet secured registration in the first place, or who are still weighing up foundation year versus specialty training options, or who are affected by the closure of the Medical Training Initiative scheme, should first make sure the fundamentals are in place before turning to the CESR/CEGPR route described here.
