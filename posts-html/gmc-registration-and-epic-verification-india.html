GMC registration from India is the final formal step in becoming a licensed doctor in the United Kingdom, and it can only happen once every other requirement in the pathway has already been satisfied. Before a doctor can register with the General Medical Council and hold a licence to practise, they must have passed both components of the UKMLA, submitted acceptable evidence of English language proficiency, and completed primary source verification of their Indian medical qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials). Put simply, GMC registration is not a parallel task you can chip away at alongside exam preparation — it is the last domino, and it falls only after the others are already standing in place.

This guide goes deep into the two stages of the journey that Indian candidates consistently find most confusing and most time-consuming: EPIC verification of an Indian MBBS degree, and the registration application itself once EPIC, English evidence, and both exam passes are confirmed. It sits alongside the site's broader guide to how to become a doctor in the UK from India, which covers the full route end to end, but this article focuses specifically and procedurally on document verification and the mechanics of the GMC registration process for Indian doctors, including the India-specific complication of the 2020/2021 transition from the Medical Council of India to the National Medical Commission.

Indian doctors make up one of the largest single cohorts of international medical graduates on the UK medical register, and consequently the GMC's systems and guidance are well accustomed to Indian degree certificates, Indian transcripts, and Indian regulatory bodies. That familiarity does not, however, make the process instant. Indian medical schools vary enormously in how quickly their academic offices respond to verification requests, and the NMC transition has introduced a genuine, practical wrinkle that catches out candidates who registered under the old MCI system. This guide sets out exactly what to expect at each stage, with realistic timeframes and a clear document checklist, so that Indian applicants can plan the administrative side of their pathway with the same rigour they bring to exam preparation.

It is worth setting expectations honestly from the outset: nothing in this guide should be read as a guarantee of a specific processing time, since both EPIC verification and GMC registration processing depend on factors outside any single candidate's control. Instead, this guide focuses on giving Indian applicants a realistic planning framework — what typically happens, in what order, and what to do when something does not go to plan — so that decisions about when to book exams, when to travel, and when to request documents are made with genuine information rather than guesswork. Before beginning any of the steps below, it is also worth double-checking that you meet the underlying eligibility criteria in the first place; the site's dedicated UKMLA and GMC registration eligibility guide sets out the full requirements across qualification recognition, English language evidence, and health and character declarations that apply to every applicant regardless of country of training.

What GMC Registration With a Licence to Practise Actually Means

GMC registration with a licence to practise is the legal authorisation that allows a doctor to work as a doctor in the United Kingdom — without it, passing the UKMLA alone does not entitle anyone to practise medicine on the NHS or in private UK healthcare. Registration is granted by the General Medical Council only after a candidate has cleared four separate hurdles: eligibility confirmation, primary source verification of their medical qualification through EPIC, acceptable evidence of English language proficiency, and passes in both components of the UKMLA — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA).

This ordering matters because many candidates mistakenly treat the GMC registration process for Indian doctors as something that can be started only after the exams are passed. In reality, EPIC verification and English language testing should both be initiated well before the exams are sat, since they run on their own independent timelines and do not depend on exam results. Sequencing GMC registration from India correctly — starting the document-heavy steps early and in parallel with exam preparation, rather than treating them as an afterthought — is the single biggest factor separating candidates who reach registration within two years of starting from those who take considerably longer.

It is also worth being precise about what registration confers. Registration with a licence to practise is what allows a doctor to take up medical employment in the UK; it is distinct from, and a prerequisite to, applying for specific NHS posts. Once registration is granted, the practical next step for most Indian doctors is applying for NHS jobs, typically starting with trust-grade or clinical fellow positions while building a UK employment history. Registering with the GMC from India therefore is not the end goal in itself — it is the gateway that makes the rest of a UK medical career possible.

There are, in practice, two broad categories of GMC registration relevant to doctors coming through the UKMLA route: provisional registration and full registration with a licence to practise. Most Indian IMGs entering through the UKMLA pathway move directly to full registration with a licence to practise, since provisional registration is more commonly associated with UK medical graduates entering the Foundation Programme straight after their primary qualification. Understanding which category applies to your specific situation matters because the supporting evidence required, and the point at which you become eligible to apply, differ slightly between the two. If in doubt about which registration type applies to your circumstances, GMC Online's own eligibility checker, together with the guidance referenced throughout this article, is the most reliable way to confirm before you submit anything.

What EPIC Is and How Primary Source Verification Works

EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) is the online platform the GMC uses to carry out primary source verification of an international medical graduate's qualifications — meaning the degree, the transcripts, and often the internship record are confirmed directly with the institution that issued them, not simply accepted on the strength of a certified photocopy. For Indian doctors, this means your MBBS degree certificate and academic transcripts are verified directly with your Indian medical school, and this step is a mandatory, non-negotiable part of GMC registration from India — it cannot be skipped, expedited by paying a larger fee, or substituted with a notarised copy of your documents.

The distinction between EPIC verification and simply submitting a certified copy is the point most candidates misunderstand at the outset. A certified copy only confirms that a photocopy matches an original document you physically hold; it says nothing about whether the issuing institution itself confirms the degree was genuinely awarded. EPIC verification, by contrast, involves the GMC's verification partner contacting your medical school directly and requesting that the school confirm, from its own records, that you were awarded the qualification claimed, on the date claimed, following a programme of the length and content claimed. This is a materially higher bar, and it is precisely why it takes weeks rather than days.

Creating an EPIC Account and Submitting Your Documents

The EPIC verification process begins with creating an EPIC account, which is done through a link accessed via your GMC Online account once you have confirmed basic eligibility. Within EPIC, you register your Indian medical school, your MBBS degree details, and your graduation year, and you then submit the required supporting documents for that qualification. The platform routes a verification request to your medical school's designated EPIC liaison or academic/examination office, which is then asked to confirm the details directly with EPIC rather than with you.

Because the verification request goes to the institution rather than to you, your own responsiveness is not the limiting factor once the request has been submitted — your medical school's is. This is an important mental shift for candidates used to systems where personal effort directly speeds up an outcome. Once you have submitted your EPIC application correctly, the most productive thing you can do is monitor progress and follow up periodically with your medical school's registrar, rather than assume there is nothing more you can do until a decision arrives.

Full, authoritative detail on how the process works, exactly which documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting verification is published directly by the regulator: see the GMC's guidance on primary source verification for international medical graduates. Candidates should read this page in full before creating an EPIC account, since it is updated periodically and takes precedence over any general summary, including this one.

How EPIC Differs From Submitting a Certified Copy

It is worth restating this distinction because it is the most common point of confusion for first-time applicants. Submitting a certified copy of your degree certificate demonstrates that the document in front of the GMC matches an original you hold; it does not demonstrate that the original itself is genuine or that the underlying academic record is complete and accurate. EPIC verification, in contrast, requires your Indian medical school to independently confirm your qualification details from its own institutional records, communicated directly to EPIC's verification team rather than relayed through you. Consequently, a candidate cannot shortcut the process by presenting an especially well-notarised or apostilled certificate — the verification always has to originate from the institution itself, regardless of how the paperwork is presented.

This distinction exists precisely because credential fraud is a genuine, historically documented problem across international medical migration generally, and primary source verification is the tool regulators worldwide use to guard against it. For Indian candidates specifically, this means that even a completely genuine degree from a well-regarded medical school still has to go through the identical verification pathway as any other applicant — there is no fast-track, no reputational shortcut, and no way to have EPIC verification waived on the basis of institutional prestige alone. Every applicant, regardless of which of India's medical schools they attended, is verified against the same standard.

What EPIC Verification Costs and Who Pays

EPIC verification carries its own fee, separate from GMC registration fees, exam fees, and English testing costs, and this fee is generally payable by the candidate at the point of submitting the EPIC application. Because EPIC fees, like all charges across the pathway, are subject to periodic revision, candidates should confirm the current amount directly on the EPIC platform or through GMC Online rather than budgeting from a figure that may already be out of date by the time they apply. Building this cost into your overall pathway budget from the outset, alongside AKT and CPSA fees and the eventual registration fee, avoids an unwelcome surprise partway through the process.

The MCI-to-NMC Transition and What It Means for Indian Applicants

India's medical regulatory landscape changed substantially in 2020 and 2021, when the Medical Council of India (MCI) was dissolved and replaced by the National Medical Commission (NMC). This transition has direct, practical consequences for Indian doctors pursuing GMC registration, because the correct body to approach for a Certificate of Good Standing or equivalent registration confirmation now depends on when and how you were originally registered.

In broad terms, doctors whose registration and disciplinary history predate the transition may need documentation that reflects both their historical MCI registration and their current status under the NMC or the relevant State Medical Council, whereas doctors who registered more recently will generally deal exclusively with NMC-era processes and records. However, exactly how each individual case is handled by the GMC — and precisely which certificates or confirmations are accepted as sufficient evidence of good standing — can depend on details specific to your registration history, your State Medical Council, and the current state of record migration from MCI to NMC systems.

This is an area where the site cannot responsibly generalise with full confidence, because the transition involved considerable administrative reorganisation, and the practical experience of individual Indian doctors requesting registration confirmation has varied. Candidates should verify their own specific requirement directly with the GMC and with the NMC or their State Medical Council before assuming a particular document will be accepted, rather than relying on a general guide such as this one for a case-specific answer. The safest approach is to request confirmation of exactly which certificate the GMC expects for your particular registration history as early as possible in your EPIC application, since resolving any ambiguity here late in the process is one of the more common causes of delay for Indian candidates specifically.

How Indian Medical Schools Typically Respond to EPIC Requests

Response times from Indian medical schools to EPIC verification requests vary widely, and this variation is one of the most significant sources of unpredictability in the entire GMC registration process for Indian doctors. Well-resourced institutions with a dedicated international affairs or examinations office — particularly those that regularly handle similar verification requests from other countries' regulators — often respond within four to ten weeks. Other institutions, especially where verification requests are handled informally or by administrative staff without a dedicated process for this kind of request, can take three to six months, and in a minority of cases considerably longer.

These figures are indicative rather than guaranteed, and they should be treated as planning ranges rather than promises. Response speed depends on factors outside any individual candidate's control: whether the request arrives during an academic term or examination period, whether the school's records for your specific graduating cohort are digitised or still held on paper, and whether the person responsible for EPIC correspondence is a permanent staff member or someone administering the task alongside other duties. Given this variability, initiating EPIC verification as early as possible — ideally well before AKT preparation begins in earnest — is the most reliable way to prevent it from becoming the bottleneck that determines your overall timeline.

Anecdotally, candidates who graduated more recently from institutions with digitised records tend to experience faster turnaround than those relying on paper archives from cohorts several years or decades in the past, since a digitised record can be located and confirmed far more quickly than one requiring a physical file to be retrieved from storage. This is not a hard rule, and older graduates from well-organised institutions can still experience swift verification; nevertheless, it is a pattern worth being aware of when estimating your own likely timeline. If your medical school has not responded within what feels like a reasonable interval, a polite, specific follow-up email — referencing your EPIC application number and the date of your original request — is generally more effective than a vague inquiry, since it gives the academic office a concrete reference point to act on rather than requiring them to search for your file from scratch.

Step-by-Step: The GMC Registration Application Process

Once EPIC verification, English language evidence, and passes in both UKMLA components are all confirmed, the registration application itself follows a defined sequence. Below is that sequence in the order candidates should typically action it.

  1. Create or return to your GMC Online account. This account, established at the very start of the pathway, is where the registration application itself is ultimately submitted, alongside EPIC tracking, exam bookings, and English language evidence uploads.
  2. Confirm all supporting evidence is complete. Before submitting, check that EPIC verification shows as complete, your English test certificate is valid (not expired) and correctly uploaded, and both AKT and CPSA results are recorded against your account.
  3. Submit the registration application itself, including any outstanding declarations regarding health, criminal record, and professional conduct — these are standard requirements for every applicant, Indian or otherwise, and should be answered accurately and completely rather than left blank or minimised.
  4. Complete identity verification. The GMC requires robust proof of identity as part of registration, which for many Indian candidates is completed in conjunction with a UK visit (often the same trip used for the CPSA) or through an approved identity-verification route specified by the GMC for applicants still overseas.
  5. Pay the registration fee and submit the completed application for processing.
  6. Await the outcome. Processing times vary, but candidates whose supporting documents are complete and unambiguous typically experience considerably faster processing than those whose files require the GMC to raise a query — covered in detail below.

Processing times for a complete, query-free application are generally measured in a small number of weeks; however, this should be treated as an indicative range rather than a guarantee, since the GMC's own processing capacity and the complexity of an individual file both affect the actual outcome. Always check current expected processing times directly on the GMC's own website rather than relying on a fixed number quoted in any third-party guide, since these timeframes are reviewed and adjusted periodically by the regulator. The GMC's official resources and guidance pages are the authoritative reference point for current fees, processing benchmarks, and required forms, and should be checked directly before every major step in the pathway rather than relied upon only at the start.

Registration fees are payable online through GMC Online at the point of application submission, and the GMC accepts standard international payment methods for overseas applicants, including Indian candidates paying from an Indian bank account or card. As with every fee mentioned in this guide, the exact amount is reviewed periodically by the regulator, so candidates should confirm the current registration fee directly on the GMC's website immediately before paying, rather than budgeting from a fee quoted at an earlier point in their planning.

Identity Verification in Practice

Identity verification deserves particular attention because it is a step some Indian candidates plan for too late. Given that many Indian applicants are still resident in India at the point their EPIC verification and exam results come through, coordinating identity verification — which may require an in-person element — alongside a planned UK trip for the CPSA is usually more efficient than treating it as a separate, later journey. Confirming exactly which identity verification route applies to your circumstances, directly through your GMC Online account guidance, well before you finalise UK travel plans avoids an unnecessary second trip.

Some Indian candidates ask whether identity verification can be completed entirely remotely, without any UK travel at all. In some circumstances an approved remote route is available; however, given that most Indian candidates already need to travel to the UK for the CPSA, combining both requirements into a single trip is usually the more efficient choice even where a remote option exists, since it avoids managing two separate verification processes on two separate timelines.

Common Delays Specific to Indian Candidates and How to Avoid Them

Several recurring issues account for a disproportionate share of the delays experienced specifically by Indian applicants during GMC registration. Recognising these in advance allows candidates to build in the kind of buffer time that prevents a minor administrative hiccup from becoming a multi-month setback.

  • Confusion between State Medical Council and NMC documentation. Because registration and disciplinary records for Indian doctors can sit with a State Medical Council, the NMC, or a combination of both depending on registration history, candidates sometimes request the wrong certificate from the wrong body, only discovering the mismatch when the GMC queries the submission. Confirming the exact required document and issuing authority before requesting anything avoids this entirely.
  • Incomplete academic transcripts. Some Indian medical schools issue transcripts that summarise overall results rather than providing the year-by-year, subject-by-subject detail the GMC's verification process expects. Requesting the most detailed transcript format your institution can issue, rather than the standard summary transcript typically given to graduates, reduces the likelihood of a follow-up request.
  • Slow academic office response to EPIC requests. As covered above, this is highly institution-dependent. Keeping a written record of every communication with your medical school's registrar or examinations office is valuable both for your own tracking and as evidence should you need to escalate an unusually slow response.
  • Translation certification that does not meet the GMC's standard. A translation completed informally by a bilingual friend or relative, however accurate, typically does not satisfy the GMC's certification requirements. Use a recognised professional translation service and confirm in advance that its certification format is one the GMC accepts.
  • Name mismatches across documents. Indian degree certificates, passports, and NMC/State Medical Council records are sometimes issued at different points in time with slightly different name renderings — for example, a middle name present on one document and abbreviated or omitted on another. Resolving this with formal evidence before submission, rather than waiting for it to be flagged, avoids an otherwise easily preventable query.
  • Sitting the English test too early or too late relative to the rest of the pathway. The English language requirement that runs alongside EPIC verification has its own certificate validity window, and timing it so the certificate remains valid through to your registration application submission requires deliberate sequencing rather than sitting the test at the first convenient opportunity.

If Your Application Is Queried by the GMC

A query from the GMC is not automatically a sign that something has gone seriously wrong — it is a routine part of the process for a meaningful proportion of applicants, and it typically means one specific piece of evidence needs clarifying or replacing rather than the whole application being in doubt. Common reasons a query is raised include an ambiguous or incomplete transcript, a name discrepancy across documents that has not been formally explained, uncertainty over which Indian regulatory body's certificate applies to your registration history given the MCI-to-NMC transition, or a translation that does not meet the required certification standard.

When a query is raised, the GMC will specify exactly what additional information or document is required, and the most effective response is a prompt, complete, and precisely targeted reply — providing exactly what has been asked for, in the format requested, rather than resubmitting a broader set of documents in the hope that one of them resolves the issue. If the query concerns which Indian body should confirm your registration status, it is worth contacting the GMC directly for clarification on the specific evidence they will accept for your circumstances, rather than guessing and risking a second round of queries. Keeping copies of every document submitted, along with dates and reference numbers for each communication, makes responding to a query considerably faster than having to reconstruct your submission history from scratch.

Most queries, once answered completely, do not significantly extend the overall timeline; however, an incomplete or vague response can trigger a second query, which does meaningfully add to processing time. Treating the first query response as the one opportunity to resolve the issue cleanly is therefore worth the extra care it takes to get right.

It is also worth understanding, in general terms, why queries happen more often for some applicants than others. Applications built from a complete, internally consistent document set — where names match across every document, transcripts are detailed rather than summarised, and the correct current Indian regulatory body has been approached for registration confirmation — are considerably less likely to generate a query in the first place. Consequently, the time invested in assembling a clean, complete file before submission is rarely wasted; it is usually recovered many times over by avoiding a query-and-response cycle that can otherwise add weeks to the overall timeline.

Realistic Timeline Planning for GMC Registration From India

Bringing every stage together, Indian candidates should plan for a realistic overall timeline rather than an optimistic best case. EPIC verification alone can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months, English language testing and its associated preparation typically adds further time, and the AKT and CPSA each carry their own preparation and booking lead times on top of the underlying administrative track. Consequently, most Indian candidates who plan carefully and avoid major setbacks should expect somewhere in the region of eighteen months to three years from beginning EPIC verification to holding full GMC registration, though individual circumstances — particularly EPIC response speed and exam performance on the first attempt — can move this substantially in either direction.

This range is deliberately wide, and it should be read as a planning tool rather than a precise prediction, since no third-party guide can account for the specific administrative speed of your particular medical school or the current processing capacity at the GMC at the time you apply. What a realistic range does allow, however, is better decision-making around finances, employment, and family planning during the pathway — candidates who assume registration will take six months are far more likely to experience financial and psychological strain than those who plan from the outset around a multi-year horizon and treat any earlier completion as a welcome bonus rather than an expectation.

Running the administrative track and the exam preparation track genuinely in parallel, rather than sequentially, is the single most effective lever available to compress this overall timeline. In practice, this means starting EPIC verification and English language testing during the same months you begin AKT preparation, rather than waiting until you feel "ready" for the exams before addressing the paperwork. Because EPIC verification in particular depends on external response times rather than personal effort, there is no efficiency gained by delaying it — every week spent waiting to start is simply a week added to your total timeline that could otherwise have been running invisibly in the background while you studied.

The underlying principle behind all of this verification activity is a straightforward one: the GMC needs to be confident that an Indian MBBS qualification is genuinely valid and equivalent to the standard expected of a UK medical graduate before granting registration, and EPIC together with the wider document checklist is simply the mechanism by which that confidence is established. Understanding this underlying purpose makes the individual steps easier to follow, since each document requested serves a specific piece of that overall confirmation rather than being arbitrary bureaucracy.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does EPIC verification take for an Indian medical degree?

Response times vary considerably by medical school, with well-resourced institutions often responding within four to ten weeks and others taking three to six months or longer. Because this variability is driven by your institution's administrative speed rather than your own effort, starting EPIC verification as early as possible in your overall pathway is the most effective way to prevent it from delaying your registration.

Can I use a certified copy of my MBBS certificate instead of EPIC verification?

No. A certified copy only confirms that a photocopy matches an original document, whereas EPIC verification requires your Indian medical school to independently confirm your qualification details from its own institutional records. EPIC verification is a mandatory step in GMC registration from India and cannot be substituted with a certified or notarised copy, however official that copy appears.

Do I need documentation from the Medical Council of India or the National Medical Commission?

This depends on your specific registration history, since the MCI was replaced by the NMC in 2020/2021 and the correct current body for a Certificate of Good Standing or equivalent confirmation can vary accordingly. Because individual cases differ, candidates should confirm directly with the GMC and the NMC or their State Medical Council exactly which certificate is required for their circumstances rather than assuming a single answer applies to every applicant.

What happens if the GMC queries my registration application?

A query typically asks for one specific piece of clarification, such as an incomplete transcript, a name discrepancy, or ambiguity over which Indian body should confirm your registration status — it does not usually mean the whole application is in jeopardy. Respond promptly and precisely with exactly what has been requested, since an incomplete first response risks triggering a second query and further delay.

Can I start the GMC registration application before passing both UKMLA components?

No. Registration with a licence to practise can only be granted once both the AKT and CPSA have been passed, alongside completed EPIC verification and valid English language evidence. However, EPIC verification and English testing should both be started well before the exams are sat, since they run on independent timelines and are frequently the true bottleneck in the overall pathway.

How do I prove my Indian medical school qualification is genuine if the school is slow to respond?

Persistent, documented follow-up with your medical school's registrar or examinations office is the main lever available, since EPIC verification must originate from the institution itself and cannot be substituted by any document you hold personally. Keeping a written record of every request and response also gives you a clear escalation trail if the delay becomes extreme, and in some cases contacting the GMC for guidance on next steps is worthwhile if a school has been unresponsive for an extended period.

Does GMC registration from India let me start working in the NHS immediately?

Registration with a licence to practise is the legal authorisation to work as a doctor in the UK, but it is a prerequisite to, not the same as, having a job. Once registered, the practical next step is following the pathway from UKMLA success to an NHS job, which typically starts with applying for trust-grade or clinical fellow posts.
