Menopause UKMLA: The Quick Answer

Menopause is defined retrospectively as 12 consecutive months of amenorrhoea in a woman not using hormonal contraception, most commonly occurring around age 51 in the UK. The single most important fact to memorise is this: in a woman aged 45 or over presenting with typical menopausal symptoms, the diagnosis is made clinically, on symptoms alone — NICE explicitly advises against routine hormone testing (follicle-stimulating hormone, FSH) in this age group, because levels fluctuate too much during the transition to be clinically useful.

Perimenopause refers to the period of hormonal fluctuation and irregular cycles that precedes the final period, typically lasting four to eight years and characterised by vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats), psychological symptoms (low mood, anxiety, irritability, brain fog), and genitourinary symptoms (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency). Management centres on shared decision-making between HRT and non-hormonal options, with premature ovarian insufficiency (menopause before age 40) treated as a genuinely distinct diagnosis requiring different investigation and different long-term risk counselling. The Perimenopause-to-Menopause Continuum

Understanding menopause as a continuum, rather than a single event, is essential groundwork for the rest of this guide. The ovaries do not simply switch off overnight; instead, the number and quality of remaining ovarian follicles declines progressively with age, leading to increasingly erratic ovulation and fluctuating oestrogen and progesterone levels, eventually ending in the cessation of ovulation altogether. This biological reality is exactly why a single hormone measurement is such an unreliable diagnostic tool during the transition — a woman's FSH and oestradiol can vary substantially from one menstrual cycle to the next, and even within the same cycle.

Perimenopause: Recognising the Transition

Perimenopause is the phase of hormonal fluctuation that precedes the final menstrual period, and it is frequently where symptoms first become troublesome enough to prompt a GP consultation — often years before a woman has actually reached the 12-month amenorrhoea threshold that defines menopause itself. Any candidate revising menopause UKMLA content should treat this recognition step as the real starting point, since many exam stems present a perimenopausal picture rather than a woman who has already reached the clear-cut, 12-month definition. Cycle changes are usually the first sign: cycles may shorten initially, then lengthen and become increasingly irregular, with skipped periods becoming more frequent as the transition progresses. Some women experience heavier, more prolonged bleeding during perimenopause as anovulatory cycles become more frequent and the endometrium is exposed to unopposed oestrogen for longer stretches; this pattern needs to be distinguished from pathological causes of abnormal uterine bleeding.

Perimenopause typically begins in a woman's mid-to-late 40s, although the age of onset varies considerably between individuals and is influenced by genetics, smoking status (smokers tend to reach menopause one to two years earlier, on average), and certain medical or surgical histories. The transition itself commonly lasts somewhere between four and eight years before the final period, although this range is highly variable, and some women experience a much shorter or longer perimenopausal phase.

The Three Symptom Domains: Vasomotor, Psychological, and Genitourinary

For exam purposes, it helps to organise menopausal symptoms into three broad domains, since AKT stems frequently test recognition of atypical presentations within each category rather than the textbook hot flush alone.

Vasomotor Symptoms

Vasomotor symptoms are the most classically recognised menopausal presentation and affect a substantial majority of women during the transition. Hot flushes are sudden sensations of intense heat, typically affecting the face, neck, and chest, often accompanied by visible flushing, sweating, and sometimes palpitations. Night sweats are the nocturnal equivalent, frequently severe enough to disrupt sleep and, in turn, to worsen fatigue and mood — a knock-on effect easy to underestimate in isolation from the other two domains. Vasomotor symptoms typically peak in the year or two around the final period, though a meaningful proportion of women experience them for considerably longer.

Psychological Symptoms

Psychological symptoms are common but frequently under-recognised as menopausal in origin, both by patients themselves and, at times, by clinicians who may instead pursue a primary mental health diagnosis without first considering the hormonal context. Low mood, anxiety, irritability, reduced self-confidence, and difficulty concentrating (often described by patients as "brain fog") are all well-documented features of the perimenopausal transition. A menopause UKMLA stem describing a woman in her mid-to-late 40s presenting with new-onset low mood, anxiety, or cognitive complaints should always prompt consideration of the perimenopausal transition as a contributing or primary cause, particularly when these symptoms cluster alongside cycle changes or vasomotor symptoms, rather than jumping straight to a primary psychiatric diagnosis without that context.

Genitourinary Symptoms

Genitourinary symptoms result from declining oestrogen's effect on the vaginal and lower urinary tract epithelium, collectively described under the umbrella term genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM). Vaginal dryness, itching, and dyspareunia (painful intercourse) are the most commonly reported features, alongside urinary urgency, frequency, recurrent urinary tract infections, and mild stress incontinence related to tissue changes rather than a primary urological pathology. Unlike vasomotor and psychological symptoms, genitourinary symptoms are frequently progressive if untreated, because the underlying tissue atrophy does not resolve spontaneously — this is precisely why local vaginal oestrogen, discussed later, remains appropriate even when systemic HRT is not suitable.

Diagnosis: Why Hormone Testing Is Not Routine After 45

Menopause diagnosis is one of the genuinely counter-intuitive areas of women's health for the UKMLA, and it is worth stating the core principle as plainly as possible before exploring the reasoning behind it: in a woman aged 45 or over presenting with typical menopausal symptoms, no hormone test is required to make the diagnosis. NICE's guideline on menopause diagnosis and management, NG23, is explicit on this point, and it represents exactly the kind of guideline-specific, slightly counter-intuitive detail that menopause UKMLA questions are designed to test.

The Clinical Diagnosis Approach for Women Over 45

NICE's guidance states that clinicians should diagnose menopause and perimenopause based on symptoms alone in women aged over 45, without laboratory tests, in the following circumstances: perimenopause based on vasomotor symptoms and irregular periods; menopause in women who have not had a period for at least 12 months and are not using hormonal contraception; and menopause based on symptoms in women without a uterus (for example, following hysterectomy), where the 12-month amenorrhoea marker obviously cannot be used. The rationale is straightforward once you understand the underlying biology described above: during the menopausal transition, FSH and oestradiol levels fluctuate substantially, sometimes within the same week, which means a single blood test can return a result consistent with either a premenopausal or a postmenopausal state in the same woman on different days. A normal FSH result therefore does not reliably exclude perimenopause, and an elevated result does not add meaningful diagnostic certainty beyond what the clinical picture already tells you.

This is a genuinely high-yield menopause UKMLA exam point precisely because it runs against the instinct many candidates bring from general internal medicine, where "confirm the diagnosis with the relevant blood test before starting treatment" is often the correct approach. Menopause is a clear exception to that instinct, and a stem describing a 48-year-old woman with classic vasomotor symptoms and irregular periods, where one answer option is "check FSH before starting treatment" and another is "diagnose clinically and discuss management options," is testing exactly this distinction. The correct answer is almost always to proceed on the clinical picture rather than order a test that will not change the outcome.

When Hormone Testing Is Appropriate

Hormone testing does have a legitimate, clearly bounded role, and knowing exactly where that boundary sits is just as important as knowing the general rule against routine testing. FSH measurement is indicated in three specific scenarios: in women aged under 40 with suspected premature ovarian insufficiency (discussed in detail in its own section below); in women aged 40 to 45 with menopausal symptoms, including a change in their menstrual cycle, where the diagnosis is genuinely less certain than in an older woman with a classic presentation; and in women who have had a hysterectomy with ovarian conservation, where the absence of periods removes the usual 12-month amenorrhoea marker but the woman is not old enough for a purely age-based clinical diagnosis to be reliable. Outside of these specific scenarios, requesting FSH in a woman presenting with typical symptoms at 45 or older represents a deviation from NICE guidance, even though it may feel like the more "thorough" option to a candidate unfamiliar with this specific rule.

It is also worth noting that women using combined hormonal contraception or certain progestogen-only methods should not have FSH measured, since exogenous hormones will distort the result; if diagnostic clarity is genuinely needed, this typically requires a temporary switch to a non-hormonal or barrier method before testing, managed in conjunction with specialist advice where appropriate.

Differential Diagnosis and Red Flags

Not every case of amenorrhoea or vasomotor-type symptoms in a woman in her 40s or 50s is menopause, and menopause UKMLA questions will occasionally test recognition of an alternative explanation. Thyroid dysfunction can produce a symptom overlap that mimics menopause closely — both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism can cause menstrual irregularity, mood disturbance, and temperature dysregulation, which is why thyroid function tests remain appropriate when the clinical picture is atypical, or when other features (weight change, tremor, goitre) point towards a thyroid cause. Pregnancy should always be excluded in a woman with amenorrhoea who remains sexually active, since perimenopausal women remain at risk of pregnancy until they have gone a full 12 months without a period. Postmenopausal bleeding — any vaginal bleeding occurring more than 12 months after the last period — is a distinct red-flag presentation and must never be attributed to "menopause" without appropriate investigation, since it is the most common presenting feature of endometrial cancer and requires urgent two-week-wait referral pathways rather than reassurance; this distinction between expected perimenopausal irregularity and true postmenopausal bleeding is one of the clearest safety-netting points in this entire topic area, and the kind of careful history-taking it demands is also covered from a communication-skills angle in the site's guide to sensitive communication in clinical stations.

Hormone Replacement Therapy: The Basics

Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) remains the most effective treatment available for vasomotor symptoms, and NICE is explicit that it should be offered to women with bothersome menopausal symptoms after a balanced discussion of the risks and benefits, rather than being withheld by default or treated as a last resort. Understanding the basic architecture of HRT — which hormones, in what combination, and by what route — is essential before the risk-benefit conversation makes full sense, and it is one of the most heavily tested areas of menopause UKMLA content precisely because so many prescribing decisions hinge on a small number of clearly defined rules.

Oestrogen-Only Versus Combined HRT

The fundamental branch point in HRT prescribing is whether a woman has a uterus. Women who have had a hysterectomy can generally be prescribed oestrogen-only HRT, since there is no endometrium at risk of the proliferative effects of unopposed oestrogen. Women who retain a uterus must be prescribed combined HRT — oestrogen alongside a progestogen — because oestrogen given alone stimulates endometrial proliferation and meaningfully increases the risk of endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial cancer over time. The progestogen component protects the endometrium, and omitting it in a woman with an intact uterus is a genuine prescribing error rather than a minor technicality; a menopause UKMLA stem testing this point is checking whether you have correctly identified the presence or absence of a uterus as the deciding factor, not the woman's age or symptom severity.

Combined HRT is further divided into cyclical (sequential) and continuous combined regimens. Cyclical regimens, in which the progestogen is given for part of each cycle, are typically used in perimenopausal women who are still having periods or who are within roughly one to two years of their last period, and they produce a regular, planned withdrawal bleed. Continuous combined regimens, in which both oestrogen and progestogen are taken every day without a break, are generally reserved for women who are clearly postmenopausal (usually at least one to two years since the last period, or aged over 54), and avoid any withdrawal bleeding altogether. Starting a continuous combined regimen too early — before a woman is truly postmenopausal — commonly causes unpredictable, erratic bleeding, which is itself a frequent source of patient concern, so getting this sequencing right matters practically as well as for exam purposes.

Routes of Administration

HRT can be delivered by several different routes, and the route chosen has direct implications for both symptom control and risk profile, particularly with respect to venous thromboembolism (VTE), discussed in detail below. Oral tablets remain widely used, but they undergo first-pass hepatic metabolism, which increases clotting factor synthesis and is the mechanistic reason oral oestrogen carries a higher VTE risk than non-oral routes. Transdermal preparations — patches, gels, and sprays — avoid this first-pass effect entirely and are associated with a meaningfully lower VTE risk than oral oestrogen; this makes transdermal delivery the preferred route for women with additional risk factors for VTE, including obesity. Vaginal (local) oestrogen, delivered as creams, pessaries, or a vaginal ring, treats genitourinary symptoms directly at the site of tissue atrophy with minimal systemic absorption, and — importantly for exam purposes — does not require concurrent progestogen even in women with a uterus, because the systemic exposure is too low to meaningfully stimulate the endometrium; this is a genuinely different rule from systemic combined HRT and is worth committing to memory as a discrete fact.

Types of Progestogen and the Mirena Coil

The progestogen component of combined HRT can be delivered as a separate tablet or patch, or via the levonorgestrel intrauterine system (commonly known by the brand name Mirena), which provides endometrial protection directly within the uterine cavity while oestrogen is given separately by any systemic route. This combination is a popular, NICE-endorsed option, particularly for women who would also benefit from the coil's contraceptive effect during the perimenopausal years — a genuinely useful dual-purpose option that combines endometrial protection with ongoing contraception, which oral or transdermal progestogen alone does not provide. This dual role is also why menopause UKMLA revision benefits from being read alongside the site's separate guide on contraceptive method counselling, since perimenopausal women frequently need both questions answered in the same consultation.

Testosterone in Menopause Management

Testosterone is not licensed for menopause management in the UK but is sometimes used off-label, under specialist guidance, for persistent low libido that has not responded to adequate systemic HRT. Candidates should be aware that testosterone supplementation exists as an option primarily for this specific indication, rather than as a routine addition to standard HRT for vasomotor or psychological symptoms.

Benefits and Risks of HRT: A Factual Risk-Benefit Framework

The risk-benefit conversation around HRT is an area where public perception, historical controversy, and current guidance have sometimes diverged, and menopause UKMLA questions expect candidates to know the current NICE position precisely, rather than a dated or oversimplified version of "HRT is dangerous" that persists in some public discourse.

Benefits of HRT

HRT remains the most effective available treatment for vasomotor symptoms, typically producing substantial improvement in hot flushes and night sweats within a few weeks of starting, with maximal benefit usually apparent by three months. Beyond symptom control, HRT reduces the risk of osteoporotic fracture by preserving bone mineral density, an effect covered in more detail in the long-term health section below, and NICE notes that HRT may reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease if started within ten years of menopause onset, or before the age of 60 — a nuance sometimes referred to as the "timing hypothesis," which recognises that the cardiovascular effects of oestrogen appear to depend meaningfully on how soon after menopause it is started, rather than being uniformly protective or uniformly harmful regardless of timing. HRT also improves genitourinary symptoms when given systemically, and local vaginal oestrogen offers a highly effective, low-risk option for genitourinary symptoms specifically, even in women who cannot or choose not to use systemic HRT.

Breast Cancer Risk

Breast cancer risk is the single most prominent risk consideration in the HRT conversation, and NICE's guidance is precise about the details rather than offering a single blanket statement. Combined HRT (oestrogen plus progestogen) is associated with a small increase in breast cancer risk, related to duration of use — increasing the longer combined HRT is taken, though decreasing again after stopping. Oestrogen-only HRT is associated with little to no increase in breast cancer risk — an important distinction, since oestrogen-only HRT is used specifically in women without a uterus, where the endometrial risk that necessitates progestogen does not apply. NICE frames the practical magnitude of this risk in relative rather than purely alarming absolute terms: it is often described to patients as being smaller than, or broadly comparable to, other everyday lifestyle risk factors such as being overweight or drinking alcohol regularly, and any excess risk should be discussed as part of an individualised, balanced conversation rather than used as a reason to withhold HRT by default.

Venous Thromboembolism Risk

VTE risk is the second major risk consideration, and — as introduced above — it depends heavily on the route of administration rather than being a fixed property of HRT as a single category. Oral oestrogen increases VTE risk due to first-pass hepatic metabolism increasing clotting factor production, while transdermal oestrogen is not associated with an increased risk of VTE compared with baseline, because it bypasses this hepatic first-pass effect. This makes the route of administration, not simply the decision to prescribe HRT at all, the key modifiable variable for a woman with additional VTE risk factors, such as obesity or a personal or strong family history of VTE. NICE guidance and clinical practice favour transdermal preparations for this reason, and this is a frequently tested practical prescribing point in menopause UKMLA stems: a case describing an obese woman requesting HRT is testing whether you recommend the transdermal route rather than declining HRT altogether.

Contraindications and Cautions

Certain conditions represent clear contraindications or require particular caution before starting HRT. Current or past breast cancer is generally a contraindication to systemic HRT, and any woman with this history requesting menopause treatment should usually be discussed with, or referred to, the relevant specialist team rather than started on HRT in primary care without that input. Undiagnosed vaginal bleeding must be investigated before HRT is started, since starting hormone treatment before excluding a serious underlying cause risks masking or delaying diagnosis. Active liver disease and a personal history of VTE both warrant caution and specialist input, with transdermal routes and, in some cases, specialist involvement being the more appropriate pathway. As with all guideline-based prescribing covered on this site, always verify current detail directly against the live NICE guidance before applying it in practice.

Non-Hormonal Management Options

Not every woman wants, or is able, to take HRT, and menopause UKMLA revision needs to cover what NICE recommends as alternatives just as thoroughly as HRT itself.

Non-Hormonal Options for Vasomotor Symptoms

For women who cannot or choose not to use HRT, several non-hormonal pharmacological options exist, with an important safety caveat that menopause UKMLA questions specifically test: NICE advises against offering certain drug classes to women whose only indication is menopausal vasomotor symptoms, unless there is another specific reason to use them. That said, in practice, certain selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are used, under specialist or shared-decision-making guidance, for troublesome vasomotor symptoms in women who cannot take HRT — for example, women with a contraindicating history of hormone-sensitive breast cancer. Clonidine, an alpha-2 agonist originally developed as an antihypertensive, is also used in some women unable to take HRT, though its effectiveness is generally more modest and its side-effect profile (dry mouth, drowsiness, postural hypotension) needs to be weighed against that limited benefit.

Lifestyle measures form an important, low-risk first step regardless of whether pharmacological treatment is also used. NICE recommends regular exercise, weight management, reducing caffeine and alcohol intake, wearing layered clothing, and using cooling strategies as reasonable measures to reduce the frequency and severity of vasomotor symptoms.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) has a specific, guideline-endorsed role in menopause management that is sometimes underestimated by candidates who associate CBT purely with mental health conditions. NICE recommends considering CBT to alleviate low mood or anxiety that arises as a result of menopause, and also notes that CBT can improve the psychological impact of vasomotor symptoms even where it does not necessarily reduce their objective frequency — an important nuance, since a stem might ask what to offer a woman with mild-to-moderate mood symptoms who prefers not to start medication, where CBT is often the most appropriate first-line answer.

Herbal and Complementary Remedies

Herbal remedies such as black cohosh and St John's Wort are sometimes used by patients for vasomotor symptoms, but NICE guidance and safe prescribing practice both counsel caution here. Evidence of benefit is limited and inconsistent, and there are genuine drug interaction risks — St John's Wort, in particular, is a potent inducer of hepatic enzymes and can significantly reduce the effectiveness of other medications, including tamoxifen, anticoagulants, and hormonal contraception. Patients should always be asked directly about herbal remedy use, since they may not volunteer this information unprompted, believing "natural" products fall outside the scope of a medical consultation.

Managing Genitourinary Symptoms Without Systemic HRT

For genitourinary symptoms specifically, vaginal moisturisers and lubricants provide symptomatic relief for dryness and dyspareunia without any hormonal content, making them an appropriate first-line or adjunct option for any woman, including those in whom systemic oestrogen is contraindicated. As already noted, local vaginal oestrogen itself can often be used even alongside a contraindication to systemic HRT, given its minimal systemic absorption, though this should always be assessed individually.

Premature Ovarian Insufficiency: A Distinct Diagnosis

Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) is defined as menopause occurring before the age of 40, and it deserves treatment as a genuinely separate diagnostic and management entity rather than simply "early menopause." This distinction matters because POI carries different diagnostic criteria, risk implications, and treatment recommendations to menopause at the typical age, and it is one of the areas of menopause UKMLA revision most likely to be tested as a standalone scenario.

Diagnostic Criteria

Unlike menopause in women aged 45 and over, POI genuinely requires hormone confirmation, precisely because the diagnosis is unexpected at this age and carries significant long-term implications. The diagnostic criteria are: elevated FSH, typically greater than 30 IU/L, measured on two separate occasions four to six weeks apart, in a woman under 40 presenting with menopausal symptoms and irregular or absent periods for at least four months. Requiring two separate elevated readings, rather than a single test, reflects the same underlying biological variability discussed earlier — even in POI, a single hormone measurement can be misleading, so the two-sample requirement is what gives the diagnosis genuine confidence.

Causes of Premature Ovarian Insufficiency

POI can be idiopathic (no cause identified, actually the most common scenario) or can result from identifiable causes: autoimmune conditions (autoimmune oophoritis, sometimes associated with autoimmune thyroid disease or Addison's disease); genetic causes, including Turner syndrome and fragile X premutation carriers; iatrogenic causes, including chemotherapy, pelvic radiotherapy, and bilateral oophorectomy; and, less commonly, infective causes such as mumps oophoritis. When POI is confirmed, further investigation is generally appropriate, particularly screening for associated autoimmune conditions and, in selected cases, genetic testing, since identifying a treatable underlying cause can meaningfully change a woman's broader care plan.

Why POI Requires Different Management

The management principle that differs most from typical-age menopause is this: HRT (or a combined oral contraceptive as an alternative) is recommended for women with POI at least until the average age of natural menopause, around 51, rather than being a discretionary symptom-control choice weighed against the same risk profile that applies to an older woman. This is because a woman with POI faces a substantially longer duration of oestrogen deficiency if left untreated, and that longer exposure carries meaningfully greater long-term risks to bone and cardiovascular health, discussed further below. The usual, more finely balanced risk-benefit conversation that applies to a 52-year-old considering HRT does not translate directly to a 32-year-old with POI — in the younger woman, treatment is generally considered replacement of a hormone her body would ordinarily still be producing, rather than an elective addition to an ageing-related decline that has already run its course. A menopause UKMLA stem describing a woman in her early 30s with confirmed POI who is reluctant to start HRT because she has heard "HRT causes breast cancer" is testing whether you can correctly explain this age-specific reasoning.

Fertility implications also distinguish POI from typical menopause and deserve sensitive discussion: POI significantly reduces but does not always completely abolish the chance of natural conception, and women wishing to conceive should be counselled accordingly and, where appropriate, referred for fertility specialist input, since a small proportion of women with POI experience spontaneous, intermittent ovarian activity.

Long-Term Health Considerations

Beyond symptom management, menopause carries longer-term physiological consequences that both UK medical students and IMGs need to understand, since these underpin much of the rationale for the HRT risk-benefit conversation covered above. No menopause UKMLA revision plan is complete without this longer-horizon view, since exam stems frequently frame these consequences as the reason behind a management decision.

Bone Health and Osteoporosis Risk

Oestrogen plays a protective role in maintaining bone mineral density by restraining osteoclast-mediated bone resorption, and the decline in oestrogen at menopause leads to accelerated bone loss, particularly in the first several years after the final period. This accelerated loss is a major contributor to postmenopausal osteoporosis and the associated increased risk of fragility fractures, particularly of the hip, spine, and wrist — a genuinely significant cause of morbidity and, especially with hip fractures, mortality in older women. HRT reduces fracture risk by preserving bone mineral density, and this benefit is a legitimate part of the overall risk-benefit conversation, particularly for women at elevated fracture risk within the window where HRT's benefits are most favourable. Beyond HRT, general bone health measures — adequate calcium and vitamin D intake, weight-bearing exercise, smoking cessation, and moderation of alcohol intake — remain relevant regardless of a woman's eventual decision about HRT. Where fracture risk is significant, formal risk assessment tools and, where indicated, bone-protective medications such as bisphosphonates may also form part of a woman's broader management, alongside the same core NICE-guideline literacy that underpins so much of AKT decision-making.

Cardiovascular Risk

Cardiovascular disease risk also rises after menopause, reflecting the loss of oestrogen's broadly favourable effects on lipid profile and vascular function, alongside the simple effect of advancing age. As introduced above, the relationship between HRT and cardiovascular risk depends significantly on timing: NICE notes that HRT may reduce cardiovascular disease risk when started within ten years of the menopause or before age 60, reflecting the "timing hypothesis" that oestrogen's vascular effects are more favourable when started closer to the natural menopausal transition rather than many years afterwards. This timing nuance is a genuinely high-yield menopause UKMLA fact: candidates should not treat HRT and cardiovascular risk as a single, universally protective or universally harmful relationship, but understand that timing of initiation is the key variable. This sits alongside general cardiovascular risk-factor management — blood pressure control, lipid management, smoking cessation, and regular physical activity.

Ongoing Monitoring and Review

Women established on HRT should have a review after the first three months of treatment to assess symptom response and tolerability, and thereafter at least annually, to reassess ongoing need and any change in personal or family risk factors. NICE is explicit that HRT should not be routinely limited to an arbitrary maximum duration — the decision to continue or stop should be individualised and revisited regularly, rather than governed by a fixed cut-off. This contradicts an older, more restrictive assumption about HRT duration that some patients (and, occasionally, candidates) may still hold from outdated public health messaging, and it is exactly the kind of granular detail a well-structured revision plan should schedule time for.

Frequently Asked Questions About Menopause Management in the UKMLA

Do you need to test FSH to diagnose menopause in a 48-year-old woman?

No. NICE guidance states that in women aged 45 or over presenting with typical menopausal symptoms, the diagnosis should be made clinically, without laboratory testing, because FSH and oestradiol levels fluctuate too much during the transition to be diagnostically reliable. Routine FSH testing is reserved for women under 40 with suspected premature ovarian insufficiency, and for women aged 40 to 45 with menopausal symptoms where the diagnosis is less certain, or following hysterectomy with ovarian conservation.

What is the difference between perimenopause and menopause?

Perimenopause is the transitional phase of fluctuating hormones and irregular periods that precedes the final menstrual period, typically lasting four to eight years and producing vasomotor, psychological, and genitourinary symptoms. Menopause itself is defined retrospectively once a woman has gone 12 consecutive months without a period, without using hormonal contraception, marking the point at which ovarian function has effectively ceased.

Does HRT cause breast cancer?

Combined HRT (oestrogen plus progestogen) is associated with a small increase in breast cancer risk that increases with duration of use and decreases again after stopping, while oestrogen-only HRT carries little to no increase in risk. NICE frames this excess risk as comparable in magnitude to other common lifestyle risk factors, such as being overweight or drinking alcohol regularly, and recommends that the decision to start or continue HRT should follow a balanced, individualised discussion rather than automatic avoidance.

Why is transdermal HRT preferred in women at higher risk of blood clots?

Oral oestrogen undergoes first-pass hepatic metabolism, which increases the production of clotting factors and raises the risk of venous thromboembolism, while transdermal oestrogen (patches, gels, or sprays) bypasses this hepatic first-pass effect and is not associated with an increased VTE risk compared with baseline. For this reason, transdermal preparations are generally preferred for women with additional VTE risk factors, such as obesity or a personal or strong family history of clots.

Can a woman with an intact uterus take oestrogen-only HRT?

No. Oestrogen given without a progestogen stimulates the endometrium and increases the risk of endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial cancer in a woman who still has a uterus. Women with an intact uterus require combined HRT, with the progestogen component protecting the endometrium, while oestrogen-only HRT is reserved for women who have had a hysterectomy.

How is premature ovarian insufficiency diagnosed and why is it treated differently from typical menopause?

Premature ovarian insufficiency is diagnosed in women under 40 with menopausal symptoms and irregular or absent periods, confirmed by an FSH level above 30 IU/L on two occasions taken four to six weeks apart. It is managed differently from typical-age menopause because HRT (or an alternative such as the combined oral contraceptive) is recommended until at least the average age of natural menopause, around 51, since the much longer duration of untreated oestrogen deficiency carries a meaningfully greater long-term risk to bone and cardiovascular health than the equivalent decision in an older woman.

What can be offered for vasomotor symptoms if a woman cannot take HRT?

Lifestyle measures, cognitive behavioural therapy, and, under specialist guidance, certain SSRIs, SNRIs, or clonidine can be considered for women who cannot or choose not to take HRT, for example those with a history of hormone-sensitive breast cancer. Herbal remedies such as black cohosh are sometimes used by patients but carry uncertain evidence of benefit and genuine interaction risks, and should always be discussed openly rather than assumed risk-free simply because they are available without prescription.

Conclusion

Menopause UKMLA questions reward candidates who can hold two things in mind at once: a diagnostic approach that deliberately avoids routine hormone testing in most women over 45, and a genuinely nuanced, guideline-specific understanding of HRT's risks and benefits rather than a dated or oversimplified view. Add to that the distinct diagnostic and management pathway for premature ovarian insufficiency, and the longer-term bone and cardiovascular consequences of the menopausal transition, and you have a topic area that the updated content map now expects in real depth — not as a single shared line inside a broader women's health heading.
