Mental health and psychiatry for the UKMLA is one of the highest-yield topic areas across both the Applied Knowledge Test and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment, accounting for a disproportionately large number of marks relative to its weight in traditional medical school curricula. The breadth of this domain — spanning diagnostic criteria, risk assessment, pharmacology, legislation, and ethical frameworks — means that candidates who systematically master psychiatry gain a significant advantage over peers who focus only on physical medicine.

Why Mental Health Is Heavily Examined in the UKMLA

The GMC's intention behind the UKMLA is to produce doctors who are safe across the full breadth of medicine at the point of licensure. Mental illness is prevalent in the UK — one in four adults experiences a mental health problem each year, and mental health presentations are among the most frequent reasons for attendance at emergency departments, GP consultations, and acute medical wards. The UKMLA blueprint reflects this reality. The AKT tests recognition, diagnostic criteria, management algorithms, pharmacology, and legal frameworks. The CPSA tests the candidate's ability to conduct a Mental State Examination, risk-assess a patient presenting with self-harm or suicidal ideation, and communicate sensitively with patients in mental health crisis.

The Mental State Examination

The Mental State Examination (MSE) is the psychiatric equivalent of a physical examination and is the structured framework used to describe and document a patient's current mental state. It is assessed directly in the CPSA and underpins AKT questions about recognising psychopathology. The MSE has ten components.

Appearance and Behaviour

Document physical appearance (dress, hygiene, self-care), level of eye contact, psychomotor activity (agitation or retardation), and any unusual behaviours. Psychomotor retardation suggests depression; agitation may indicate mania, anxiety, or substance intoxication. Waxy flexibility and posturing suggest catatonia, which can occur in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or organic causes.

Speech

Describe rate (fast in mania, slow in depression), volume (loud in mania), and form. Pressured speech (difficult to interrupt) is characteristic of mania. Poverty of speech (reduced output) suggests depression or negative symptoms of schizophrenia. Circumstantial speech (eventually reaches the point but with excessive detail) and tangential speech (never reaches the point) are formal thought disorder features.

Mood and Affect

Mood is the sustained emotional state as reported by the patient ("how would you describe your mood?"). Affect is the observable emotional expression. Affect may be blunted (reduced range and intensity), flat (absent), labile (rapidly shifting), incongruent (affect does not match the content of speech — laughing while describing distressing events, characteristic of psychosis), or restricted. Note any dysphoria, anhedonia, euphoria, or irritability.

Thought Form and Content

Thought form describes how thoughts are organised. Flight of ideas (rapid succession of loosely connected thoughts — a hallmark of mania), thought block (sudden cessation of thought, followed by a new unrelated thought), and loosening of associations (knight's move thinking — thoughts that appear unconnected) are all assessable. Thought content covers the subjects the patient thinks about: delusions (fixed, false, unshakeable beliefs held with certainty that are out of keeping with the patient's cultural background), obsessions (intrusive, unwanted, ego-dystonic thoughts), overvalued ideas, and thought insertion, withdrawal, or broadcasting (passivity phenomena — pathognomonic of psychosis when present).

Perception

Hallucinations are perceptions without an external stimulus. They can affect any modality: auditory (most common in schizophrenia — third-person voices discussing the patient, running commentary, command hallucinations), visual (more common in organic and substance-related psychosis), gustatory, olfactory, or tactile. Illusions are misperceptions of a real stimulus. Depersonalisation (feeling detached from oneself) and derealisation (feeling the world is unreal) can occur in anxiety, depression, and dissociative disorders.

Cognition

Formal cognitive testing in the MSE includes orientation to time, place, and person; short-term memory (three-object recall at five minutes); attention and concentration (serial sevens or WORLD backwards); and, if indicated, longer assessments such as the MMSE or MoCA for suspected dementia. In the acute assessment context, brief screening for delirium (fluctuating consciousness, inattention, disorientation) is essential.

Insight and Judgement

Insight refers to the patient's awareness that they are unwell, that their experiences may be symptoms of an illness, and their willingness to accept treatment. Full insight, partial insight, and absent insight are the three broad categories. Absent insight is clinically significant because it affects treatment adherence and informs decisions about the Mental Health Act. Judgement refers to the patient's ability to make appropriate decisions given their current mental state.

Depression

Diagnostic Criteria and Assessment Tools

ICD-11 (and the older ICD-10 still used in many UK settings) classifies depressive episodes by the number and type of core and additional symptoms. Core symptoms are depressed mood, loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia), and reduced energy or fatigue. Additional symptoms include reduced concentration, low self-esteem, guilt or feelings of worthlessness, pessimism, disturbed sleep (early morning waking, insomnia, or hypersomnia), disturbed appetite (usually reduced but can be increased in atypical depression), psychomotor agitation or retardation, and suicidal ideation or acts of self-harm. A mild depressive episode requires two core and two additional symptoms for at least two weeks. Moderate requires two core and three to four additional symptoms. Severe requires three core and four or more additional symptoms, with or without psychotic features.

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) is the validated self-report tool most widely used in primary care and recommended by NICE for initial assessment and monitoring of depression. Scores range from 0 to 27: minimal (0 to 4), mild (5 to 9), moderate (10 to 14), moderately severe (15 to 19), severe (20 to 27). The PHQ-9 is also used to monitor treatment response — a drop of five or more points represents a clinically meaningful improvement.

NICE NG222 Stepped Care and Treatment

The 2022 NICE guideline (NG222) replaced the previous stepped-care model with a more flexible choice-based approach. For mild depression, offer a guided self-help or group CBT programme before medication. For moderate-to-severe depression, offer a choice between antidepressant medication and psychological therapy (high-intensity CBT, interpersonal therapy, behavioural activation, or counselling). SSRIs are first-line pharmacotherapy. Sertraline 50 mg daily (increasing to maximum 200 mg daily) is the preferred first-choice SSRI because of its favourable safety profile, lower drug interaction risk, and evidence base. Fluoxetine is also frequently used and has a long half-life that makes it useful when adherence is a concern. Citalopram and escitalopram carry a dose-dependent risk of QT interval prolongation; maximum doses should not be exceeded (citalopram 40 mg/day, escitalopram 20 mg/day — lower in the elderly). If an adequate trial of an SSRI at an adequate dose (at minimum four to six weeks, ideally longer) does not produce sufficient response, switch to a different SSRI or to an SNRI (venlafaxine or duloxetine) or mirtazapine. Mirtazapine is particularly useful when sedation is desired (it causes histamine blockade at low doses, producing sedation that reduces at higher doses). Continue antidepressants for at least six months after remission. Patients with recurrent depression (three or more episodes) should continue treatment for at least two years. When stopping, taper slowly to avoid discontinuation syndrome (particularly with paroxetine and venlafaxine — both have short half-lives).

When to Refer to Secondary Care

Refer to the crisis team or emergency services immediately if the patient is at imminent risk of self-harm or suicide. Refer to secondary care (Community Mental Health Team) if the patient has psychotic features with depression, if they have failed two adequate antidepressant trials, if they have complex needs including comorbid personality disorder or substance misuse, or if they have bipolar depression (where antidepressant monotherapy can precipitate mania).

Anxiety Disorders

Generalised Anxiety Disorder

GAD is characterised by excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple domains for at least six months, accompanied by at least three of: restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulties, irritability, muscle tension, and sleep disturbance. The GAD-7 (Generalised Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale) is used to screen and monitor: scores of 5 to 9 (mild), 10 to 14 (moderate), and 15 to 21 (severe). Management follows the NICE four-step model as described in the NICE guidelines section above. SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) or SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) are first-line pharmacotherapy. Pregabalin (an alpha-2-delta ligand) is recommended as an option for GAD when SSRIs/SNRIs are not tolerated or effective, though its abuse potential means it should be used with caution and is a controlled drug. Buspirone is an alternative non-sedating anxiolytic. Benzodiazepines should not be used for long-term management of GAD because of dependency risk, but may be prescribed for short-term (two to four weeks maximum) crisis management.

Panic Disorder

Panic disorder involves recurrent, unexpected panic attacks (discrete episodes of intense fear peaking within minutes and accompanied by at least four somatic or cognitive symptoms — palpitations, sweating, trembling, dyspnoea, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, derealization, fear of losing control, fear of dying, paraesthesiae, chills/flushing) followed by at least one month of persistent concern about further attacks or maladaptive behavioural change. CBT (including interoceptive exposure) is first-line treatment. SSRIs are the preferred pharmacotherapy. Avoidance of triggers, agoraphobia, and safety behaviours should be addressed in therapy.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

OCD involves obsessions (intrusive, ego-dystonic thoughts, images, or urges) and compulsions (repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety generated by obsessions). OCD symptoms cause significant distress and are time-consuming (more than one hour per day). Treatment: ERP (exposure and response prevention) plus an SSRI is first-line for moderate-to-severe OCD. Higher doses of SSRIs are often needed for OCD than for depression. Clomipramine (tricyclic antidepressant) is an alternative if SSRIs are ineffective. Antipsychotic augmentation (risperidone, quetiapine) is used for treatment-resistant cases.

PTSD

PTSD follows exposure to an extremely threatening or horrific event and is characterised by: re-experiencing symptoms (intrusive memories, nightmares, flashbacks); avoidance of trauma-related cues; negative alterations in cognition and mood; and hyperarousal (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, sleep disturbance, irritability, concentration difficulties). Symptoms must persist for more than one month and cause significant impairment. Trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT) and EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) are both first-line treatments per NICE NG116. SSRIs (paroxetine or sertraline) are second-line pharmacotherapy. Prazosin may be used for trauma-related nightmares.

Psychosis and Schizophrenia

Positive and Negative Symptoms

Positive symptoms are additions to normal experience: hallucinations, delusions, formal thought disorder, and catatonia. Negative symptoms are losses from normal experience: alogia (reduced speech output), avolition (lack of motivation), affective flattening (blunted affect), anhedonia, and social withdrawal. Negative symptoms are harder to treat and contribute more to long-term disability than positive symptoms. Cognitive symptoms include impaired working memory, attention, and executive function.

First Episode Psychosis

First episode psychosis (FEP) should be assessed urgently — Early Intervention in Psychosis (EIP) teams aim to see patients within two weeks of referral. The initial assessment excludes organic causes of psychosis (delirium, substance intoxication, brain tumour, encephalitis, thyroid disease). If organic causes are excluded, an antipsychotic is started at the lowest effective dose. The choice of antipsychotic should involve a discussion of side effects with the patient. Psychoeducation, CBT for psychosis (CBTp), family therapy, and supported employment are all recommended.

Antipsychotics and Side Effects

Antipsychotics are classified as typical (first-generation, D2-receptor antagonists) and atypical (second-generation, with additional serotonin antagonism). Typical antipsychotics (haloperidol, chlorpromazine) carry a higher risk of extrapyramidal side effects (EPSE): acute dystonia (sustained muscle spasm — treat with procyclidine), akathisia (subjective motor restlessness — most distressing to patients, treat with propranolol or benzodiazepine), Parkinsonism (drug-induced tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia — treat with procyclidine), and tardive dyskinesia (late-onset involuntary movements of face and tongue — may be irreversible). Atypical antipsychotics (risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, clozapine) have lower EPSE risk but cause metabolic syndrome — weight gain, dyslipidaemia, impaired glucose tolerance. Metabolic monitoring (weight, waist circumference, blood glucose, fasting lipids) is mandatory at baseline and every three months. Risperidone causes hyperprolactinaemia (galactorrhoea, menstrual disturbance, sexual dysfunction, osteoporosis) via D2 blockade in the tuberoinfundibular pathway. QTc prolongation is a risk with many antipsychotics — check ECG at baseline and when using haloperidol, sertindole, or amisulpride. Clozapine is reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (failure of at least two antipsychotics at adequate dose) due to the risk of agranulocytosis — mandatory weekly FBC for the first eighteen weeks, then fortnightly, then monthly. Clozapine also causes hypersalivation, constipation (potentially fatal if severe — monitor bowel function), myocarditis (rare but serious — check CRP and troponin in first month), and metabolic syndrome.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar I disorder requires at least one manic episode (elevated or expansive mood, increased energy, decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, pressured speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, and impulsive high-risk behaviours, lasting at least seven days or requiring hospitalisation). Bipolar II is characterised by at least one hypomanic episode (same features but lasting four to six days, less severe, no hospitalisation) and at least one major depressive episode. Cyclothymia involves chronic fluctuating mood with subsyndromal hypomanic and depressive symptoms for at least two years.

Acute mania management: antipsychotics (haloperidol, olanzapine, risperidone, or quetiapine) are first-line. Add a mood stabiliser (lithium or sodium valproate) for long-term prevention. Lithium is the gold-standard mood stabiliser. Sodium valproate is highly effective but absolutely contraindicated in women of childbearing potential unless there is a pregnancy prevention programme in place (teratogenicity — neural tube defects, fetal valproate syndrome). Lamotrigine is preferred in bipolar depression and for the depressive pole maintenance. Avoid antidepressant monotherapy in bipolar depression as it can precipitate mania or rapid cycling.

Lithium Monitoring and Toxicity

Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index. Target serum lithium level is 0.4 to 1.0 mmol/L for maintenance (0.8 to 1.0 mmol/L for acute mania). Levels are checked twelve hours after the last dose. Monitoring: serum lithium every three to six months once stable; renal function (lithium is renally cleared and nephrotoxic) and thyroid function (lithium causes hypothyroidism) every six months; eGFR and TFTs at baseline. Signs of lithium toxicity: levels above 1.5 mmol/L — coarse tremor, diarrhoea, vomiting, confusion, ataxia; levels above 2.0 mmol/L — seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, renal failure, death. Toxicity is precipitated by dehydration (illness, hot weather), NSAIDs (reduce renal lithium excretion), ACE inhibitors and diuretics (same mechanism), and sodium depletion. Treatment of toxicity: stop lithium, rehydrate with IV saline, monitor levels; haemodialysis if level above 3.5 mmol/L or if severe toxicity.

Eating Disorders

The SCOFF questionnaire screens for eating disorders: Do you make yourself Sick because you feel uncomfortably full? Do you worry you have lost Control over how much you eat? Have you recently lost more than One stone in a three-month period? Do you believe yourself to be Fat when others say you are thin? Would you say that Food dominates your life? Two or more positive answers suggest a probable eating disorder and warrants further assessment.

Anorexia nervosa is characterised by restriction of energy intake, intense fear of weight gain, and distorted body image. MARSIPAN (Management of Really Sick Patients with Anorexia Nervosa) defines physical risk criteria for hospital admission: BMI below 13 kg/m2 in adults (or rapid weight loss), blood glucose below 3 mmol/L, potassium below 2.5 mmol/L, sodium below 130 mmol/L, QTc above 450 ms, postural hypotension, or severe muscle weakness. Refeeding syndrome (hypophosphataemia, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia when reintroducing nutrition to a malnourished patient) is life-threatening — phosphate should be monitored and supplemented; commence feeding slowly. Bulimia nervosa involves recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviours (self-induced vomiting, laxative misuse, excessive exercise). Binge-eating disorder involves recurrent binges without compensatory behaviours. SSRI treatment (fluoxetine 60 mg daily for bulimia) and CBT-ED (CBT adapted for eating disorders) are the main evidence-based treatments.

Suicide Risk Assessment

Columbia Protocol and Risk Factors

The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) is the structured tool recommended for assessing suicidal ideation and behaviour. In the CPSA, candidates are expected to sensitively but directly ask about suicidal ideation, intent, and plans. Risk factors for suicide include: male sex, age (bimodal peak in young adults and older men), mental illness (depression, schizophrenia, alcohol misuse, personality disorder), previous self-harm or suicide attempts (the single strongest predictor of future suicide), social isolation, unemployment, financial difficulties, recent bereavement or relationship breakdown, chronic pain or physical illness, family history of suicide, and access to lethal means (particularly firearms or stockpiled medications). Protective factors include: social support, children at home, religious beliefs, engagement with services, hope for the future, and reasons for living.

Immediate Management

If a patient presents with suicidal ideation, assess the immediate risk level. For high-risk patients (specific plan, intent, access to means, or previous serious attempt), arrange immediate psychiatric assessment — do not leave the patient alone. Contact the on-call psychiatry team or crisis team. For patients presenting to the emergency department after self-harm, NICE NG225 (self-harm: assessment, management, and preventing recurrence, 2022) recommends a psychosocial assessment within 24 hours by a trained professional, regardless of the apparent seriousness of the act. The assessment explores precipitants, intent, mental state, risk, and coping resources. Following assessment, a care plan should be agreed, including safety planning, crisis contact details, and follow-up arrangements.

The NR is determined by a statutory hierarchy under the MHA, not automatically the next of kin named by the patient.

MHA vs MCA — Key Distinction

The Mental Capacity Act (MCA) 2005 governs treatment of adults who lack capacity to make a specific decision. A patient lacks capacity if they cannot understand, retain, weigh up, or communicate information relevant to a decision. Best interest decision-making under the MCA does not require legal detention. The MHA is used when a patient has capacity but refuses treatment for a mental disorder — the MHA overrides that refusal for mental disorder treatment only. Critically, treatment for physical disorders cannot be given under the MHA; it requires either MCA best interest principles (if lacking capacity) or voluntary consent.

CAMHS Referral and Capacity in Minors

Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) should be considered when a young person (under 18) has a mental health problem significantly affecting functioning or that cannot be managed in primary care or by tier 1 services (school counsellors, youth workers). Urgent CAMHS or paediatric referral is needed if there is immediate risk to life from self-harm, eating disorder with physical compromise, psychosis, or severe risk to the child or others. Under the age of 16, consent and capacity follow Gillick competence principles — a child under 16 may consent to treatment if they fully understand the nature and consequences of the decision. For refusal of treatment in a minor, parental consent overrides the child's refusal up to age 18 in life-threatening situations in England and Wales.

Frequently Asked Questions About Mental Health in the UKMLA

How do I remember which MHA section to apply?

Section 2 is for Assessment (28 days, two doctors). Section 3 is for Treatment (six months, two doctors, nearest relative can object). Section 4 is emergency Assessment when one doctor and an AMHP is available but a second doctor is not (72 hours). Section 5(2) is the doctor's hold for an already-admitted inpatient (72 hours). Section 5(4) is the nurse's hold (six hours) awaiting a doctor. Section 136 is police taking a person from a public place to a place of safety (24 hours).

What is the difference between illusions and hallucinations?

A hallucination is a perception that occurs without any external stimulus — the patient hears a voice when no one is speaking. An illusion is a misinterpretation of a real external stimulus — the patient misinterprets the pattern on a curtain as a face. Both can occur in psychosis, delirium, and substance intoxication, but hallucinations are more specific to psychosis when they occur in clear consciousness.

When would you choose mirtazapine over an SSRI?

Mirtazapine is preferred when sleep disturbance is prominent (it is sedating at lower doses), when appetite suppression is a problem (mirtazapine increases appetite and causes weight gain), when the patient has significant GI side effects from SSRIs, or when a rapid response is needed as mirtazapine tends to produce earlier sedation. It has minimal sexual side effects and is relatively safe in cardiac disease. The main downsides are weight gain and sedation at lower doses.

Which features of anorexia warrant emergency hospital admission?

Using MARSIPAN criteria: BMI below 13 kg/m2 in adults, rapid weight loss (more than 1 kg per week), or any of the following physical complications — blood glucose below 3 mmol/L, potassium below 2.5 mmol/L, sodium below 130 mmol/L, phosphate below 0.5 mmol/L, QTc above 450 ms, postural BP drop of more than 10 mmHg, pulse below 40 bpm or above 120 bpm, oxygen saturation below 92%, or severe proximal muscle weakness (unable to stand from sitting without using arms).

Can a patient on a Community Treatment Order refuse depot antipsychotic injection?

Yes — a patient on a CTO can refuse treatment in the community. However, if they refuse treatment and meet the criteria for recall, they can be recalled to hospital under the CTO's powers. Once recalled and assessed on the ward, if they continue to refuse and lack insight, treatment can be administered under Part IV of the MHA (providing appropriate consent and certification requirements are met). The CTO does not give blanket authority to treat in the community against a patient's will.

Conclusion

Mental health is not a peripheral topic in the UKMLA — it is central to both the AKT and the CPSA and heavily represented in the exam blueprint. Mastering the MSE, knowing the diagnostic criteria and NICE management pathways for depression, anxiety disorders, psychosis, and bipolar disorder, and being confident with the Mental Health Act will put you well ahead of your peers.
