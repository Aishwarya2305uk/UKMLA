Practising medicine in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland raises a question that trips up UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) alike: does moving to work outside England mean applying for a different registration, sitting a different exam, or satisfying a different regulator? The short answer is no. The General Medical Council (GMC) is a single UK-wide regulator, and a doctor who is registered and licensed with the GMC is registered and licensed to practise in England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland simultaneously — there is no separate Scottish medical register, no distinct Welsh licence, and no additional Northern Irish authorisation to apply for. What genuinely does change when a doctor crosses from England into one of the other three nations is not their regulatory status but the health system they join: the employer, the postgraduate training administration, and some of the clinical guidance bodies all differ by nation, even though the underlying GMC registration does not.

This distinction matters for two very different audiences reading this guide. UK medical students weighing up a Foundation Programme post in Cardiff versus one in Manchester, or a specialty training number in Glasgow versus Leeds, need to understand that the choice is about the NHS structure and lifestyle they are joining, not about a second registration hurdle. IMGs who have just passed the UKMLA and secured GMC registration, meanwhile, sometimes assume — incorrectly — that a job offer in Belfast or Cardiff requires a fresh regulatory application on top of the one they have already completed. It does not. Once a doctor holds full GMC registration with a licence to practise, that status travels with them anywhere in the UK.

What follows sets out, nation by nation, exactly what does and does not change when practising medicine in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland compared with England: the employing health bodies, the postgraduate deaneries responsible for training administration, the small but real differences in clinical guidance arrangements, and some practical relocation considerations that are worth weighing up even though they have nothing to do with GMC registration itself.

Quick Answer: GMC Registration Is the Same Across All Four UK Nations

The GMC is a single, UK-wide regulator, and a doctor's registration and licence to practise are identical whether they work in England, Scotland, Wales or Northern Ireland — no separate registration, licence, or additional GMC fee is required to move between nations. The GMC itself operates offices in all four nations precisely because it regulates doctors, physician associates and anaesthesia associates across the whole of the UK as one system, not four separate ones. A doctor who registers with the GMC after passing the UKMLA, or a UK graduate who moves from provisional to full registration after Foundation Year 1, holds exactly the same registration number and status regardless of where in the UK they subsequently choose to work.

This means a UK medical graduate can accept a Foundation Programme post in Wales, move to a specialty training number in Scotland two years later, and finish their career as a consultant in Northern Ireland without ever needing to re-register, re-apply, or pay an additional GMC fee purely because of the change in nation. Likewise, an IMG registered with the GMC while working in an NHS trust in England can take up a locally employed doctor post in Belfast, or a specialty training post in Edinburgh, on the strength of the same registration they already hold — the job move is an employment change, not a regulatory one.

What a doctor does need to keep current, wherever in the UK they work, is their annual retention fee and their revalidation cycle — both administered centrally by the GMC regardless of nation. The site's guide to the annual fee every registered doctor pays to stay on the register explains exactly how that recurring charge works and applies identically whether a doctor's employer is in Leeds, Cardiff, Aberdeen or Belfast. Because registration itself is uniform, the real decisions facing a doctor considering practising medicine in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland are about employer, training administration, and lifestyle — not regulation — and the rest of this guide works through each of those in turn.

It is also worth being clear about what "the GMC confirms this" actually looks like in practice, since doctors sometimes want a definitive, citable source rather than a general assurance. The GMC's own published position on its registers makes the four-nation, single-register structure explicit: the GMC registers and licenses doctors, physician associates, and anaesthesia associates across the whole of the United Kingdom as one system, with regional offices in England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland supporting that single register rather than maintaining four separate ones. Anyone who wants to double-check this directly, rather than relying on secondary summaries, can consult that page as the primary source.

What Actually Differs: NHS Structure and Employer Bodies

Although GMC registration is uniform across the UK, the health service a doctor is employed by is not — NHS Scotland, NHS Wales, and Health and Social Care Northern Ireland (HSC) are each distinct, separately governed health systems with their own employing organisations, separate from NHS England. Devolution gave each of the four nations responsibility for running its own health service, so while "the NHS" is a familiar shorthand across the whole UK, the reality on the ground is four separately administered systems that happen to share a name (three of them do; Northern Ireland's system is formally called Health and Social Care, or HSC, reflecting its combined health and social care structure). A doctor moving from an NHS England trust to a health board in Scotland, for example, is changing employer entirely, even though their GMC registration, clinical competence, and professional standing carry over unchanged.

This distinction is easy to underestimate because day-to-day clinical practice feels broadly similar across all four systems — the same GMC standards, the same Good Medical Practice guidance, and largely the same clinical training pathways apply everywhere. Where the difference actually shows up is in employment terms, local induction, IT and payroll systems, and which body a doctor's contract sits with. Each nation's system is described in turn below.

NHS Scotland

NHS Scotland is organised around 14 regional health boards, each of which directly employs medical staff working within its area, rather than a single national employer. Health boards such as NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde, NHS Lothian, and NHS Grampian each recruit, employ, and manage their own medical workforce, meaning a doctor's actual employer in Scotland is the relevant health board, not "NHS Scotland" as a single entity. Job vacancies, local induction processes, and much of the day-to-day HR administration therefore sit at health board level, even though national workforce policy is set centrally by the Scottish Government.

For doctors exploring roles in Scotland, the NHS Scotland Careers website is the starting point, since it aggregates vacancies and career information across all the individual health boards rather than requiring a doctor to search each board's own site separately. This distinction between "NHS Scotland" as a system and the health board as the actual contracting employer is one of the first practical things doctors moving north of the border need to understand, since it affects who to contact about a job offer, a contract query, or an occupational health matter. It is a similar distinction to the one covered in the site's broader guide to the pathway from UKMLA success into an NHS job, which explains how trust-level recruitment works across the UK more generally, before nation-specific detail is added on top.

NHS Wales

NHS Wales is organised into health boards and trusts that directly employ medical staff, coordinated nationally in part through Health Education and Improvement Wales (HEIW), which oversees workforce education, training, and some recruitment functions. Health boards such as Cardiff and Vale University Health Board, Betsi Cadwaladr University Health Board, and Aneurin Bevan University Health Board are the bodies that actually employ doctors working in Wales, similar in structure to the Scottish health board model, while national bodies such as HEIW coordinate training and workforce planning across the whole of Wales.

Doctors considering a move to Wales should be aware that, as in Scotland, the specific health board is the employer of record, and terms of employment, local induction, and much day-to-day administration are managed at that level rather than by a single Wales-wide HR department. HEIW's own NHS Wales careers pages provide a useful national-level starting point for understanding how the Welsh system fits together before narrowing down to a specific health board's vacancies via the general NHS Jobs recruitment site.

Health and Social Care Northern Ireland (HSC)

Health and Social Care Northern Ireland (HSC) is the umbrella term for Northern Ireland's combined health and social care system, structured around five HSC Trusts — Belfast, Northern, South Eastern, Southern, and Western — each of which employs medical staff directly. Northern Ireland's system is distinctive among the four UK nations in that health and social care services are integrated under a single structure rather than administered separately, which is reflected in the HSC name itself. As with Scotland and Wales, the individual Trust is the actual employer a doctor's contract sits with, not "HSC" as a single national body.

Doctors interested in working in Northern Ireland can search current vacancies across all five Trusts through the centralised HSC Jobs recruitment portal, which functions similarly to the aggregated recruitment sites used in Scotland and Wales — a single point of entry that then routes applicants to the specific Trust doing the hiring. Because Northern Ireland's system explicitly combines health and social care, doctors moving there for the first time may notice some structural and terminology differences from the health-only systems in England, Scotland, and Wales, even though the clinical work itself is governed by the same GMC standards throughout.

Why Employer Structure, Not Registration, Drives the Job Application Process

Because each nation's employer structure is genuinely separate, the practical mechanics of applying for a job differ slightly by nation even though the underlying eligibility rules do not. A doctor applying to NHS Scotland, NHS Wales, or HSC Northern Ireland will typically use that nation's own recruitment portal rather than the general NHS Jobs site used most commonly in England, and each employer will run its own shortlisting, interview, and pre-employment check process — including standard checks like Disclosure and Barring Service equivalents (Disclosure Scotland in Scotland, AccessNI in Northern Ireland) and occupational health clearance. None of this differs from what any doctor already experiences moving between two NHS England trusts; it is simply that the "trust" in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland is called a health board or a HSC Trust instead, and sits under a different national umbrella. Doctors who have already been through the process described in the site's guide to the different types of GMC registration will recognise that registration type and category are settled well before any of this employer-level process begins, and do not change depending on which employer ultimately makes the job offer.

Postgraduate Training Administration Differs by Nation

Each UK nation has its own postgraduate deanery or training body responsible for coordinating Foundation and specialty training locally, even though recruitment for many national training programmes is still coordinated on a UK-wide basis. This is one of the areas most likely to genuinely confuse a doctor moving between nations, because the picture is neither fully centralised nor fully separate — it is a mix of both, and which applies depends on the specific stage of training and the specific programme.

In England, postgraduate medical training is coordinated through NHS England's regional structures (following the 2023 transfer of Health Education England's functions into NHS England). In Scotland, the equivalent function sits with NHS Education for Scotland (NES), operating through the Scotland Deanery, which is itself divided into regional sub-units. In Wales, Health Education and Improvement Wales (HEIW) coordinates postgraduate training, including running the Wales Foundation School for newly qualified doctors. In Northern Ireland, the Northern Ireland Medical and Dental Training Agency (NIMDTA) performs the equivalent role, including overseeing the Northern Ireland Foundation School.

Foundation Programme Recruitment Across the Four Nations

Despite each nation running its own deanery or training body, Foundation Programme recruitment for UK medical graduates is coordinated through a single, UK-wide national process, with applicants able to express preferences across foundation schools throughout all four nations rather than applying separately to each one. This UK-wide coordination at the recruitment stage, sitting on top of nation-specific administration once a doctor is actually allocated a post, is exactly the kind of nuance that catches people out: the application is national, but the day-to-day training experience, educational supervision, and local rotations that follow are administered by whichever nation's deanery or foundation school the doctor is allocated to.

For UK medical students who have not yet worked through how the Foundation Programme fits together with GMC registration, the site's dedicated guide to the route from finishing medical school into an NHS job explains the sequence in full, including how provisional registration, F1 allocation, and the national application process interact — information this guide does not repeat in depth, since the focus here is specifically on how that pathway plays out differently once a doctor is allocated to Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland rather than England.

Specialty Training Recruitment: Mostly UK-Wide, Administered Locally

Specialty training recruitment for most programmes also runs through UK-wide recruitment processes, meaning a doctor can typically apply for a specialty training post advertised across all four nations through a single national application round for that specialty, rather than four separate nation-specific competitions. Once appointed, however, the doctor's training programme, rotations, educational supervision, and day-to-day administration are managed by whichever nation's deanery or training body they have joined — NES in Scotland, HEIW in Wales, NIMDTA in Northern Ireland, or the relevant NHS England regional structure in England.

This split matters practically for both UK graduates and IMGs planning ahead: the application stage is often genuinely UK-wide (so ranking a preference for a Scottish, Welsh, or Northern Irish region is usually possible within the same national application, rather than requiring a wholly separate process), but life inside the training programme afterwards is shaped by nation-specific administration, local rotation patterns, and the particular deanery's own processes for things like study leave approval, exam funding support, and inter-deanery transfers. A doctor considering, or already holding, a specialty training number who wants to move between nations partway through training should expect an inter-deanery transfer process — a genuinely nation-specific administrative step, distinct from the initial UK-wide application.

What This Means in Practice for IMGs

For IMGs specifically, postgraduate training administration differences are usually most relevant after a locally employed doctor (LED) post or an initial specialty training appointment, rather than at the point of GMC registration itself — registration remains identical regardless of nation, as established above. An IMG who takes an LED post in a Scottish health board, for example, deals with NES/Scotland Deanery structures for any subsequent formal training application, rather than the English NHS regional structure they might have expected from research focused mainly on England. IMGs planning ahead should research the specific deanery or training body attached to whichever nation they are targeting, rather than assuming the English structure applies universally, since nation-specific processes (study leave, out-of-programme approvals, and some funding arrangements) can differ in the details even where headline national recruitment is shared. For UK Medical Students: Ranking Foundation and Specialty Preferences Across Nations

UK medical students approaching the national Foundation Programme application, or later a specialty training application, are typically asked to rank preferences across a list that spans all four nations, not just English regions. A student ranking a Scottish foundation school above an English one, for example, is making exactly the same kind of preference decision as ranking one English region above another — the application mechanics are the same, and the eligibility criteria do not change based on nation. What does change is which deanery or foundation school ends up administering that student's training day-to-day: NES/Scotland Deanery, HEIW/Wales Foundation School, NIMDTA/Northern Ireland Foundation School, or an NHS England regional structure. Students who want the fuller picture of how the UKMLA, provisional registration, and this national application process fit together before making nation preferences should read the site's guide to the UKMLA-to-NHS job pathway, which walks through the sequence UK graduates follow from finals through to their first NHS post.

Revalidation and Appraisal: Centrally Governed, Locally Delivered

Revalidation itself — the recurring process through which the GMC confirms a doctor remains up to date and fit to practise — is a single, UK-wide GMC process, not something that varies by nation in its underlying requirements. What does vary slightly by nation is the specific "responsible officer" and appraisal infrastructure that supports it: NHS Scotland, NHS Wales, and HSC Northern Ireland each run their own local appraisal systems and designated bodies, feeding into the same national revalidation framework the GMC oversees centrally. A doctor moving between nations partway through a revalidation cycle should expect to be linked to a new responsible officer at their new employer, but the underlying five-year revalidation cycle and its evidence requirements remain governed by the same GMC-wide rules regardless of which nation that employer sits in. The site's guide to the GMC revalidation process explains the evidence requirements and appraisal cycle in full detail, and applies identically to a doctor's revalidation obligations whether their responsible officer sits in England, Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland.

Clinical Guidance Bodies: Where Devolved Health Policy Genuinely Differs

Health policy and some clinical guidance arrangements are devolved matters, meaning genuine differences exist between nations beyond employer structure and training administration — the clearest example is that NICE guidance formally applies to England and Wales, while Scotland runs its own parallel system through the Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (SIGN) and Healthcare Improvement Scotland. This is worth stating plainly because it is the one area where "practising medicine is basically the same everywhere in the UK" genuinely needs qualifying — devolved health policy is real, and a doctor moving between nations should not assume every piece of clinical guidance, drug approval decision, or service specification is identical UK-wide.

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) produces evidence-based guidance and quality standards that formally apply in England, with an agreement in place so that NICE guidelines and quality standards are also made available for use in Wales (though not all NICE products are mandatory there). Scotland, by contrast, has never operated under the same NICE arrangement in the same way — Scotland has its own guideline-development body, SIGN, and Healthcare Improvement Scotland plays the equivalent national role in evaluating and implementing evidence-based guidance and health technology decisions for NHS Scotland. Northern Ireland has its own arrangements too, drawing on NICE, SIGN, and locally adapted guidance depending on the topic, rather than a single equivalent body mirroring either England or Scotland exactly.

The practical takeaway for doctors is not that clinical standards diverge wildly between nations — the underlying evidence base, and the GMC's overarching expectations under Good Medical Practice, are consistent everywhere — but that the specific guideline documents, drug formularies, and service specifications a doctor is expected to follow day-to-day can differ by nation. A clinician moving from an English trust to a Scottish health board, for example, should expect local induction to cover which guidance documents apply locally, since the answer is not always "the same NICE guideline you used in England." This is precisely the kind of detail that should always be confirmed through current local induction and the relevant national guidance body's own published position, rather than assumed, since devolved health policy can and does change.

Why This Rarely Affects Registration or Day-to-Day Competence

It is worth being explicit that none of this devolved variation in guidance bodies changes a doctor's GMC registration, licence to practise, or their fundamental clinical competence requirements — those remain governed by the GMC UK-wide, as set out earlier in this guide. What changes is the specific reference guidance a doctor consults for a given condition or treatment decision in a given nation, which is a matter of local clinical practice and induction, not of registration or regulatory status. Doctors relocating between nations should treat this as a "know your local guidance" adjustment during induction, similar in spirit to learning a new trust's local antibiotic formulary — a normal part of moving employer, not a regulatory hurdle.

Practical Relocation Considerations Beyond Registration and Employer Structure

Beyond registration, employer structure, and guidance bodies, doctors considering practising medicine in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland should weigh genuine lifestyle and practical relocation factors, including cost of living differences and the realities of rural or remote practice, which can be more pronounced outside the largest English cities. These considerations do not affect eligibility to work in any of the four nations, but they are frequently the deciding factor for doctors — both UK graduates ranking Foundation Programme or specialty training preferences, and IMGs weighing up where to build a career — once the regulatory and employer-structure questions have been answered.

Cost of Living and Location

Cities such as Glasgow, Edinburgh, Cardiff, and Belfast are generally recognised as offering a lower cost of living than London and much of South East England, though costs vary considerably by specific city and neighbourhood within each nation, just as they do across England. Doctors relocating for a post outside England sometimes find this a meaningful practical advantage, particularly early in a career when salary levels are broadly similar UK-wide (NHS pay structures follow a nationally negotiated framework, with some local variation in high-cost-area supplements that mainly apply within England) but living costs can differ substantially. The site's guide to how NHS pay scales and banding actually work covers the pay-structure side of this in detail and is a useful companion to any relocation decision, since take-home pay and cost of living need to be considered together rather than pay in isolation.

Rural and Remote Practice

Scotland and Wales, in particular, include substantial rural and remote geography — the Scottish Highlands and Islands, and large parts of rural and West Wales, for example — where medical posts can involve a genuinely different working pattern from a large urban teaching hospital: broader generalist responsibility, less immediate access to some specialist services, and, in some remote Scottish posts, formally recognised "remote and rural" practice arrangements with their own dedicated training and support structures. Doctors who enjoy broader clinical scope, community-embedded practice, and a different pace of life sometimes find these posts genuinely rewarding, while others prefer the sub-specialisation and case volume available in larger urban centres. Neither is objectively better — the right choice depends entirely on a given doctor's career goals and personal preferences — but it is a real and practical difference worth researching specifically for any post outside a major city, regardless of which of the four nations it is in.

Rural and remote posts in Scotland and Wales are also sometimes easier to secure for doctors earlier in their career or newly arrived in the UK, since these areas can face more persistent recruitment challenges than large teaching hospitals in major cities. This is not a lowering of the eligibility bar in any sense — the same GMC registration and clinical competence requirements apply — but it is a genuine, practical route worth considering for doctors open to a rural or semi-rural working pattern while they build UK clinical experience and references.

Family, Community, and Long-Term Settling Considerations

For IMGs in particular, relocation decisions often involve factors well beyond the job itself — school systems for children, the presence of an existing diaspora community, direct flight connections to a home country, and long-term visa and settlement planning. These considerations do not differ in principle between England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland (UK immigration rules are set nationally, not devolved), but the practical experience of, say, a large South Asian community and international flight connections through a major English city differs from what a doctor might find in a smaller Scottish or Welsh town. IMGs planning a relocation from overseas for the first time, rather than a move between UK nations, should also read the site's practical guide to relocating to the UK, which covers visa, housing, and initial settling-in logistics that apply regardless of which UK nation a doctor is ultimately heading to.

None of these lifestyle and practical factors should be confused with eligibility or registration requirements — as established throughout this guide, a doctor's right to work anywhere in the UK depends on their GMC registration and licence to practise, immigration status where relevant, and the terms of their specific job offer, not on which of the four nations they choose. The considerations in this section are about quality of life and career fit, and are worth researching thoroughly, but they are never a barrier to practising medicine in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland for a doctor who is already appropriately registered and qualified for the specific role. Doctors who are still working through the underlying eligibility requirements themselves, rather than the nation-choice question addressed in this guide, should consult the site's UKMLA eligibility criteria for the full detail on what is required before registration and a UK job become possible at all.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a separate registration to work as a doctor in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland?

No. GMC registration and a licence to practise are valid across the whole of the UK, and there is no separate Scottish, Welsh, or Northern Irish medical registration or licence to apply for. A doctor registered with the GMC can accept a job offer in any of the four nations without any additional regulatory application specific to that nation.

Does my GMC annual retention fee change if I work outside England?

No, the GMC's annual retention fee is set and administered centrally by the GMC and does not vary by which of the four UK nations a doctor is working in. The fee, and the process for paying it, are identical regardless of employer or location within the UK.

Is the UKMLA different for doctors planning to work in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland?

No, the UKMLA is a single UK-wide assessment set by the GMC, and passing it leads to the same GMC registration regardless of which of the four nations a doctor subsequently chooses to work in. There is no nation-specific version of the exam and no additional assessment required to work outside England.

Who is my actual employer if I take an NHS job in Scotland or Wales?

In Scotland, the employer is the specific regional health board (such as NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde or NHS Lothian), not "NHS Scotland" as a single body. In Wales, the employer is similarly the relevant health board or trust, coordinated nationally in part through Health Education and Improvement Wales. In both cases, "NHS Scotland" and "NHS Wales" describe the overall system, while the health board or trust is the specific contracting employer.

What is HSC and how does it differ from "NHS Northern Ireland"?

HSC stands for Health and Social Care, the official term for Northern Ireland's combined health and social care system, structured around five HSC Trusts (Belfast, Northern, South Eastern, Southern, and Western) that directly employ medical staff. Northern Ireland does not use the "NHS" branding in the same way as England, Scotland, and Wales, reflecting its integrated health-and-social-care structure rather than a separate regulatory or registration difference.

If I train in Scotland, can I later transfer to specialty training in England, or vice versa?

Yes, doctors can move between nations during specialty training through an inter-deanery transfer process, since specialty training recruitment for most programmes is coordinated UK-wide even though day-to-day administration sits with each nation's own deanery or training body. The specific transfer process and eligibility windows are set out by the relevant training bodies and should be checked directly with the deanery or training programme involved, since arrangements can vary by specialty and by year.

Does NICE guidance apply if I work in Scotland?

Not automatically in the same way as in England and Wales — Scotland operates its own parallel guideline system through the Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (SIGN) and Healthcare Improvement Scotland, rather than being bound by NICE guidance by default. Doctors moving to a Scottish health board should confirm through local induction which guidance documents apply in their specific clinical setting, rather than assuming an NICE guideline used previously in England still applies unchanged.

Is it harder for an IMG to get a job in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland compared with England?

Not inherently — the same GMC registration, right-to-work, and visa requirements apply regardless of which UK nation an IMG is applying to work in, and vacancies exist across all four nations. Some IMGs find rural or remote posts in Scotland and Wales specifically easier to secure due to recruitment challenges in those areas, though the right choice of location depends on individual career goals, family circumstances, and personal preference rather than any difference in eligibility.

Conclusion

Practising medicine in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland does not require a doctor to clear any additional regulatory hurdle beyond the GMC registration and licence to practise that already apply UK-wide — the GMC's single, four-nation regulatory model means a doctor's registration status is identical no matter which of the four nations they work in. What genuinely changes is practical rather than regulatory: the employing health board or trust, the postgraduate deanery or training body responsible for local training administration, some devolved clinical guidance arrangements such as NICE versus SIGN, and lifestyle factors like cost of living and rural practice opportunities. For both UK medical students weighing up Foundation Programme and specialty training preferences, and IMGs planning where in the UK to build a career, the decision about which nation to work in should be approached as a question of employer, training environment, and personal fit — not as a question of eligibility, since eligibility is settled the moment GMC registration is granted.
