Medical statistics UKMLA questions rarely ask you to calculate anything from scratch — they ask you to recognise what a number means and apply it to a clinical decision in under 90 seconds. A single-best-answer item might give you a sensitivity and specificity, a relative risk reduction, or a p-value buried in a results table, and expect you to know instantly whether that finding changes what you do for the patient in front of you. This guide sets out the statistics and evidence-based medicine (EBM) concepts that appear most often in the Applied Knowledge Test, translated into the practical, exam-ready form you actually need — not a full biostatistics course, but the working knowledge that turns an unfamiliar-looking data question into a fast, confident answer.

Both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) consistently rate statistics and critical appraisal as one of the least intuitive AKT domains, mostly because undergraduate teaching on the subject is often theoretical and delivered years before the exam. The good news is that the AKT tests a narrow, predictable set of concepts repeatedly: diagnostic test performance, measures of treatment effect, the hierarchy of study designs, the practical meaning of p-values and confidence intervals, and the basics of bias and confounding. Master these five areas and you will comfortably handle the large majority of statistics-flavoured questions you encounter.

This article works through each concept with the same worked-example approach used elsewhere on this site for interpreting blood test and investigation data, because the underlying exam skill is identical: recognise a pattern, apply a pre-learned framework, and select the answer consistent with the stem — without re-deriving the underlying maths from first principles under time pressure.

Quick Answer: What Medical Statistics Topics Does the UKMLA AKT Actually Test?

Medical statistics UKMLA revision boils down to a short, predictable list, since the AKT tests medical statistics almost exclusively through applied, clinical-context questions rather than abstract calculation. The topics that recur most often are: sensitivity, specificity, positive and negative predictive value; relative risk, absolute risk reduction and number needed to treat; the relative strength of evidence of different study designs; the practical interpretation of p-values and confidence intervals; and recognising common sources of bias and confounding in a described study. You are far more likely to be asked "what does this result tell you about this patient" than "calculate this from first principles," although simple two-by-two table calculations do appear and are worth being fluent in.

Because these questions are usually wrapped in a short vignette — a results table, a trial abstract, or a screening scenario — the fastest route to a correct answer is a consistent framework applied every time: identify what type of statistic is being described, recall its practical meaning, and match that meaning to the clinical question being asked. The sections below build exactly that framework, one concept at a time.

Sensitivity, Specificity, and Predictive Values

Diagnostic test performance is one of the most heavily tested medical statistics UKMLA topics, and it is testable almost entirely through a single tool: the two-by-two table. Every sensitivity/specificity question can be reduced to the same four cells — true positive, false positive, false negative, true negative — and once you can build that table from a clinical vignette, the calculations themselves are simple arithmetic.

Building the Two-by-Two Table

Disease PresentDisease Absent
Test PositiveTrue Positive (TP)False Positive (FP)
Test NegativeFalse Negative (FN)True Negative (TN)

From this table, four core statistics are derived, each answering a different clinical question:

  • Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN) — of everyone who actually has the disease, what proportion does the test correctly identify? A highly sensitive test is useful for ruling out disease when negative (mnemonic: SnNOut).
  • Specificity = TN / (TN + FP) — of everyone who does not have the disease, what proportion does the test correctly identify as negative? A highly specific test is useful for ruling in disease when positive (mnemonic: SpPIn).
  • Positive predictive value (PPV) = TP / (TP + FP) — given a positive result, what is the probability the patient actually has the disease?
  • Negative predictive value (NPV) = TN / (TN + FN) — given a negative result, what is the probability the patient truly does not have the disease?

The single most exam-relevant fact in this whole section is this: sensitivity and specificity are fixed properties of the test itself (assuming the same population and test threshold), whereas PPV and NPV vary with disease prevalence. This distinction is tested repeatedly, often as the "trick" that separates the correct answer from a plausible distractor.

Why Prevalence Changes Predictive Value but Not Sensitivity or Specificity

Consider a screening test with 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity used first in a high-prevalence population (a specialist clinic where 50% of patients truly have the condition) and then in a low-prevalence population (a general screening programme where only 1% of the population is affected). The sensitivity and specificity remain 95% in both settings — they are intrinsic to the test. However, the PPV collapses in the low-prevalence setting: because so few people in the screened population actually have the disease, even a small false-positive rate generates far more false positives than true positives in absolute numbers, so a positive result is far less likely to be a true positive. This is exactly why national screening programmes always pair an initial test with a confirmatory second test — a concept explored in more depth in our guide to screening programme principles and population health, since population-level test performance and individual diagnostic reasoning rely on the same underlying mathematics but are applied differently.

A useful AKT shortcut: if a question describes a test being used to screen a low-risk, asymptomatic population, expect the correct answer to hinge on a lower PPV than intuition suggests, even for a test with impressively high sensitivity and specificity. Conversely, in a high-prevalence, symptomatic population (for example, testing patients already presenting with a compatible clinical picture), the same test will have a substantially higher PPV.

Likelihood Ratios: The Underused AKT Shortcut

Likelihood ratios combine sensitivity and specificity into a single number describing how much a test result shifts the probability of disease, and they appear with increasing frequency in AKT questions because they map directly onto real clinical reasoning. The positive likelihood ratio (LR+) = sensitivity / (1 − specificity) tells you how much more likely a positive result is in a person with the disease compared with a person without it; a value well above 1 (typically greater than 10) is considered strong evidence in favour of disease. The negative likelihood ratio (LR−) = (1 − sensitivity) / specificity tells you how much a negative result reduces the probability of disease; a value close to 0 (typically below 0.1) is strong evidence against disease. You are not usually expected to calculate a post-test probability using Bayes' theorem in full under exam conditions, but recognising that a very high LR+ or very low LR− makes a test result clinically decisive — regardless of the underlying prevalence — is a frequently tested concept.

Relative Risk, Absolute Risk, and Number Needed to Treat

Measures of treatment effect form the second major pillar of medical statistics in the UKMLA AKT, and they are tested because they underpin one of the most important skills in evidence-based practice: correctly judging how much benefit a treatment actually provides, rather than being misled by an impressive-sounding percentage.

Relative Risk and Relative Risk Reduction

Relative risk (RR) is the risk of an outcome in the intervention group divided by the risk of that outcome in the control group. An RR of 1.0 indicates no difference between groups; an RR below 1.0 indicates the intervention reduces the outcome (protective); an RR above 1.0 indicates the intervention increases the outcome (harmful). The relative risk reduction (RRR) is calculated as (1 − RR) × 100%, and it is the figure most often quoted in media headlines and drug marketing because it tends to look larger and more dramatic than the equivalent absolute figure.

Absolute Risk Reduction: The Number That Matters Most to an Individual Patient

The absolute risk reduction (ARR) is simply the difference between the event rate in the control group and the event rate in the treatment group. Unlike relative risk, which can make a tiny real-world benefit sound impressive (a drug that reduces risk from 2 in 10,000 to 1 in 10,000 has an RRR of 50%, which sounds substantial, but an ARR of only 0.0001), the absolute risk reduction reflects the true size of benefit a patient can expect. AKT questions frequently present both a relative and an absolute figure for the same trial and test whether you can correctly identify which one is more clinically meaningful for shared decision-making with an individual patient — the answer is almost always the absolute figure.

Number Needed to Treat (NNT) and Number Needed to Harm (NNH)

The number needed to treat is calculated as 1 / ARR (with ARR expressed as a decimal, not a percentage), and it answers a highly practical clinical question: how many patients must receive this treatment for one additional patient to benefit, compared with the control? A lower NNT indicates a more effective treatment — an NNT of 5 means one extra good outcome for every five patients treated, whereas an NNT of 200 means the treatment benefits far fewer of the people who receive it. The equivalent concept for adverse effects is the number needed to harm (NNH), calculated the same way using the absolute risk increase for a harmful outcome instead of a beneficial one. A treatment with both a low NNT and a low NNH requires careful individualised discussion of the trade-off, whereas a low NNT paired with a high NNH represents a clearly favourable risk-benefit profile.

A frequently tested variant asks you to distinguish odds ratio from relative risk. The odds ratio (OR) compares the odds of an event (event occurring divided by event not occurring) between two groups, rather than the risk (event occurring divided by total number at risk). Odds ratios are the standard effect measure in case-control studies, where the true incidence of disease in the population cannot be directly calculated because cases and controls are selected by the researcher rather than followed prospectively — this is precisely why case-control designs report odds ratios rather than relative risk. When the outcome being studied is rare (generally under 10% incidence), the odds ratio closely approximates the relative risk, and this approximation is itself a testable AKT fact.

Study Designs and Their Relative Strength of Evidence

Ranking study designs by strength of evidence is a core plank of medical statistics UKMLA revision, and recognising study design, and ranking designs by their strength of evidence, is one of the most consistently tested critical-appraisal skills in the AKT. A well-prepared candidate should be able to read a one- or two-sentence description of a study's methodology and correctly identify which design it describes within a few seconds, since the design itself determines what conclusions can and cannot be safely drawn from the results.

Randomised Controlled Trials (RCTs)

In a randomised controlled trial, participants are randomly allocated to receive either the intervention being studied or a comparator (placebo or standard care), and outcomes are then compared between groups. Randomisation is the single most powerful tool in clinical research because, done properly, it distributes both known and unknown confounding variables evenly between groups, meaning any difference in outcome can more confidently be attributed to the intervention itself rather than to underlying differences between the groups. RCTs are considered the strongest form of evidence for questions about the effectiveness of an intervention, and this is why NICE and other guideline bodies weight RCT evidence most heavily when formulating recommendations — a principle also discussed in our guide to the AKT exam pattern, since applying NICE-derived management decisions correctly depends on understanding the evidence base behind them.

Cohort Studies

A cohort study follows a group of people over time, comparing outcomes between those exposed to a particular factor (a risk factor, an environmental exposure, a treatment received in routine care) and those who are not exposed, without the researcher assigning exposure. Cohort studies can be prospective (the cohort is identified and then followed forward in time) or retrospective (existing records are used to reconstruct exposure and outcome after the fact). Cohort studies are well suited to studying rare exposures or long-term outcomes and can calculate incidence and relative risk directly, but because exposure is not randomly assigned, they remain vulnerable to confounding — people who choose or experience a particular exposure often differ systematically from those who do not.

Case-Control Studies

A case-control study works in the opposite direction to a cohort study: it starts by identifying a group of people who already have the outcome of interest (cases) and a comparable group who do not (controls), then looks backward to compare the frequency of a prior exposure between the two groups. Case-control studies are efficient and relatively inexpensive, making them well suited to studying rare diseases, but they are more susceptible to recall bias (cases may remember or report past exposures differently from controls) and cannot calculate incidence, since the proportion of cases and controls is fixed by the researcher rather than reflecting true population frequency. As above, this is why case-control studies report an odds ratio rather than relative risk.

Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

A systematic review uses a pre-specified, reproducible methodology to identify, appraise, and synthesise all available evidence addressing a particular clinical question. When the included studies are sufficiently similar in design and outcome measures, their results can be statistically pooled into a meta-analysis, producing a single combined effect estimate with a narrower confidence interval than any individual study could achieve alone. Because a well-conducted systematic review incorporates multiple RCTs (when available) and explicitly assesses the quality and heterogeneity of the underlying evidence, it sits at the top of most evidence hierarchies — although a systematic review is only as strong as the studies it includes, and a review built primarily from low-quality observational studies does not outrank a single well-conducted RCT.

Case Reports, Case Series, and Expert Opinion

At the lower end of the evidence hierarchy sit case reports (a detailed description of a single patient), case series (a description of a small group of patients with a similar presentation or exposure, without a comparison group), and expert opinion or narrative reviews not based on a systematic search methodology. These designs are valuable for generating hypotheses, describing genuinely novel presentations, or summarising practical experience, but they cannot establish causation because there is no control group and no attempt to account for confounding or bias — they are frequently the correct answer when a question asks which type of evidence is weakest, or which design is appropriate only for a very rare or newly described condition.

P-Values and Confidence Intervals: A Practical Interpretation

Statistical significance is one of the most frequently misunderstood concepts among candidates, largely because the technically correct definition of a p-value is genuinely counter-intuitive. For AKT purposes, and for medical statistics UKMLA revision generally, a practical, clinically usable interpretation is both sufficient and more useful than the full theoretical definition.

What a P-Value Actually Tells You

The p-value is the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the one found in the study, if the null hypothesis (that there is truly no difference between groups) were actually true. By longstanding convention, a p-value below 0.05 is considered "statistically significant," meaning the observed result would be unlikely to occur by chance alone if there were genuinely no underlying difference — but a p-value does not tell you the size of an effect, and it does not tell you the probability that the null hypothesis is true. A very large study can return a statistically significant p-value for a clinically trivial difference, and a smaller or underpowered study can fail to reach significance despite a clinically important effect existing in reality. The AKT frequently tests exactly this distinction: a question describing a "statistically significant" finding with a tiny effect size is testing whether you can correctly say the result is unlikely to be clinically meaningful, despite technically being significant.

Confidence Intervals: Precision, Not Just Presence, of an Effect

A confidence interval (CI) gives a range of values within which the true population effect is likely to lie, based on the sample data collected, and it conveys information a single p-value cannot: the precision of the estimate. A 95% confidence interval is conventionally interpreted as meaning that if the study were repeated many times, 95% of the calculated intervals would contain the true population value. A narrow confidence interval indicates a more precise estimate (typically from a larger sample size), while a wide confidence interval indicates greater uncertainty, often from a smaller sample.

The single most exam-relevant confidence interval rule is this: for a relative risk or odds ratio, if the 95% confidence interval crosses 1.0, the result is not statistically significant, because a value of 1.0 represents no difference between groups. For an absolute difference (such as a difference in means, or an absolute risk reduction), if the 95% confidence interval crosses 0, the result is not statistically significant, because zero represents no difference. AKT questions often present a forest plot or a results table with confidence intervals and expect you to determine significance directly from whether the interval crosses this reference value, without needing the exact p-value at all — this is, in fact, a faster and more reliable approach than trying to estimate a p-value visually.

Type I and Type II Errors

A Type I error (a "false positive" in the statistical sense) occurs when a study concludes there is a real difference between groups when, in truth, there is none — the probability of this is denoted by alpha, conventionally set at 0.05. A Type II error (a "false negative" in the statistical sense) occurs when a study fails to detect a real difference that does genuinely exist, often because the study was underpowered — too small a sample size to reliably detect the true effect. The power of a study is the probability of correctly detecting a true effect if one exists, and is calculated as 1 minus the probability of a Type II error; conventionally, studies aim for at least 80% power. When an AKT question describes a "negative" trial result with a small sample size, the correct interpretation is often that the study may simply have been underpowered to detect a genuine difference, rather than proof that no difference exists.

Bias, Confounding, and Critical Appraisal Basics

The final major pillar of medical statistics UKMLA knowledge tested in the AKT is the ability to spot flaws in a described study — recognising when a result might be misleading because of how the study was designed or conducted, rather than because the underlying clinical relationship does not exist.

Selection Bias

Selection bias occurs when the way participants are recruited into a study means the sample is not representative of the population the researchers intend to generalise to. Common exam-testable examples include recruiting participants only from a specialist referral centre (over-representing more severe or unusual cases), or a low response rate to a survey (respondents may differ systematically from non-respondents). Selection bias undermines the external validity of a study's conclusions even if the internal analysis is statistically sound.

Recall Bias

Recall bias is particularly relevant to case-control studies, where participants are asked to remember past exposures. People who have experienced an adverse outcome (cases) often recall or report past exposures more thoroughly or differently than unaffected controls, simply because they have spent more time searching for an explanation for their illness. This systematically distorts the measured association between exposure and outcome, usually inflating it.

Confounding

A confounding variable is a factor that is independently associated with both the exposure being studied and the outcome, and which can therefore create or mask an apparent association between the two even when no true causal relationship exists. The classic teaching example is the association between carrying a cigarette lighter and lung cancer: lighter-carrying does not cause cancer, but smoking (the true confounder) is strongly associated with both carrying a lighter and developing lung cancer, creating a spurious statistical association. AKT questions test confounding by describing a plausible-sounding association and asking you to identify the most likely confounding variable, or by asking which study design (randomisation in an RCT) best controls for confounding, known and unknown, by design rather than by statistical adjustment after the fact.

Publication Bias and Lead-Time Bias

Publication bias arises because studies with positive, statistically significant, or novel findings are more likely to be submitted and accepted for publication than studies with negative or null results, which can skew the overall published evidence base — and by extension, any systematic review or meta-analysis built from it — toward overstating a true effect. Lead-time bias is a specific and frequently tested concept in the context of cancer screening: it describes the apparent improvement in survival time that occurs simply because a screening test detects a disease earlier in its natural course, even if the screening does not actually change the date of death. A patient whose cancer is detected three years earlier by screening will appear to "survive longer from diagnosis," even if their actual date of death is unchanged — a distinction covered in more depth in the discussion of screening principles in our guide to public health and screening in the UKMLA, since lead-time bias is one of the most important reasons screening programmes must demonstrate a genuine reduction in disease-specific mortality, not merely improved apparent survival statistics.

Blinding and Allocation Concealment

Blinding (masking) prevents participants, clinicians, or outcome assessors from knowing which study arm a participant has been allocated to, reducing the risk that knowledge of group allocation influences reported outcomes or clinical management. A study can be single-blind (participants only), double-blind (participants and investigators), or occasionally triple-blind (participants, investigators, and the statisticians analysing the data). Allocation concealment is a related but distinct concept, referring to ensuring that the person enrolling a participant into a trial cannot predict or influence which group the next participant will be allocated to — this protects the integrity of randomisation itself, whereas blinding protects the integrity of outcome assessment after allocation has already occurred. AKT questions sometimes test whether you can distinguish these two related but separate safeguards, since a trial can have excellent allocation concealment but poor blinding, or vice versa.

How These Concepts Combine in a Typical AKT Question

In practice, a real medical statistics UKMLA item rarely tests one statistical concept in isolation — it typically combines a study design description with a results table and asks you to draw a conclusion that depends on correctly reading both together. A representative example: a vignette describes a double-blind RCT comparing a new antihypertensive against placebo, reports a relative risk reduction of major cardiovascular events of 30% with a 95% confidence interval that does not cross 1.0, but also states the absolute risk reduction is 0.4%. The question then asks for the number needed to treat. Working through this systematically — confirming the result is statistically significant from the confidence interval, then calculating NNT as 1 divided by 0.004, giving an NNT of 250 — mirrors exactly the kind of layered reasoning the exam rewards, and is the same "recognise pattern, apply framework" approach used throughout AKT preparation, including for topics as different as reading a patient's blood results or working through an unfamiliar clinical stem generally, as discussed in our broader guide to the AKT exam pattern.

A second common question format presents two studies addressing the same clinical question — often one RCT and one observational cohort study with contradictory findings — and asks which result should be trusted more, or why the two studies might have produced different answers. The expected reasoning is almost always that the RCT's randomisation better controls for confounding, and that the cohort study's discordant result may reflect an unmeasured confounder rather than a true difference in treatment effect. Being able to state this reasoning explicitly, rather than just "knowing" the RCT is stronger evidence, is what separates a confident answer from a guessed one.

A third recurring pattern tests standard-setting and result interpretation rather than trial statistics directly — for example, a question describing how a pass mark for an examination is determined by panel judgement rather than a fixed percentage. This is a genuinely different statistical concept from the treatment-effect and diagnostic-accuracy material covered above, and candidates who conflate the two often lose easy marks; our dedicated guide to how the Angoff method sets an examination pass mark covers this specific topic in full, since it is tested as its own discrete AKT area rather than as an extension of EBM statistics.

Common Pitfalls When Answering Statistics Questions Under Time Pressure

Several recurring error patterns account for a disproportionate share of marks lost on medical statistics UKMLA questions, and being aware of them in advance is often enough to avoid them on exam day.

  • Confusing sensitivity with PPV. Many candidates default to reading "high sensitivity" as "a positive result is probably correct," when in fact a highly sensitive test's strength lies in ruling disease out when negative, not confirming it when positive — that is the role of specificity and PPV.
  • Treating relative risk reduction as automatically clinically important. An impressive-sounding RRR paired with a tiny ARR (and therefore a very high NNT) may represent a marginal real-world benefit; the question is often designed specifically to test whether you notice this.
  • Assuming a "non-significant" result proves no effect exists. A wide confidence interval crossing the null value more often reflects an underpowered study (a Type II error risk) than genuine proof of no difference — particularly in smaller trials.
  • Forgetting that case-control studies cannot calculate relative risk. If a question's answer options include "relative risk" and "odds ratio" for a study described as case-control, the odds ratio is virtually always correct, since incidence cannot be determined from a case-control design.
  • Overlooking the difference between statistical and clinical significance. A p-value below 0.05 tells you a result is unlikely to be due to chance; it does not, by itself, tell you whether the effect size is large enough to change practice for an individual patient.

Medical Statistics UKMLA Revision for UK Medical Students vs IMGs

UK medical students typically encounter statistics and EBM teaching integrated across several years of an integrated curriculum, often through critical appraisal exercises tied to specific modules, which can leave the underlying concepts feeling scattered rather than consolidated into one clear exam-ready framework by the time AKT revision begins — the priority for this group is usually less about learning new material and more about actively consolidating and drilling application under timed conditions using SBA-style question banks.

IMGs, in contrast, may have trained under an assessment system that placed less emphasis on critical appraisal as a distinct, separately examined skill, or may have learned the concepts in a different language and be encountering the specific English terminology (number needed to treat, absolute risk reduction, confounding) for the first time in AKT preparation materials. For this group, building a small glossary of the exact terms used above, alongside their formulas, and drilling recognition of study design descriptions in isolation before combining them with results-interpretation questions, tends to be the more efficient route. Both groups benefit from working through realistic past-paper-style statistics questions repeatedly rather than re-reading definitions passively, since the exam tests applied recognition under time pressure rather than recall of a textbook definition in isolation.

Frequently Asked Questions About Medical Statistics UKMLA Topics

How much medical statistics content actually appears in the UKMLA AKT?

Medical statistics UKMLA questions typically make up a modest but consistent proportion of the AKT, usually appearing as a handful of dedicated questions per sitting plus additional statistics embedded within clinical vignettes on other topics, such as a results table accompanying a question about screening or treatment choice. It is rarely the single largest content area, but because many candidates find it disproportionately unfamiliar, it is one of the highest-yield areas to revise properly relative to the time invested.

Do I need to memorise the formulas for sensitivity, specificity, NNT, and the rest?

Yes — the underlying formulas (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, relative risk, absolute risk reduction, and number needed to treat) are simple enough to memorise completely and are frequently required to answer a two-by-two table or trial-results question directly, rather than being provided in the question stem. Practising building a two-by-two table from a written clinical description, rather than only memorising the formulas in isolation, is the more exam-relevant skill.

What is the easiest way to remember sensitivity versus specificity?

The mnemonics SnNOut and SpPIn are the most widely used memory aids: a highly Snsitive test, when Negative, helps rule Out disease; a highly Specific test, when Positive, helps rule In disease. Pairing this mnemonic with practice at building the underlying two-by-two table from a clinical scenario, rather than relying on the mnemonic alone, produces more reliable exam performance.

Why do case-control studies report an odds ratio instead of relative risk?

Relative risk requires knowing the true incidence of an outcome in exposed and unexposed groups, which cannot be calculated in a case-control study because the researcher deliberately selects a fixed number of cases and controls rather than following a population forward over time. The odds ratio can still be validly calculated from this design, and when the outcome being studied is rare, the odds ratio closely approximates what the relative risk would have been, which is why the two measures are often discussed together in AKT-style questions.

How do I know if a confidence interval means a result is statistically significant?

For a ratio measure such as relative risk or odds ratio, the result is statistically significant if the 95% confidence interval does not cross 1.0. For an absolute difference measure, such as a mean difference or an absolute risk reduction, the result is statistically significant if the 95% confidence interval does not cross 0. This rule lets you determine significance directly from a results table or forest plot without needing the exact p-value.

Why is a systematic review considered stronger evidence than a single RCT?

A well-conducted systematic review and meta-analysis pools data from multiple studies, typically producing a larger effective sample size, a narrower confidence interval, and a more precise overall effect estimate than any single trial could achieve alone, while also explicitly assessing the quality and consistency of the underlying evidence base. This is why systematic reviews of RCTs sit above individual RCTs in most evidence hierarchies, including the version most commonly referenced in AKT preparation — although a systematic review's strength is entirely dependent on the quality of the studies it includes.

What is the difference between a Type I error and a Type II error?

A Type I error is a false-positive conclusion — a study wrongly concludes there is a real difference between groups when none truly exists — and its probability is denoted by the significance threshold, alpha, conventionally 0.05. A Type II error is a false-negative conclusion — a study fails to detect a real difference that genuinely exists, often because the sample size was too small to provide adequate statistical power, conventionally set at a minimum of 80%.

Conclusion

Success with medical statistics UKMLA questions comes down to recognising a small, recurring set of concepts and applying them consistently, rather than trying to derive answers from first principles under exam pressure. Build fluency with the two-by-two table and its four derived statistics, understand why absolute measures of risk matter more to individual patients than relative ones, memorise the evidence hierarchy from systematic reviews down to expert opinion, learn to read significance directly from a confidence interval, and practise spotting the common forms of bias and confounding described in exam vignettes. Combined with focused question-bank practice, this framework will let you approach statistics and evidence-based medicine questions with the same confidence as any core clinical topic on the exam.
