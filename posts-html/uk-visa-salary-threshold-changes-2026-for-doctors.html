The UK visa salary threshold 2026 rules changed in ways that genuinely matter for internationally trained doctors holding, or about to apply for, Skilled Worker sponsorship — not because the headline numbers moved dramatically, but because how those numbers are checked changed. This article does not repeat the general explanation of what the Health and Care Worker visa is or how the application process works — the site's existing general guide to the UK visa for international doctors already covers eligibility, the Certificate of Sponsorship mechanism, dependants, and settlement in detail. Instead, this post focuses narrowly and specifically on what actually changed in 2026: the new pay-period compliance rule that took effect on 8 April 2026, the transitional £31,300 threshold still available to some existing visa holders, and the planned withdrawal of the Immigration Salary List in December 2026.

Most doctors reading this will be relieved to learn that, for the overwhelming majority of NHS-employed doctors on standard NHS pay scales, none of these changes create a practical problem. The doctors who genuinely need to read this carefully are a smaller group: those on part-time, locum, or non-standard contracts, those whose pay sits close to a threshold boundary, and those relying on the Immigration Salary List's flexibilities for a role that is about to lose them. The sections below explain exactly who falls into each category, what the current figures actually are, and what a doctor or their sponsoring Trust needs to check before assuming "it'll be fine."

As with all UK immigration content, a standing caveat applies throughout: rules, thresholds, and fees are reviewed and amended by the Home Office relatively frequently, sometimes with limited notice, and this article is not legal or immigration advice. Every figure below should be verified directly on gov.uk's Skilled Worker visa pages, the NHS Employers guidance on the Health and Care Worker visa salary threshold, or with a sponsoring Trust's HR and international recruitment team before any decision is made.

Quick Answer: What Changed in the UK Visa Salary Threshold for 2026?

The UK visa salary threshold 2026 for the general Skilled Worker visa is £41,700 a year, or the specific "going rate" published for the applicant's occupation code, whichever is higher. Most NHS doctors, however, do not use the general route at all — they are sponsored through the Health and Care Worker visa, a dedicated sub-category with its own, lower thresholds: £25,000 a year, or £12.82 an hour, or the specific going rate for the role, whichever is highest. For doctors employed on national NHS pay scales, the salary must meet the relevant published NHS pay band for their grade rather than either of these generic figures.

What genuinely changed in 2026 is not primarily the headline numbers — both thresholds had already been set before the year began — but a new compliance mechanism. From 8 April 2026, sponsors must ensure a sponsored worker's pay meets the relevant threshold in every individual pay period (monthly, or whatever cycle applies under the contract), rather than simply averaging out correctly across the year. A second, separate change is scheduled for December 2026: the Immigration Salary List, which currently gives limited flexibility on thresholds for certain shortage roles, is due to be withdrawn, after which those flexibilities largely disappear. A third, smaller detail affects only a subset of existing visa holders: a transitional salary threshold of £31,300 may still apply when extending a visa granted before certain earlier cut-off dates, but only until 1 December 2026 in most cases. The remainder of this guide unpacks each of these three changes in turn, explains who they actually affect, and sets out a practical checklist for doctors and Trust HR teams alike.

The General Skilled Worker Threshold vs the Doctor-Specific Health and Care Worker Threshold

Before examining what changed, it is worth being precise about which threshold applies to which doctor, since conflating the two is one of the most common sources of confusion among IMGs researching the UK visa salary threshold 2026 rules online.

The General Skilled Worker Visa: £41,700

The general Skilled Worker visa — the route used across most UK industries, not specifically healthcare — carries a standard salary threshold of £41,700 a year. An applicant sponsored under this general route must be paid at least the higher of this general threshold or the specific "going rate" published for their occupation's Standard Occupation Classification (SOC) code, whichever figure is greater for their particular role. A minimum hourly rate also applies alongside the annual figure, calculated against a standard working week, so that a role cannot satisfy the threshold on paper while genuinely underpaying an employee working unusually long hours.

In practice, very few doctors are sponsored through this general route, because a dedicated, more favourable route exists specifically for healthcare professionals. Understanding the general threshold still matters, though, since it is the figure most commonly (and often incorrectly) quoted in generic immigration commentary that doctors encounter when researching "UK visa salary threshold 2026" without realising a separate, lower figure applies to their own profession.

The Health and Care Worker Visa: £25,000, £12.82 an Hour, or the Going Rate

The Health and Care Worker visa is a variant of the Skilled Worker visa built specifically for doctors, nurses, and a defined list of other health and social care professionals. Its salary threshold is set considerably lower than the general route's: an applicant must be paid at least £25,000 a year, or £12.82 an hour, or the specific going rate published for their occupation code — whichever of these three figures is highest for the role in question. This structure exists because healthcare roles, particularly at entry level, would otherwise struggle to clear the general £41,700 threshold despite being genuinely skilled, safety-critical positions that the NHS depends on filling internationally.

For roles that sit on national NHS pay scales — which includes the overwhelming majority of NHS-employed doctors, from Foundation Year trainees through to consultants — none of these three generic figures is actually what gets checked. Instead, the applicant's salary must meet the relevant published NHS pay band for their specific grade. In practice, this means the going-rate figure for doctors and dentists sponsored through an NHS Trust ranges roughly from the low-£30,000s for foundation-year doctors up to well over £100,000 for senior consultant posts, depending on grade, banding supplements, and specialty. Because these NHS pay scales are already set, published, and reviewed independently of the general immigration salary thresholds, a doctor employed on a standard NHS contract is, in the great majority of cases, comfortably clear of both the £25,000 general Health and Care Worker floor and the general Skilled Worker figure without either side needing to do anything special.

Why the Distinction Matters in Practice

The practical significance of this distinction is straightforward: a doctor should never assume that a headline figure quoted for "the UK visa salary threshold" applies directly to their own sponsorship case without checking which route and which specific NHS pay band applies to them. A Trust's international recruitment or HR team will confirm the correct going rate for a specific grade and specialty at the point of issuing a Certificate of Sponsorship, and this confirmation should always be treated as more authoritative than a generic figure read in a forum post, a recruitment agency's marketing material, or an older article that predates a threshold uprating. What Actually Changed in 2026: The Pay-Period Compliance Rule

The single most significant change to the UK visa salary threshold 2026 regime is not a change to any headline number at all — it is a change to how compliance with the threshold is measured over time. From 8 April 2026, the Home Office introduced a requirement that sponsors demonstrate a sponsored worker's salary meets the relevant threshold in every individual pay period, rather than relying on an annualised average across the year.

How Salary Compliance Used to Be Assessed

Previously, a Trust could, in effect, satisfy a salary threshold by showing that a worker's total pay across a full year averaged out to at least the required annual figure, even if pay in any single month fell below a strict pro-rata equivalent of that threshold — for example, because of a temporarily reduced working pattern, an unpaid period of leave, or an irregular shift pattern that produced uneven monthly totals. As long as the year-end total cleared the bar, the arrangement was generally treated as compliant.

How Compliance Is Assessed Now

Under the new rule, this averaging approach is no longer sufficient on its own. A sponsor must now be able to show that pay received in each individual pay period — typically each calendar month, or whatever pay cycle is set out in the employment contract — meets or exceeds the minimum amount required for that specific period, calculated against the applicable hourly or annualised threshold and the hours actually worked. For a worker paid monthly, this generally means the Home Office assesses compliance across defined windows (commonly a rolling several-month period) using actual payroll records and recorded working hours, rather than accepting a single annual total as proof after the fact.

In plain terms, this means a Trust can no longer structure someone's pay so that a short-term dip in one month — a period of reduced hours, an unusually short pay period, or a gap between contracts — is simply "made up for" by higher pay in a later month, at least not in a way that satisfies the Home Office's compliance check for the period in which the shortfall actually occurred. The salary genuinely has to clear the bar in every pay period, not just on average across the year.

Why This Was Introduced

The Home Office's stated rationale for this change is to close a compliance gap that had allowed some sponsors, across all sectors (not healthcare specifically), to technically satisfy an annual salary threshold on paper while a sponsored worker's actual monthly income fell short of a fair equivalent for extended periods — a pattern regulators considered inconsistent with the purpose of a minimum salary requirement in the first place. For sponsors managing large numbers of certificates, including NHS Trusts, this shifts the compliance burden from an end-of-year reconciliation exercise to an ongoing, month-by-month payroll discipline.

What This Means for Most NHS-Employed Doctors

For a doctor on a standard, full-time NHS contract with a stable monthly salary that already sits comfortably above the relevant threshold for their grade, this change is, in practical terms, a non-event. Standard NHS pay scales are structured to pay a consistent monthly amount that already clears the applicable threshold every month, so there is no realistic scenario in which a compliant full-time NHS doctor's pay dips below the required figure in any given period. The rule exists to catch edge cases and irregular arrangements, not to create new risk for the typical sponsored doctor working a standard rota on a standard salary.

Who Genuinely Needs to Pay Attention to This Change

Although most doctors can treat the pay-period compliance rule as background information rather than a personal risk, several specific groups should actively check their own arrangements rather than assuming the change does not affect them.

Doctors on Part-Time or Reduced-Hours Contracts

A doctor working part-time, or on a "less than full time" (LTFT) training arrangement, is paid a pro-rata salary calculated against their actual contracted hours. Because the relevant threshold is itself pro-rated against hours worked, a part-time doctor's monthly pay should, in principle, still clear the adjusted threshold for their reduced working pattern. However, doctors moving between different working patterns during a training rotation — for example, increasing or reducing hours between placements — should confirm with their Trust's HR team that payroll has correctly recalculated the applicable threshold for each new working pattern, since a delay in updating pro-rata calculations is exactly the kind of gap the new pay-period rule is designed to catch.

Doctors on Locum, Bank, or Non-Standard Contracts

Doctors working under locum arrangements, bank contracts, or any pay structure where hours and income vary meaningfully from one pay period to the next face genuinely more exposure under the new rule than doctors on a fixed full-time salary. A locum doctor whose hours — and therefore pay — fluctuate from month to month could, in a quiet period, see their pay dip below the threshold for that specific pay period, even if their average annual earnings comfortably clear the bar. Doctors in this position should discuss with their sponsoring Trust or agency exactly how compliance is being monitored under the new rule, since the burden of demonstrating compliance sits with the sponsor, but the consequence of a compliance failure — potential risk to continued sponsorship — falls most heavily on the individual doctor.

Doctors Close to a Threshold Boundary

Any doctor whose salary sits only narrowly above the relevant threshold for their grade — rather than comfortably clear of it — should treat the pay-period rule as a reason for closer attention, not alarm. A doctor a few hundred pounds above the applicable monthly figure has far less margin to absorb an unpaid-leave period, a temporarily reduced on-call supplement, or a payroll administrative delay than a doctor whose pay sits well above the floor. In these cases, it is worth proactively asking HR to confirm, in writing, that the current pay arrangement is structured to clear the threshold in every individual pay period under the new rule, rather than discovering an issue only if a Home Office compliance check is ever triggered.

Doctors Recently Changed to a New Certificate of Sponsorship

Because the new pay-period rule applies specifically to Certificates of Sponsorship issued on or after 8 April 2026, doctors who have recently changed employer, changed role, or had a new certificate issued for any reason should confirm which set of rules applies to their specific certificate. A doctor still working under an older certificate issued before that date may, depending on the specific transitional arrangements in place, be assessed under the previous approach until their certificate is renewed or replaced — but this should be confirmed with HR rather than assumed, since transitional rules of this kind are frequently more nuanced in practice than a single date cut-off suggests.

The Immigration Salary List: What It Currently Does, and Why It Is Being Withdrawn

A second, entirely separate change affects the Immigration Salary List (ISL) — the successor to the previous shortage occupation list — which currently allows certain roles judged to be in short supply to benefit from salary flexibilities not available to occupations outside the list.

What the Immigration Salary List Currently Provides

For an occupation included on the Immigration Salary List, the relevant salary threshold is generally set at a reduced level compared with the standard going rate that would otherwise apply, reflecting the government's judgement that the role is in genuine shortage and that strict threshold enforcement risks making recruitment into that role impractical. Some healthcare support roles have, at various points, appeared on this list, and a small number of allied clinical and care roles continue to benefit from this flexibility as of 2026.

Withdrawal Scheduled for December 2026

The Immigration Salary List is due to be withdrawn, with most roles currently on the list showing a scheduled removal date of 31 December 2026 (a small number of specific care-worker occupation codes have a later removal date, but this does not apply to medical practitioner roles). After withdrawal, the salary flexibilities the list currently provides will no longer apply to new sponsorship cases in the same way, meaning any role that had been relying on an ISL-discounted threshold will, from that point onward, need to satisfy the standard going rate for its occupation code without that discount.

Practical Relevance for Doctors Specifically

For the great majority of NHS-employed doctors, whose salary is already set by national NHS pay scales rather than by reference to the Immigration Salary List's discounted thresholds, this withdrawal has limited direct practical effect — a doctor's pay band already clears the relevant threshold regardless of whether the ISL exists or not. The withdrawal is more directly relevant to certain allied healthcare support roles and to any doctor whose specific role or grade had, unusually, been relying on an ISL-based flexibility rather than the standard NHS pay-scale going rate. Doctors uncertain whether their own role currently benefits from any ISL-related flexibility should ask their Trust's HR or international recruitment team to confirm this directly, since the answer depends on the specific occupation code and grade recorded on the Certificate of Sponsorship, not on the doctor's job title alone. This is one of the clearest examples of why the UK visa salary threshold 2026 framework cannot be reduced to a single number — the correct figure always depends on the specific route, occupation code, and pay scale involved.

The Transitional £31,300 Threshold: Who Can Still Use It

A third, narrower change concerns doctors who were sponsored under earlier immigration rules and are now extending or switching their visa, rather than making a fresh application.

What the Transitional Threshold Covers

Certain sponsored workers who were first granted their visa, or whose Certificate of Sponsorship was first assigned, before specific earlier cut-off dates set by the Home Office may benefit from transitional protections that allow a lower general salary threshold to continue applying when they extend their visa or change employer, rather than being required to meet the current, higher general threshold from scratch. For eligible cases, this transitional general threshold is set at £31,300 — lower than the current £41,700 general Skilled Worker figure, though still higher than the Health and Care Worker visa's own £25,000 floor, which is why this transitional provision matters more for general Skilled Worker cases than for most doctor-specific sponsorship.

Why the Timing Matters

When extending a visa before 1 December 2026, a doctor who qualifies under these transitional arrangements may still be able to rely on the lower £31,300 figure rather than the current general threshold. This window is directly connected to the wider set of 2026 changes described throughout this article, since it runs on broadly the same timeline as the Immigration Salary List's own December 2026 withdrawal — both changes tighten the overall salary framework at a similar point in the year, even though they operate through different mechanisms.

Who Should Actually Check This

This transitional threshold is most relevant to doctors who have held sponsored status for an extended period already, rather than doctors making a first application from overseas, since eligibility depends on the date their sponsorship history began under the relevant earlier rules. A doctor unsure whether they qualify should not assume either way — this is precisely the kind of detail that depends on exact dates recorded on historical Certificates of Sponsorship, and is best confirmed directly with a Trust's HR team or a regulated immigration adviser rather than inferred from a general article.

Who Genuinely Needs to Worry About These 2026 Changes

Bringing the three changes together, it is worth stating plainly which doctors face genuine practical risk from the 2026 salary threshold changes, and which do not — since unnecessary anxiety about immigration rules is its own real cost, and this guide's purpose is to separate signal from noise.

Doctors Who Generally Do Not Need to Worry

  • Full-time NHS doctors on standard national pay-scale contracts, whose monthly salary already clears the relevant threshold for their grade with meaningful margin.
  • Doctors on stable Foundation Programme, core training, specialty training, or consultant contracts with consistent monthly pay and no recent change in working pattern.
  • Doctors applying for a first Health and Care Worker visa from overseas into a standard, full-time NHS post, where the sponsoring Trust's HR team routinely confirms threshold compliance as part of the standard offer process.

Doctors Who Should Actively Check Their Own Position

  • Doctors on part-time, LTFT training, or recently changed working-hours arrangements, where pro-rata threshold recalculation needs to keep pace with each change.
  • Doctors working locum, bank, or otherwise variable-hours arrangements, where monthly pay can genuinely fluctuate below a pay-period-specific threshold even if annual earnings are healthy.
  • Doctors whose salary sits only narrowly above the applicable threshold for their grade, with little margin to absorb an unpaid-leave period or payroll delay.
  • Doctors extending or switching visas around the December 2026 deadline, who may be affected by either the Immigration Salary List withdrawal or the closing transitional £31,300 window, depending on their sponsorship history.

In every one of these cases, the appropriate response is the same: raise the question directly and in writing with the sponsoring Trust's HR or international recruitment team, rather than relying on generic online guidance (including this article) to make a final determination about an individual case. Trusts that sponsor large numbers of international doctors generally have a dedicated international recruitment or immigration compliance team precisely because these calculations require access to exact payroll records, contracted hours, and Certificate of Sponsorship dates that no general guide can substitute for.

Practical Steps for Doctors and Sponsoring Trusts

Beyond simply knowing which category applies, there are concrete, practical steps that reduce risk under the new pay-period compliance regime introduced alongside the UK visa salary threshold 2026 changes.

Keep Personal Payslip Records

Doctors should keep their own copies of every payslip throughout their sponsored employment, independent of whatever records their Trust's payroll department retains. Should a compliance question ever arise — whether initiated by the Home Office, by the Trust itself, or in connection with a future visa extension or indefinite leave to remain application — having an independent, complete personal record considerably simplifies resolving any query quickly.

Query Any Unusually Low Pay Period Immediately

A doctor who notices a pay period where take-home salary looks unusually low — because of an administrative payroll error, a delayed pay increment, an unrecorded change in hours, or any other reason — should query this with HR immediately rather than waiting for the next payslip to "average it out." Under the new rule, a single non-compliant pay period is itself a potential issue, not something a later, higher payment automatically corrects after the fact.

Confirm Threshold Recalculation After Any Change in Hours or Role

Any doctor moving between full-time and part-time work, changing training placement, moving specialty, or receiving a grade change should proactively confirm with HR that the applicable salary threshold has been recalculated correctly for the new arrangement, and that payroll has been updated to reflect it from the correct effective date, rather than assuming the change will be picked up automatically.

Ask About Immigration Salary List Reliance Before December 2026

Doctors uncertain whether their specific role or occupation code currently benefits from an Immigration Salary List flexibility should ask HR to confirm this directly well before the scheduled December 2026 withdrawal, so that any necessary adjustment to salary or role classification can be planned for in advance rather than discovered at the point of a visa renewal.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the UK visa salary threshold for doctors in 2026?

Most NHS doctors are sponsored through the Health and Care Worker visa, which requires a salary of at least £25,000 a year, £12.82 an hour, or the specific going rate for the role, whichever is highest — but doctors on national NHS pay scales must instead meet the relevant published NHS pay band for their grade. The general Skilled Worker visa threshold of £41,700 applies to most other UK industries and is rarely the figure actually used for NHS doctor sponsorship.

Do I need to worry about the new pay-period rule if I work full-time for the NHS?

Generally, no. A full-time NHS doctor on a standard national pay-scale contract with consistent monthly pay already clears the relevant threshold every month with meaningful margin, so the 8 April 2026 pay-period compliance rule does not usually create any practical risk. The rule is primarily relevant to doctors on part-time, locum, bank, or otherwise variable-pay arrangements, where monthly income can genuinely fluctuate.

What happens when the Immigration Salary List is withdrawn in December 2026?

Occupations currently benefiting from a reduced, shortage-based salary threshold under the Immigration Salary List will need to meet the standard going rate for their occupation code once the list is withdrawn, scheduled for 31 December 2026 for most roles. Most NHS doctors are unaffected in practice, since their salary is already set by national NHS pay scales rather than by any Immigration Salary List discount.

Can I still use the lower £31,300 salary threshold in 2026?

Some doctors with sponsorship history predating specific earlier Home Office cut-off dates may still be able to rely on a transitional £31,300 general threshold when extending or changing sponsorship before 1 December 2026, rather than the current £41,700 general figure. Eligibility depends on exact historical Certificate of Sponsorship dates and should be confirmed with a Trust's HR team or a regulated immigration adviser rather than assumed.

Does the salary threshold apply to my basic pay only, or does it include overtime and on-call supplements?

Home Office guidance on salary thresholds generally counts only guaranteed basic gross pay toward the relevant figure, excluding elements such as overtime, discretionary bonuses, and certain allowances that are not guaranteed as part of the base contract. Doctors uncertain which elements of their specific NHS pay package count toward their threshold should ask their Trust's HR team to confirm exactly how their salary is being calculated for sponsorship purposes.

I am on a part-time or less-than-full-time (LTFT) training contract — does the threshold still apply to me?

Yes, but the applicable threshold is pro-rated against actual contracted hours rather than assessed as if the doctor worked full-time. Doctors moving between different working patterns during training should confirm with HR that the threshold and payroll calculation have both been correctly updated for the new pattern, since a delay in recalculation is exactly the kind of gap the new pay-period compliance rule is designed to catch.

Who should I contact if I think my pay might not meet the current threshold?

The correct first point of contact is always the sponsoring Trust's HR or international recruitment team, since they hold the payroll records, contracted hours, and Certificate of Sponsorship details needed to assess compliance accurately, and the compliance obligation itself sits with the sponsor rather than the individual doctor. Doctors with more complex circumstances, or who are extending or switching visas around the 2026 transitional deadlines, may also want to consult a regulated immigration adviser for case-specific confirmation.

Conclusion

The UK visa salary threshold 2026 changes are, in practice, more about how compliance is measured than about the headline figures themselves — the shift to pay-period-by-pay-period checking from 8 April 2026, the closing transitional £31,300 window, and the scheduled December 2026 withdrawal of the Immigration Salary List all tighten the overall framework without materially changing what a standard, full-time NHS-employed doctor on a national pay scale needs to do. The doctors who genuinely need to check their own position are those on part-time, locum, or variable-pay arrangements, or those whose salary sits close to a threshold boundary — for everyone else, the practical answer remains simple: a standard NHS Trust contract, correctly administered, continues to clear these thresholds comfortably.
