Foundation Programme 2026 Recruitment Changes: What's New for Applicants

The Foundation Programme 2026 recruitment changes mark the biggest shake-up to how UK medical graduates enter FY1 since Preference Informed Allocation (PIA) itself was introduced in 2024. If you are a final-year medical student preparing your Oriel application for August 2026, three things are different this year: Foundation Priority Programmes (FPP) no longer run as a separate recruitment process, certain applicant groups are now formally prioritised in allocation under new legislation, and the paperwork trail for proving your primary medical qualification has tightened further. This guide assumes you already understand what the Foundation Programme itself involves — FY1, FY2, provisional registration, the ARCP process — and focuses entirely on what has changed in the application and allocation process for the 2026 recruitment round.

None of this changes what you need to do to become eligible in the first place: you still need to pass the UKMLA, obtain your primary medical qualification, and apply through Oriel in your final year. What has changed is how your preferences translate into an actual Foundation School and post, who gets priority when demand exceeds supply, and what evidence UKFPO will and will not accept. This matters immediately and practically — get the mechanics of PIA wrong, misunderstand where FPP posts have gone, or miss a documentation deadline, and you risk a delayed or disrupted start even after passing every academic hurdle.

Quick Answer: What Changed for Foundation Programme 2026 Recruitment

The headline Foundation Programme 2026 recruitment changes are: Foundation Priority Programmes are no longer a separate recruitment stream and are instead folded into the main Foundation Programme vacancy on Oriel, allocated through Preference Informed Allocation alongside every standard post. Allocation continues to use PIA, the computer-generated ranking system introduced in 2024, which means there is still no Situational Judgement Test (SJT) to sit and no Educational Performance Measure (EPM) score influencing your Foundation School allocation. New for 2026, allocation prioritises applicants holding a primary medical qualification from a UK or Republic of Ireland medical school, followed by those from Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, or Switzerland, reflecting the wider Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026. The Dean's Statement requirement that was previously used to confirm English language proficiency remains removed, and updated PMQ documentation rules continue to apply with strict submission deadlines.

In practical terms: you still rank your preferred Foundation Schools, you are still not scored on an exam-style test to determine that ranking, and you still need your degree evidence submitted correctly and on time. What is new is who is placed ahead of whom in the queue when a Foundation School or programme is oversubscribed, and the fact that FPP posts are now just another preference on the same list rather than a separate early application.

Who Is Affected by the 2026 Changes

Every applicant to the 2026 Foundation Programme is affected by the shift in how FPP posts are recruited, since they now appear inside the same Oriel vacancy list rather than a standalone process with its own timeline. The prioritisation changes affect applicants differently depending on where their primary medical qualification was awarded: UK and Republic of Ireland medical graduates, and graduates from Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, or Switzerland, benefit directly from the new priority ordering, while international medical graduates (IMGs) from other countries continue to compete for places in line with current eligibility rules, subject to visa sponsorship and vacancy availability after prioritised groups are allocated. IMGs considering the research-and-teaching route into foundation training should also read about the Academic Foundation Programme route, since AFP applications run on an earlier, separate timeline from the main FP/FPP round described here.

Foundation Priority Programmes: The First of the 2026 Recruitment Changes

Foundation Priority Programmes are posts in hard-to-fill locations or specialties that have historically been recruited to slightly differently from the standard two-year Foundation Programme, often with their own early offers window. For 2026, that separate FPP recruitment process has been discontinued. Instead, FPP vacancies are transferred directly into the main 2026 Foundation Programme vacancy list on Oriel, meaning applicants preference them alongside every other Foundation School and programme in the standard application window rather than through a distinct early process.

Why FPP Recruitment Was Folded into the Main Round

The change was driven by the practicalities of implementing the Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026. Running FPP as a standalone recruitment stream depended on an allocation mechanism that could not accommodate the new prioritisation requirements the Act introduces. Rather than build a second, parallel system capable of applying the same priority rules twice, UKFPO consolidated FPP posts into the single main allocation round, where Preference Informed Allocation already runs the necessary ranking logic for every applicant in one pass. As a result, the FPP offers stage that used to run in the January-to-February window ahead of the main round has been withdrawn for 2026, and there is no separate FPP-specific offers date to track.

What This Means for Your Application Strategy

If you were planning to apply to an FPP post in 2026, you no longer submit a distinct FPP application or wait for an earlier offer. Instead, FPP programmes simply appear as options within your Foundation School and programme preferences during the standard FP application window, and you rank them the same way you would rank any other programme you are interested in. This is a genuinely simpler experience in one respect — one timeline, one set of preferences, one allocation event — but it also means you should check your chosen Foundation School's programme list carefully to confirm whether the FPP posts you are interested in are included, rather than assuming they will be flagged separately as they were in previous years. Foundation Schools continue to publish their own guidance on which of their programmes carry the priority designation, so cross-reference your own Foundation School's website alongside the national Oriel vacancy list before finalising your preference order.

Historically, FPP posts have tended to sit in geographically less popular locations or in specialties that struggle to fill their Foundation Programme rotations through ordinary demand. Folding them into the main list does not change the underlying reason these posts exist — Foundation Schools still need doctors in these locations and specialties — but it does mean an applicant genuinely open to a wider geographic spread, rather than fixated on a small number of the most sought-after cities, may find their overall PIA outcome noticeably smoother, since a broader and more realistic preference list interacts better with a rank-and-preference algorithm than a narrow list concentrated entirely on the most oversubscribed schools.

How Preference Informed Allocation (PIA) Actually Works

Preference Informed Allocation is the system that has determined Foundation School allocation since 2024 and continues to be used for the 2026 round, including for the newly folded-in FPP posts. Understanding exactly how it works is the single most useful thing a final-year student can do before finalising an Oriel application, because PIA behaves very differently from the points-based system many students expect from older guidance, sibling exams, or forum discussions from before 2024.

Ranking Your Preferences, Not Your Score

Under PIA, you submit a ranked list of Foundation School preferences during the application window rather than a score-driven application. Once the preferencing window closes, an allocation algorithm run through the Oriel system generates a computer-generated rank for every eligible applicant. That rank, combined with your submitted preference order, determines which Foundation School you are allocated to — the system works to give as many applicants as possible their higher-ranked preferences, subject to the number of places available at each school. Historical data from recent PIA rounds shows the majority of applicants receive one of their top few choices, though a first-choice allocation is never guaranteed for every applicant, particularly for the most sought-after Foundation Schools in and around London and other major cities.

Because the computer-generated rank is not derived from academic performance, exam marks, or any test you sit, there is nothing content-specific to revise for in order to influence it. The only actions genuinely within your control are: submitting your eligibility application correctly and on time, submitting a complete and accurate preference list, and meeting every documentation deadline so you remain an eligible applicant in the pool when the algorithm runs.

No SJT, No EPM: What Actually Changed From the Old System

Before PIA, Foundation School allocation used the Foundation Programme Application System (FPAS), which combined two scored components: the Educational Performance Measure (EPM), based on your medical school performance decile plus additional achievements such as degrees and publications, and the Situational Judgement Test (SJT), a separate national exam assessing professional judgement. Combined, these produced a total score out of 100 that ranked every applicant nationally, and your score — not simply your preferences — largely determined which Foundation School you could realistically expect.

Under PIA, neither component exists in the allocation process. You are not required to sit an SJT, and your EPM-equivalent academic performance measure has no bearing on which Foundation School you are allocated to. This is arguably the single most important practical difference for a 2026 applicant coming from a medical school where senior students, tutors, or older guidance documents may still describe the old points-based approach: there is no SJT revision to schedule into your final year, and worrying about your decile ranking relative to classmates is no longer relevant to Foundation School allocation. Your medical school still uses your academic performance for its own internal purposes, such as ranking within specific local schemes, but this figure has no role in the national Foundation School allocation itself. This is genuinely good news for time management in your final year: the hours previously spent on dedicated SJT question banks can instead go towards your UKMLA revision plan, which remains the one assessment that stands directly between you and Foundation Programme eligibility in the first place.

White Space Questions Under PIA

The application also includes white space questions — short written responses covering professionalism, teamwork, clinical reasoning, and reflective practice. Under the current system these are used to confirm eligibility and professional suitability rather than to competitively rank applicants against one another. Treat them as a genuine professional-judgement check rather than a scoring exercise: give specific, honest answers drawn from real clinical placements or extracurricular experience, since generic or vague responses are more likely to raise a suitability query than a strong, specific answer would.

Who Is Prioritised in 2026 Allocation

The most significant substantive change for 2026 sits alongside PIA rather than replacing it: certain groups of applicants are now formally prioritised in how places are allocated, reflecting the wider Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026. This does not replace the PIA ranking mechanism described above — it changes who is considered within the pool, and in what order, before the preference-and-rank matching process runs.

The 2026 Priority Order

For 2026, priority in Foundation Programme allocation is given first to applicants holding a primary medical qualification (PMQ) from a medical school in the United Kingdom or the Republic of Ireland. A second priority tier applies to applicants with a primary medical qualification from a medical school in Iceland, the Principality of Liechtenstein, Norway, or Switzerland — a group whose priority reflects existing international mutual-recognition arrangements rather than a new concession. Applicants outside these groups, including the majority of international medical graduates, continue to be eligible to apply and continue to go through the same Oriel process, PIA ranking, and white space questions, but are considered for allocation after the prioritised groups within the same recruitment round.

The practical effect of this ordering is straightforward: within a given Foundation School's available places, prioritised applicants are matched to their ranked preferences before other eligible applicants are considered for any places that remain. This is a meaningful change for IMGs planning a 2026 application, since it means allocation outcomes may be less predictable than in previous cycles, particularly for the most competitive Foundation Schools. It does not remove IMG eligibility to apply, and it does not affect the underlying requirement to hold, or be eligible to obtain, GMC registration and the right to work in the UK.

What Prioritisation Means in Practice for UK Students

For the primary audience of this guide — UK final-year medical students — the prioritisation change is largely reassuring rather than disruptive: as a holder of a UK PMQ, you sit in the first priority tier regardless of where within the UK you studied. Your practical application steps do not change because of this rule; you still complete the same eligibility application, the same FP and FPP preference exercise, and the same PIA ranking process as before. The main reason to understand the prioritisation order at all is so you can accurately advise or reassure international classmates and colleagues who ask about it, and so you understand why national conversation and NHS workforce commentary around the 2026 round has focused heavily on this issue.

What Prioritisation Means in Practice for IMGs

For IMGs, the prioritisation order is the single most consequential change in the 2026 recruitment cycle, and it sits on top of, not instead of, the existing eligibility requirements around GMC registration, right to work, and — where relevant — PLAB. IMGs remain able to apply through the standard Oriel process and are considered for any places not filled by prioritised applicants within a Foundation School's allocation. Given that outcomes will depend on how many places remain after prioritised applicants are matched, IMGs applying for 2026 should treat their Foundation School preference order and the completeness of their documentation as more important than ever, since there is no scoring mechanism to compensate for an incomplete or late application. IMGs weighing whether to pursue the standard Foundation Programme at all, versus applying directly for specialty or core training posts instead, may find it useful to compare the two routes in more depth via this site's dedicated comparison of the Foundation Year and direct specialty-training pathways for IMGs.

Removed Dean's Statement and Updated PMQ Evidence Requirements

Two further changes, both carried over from recent years and continuing to apply for 2026, affect how you prove your qualifications to UKFPO. Neither is brand new to 2026 specifically, but both remain live requirements that trip up applicants who rely on outdated guidance or a previous year's application notes.

What Happened to the Dean's Statement

The Dean's Statement was previously used as a route to confirm English language proficiency based on a letter from the applicant's medical school dean. That route has been removed: a primary medical qualification is now only accepted as evidence of English language proficiency where it was awarded by a medical school in a country the GMC recognises as having English as a first and native language. If your PMQ was awarded outside that list of countries, you must instead provide a valid IELTS or OET certificate meeting the GMC's required score bands — your medical school dean confirming your language ability is no longer an accepted substitute. UK medical students are very rarely affected by this, since a UK-awarded PMQ satisfies the English-language requirement directly, but it is a critical planning point for IMGs and for UK students whose medical training was completed substantially overseas.

Current PMQ Documentation Requirements

Separately from the English-language route above, UKFPO requires specific documentary evidence of your primary medical qualification itself. Only two forms of evidence are accepted: a clear, legible scanned copy of your actual PMQ certificate showing the degree(s) awarded, or, if your certificate has not yet been issued, a completed and signed confirmation form from your medical school (sometimes still informally referred to by applicants as a "dean's form," distinct from the removed English-language Dean's Statement described above) verifying the qualification you are on track to receive. Transcripts, results letters, or medical register extracts are explicitly not accepted in place of these two documents. If you have applied in a previous recruitment year, you cannot reuse an old PMQ confirmation form — only the current year's version of the form, correctly dated, will be accepted.

These evidence deadlines are strict and are enforced without much flexibility, since UKFPO must verify every applicant's qualification before allocation runs. Missing your PMQ evidence deadline can result in automatic withdrawal from the process altogether, so this is one of the few purely administrative tasks in the entire application that deserves the same level of attention as your UKMLA revision schedule. If your name on your PMQ documentation differs from your application — following marriage, a legal name change, or a simple spelling variation — you will also need supporting documentation (a marriage certificate, deed poll, or a signed and stamped letter from your medical school) to resolve the discrepancy before your evidence is accepted.

Why the System Changed: From FPAS to PIA in Context

To understand why the Foundation Programme 2026 recruitment changes look the way they do, it helps to understand the problem PIA was originally designed to solve, since the 2026 prioritisation update builds directly on that same infrastructure. Before 2024, the Foundation Programme Application System ranked every applicant nationally by a combined EPM and SJT score, then matched applicants to Foundation Schools in strict score order. In practice, this meant a handful of marks on a single situational judgement exam, sat on one day, could meaningfully shift which city or region a graduate ended up training in for two years — a high-stakes outcome resting on a relatively narrow measure of professional judgement, compounded by well-documented concerns about coaching, question-bank memorisation, and inequity of access to SJT preparation resources between medical schools.

The move to PIA in 2024 was explicitly framed as a fairness reform: rather than ranking applicants by an exam score with limited proven predictive value for actual clinical performance, the system would instead prioritise applicants' own stated preferences, using a computer-generated random-but-fair rank purely to resolve situations where more applicants want a given school than there are places. Early PIA data supports the fairness framing in one important respect — the majority of applicants across recent cycles have received one of their top few preferences — while also generating ongoing debate, reflected in independent survey research on foundation doctors' perceptions of PIA, about whether removing a merit-based element entirely is the right trade-off. The 2026 prioritisation order layered on top of PIA does not reintroduce a merit-based score; it instead reintroduces a different kind of ordering principle, based on where an applicant's primary medical qualification was awarded rather than on any assessed performance.

The Specialised Foundation Programme's Separate Dual-Pathway Change

It is worth distinguishing the FPP changes covered in this guide from a related but separate 2026 change affecting the Specialised Foundation Programme (SFP) — the academic/research-integrated variant covered in more detail in the Academic Foundation Programme section below. For 2026, SFP recruitment in England moved to a dual-pathway model: a portion of SFP posts are selected directly by an applicant's own medical school under Pathway 1, while the remaining posts are allocated through the main national process under Pathway 2, using the same Preference Informed Allocation mechanism described above. Do not confuse this SFP-specific dual-pathway change with the FPP consolidation described earlier in this guide — FPP posts (geographically or specialty hard-to-fill standard posts) and SFP posts (academically-integrated posts) are distinct programmes with separate application routes, even though both now rely on PIA for at least part of their allocation.

Post-Matching, Linked Applications, and Rotation Allocation After PIA

PIA allocates you to a Foundation School — it is not the final step in determining your day-to-day working life as an FY1 doctor. Once your Foundation School is confirmed, a further, school-level process determines your specific Trust, rotation sequence, and the particular specialties you will work in across your six four-month placements. This second stage varies considerably between Foundation Schools: some allow applicants to rank specific programmes or Trusts within the school directly, while others use a further combination of preference and randomised allocation. If you have not yet reviewed how these placements actually work day to day, the general FY1/FY2 explainer on this site covers rotation structure, the ARCP process, and the Foundation Programme Curriculum 2021 in full, and is worth reading alongside this guide rather than in place of it.

Linked Applications for Personal Circumstances

Applicants with specific personal circumstances — caring responsibilities, a disability or health condition, or a partner with fixed employment in a particular region — can apply for a linked application, which increases the likelihood (though never the guarantee) of an allocation to a particular Foundation School. Evidence of the underlying circumstance is required, and linked applications must be submitted within the same application window described later in this guide, not afterwards. This mechanism is unchanged by the 2026 prioritisation update: a linked application and a PMQ-based priority tier operate independently of one another, and an applicant can be affected by both simultaneously — for example, a UK graduate with a linked application still receives first-tier PMQ priority in addition to whatever weighting their linked circumstances provide.

What Happens if Your Foundation School Allocation Is Delayed or Contested

Occasionally, an applicant's allocation is delayed pending verification of eligibility, PMQ evidence, or a Clinical Assessment outcome. If this happens to you, contact your Foundation School and UKFPO promptly rather than waiting for the situation to resolve itself — verification queries have their own deadlines and can affect whether you are included in the main allocation run at all. Applicants who believe their allocation has been processed incorrectly (for example, a prioritisation tier applied wrongly due to a PMQ country being misrecorded) should raise this directly with UKFPO as soon as the allocation is released, since these issues are far easier to correct before FY1 start than afterwards.

What to Expect: The 2026 Application Timeline Step by Step

The practical shape of the 2026 application year follows a sequence of fixed windows, each with its own evidence and action requirements. The exact calendar dates are published and updated by UKFPO directly, so always check the current dates on the official UKFPO timeline page before planning around any specific date mentioned here — the sequence and purpose of each stage is the more durable, transferable part of this guide. Knowing this sequence in advance is one of the most useful practical takeaways from the Foundation Programme 2026 recruitment changes as a whole, since it lets you plan your final year around fixed, non-negotiable dates rather than reacting to them as they arrive.

Step 1: Eligibility Application

Your first action in your final year is submitting the eligibility application through Oriel, during a window that opens in early summer. This stage confirms you meet the basic requirements to apply at all — degree progress, right to work status where relevant, and any PLAB requirements for applicants who need it. Submit this promptly rather than close to the deadline; a late or incomplete eligibility application can knock you out of the process before you even reach the preferencing stage. It is worth cross-checking this window against your own UKMLA sitting schedule, since AKT and CPSA results, eligibility application deadlines, and your final-year academic calendar can overlap in ways that catch students off guard if planned in isolation.

Step 2: PMQ and Supporting Evidence Submission

Following your eligibility application, you must submit your PMQ evidence — either your certificate or the current year's confirmation form described above — by a fixed deadline in early autumn. This is also the point at which any English language evidence (IELTS/OET, if applicable) needs to be finalised. Treat this stage as equally binding as your exam deadlines; automatic withdrawal for missed evidence deadlines has no routine appeals mechanism.

Step 3: FP and FPP Application Window

The main Foundation Programme and (as of 2026) Foundation Priority Programme application window opens in late September and typically runs for around two weeks. This is where you submit your ranked Foundation School preferences, complete your white space questions, and — if applicable — indicate interest in FPP-designated programmes now sitting inside the same list. Since FPP posts sit inside the same Oriel vacancy list as standard posts for 2026, you rank them in whatever position genuinely reflects your preference relative to every other programme at your chosen Foundation School — there is no separate FPP-only ranking exercise to complete, and your Foundation School's own published guidance will confirm which listed programmes carry the FPP designation. Take your time on preference ordering more broadly: because PIA works from your genuine preference order, ranking strategically rather than honestly (for example, ranking a school you would not actually accept above one you would, in an attempt to "game" the algorithm) tends to produce worse outcomes than a straightforward honest ranking.

Step 4: Academic Foundation Programme Applications (If Applicable)

Applicants pursuing the Academic Foundation Programme route submit a separate application, including a personal statement and evidence of research, teaching, or leadership experience, on an earlier timeline than the standard FP window. This route remains distinct from the main PIA process described in this guide; IMGs specifically interested in it should read the dedicated overview of the UKMLA-to-Academic-Foundation-Programme pathway for the eligibility detail that route requires.

Step 5: Clinical Assessment (Where Required)

Certain applicant categories — most commonly those relying on an alternative route to standard UK medical school graduation — sit a Clinical Assessment as part of establishing their eligibility, with results released several weeks later. If this applies to you, confirm well in advance whether your specific circumstances require it, since it sits on a fixed autumn timeline that will not be repeated before allocation.

Step 6: PIA Allocation to Foundation School

After the FP and FPP preferencing window closes, the PIA algorithm runs, generating each eligible applicant's computer-generated rank and matching it against submitted preferences and the 2026 prioritisation order described above. Foundation School allocation results are then released to applicants. This is the single most anticipated date in the whole cycle for most students, and it is worth remembering that your individual rank is not disclosed to you before this point.

Step 7: GMC Provisional Registration

Once you have passed the UKMLA and know your Foundation School allocation, you apply for provisional GMC registration, which must be in place before your FY1 start date. GMC registration applications have their own deadline in late spring, well before the August start, so this step should be actioned as soon as your UKMLA result and degree confirmation are both available — do not wait until results day if any part of your documentation can be prepared earlier. The full mechanics of this step — what the GMC application requires, typical processing times, and how it interacts with your degree certification — are covered in this site's GMC registration guide, which is worth working through in parallel with your Foundation School preferencing rather than leaving until after allocation.

Step 8: FY1 Start

Foundation Programme posts for the 2026 cohort begin in early August, immediately following medical school graduation. By this point, every stage above — eligibility, PMQ evidence, PIA allocation, and GMC provisional registration — needs to be complete, since Foundation Schools set fixed start dates and have very limited ability to defer individual applicants.

Frequently Asked Questions About Foundation Programme 2026 Recruitment Changes

Do I still need to sit the Situational Judgement Test for the 2026 Foundation Programme?

No. The Situational Judgement Test was removed from Foundation School allocation when Preference Informed Allocation was introduced in 2024, and this remains unchanged for the 2026 recruitment round. You do not need to register for, prepare for, or sit an SJT as part of your Foundation Programme application.

Are Foundation Priority Programmes still available in 2026?

Yes, but they are no longer recruited through a separate process. FPP posts have been transferred into the main 2026 Foundation Programme vacancy list on Oriel, so you preference them alongside standard programmes during the single FP and FPP application window, and they are allocated through the same Preference Informed Allocation run as every other post.

Who is prioritised in the 2026 Foundation Programme allocation?

Applicants holding a primary medical qualification from a medical school in the United Kingdom or the Republic of Ireland receive first priority in 2026 allocation, followed by applicants with a primary medical qualification from Iceland, the Principality of Liechtenstein, Norway, or Switzerland. Other eligible applicants, including most international medical graduates, continue to be considered for any remaining places within each Foundation School's allocation.

Does the Educational Performance Measure still affect my Foundation School allocation?

No. The Educational Performance Measure has no role in Foundation School allocation under Preference Informed Allocation, and this has not changed for 2026. Your medical school may still use academic performance measures for its own internal purposes, but they do not feed into the national PIA ranking that determines your Foundation School.

Can I still submit a Dean's Statement to prove my English language proficiency?

No, the Dean's Statement route for confirming English language proficiency has been removed and continues not to be accepted for 2026. Your primary medical qualification is only accepted as English language evidence if it was awarded in a country the GMC recognises as having English as a first and native language; otherwise, you must provide a valid IELTS or OET certificate.

How does the Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026 affect my Foundation Programme application?

The Act is the legal basis for the 2026 prioritisation order described above, requiring that applicants with a UK or Republic of Ireland primary medical qualification, and applicants from Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, or Switzerland, are prioritised in foundation and specialty training allocation — not solely Foundation Programme allocation. It does not change the process you follow to apply, but it does affect the order in which applicants are matched to places, particularly in oversubscribed Foundation Schools. A full explanation of the Act, including its specialty-training implications, and its wider implications for IMGs, is covered in a dedicated article on this site.

Will these changes affect graduate-entry medical students differently from standard-entry students?

No — graduate-entry and standard-entry UK medical students both hold a UK primary medical qualification and therefore sit in the same first-priority tier for 2026 allocation, and both go through an identical PIA application process. Graduate-entry students planning their final-year timeline alongside other commitments may still find it useful to review the site's dedicated guidance for graduate-entry medical students preparing for the UKMLA, since exam-preparation timing interacts with the application windows described in this guide.

What happens if I fail the UKMLA after being allocated a Foundation School place under PIA?

Your PIA allocation to a Foundation School is separate from, but dependent on, passing the UKMLA and obtaining provisional GMC registration in time for your FY1 start date. If you do not pass in time, you will not be able to take up your allocated post for that cohort and will need to explore deferred entry through UKFPO. The practical consequences of this scenario — for your training timeline, visa status, and next steps — are covered in detail in this site's guide to what happens if you fail the UKMLA and how it affects your Foundation Year entry.

Conclusion: Applying With Confidence Under the 2026 Rules

The Foundation Programme 2026 recruitment changes are best understood as a consolidation and a prioritisation update layered on top of a process — PIA — that has already been running successfully since 2024. If you are a UK final-year student, your core task list has not changed: pass the UKMLA, submit accurate and timely evidence, rank your Foundation School preferences honestly, and secure provisional GMC registration ahead of your FY1 start. If you are an IMG, the same task list applies, but you should plan with a clearer understanding that allocation now formally prioritises UK, Republic of Ireland, and specific EFTA-country graduates ahead of you within any given Foundation School's places. In either case, the practical mechanics — no SJT, no EPM, ranked preferences, strict documentation deadlines, and FPP posts now sitting inside the main vacancy list — are the details worth acting on well before the application windows close.
