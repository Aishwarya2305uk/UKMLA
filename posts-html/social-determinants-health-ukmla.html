Social Determinants of Health and Health Inequalities for the UKMLA

Health inequalities are systematic, avoidable, and unjust differences in health between different groups of people. Understanding why these gaps exist — and what can be done about them — is central to modern medical practice and features prominently in the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test (AKT). This post covers the major frameworks, UK data, policy levers, and epidemiological concepts you need to know.

Foundational Frameworks

The Marmot Reviews

Sir Michael Marmot has shaped UK health policy more than almost any other academic. His two landmark reviews frame how we think about health inequalities in England.

Marmot Review 2010 — "Fair Society, Healthy Lives"

Commissioned by the government and published in 2010, this review established six overarching policy objectives to reduce health inequalities:

  • Give every child the best start in life — early years experience sets the trajectory for lifelong health and wellbeing
  • Enable all children, young people and adults to maximise their capabilities through education and lifelong learning — educational attainment is a strong predictor of life expectancy
  • Create fair employment and good work for all — unemployment and poor-quality work damage health; good work is protective
  • Ensure a healthy standard of living for all — income, poverty, and the ability to afford basic necessities are foundational
  • Create and develop healthy and sustainable places and communities — the physical and social environment in which people live matters enormously
  • Strengthen the role and impact of ill health prevention — NHS and public health systems should address inequalities in access to and outcomes from preventive care

One of the most important concepts from the 2010 review is the social gradient in health. Health does not simply divide the population into "the poor" and "everyone else." Instead, health improves incrementally as socioeconomic position improves — all the way up the social ladder. A middle manager is healthier than a junior employee; a senior consultant is healthier than a middle manager. This gradient means that health inequalities affect the majority of the population, not just those in extreme poverty. Policy responses must therefore go beyond targeting only those at the very bottom.

Marmot Review 2020 — "Health Equity in England: The Marmot Review 10 Years On"

A decade after the original review, Marmot returned to assess progress. The findings were stark:

  • For the first time in over 120 years, improvements in life expectancy in England had stalled
  • The life expectancy gap between the most and least deprived areas had widened
  • Healthy life expectancy had actually fallen in the most deprived areas, particularly for women
  • The decade of austerity from 2010 was identified as a major driver — cuts to public services, welfare reform, and reduced local authority spending had disproportionately affected the poorest communities

Later in 2020, Marmot published a rapid response — COVID-19 and Health Equity — demonstrating that the pandemic had further widened existing inequalities, with the greatest mortality burden falling on the most deprived communities and ethnic minority groups.

WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health (CSDH)

The WHO CSDH, chaired by Marmot, produced its final report in 2008 with the defining statement that health inequalities arise from "the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work and age." These conditions are themselves shaped by political, social, and economic forces.

The CSDH framework distinguishes two levels of determinants:

  • Structural determinants — the root causes that drive inequality in the distribution of resources and power. These include: socioeconomic position, political context, governance structures, macroeconomic policies, social policies (welfare, education, housing), and cultural and societal values. Structural determinants generate the social stratification that determines people's position in society.
  • Intermediary determinants — the mechanisms through which structural position translates into differential health outcomes. These include: material circumstances (housing quality, neighbourhood, working conditions, income), psychosocial factors (stress, social support, sense of control), health behaviours (smoking, diet, physical activity, alcohol — which are themselves socially patterned), and biological factors (genetics, age, sex).

The framework emphasises that health behaviours are not simply a matter of individual choice — they are shaped by the social and material conditions in which people live. Telling a person in fuel poverty to eat more healthily without addressing their income is unlikely to work.

Dahlgren-Whitehead Rainbow Model (1991)

Göran Dahlgren and Margaret Whitehead developed the "rainbow model" — one of the most widely used visual frameworks for understanding the social determinants of health. It consists of concentric layers, with individual biology at the centre and progressively wider social forces radiating outwards:

  • Core: Age, sex, and constitutional (genetic) factors — the fixed biological characteristics of the individual
  • Layer 1: Individual lifestyle factors — behaviours such as smoking, diet, physical activity, and alcohol use
  • Layer 2: Social and community networks — social support, community cohesion, relationships
  • Layer 3: Living and working conditions — housing, employment, working environment, access to food, healthcare, and education
  • Layer 4: General socioeconomic, cultural and environmental conditions — the macro-level political economy, welfare state, cultural norms, and the physical environment

The model illustrates that the outermost layers are the most powerful — and the most modifiable through policy — while also showing that individual behaviour operates within the constraints imposed by the layers around it.

Key Social Determinants — UK Evidence

Income and Poverty

Poverty in the UK is most commonly defined in relative terms: relative poverty means living in a household with income below 60% of the national median (after housing costs). Absolute poverty refers to income below a fixed real-terms threshold. Approximately 14.5 million people in the UK live in relative poverty — including around four million children.

The health consequences of low income are extensive and well-documented:

  • Fuel poverty — inability to afford adequate heating — is associated with cold-related illness, respiratory disease (especially in children and older adults), and cardiovascular events
  • Food insecurity — insufficient access to adequate nutrition — is associated with malnutrition, obesity (paradoxically, because energy-dense foods are cheaper), type 2 diabetes, and mental health problems
  • Low income is independently associated with higher rates of cardiovascular mortality, type 2 diabetes, depression, and anxiety
  • The stress of financial insecurity itself — through chronic activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis — contributes to cardiovascular disease, immune dysfunction, and mental health conditions

Education

Educational attainment is a powerful predictor of health across the life course:

  • Remaining in education past the age of 16 is associated with a two to three year longer life expectancy
  • Education improves health literacy — the ability to obtain, understand, and act on health information — which affects help-seeking behaviour, medication adherence, and chronic disease self-management
  • Higher educational attainment leads to higher earning potential, which buffers many downstream material risks
  • Early educational investment (high-quality early years provision) has life-long returns — this underpins the Marmot Review's first priority of "giving every child the best start in life"

Employment

Work is protective of health — good work provides income, social connection, structure, and purpose. However, the quality of work matters as much as its presence:

  • Unemployment approximately doubles the risk of depression and anxiety, even after controlling for pre-existing mental health conditions
  • Precarious work — zero-hours contracts, gig economy work, temporary contracts — is associated with worse mental and physical health than stable employment, partly due to income insecurity and lack of access to sick pay or occupational health support
  • Occupational health hazards — exposure to dust, chemicals, noise, or physical demands — disproportionately affect lower-paid workers
  • Marmot's own Whitehall Studies of British civil servants were foundational. They demonstrated that even within a relatively homogeneous group (all employed, all with access to the NHS), the grade of civil service employment predicted mortality and cardiovascular disease risk in a stepwise fashion — higher grade, longer life. This was a landmark demonstration of the social gradient.

Housing

Housing quality has direct and indirect effects on health:

  • Overcrowding facilitates transmission of respiratory infections, including tuberculosis — overcrowding remains a significant driver of TB rates in deprived urban areas in the UK
  • Damp and mould cause and exacerbate asthma, other respiratory diseases, and fungal infections. The tragic death of two-year-old Awaab Ishak in 2020 from a respiratory condition caused by mould in his social housing property led to "Awaab's Law" requiring landlords to address mould within strict timeframes
  • Fuel poverty intersects with housing — poorly insulated homes are more expensive to heat, disproportionately affecting those on low incomes
  • Homelessness is associated with catastrophic health outcomes. The average age of death for people sleeping rough in the UK is approximately 45 years — less than half the national average. This reflects the convergence of mental illness, substance use, trauma, violence, exposure to the elements, and profound difficulty accessing healthcare

Social Support and Isolation

Social isolation is now recognised as a major public health threat:

  • Research by Julianne Holt-Lunstad and colleagues demonstrated that social isolation is associated with a 26% increase in mortality — equivalent in effect size to smoking around 15 cigarettes per day
  • Loneliness affects physical health through multiple pathways: increased inflammation, impaired immune function, disrupted sleep, and less healthy behaviours
  • The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically intensified the loneliness epidemic — enforced isolation, bereavement, and disruption to community networks had lasting effects, particularly for older adults and people with mental health conditions
  • Social prescribing — connecting patients with community resources and activities — is now embedded in primary care as a response to loneliness and its health consequences

Ethnicity and Structural Racism

Ethnic inequalities in health outcomes are among the most stark and persistent in the UK:

  • Maternal mortality: According to MBRRACE-UK 2023 data, Black women are 3.7 times more likely to die during or after pregnancy than White women. Asian women are 1.8 times more likely. These gaps have persisted for decades and cannot be explained by clinical risk factors alone.
  • Cardiovascular disease: South Asian populations have significantly higher rates of type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and premature coronary artery disease — partly reflecting genetic predisposition but also socioeconomic and cultural factors
  • COVID-19: Black, Asian, and minority ethnic communities experienced two to four times higher age-adjusted mortality from COVID-19, reflecting overcrowded housing, higher-risk occupations, and pre-existing cardiometabolic conditions
  • Structural racism encompasses institutional discrimination, implicit bias in healthcare (including under-treatment of pain, assumptions about biology, miscalibration of clinical tools such as spirometry reference ranges), and socioeconomic disadvantage rooted in historical and ongoing racial inequity
  • Systemic barriers to care — language barriers, cultural insensitivity, mistrust of healthcare institutions — reduce uptake of preventive services and delay presentation with serious illness

Geography

Where people live profoundly shapes their health:

  • The north-south divide in England is well-documented — life expectancy in parts of the North East and North West is significantly lower than in the South East and London
  • Rural health presents a paradox — rural areas often have older, wealthier populations with better average health, but rural poverty is hidden and health services are harder to access (greater distances, transport poverty, fewer GP surgeries, longer ambulance response times)
  • "Left behind" coastal communities — former seaside towns and deindustrialised areas — have seen economic decline, population ageing, reduced services, and rising rates of mental illness and substance use
  • Access to green space, air quality, and proximity to environmental hazards are all distributed unequally by geography and socioeconomic status

Health Inequalities Data for the UK

The following figures should be committed to memory for the UKMLA:

  • Life expectancy gap: There is a 10-year gap in life expectancy between the most and least deprived deciles. Men in the most deprived areas live to approximately 74 years; men in the least deprived areas live to approximately 84 years. For women, the range is approximately 79 to 87 years.
  • Healthy life expectancy gap: The gap in years lived in good health is even wider — approximately 20 years between the most and least deprived deciles. People in deprived areas not only die younger but spend a much greater proportion of their shorter lives in poor health.
  • Infant mortality: Three to four times higher in the most deprived decile compared with the least deprived
  • Cardiovascular disease mortality: Approximately three times higher in the most deprived areas
  • Cancer screening uptake: Consistently lower in deprived populations and among many ethnic minority groups — contributing to later-stage diagnosis and worse outcomes
  • Mental health: Depression and anxiety are two to three times more prevalent in the lowest income quintile compared with the highest
  • COVID-19: Black, Asian, and deprived communities experienced two to four times higher age-adjusted mortality in the first wave

The Inverse Care Law

First described by Julian Tudor Hart in 1971, the Inverse Care Law states: "The availability of good medical care tends to vary inversely with the need for it in the population served."

In practice, this means:

  • GP surgeries in deprived areas have worse GP-to-patient ratios than those in affluent areas, despite their patients having more complex, multimorbid needs
  • Patients in deprived areas often have shorter consultations and are less able to advocate for themselves or articulate symptoms in ways that prompt investigation
  • Specialist services — including mental health, substance use, and tertiary care — are less accessible from deprived areas due to geography, transport, and practical barriers
  • The NHS, despite being a universal system, does not deliver equal care — resourcing has historically favoured areas of lower need

Tudor Hart's observation has held true for over 50 years and remains a key driver of NHS inequality strategy, including the development of Core20PLUS5.

NHS Strategy: Core20PLUS5

Launched by NHS England in 2021, Core20PLUS5 is the national approach to addressing health inequalities within the NHS. It provides a framework for Integrated Care Systems and NHS providers to focus effort where it is most needed.

The framework has two components:

  • Core20: The most deprived 20% of the national population, as defined by the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD)
  • PLUS: Inclusion health groups — populations experiencing extreme disadvantage who often fall outside standard health data and commissioning frameworks. These groups include people experiencing homelessness, Gypsies, Roma and Travellers, sex workers, people with multiple disadvantage (such as those with intersecting homelessness, substance use, mental illness, and offending histories), and asylum seekers and refugees

Maternity Reduce inequalities in maternal and neonatal outcomes Improve access to antenatal care; address ethnic disparities; continuity of carer
Severe mental illness (SMI) physical health Reduce premature mortality in people with SMI Annual physical health checks; cardiometabolic monitoring; smoking cessation

The NHS Long Term Plan (2019) also embedded health inequalities as a cross-cutting priority, with specific commitments around prevention, early diagnosis, and reducing unwarranted clinical variation.

Protected Characteristics and the Equality Act 2010

The Equality Act 2010 is the primary legislation protecting individuals from discrimination in England, Scotland, and Wales. It consolidates and replaces previous anti-discrimination law. There are nine protected characteristics:

  • Age
  • Disability
  • Gender reassignment
  • Marriage and civil partnership
  • Pregnancy and maternity
  • Race
  • Religion or belief
  • Sex
  • Sexual orientation

For healthcare organisations and other public bodies, the Act imposes a Public Sector Equality Duty (PSED) — the general duty to:

  • Eliminate unlawful discrimination, harassment, and victimisation
  • Advance equality of opportunity between those who share a protected characteristic and those who do not
  • Foster good relations between different groups

For disabled people specifically, the Act imposes a duty to make reasonable adjustments. This duty is anticipatory — organisations must think in advance about what adjustments might be needed for disabled people in general, not wait for individual requests. In healthcare this means, for example, ensuring accessible appointment systems, providing information in accessible formats, and making physical environments navigable for wheelchair users.

LGBTQ+ populations experience significant health inequalities, including higher rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidality (particularly in younger people and transgender individuals), barriers to accessing healthcare (including fear of discrimination, misgendering, and lack of cultural competence among clinicians), and disproportionate rates of substance use. Trans and non-binary patients face specific challenges including difficulty registering with GPs, barriers to gender-affirming care, and misgendering in clinical encounters which can deter help-seeking.

Inclusion Health Populations

Inclusion health is a term used to describe people who are socially excluded and face multiple overlapping risk factors for poor health. These groups often fall between traditional service boundaries and are typically under-represented in routine health data.

People Experiencing Homelessness

People who are sleeping rough or in unstable housing face catastrophic health inequalities:

  • Average age of death for rough sleepers in the UK: approximately 45 years
  • Extremely high rates of multimorbidity — mental illness, substance use disorders, respiratory disease, skin conditions, and trauma are all far more prevalent
  • Practical barriers to registering with a GP (no fixed address, no ID documents) mean that secondary care — particularly emergency departments — frequently becomes the only point of access
  • NHS England has produced guidance on registering homeless patients without an address, but implementation is inconsistent

Gypsies, Roma, and Travellers

This is among the groups with the worst health outcomes of any in the UK, including lower life expectancy, higher infant mortality, and higher rates of mental health conditions. Barriers include discrimination from healthcare providers, cultural barriers, mobility (which disrupts continuity of care), and literacy challenges that affect engagement with written health information.

Sex Workers

Sex workers face disproportionate rates of violence, sexually transmitted infections, substance use, and mental health conditions. Criminali sation (or the criminalisation of clients under the Nordic model) creates barriers to safety and makes it harder to access healthcare without fear of legal consequences or stigma.

People with Multiple Disadvantage

The concept of multiple exclusion homelessness describes people who experience several of the following simultaneously: homelessness, substance use, mental illness, and contact with the criminal justice system. These intersecting disadvantages are mutually reinforcing and are extremely difficult to address without comprehensive, coordinated services. People in this group are among the hardest to reach and face the greatest barriers to both physical and mental healthcare.

Asylum Seekers and Refugees

The health needs of this population are complex and often urgent:

  • High prevalence of PTSD, depression, and complex trauma — related to pre-flight experiences, the dangers of migration itself, and the stresses of the asylum process
  • Language barriers — the NHS is required to provide interpreter services, but in practice access is inconsistent and rushed consultations often proceed without adequate interpretation
  • Entitlement confusion — there is widespread misunderstanding (among both patients and clinicians) about which NHS services different immigration statuses are entitled to. Failed asylum seekers are generally not entitled to free secondary care, with important exceptions:
    • Emergency care is always free for anyone
    • Treatment for communicable diseases (to protect public health) is always free
    • Mental health treatment provided under a section of the Mental Health Act is always free
    • Primary care (GP registration) is available to all, regardless of immigration status
  • Destitution — failed asylum seekers may lose accommodation and financial support, creating acute vulnerability

Prevention Across the Spectrum

Prevention is conventionally divided into three levels, though some frameworks now add primordial and quaternary prevention. The UKMLA requires you to distinguish between these levels and to give clinical examples.

Level of Prevention Definition Examples
Primary prevention Prevent disease before it occurs — act on healthy individuals or populations to reduce incidence Childhood vaccination programmes; minimum unit pricing for alcohol (£0.50/unit in Scotland — associated with reductions in alcohol-related deaths); Soft Drinks Industry Levy (2018) — reformulation reduced sugar content of major drinks before levy took effect; smoking cessation services; folic acid supplementation in pregnancy; water fluoridation
Secondary prevention Early detection of disease before symptoms cause significant harm — act on people in early or pre-symptomatic stages to reduce prevalence NHS Health Check (offered every 5 years to adults aged 40–74; assesses CVD risk, type 2 diabetes, CKD, dementia risk); cervical, breast, and bowel cancer screening programmes; abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) screening for men aged 65; diabetic eye screening
Tertiary prevention Reduce the impact of established disease — limit disability, prevent complications, support rehabilitation Cardiac rehabilitation after myocardial infarction; stroke care pathways and neurorehabilitation; pulmonary rehabilitation for COPD; structured diabetes education (DESMOND, DAFNE); falls prevention programmes for older adults

Tobacco Control

Tobacco remains the single largest preventable cause of illness and premature death in England. Key policy and clinical tools include:

  • Smokefree NHS estates — hospital grounds are smokefree, and inpatient admission is an opportunity for smoking cessation intervention (Very Brief Advice)
  • Tobacco and Vapes Bill (2024) — introduces a progressive smokefree generation (those born on or after 1 January 2009 will never legally be able to purchase tobacco); also includes restrictions on vaping products marketed to children
  • Stop smoking services — NHS-funded behavioural support and pharmacotherapy (nicotine replacement therapy, varenicline, bupropion)
  • Clinicians should offer smoking cessation support at every opportunity — "Every Contact Counts"

Alcohol

Alcohol is the fifth largest risk factor for disease globally and is socially patterned — heavy harmful drinking is more prevalent in deprived communities, though alcohol misuse occurs across all socioeconomic groups.

  • AUDIT-C: a three-question validated screening tool for hazardous alcohol use, suitable for use in primary care
  • CAGE: a four-question tool (Cut down, Annoyed, Guilty, Eye-opener) used to screen for alcohol dependence
  • Brief interventions: a short structured conversation with a patient identified as drinking at hazardous or harmful levels — evidence-based, effective in reducing consumption, feasible in primary care
  • Safeguarding considerations: alcohol dependence in a parent or carer is a significant risk factor for child harm — practitioners must consider the welfare of any children in the household
  • Referral pathways: community alcohol teams, structured community detoxification (with chlordiazepoxide regimen), inpatient detoxification for those at high risk of complicated withdrawal (Wernicke's encephalopathy — thiamine must always be given before glucose)
  • Minimum unit pricing: Scotland introduced a minimum price of 50p per unit of alcohol in 2018. Evaluations show a reduction in alcohol-specific deaths, particularly from liver disease in heavy drinkers — a success of pricing as a population-level intervention

Epidemiological Foundations for the UKMLA AKT

Public health questions in the AKT require you to interpret statistical outputs from epidemiological studies. The following concepts are essential.

Study Design Hierarchy

The hierarchy of evidence, from highest to lowest quality for establishing causation:

  • Systematic review and meta-analysis — pools data from multiple high-quality studies; sits at the top of the evidence hierarchy when well-conducted
  • Randomised controlled trial (RCT) — the gold standard for establishing causation; randomisation eliminates confounding at the group level
  • Cohort study — follows a group over time; good for studying rare exposures and multiple outcomes; can establish temporality; susceptible to confounding and loss to follow-up
  • Case-control study — identifies cases (people with the outcome) and controls (without); compares prior exposure; efficient for rare outcomes and long latency diseases; susceptible to recall bias and selection bias
  • Cross-sectional study — snapshot in time; measures prevalence; cannot establish temporality; useful for planning services and generating hypotheses
  • Ecological study — uses group-level rather than individual data; prone to the ecological fallacy (associations at group level do not necessarily apply to individuals)

Measures of Association

  • Relative risk (RR): the risk of an outcome in the exposed group divided by the risk in the unexposed group. Used in cohort studies and RCTs. RR = 1 means no association; RR > 1 means the exposure is associated with increased risk; RR < 1 means the exposure is protective.
  • Odds ratio (OR): the odds of exposure among cases divided by the odds of exposure among controls. Used in case-control studies. When the outcome is rare, OR approximates RR.
  • Number needed to treat (NNT): 1 / absolute risk reduction. The number of patients who need to receive a treatment for one additional patient to benefit. Lower NNT = more effective intervention.
  • Number needed to harm (NNH): 1 / absolute risk increase. The number of patients who need to receive an intervention for one additional patient to be harmed.
  • Population attributable risk (PAR): the proportion of the disease in the total population attributable to the exposure. Important for population health decisions — even a modest exposure with high population prevalence can have a large PAR.
  • Sensitivity: the ability of a test to correctly identify those with the disease (true positive rate). A highly sensitive test misses few cases — useful for ruling out disease ("SnNout" — Sensitive, Negative rules out).
  • Specificity: the ability of a test to correctly identify those without the disease (true negative rate). A highly specific test produces few false positives — useful for ruling in disease ("SpPin" — Specific, Positive rules in).

Confounding and Bias

  • Confounding: a confounder is a variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, and which distorts the apparent relationship between them. For example, in a study of alcohol and lung cancer, smoking is a confounder (smokers drink more; smokers get lung cancer). Confounding can be controlled at the design stage (randomisation, restriction, matching) or at the analysis stage (stratification, multivariable regression).
  • Selection bias: occurs when the study population is not representative of the target population — e.g., healthy worker effect (workers are healthier than the general population because sick people are less likely to be employed).
  • Information bias / measurement bias: systematic error in measuring exposure or outcome. Includes:
    • Recall bias: cases (people with a disease) remember past exposures more accurately or vividly than controls — particularly relevant in case-control studies
    • Observer bias: the assessor's knowledge of the patient's exposure or disease status influences their measurements or interpretation

Bradford Hill Criteria for Causation

Sir Austin Bradford Hill proposed nine criteria to help assess whether an observed statistical association represents a causal relationship:

  • Strength of association — large relative risks are less likely to be due to confounding
  • Consistency — the association is replicated in different populations and settings
  • Specificity — one exposure causes one specific disease
  • Temporality — exposure must precede outcome (the only criterion that is absolutely required for causation)
  • Biological gradient (dose-response) — greater exposure leads to greater effect
  • Plausibility — the association makes biological sense
  • Coherence — the association fits with existing knowledge about the disease
  • Experiment — the association can be tested experimentally (e.g., removing the exposure reduces disease)
  • Analogy — similar exposures have similar effects (e.g., if one drug of a class causes a defect, it is plausible that related drugs might too)

No single criterion is sufficient alone. Temporality is the only essential criterion — an exposure must precede the outcome for a causal relationship to be possible.

Key Points Summary

  • The social gradient means health inequalities affect the whole population, not just the very poor — the Marmot Reviews established this and called for action across all policy domains
  • The Dahlgren-Whitehead rainbow model shows that behaviour sits within, and is constrained by, material and structural forces — blaming individuals for unhealthy behaviours without addressing those forces is ineffective and unjust
  • The UK has a 10-year life expectancy gap and a 20-year healthy life expectancy gap between the most and least deprived deciles — these figures have not improved meaningfully in the last decade
  • The Inverse Care Law — those with greatest health need receive least care — is a consequence of how healthcare resources have been distributed historically
  • Core20PLUS5 is the NHS framework to address inequalities: targeting the most deprived 20% plus inclusion health groups across five clinical priority areas
  • Black women are 3.7 times more likely to die in pregnancy than White women — structural racism is a healthcare quality and safety issue, not merely a social one
  • Inclusion health populations — people experiencing homelessness, Travellers, sex workers, people with multiple disadvantage, and asylum seekers — have the most extreme unmet health needs and the greatest barriers to care
  • Prevention operates at three levels: primary (prevent onset), secondary (detect early), and tertiary (reduce impact of established disease)
  • For the AKT, know your study designs, how to interpret RR, OR, NNT, sensitivity and specificity, and the key sources of bias and confounding
  • Temporality is the only absolute requirement for causation in the Bradford Hill criteria
