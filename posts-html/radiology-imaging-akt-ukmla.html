Radiology and medical imaging for the UKMLA AKT is a topic area that consistently surprises candidates who underestimate its scope — it is not simply about recognising a pneumothorax on a chest X-ray, but extends to understanding which imaging modality to request, when to avoid contrast, how to interpret common CT and MRI findings, and how the IRMER regulations govern imaging requests in clinical practice. A systematic approach to image interpretation is tested in the AKT through clinical vignettes with imaging findings, and the ability to request appropriate investigations is assessed throughout both papers.

Systematic Chest X-Ray Interpretation

The chest X-ray (CXR) is the most commonly requested radiological investigation in hospital medicine and is heavily tested in the UKMLA. A systematic approach prevents missed diagnoses and is expected in both AKT extended matching questions and CPSA data interpretation stations. The most widely taught mnemonic for CXR interpretation is ABCDE: Airway, Breathing, Cardiac, Diaphragm and below, Everything else.

A — Airway

Assess the trachea: it should be central, or very slightly deviated to the right in normal adults due to the aortic arch. Significant tracheal deviation is an important sign: deviation away from the affected side suggests a tension pneumothorax, large pleural effusion, or haemothorax pushing the mediastinum away; deviation towards the affected side suggests lobar collapse or pulmonary fibrosis pulling the mediastinum across. Check the carina — the angle of the carina (normally less than 70 degrees) widens with left atrial enlargement as the left main bronchus is pushed inferiorly. Confirm an endotracheal tube tip (if present) is at or just below the clavicular heads, above the carina — ideally 3 to 5 cm above the carina.

B — Breathing (Lung Fields)

Compare both lung fields systematically, starting at the apices and working down. Normal lung appears uniformly black (air-filled) with visible lung markings extending to the periphery. Key abnormal findings:

Consolidation: increased opacity (white) within the lung fields with air bronchograms (visible air in the bronchi within consolidated lung — confirms the opacity is in the lung parenchyma, not in the pleural space). Consolidation has a non-geometric pattern that follows the shape of the lobe or segment. Common causes: bacterial pneumonia (lobar or segmental), aspiration pneumonia (right lower lobe most common due to the vertical right main bronchus), pulmonary infarction from PE (classically a "Hampton's hump" — a wedge-shaped peripheral opacity abutting the pleura).

Pneumothorax: absence of lung markings beyond a visible white pleural line. The lung edge is seen as a sharply defined white line with no lung markings beyond it. On an expiratory film, a small pneumothorax is more easily seen. Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis: the patient is haemodynamically compromised with tracheal deviation away from the pneumothorax and absent breath sounds. Do not wait for a CXR if tension pneumothorax is suspected — immediate needle decompression in the second intercostal space, mid-clavicular line.

Pleural effusion: a pleural effusion appears as increased opacity in the lower zones. A small effusion (above 200 mL) blunts the costophrenic angle on the PA film. A moderate effusion shows a meniscus sign — a curved upper border of the opacity, highest laterally and lowest medially, due to the capillary action of fluid along the pleural surface. A large effusion causes a completely white hemithorax. On a supine film (AP portable CXR), a pleural effusion causes diffuse haziness over the lung rather than a meniscus, because the fluid layers posteriorly.

Pulmonary oedema: cardiogenic pulmonary oedema has a characteristic appearance. Upper lobe blood diversion (vessels in the upper zones appear larger than those in the lower zones — the reverse of normal). Kerley B lines: short horizontal white lines perpendicular to the pleural surface at the lung periphery (costophrenic angles), representing oedema in the interlobular septa. Bat-wing or perihilar oedema: bilateral, confluent, perihilar opacities giving a characteristic bat-wing appearance. Pleural effusions (bilateral). Cardiomegaly (cardiothoracic ratio above 0.5 on a PA film).

C — Cardiac

Assess cardiac size: cardiothoracic ratio (CTR) is the maximum transverse cardiac diameter divided by the maximum transverse thoracic diameter on a PA film. Normal CTR is below 0.5. Cardiomegaly (CTR above 0.5) suggests cardiac failure, pericardial effusion, or dilated cardiomyopathy. Pericardial effusion: the cardiac silhouette is enlarged with a globular, flask-shaped appearance and clear lung fields (important distinction from pulmonary oedema, which would show bilateral lung changes).

Mediastinal widening: a widened mediastinum (more than 8 cm at the level of the aortic knuckle on a PA film, or any mediastinal widening on a portable AP film which artificially magnifies the mediastinum) raises the concern for aortic dissection. Other causes include thoracic aortic aneurysm, mediastinal lymphadenopathy (malignancy, sarcoidosis, infection), and superior vena cava obstruction.

D — Diaphragm and Below

Both hemidiaphragms should be visible and dome-shaped. The right hemidiaphragm is usually slightly higher than the left (due to the liver below). Loss of the diaphragmatic outline (silhouette sign) indicates adjacent consolidation — loss of the right lower hemidiaphragm outline suggests right lower lobe consolidation; loss of the left heart border outline suggests lingular (left upper lobe) consolidation. Free air under the diaphragm (pneumoperitoneum) on an erect CXR or erect AXR appears as a crescent of air beneath the diaphragm — caused by bowel perforation. Check the subdiaphragmatic area for air and the costophrenic angles for pleural fluid.

E — Everything Else

Bones: look systematically at all visible ribs, clavicles, and vertebrae for fractures, lytic lesions, or sclerotic deposits. Rib fractures in unusual patterns (posterior rib fractures, multiple ribs at different stages of healing) should raise concern for non-accidental injury in children. Sclerotic (dense, white) lesions in vertebral bodies — consider bony metastases (blastic — prostate, breast). Lytic (dark) vertebral lesions — myeloma, lytic metastases. Soft tissues: check for surgical emphysema (subcutaneous air — feels crepitant clinically, appears as air tracking in the soft tissues on CXR), pacemaker leads and devices, surgical clips, and indwelling lines (central line tip should be in the SVC, not in the right ventricle — risk of arrhythmia if too far).

Abdominal X-Ray Interpretation

The abdominal X-ray (AXR) is less commonly requested now that CT scanning is so accessible, but it remains useful in specific situations and is tested in the AKT. Key findings:

Bowel Gas Pattern

Normal bowel contains small amounts of gas in the stomach and colon. The small bowel is centrally located, has a valvulae conniventes (circular folds that go completely across the bowel lumen), and is typically less than 3 cm in diameter. The large bowel is peripheral (frames the abdomen), has haustral folds (semilunar folds that do not cross the lumen completely), and is typically less than 6 cm in diameter (caecum less than 9 cm). Dilated small bowel (above 3 cm) with multiple air-fluid levels suggests small bowel obstruction. Dilated large bowel (above 6 cm) without small bowel dilatation suggests large bowel obstruction or pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie's syndrome). In sigmoid volvulus, the dilated sigmoid colon has a characteristic coffee bean or inverted U shape arising from the pelvis. In caecal volvulus, a dilated caecum appears in the left upper quadrant or central abdomen.

Rigler Sign (Double Wall Sign)

In a normal AXR, only the inner (mucosal) wall of the bowel is visible because there is no air outside the bowel. In pneumoperitoneum (free intraperitoneal air), both the inner and outer bowel walls become visible because air on both sides of the wall outlines both surfaces — this is the Rigler sign. It indicates bowel perforation until proved otherwise.

Calcification

Renal calculi: approximately 90% of renal calculi are radio-opaque (calcium-containing stones) and visible on AXR — they appear as dense white opacities overlying the renal shadow, proximal ureter, or urinary bladder. Uric acid stones are radiolucent (not visible on AXR) and require CT KUB. Gallstones: only approximately 10 to 20% of gallstones are radio-opaque; most are not visible on AXR. CT or ultrasound is the appropriate investigation for suspected biliary pathology. Vascular calcification: linear calcification in the aortic wall or iliac vessels indicates atherosclerosis. An abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) may be visible as a large oval area of calcification in the midline; however, ultrasound is the definitive investigation for AAA diagnosis and screening.

CT Head Interpretation

CT head is the first-line imaging investigation for acute neurological emergencies in adults. It is performed without contrast in the acute setting (haemorrhage appears hyperdense on unenhanced CT). The key principle: bright (hyperdense) = acute blood; dark (hypodense) = infarct, oedema, or chronic change.

Types of Intracranial Haemorrhage

Extradural haematoma (EDH): biconvex (lens-shaped) hyperdense collection between the skull and the dura. It does not cross suture lines (the dura is tightly adherent to the skull at sutures). Classically caused by a blow to the temporal region rupturing the middle meningeal artery — typically follows a lucid interval (brief loss of consciousness → recovery → rapid deterioration as haematoma expands). Subdural haematoma (SDH): crescent-shaped hyperdense collection following the brain surface, crossing suture lines but not crossing the midline. Caused by bridging vein rupture — often in elderly patients (cerebral atrophy creates tension on bridging veins) after minor head trauma. Acute SDH is hyperdense; chronic SDH (weeks old) is hypodense or isodense to brain parenchyma and can be missed. Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH): blood in the subarachnoid space appears as high-density material filling the cisterns and sulci — the basal cisterns, Sylvian fissures, and interhemispheric fissure are the first areas to fill. Star-shaped pattern of blood filling the basal cisterns. CT is approximately 98% sensitive for SAH in the first six hours but sensitivity falls to approximately 85 to 90% at twelve hours and continues to decline. LP at twelve hours (looking for xanthochromia) is used if CT is negative but SAH remains clinically suspected. Most commonly caused by ruptured berry aneurysm at circle of Willis branch points. Intracerebral haematoma (ICH): a focal, homogeneous, hyperdense lesion within the brain parenchyma. Location provides important clues: hypertensive ICH typically occurs in the basal ganglia (putamen most common), thalamus, cerebellum, and pons. Lobar haematomas raise concern for underlying structural lesion (tumour, AVM, amyloid angiopathy) and may require contrast-enhanced CT or MRI for further evaluation.

Ischaemic Changes on CT Head

In the first three to six hours after ischaemic stroke, CT may appear normal or show only subtle early changes: loss of the grey-white matter differentiation (hypodense cortex and underlying white matter become indistinct), loss of the insular ribbon (normal thin hyperdense insular cortex becomes isodense to underlying white matter in MCA territory infarcts), and the hyperdense MCA sign (thrombus in the MCA appears as a hyperdense vessel compared to the contralateral side). By twelve to twenty-four hours, a clear area of low attenuation (hypodensity) becomes visible in the affected territory. Established infarct areas become progressively more hypodense, with mass effect (swelling), and cortical gyral enhancement on contrast CT due to blood-brain barrier breakdown.

Midline Shift and Herniation

The midline structures (falx cerebri, third ventricle, pineal gland) should be at the centre of the cranium. Midline shift indicates significant mass effect from a haematoma, large infarct with oedema, or tumour. More than 5 mm shift is clinically significant; above 10 mm is usually associated with altered consciousness. Transtentorial herniation (uncal herniation): the uncus of the temporal lobe herniates through the tentorium cerebelli, compressing the ipsilateral oculomotor nerve (CN III) — producing a dilated, unreactive ipsilateral pupil — and the ipsilateral cerebral peduncle, causing contralateral weakness (then may compress the contralateral peduncle against the tentorium — false localising sign — producing ipsilateral weakness). Tonsillar herniation (coning): the cerebellar tonsils herniate through the foramen magnum, compressing the medulla and causing cardiorespiratory arrest. This is the mechanism of death from elevated ICP.

Ultrasound

Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves and has the advantage of being radiation-free, portable, and real-time. Key applications tested in the AKT: abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) screening (UK national programme — one-time scan at age 65 for men; an AAA is defined as an aortic diameter of 3 cm or above; elective repair is considered at 5.5 cm or above); FAST scan (Focused Assessment with Sonography in Trauma) — assesses for free fluid in the pericardial sac, hepatorenal space (Morrison's pouch), splenorenal space, and pelvis (pouch of Douglas) — used in trauma to rapidly identify haemoperitoneum or haemopericardium; biliary system — first-line for suspected gallstones (sensitivity approximately 95% for gallstones; also identifies biliary dilation, cholecystitis, and common bile duct dilatation); obstetric ultrasound — dating scan at eleven to thirteen weeks (nuchal translucency for Down's syndrome risk), anomaly scan at eighteen to twenty weeks (structural defects); renal ultrasound — first-line for hydronephrosis, renal cysts, suspected renal masses, and monitoring of CKD.

Radiation Doses and Safety

Understanding relative radiation doses is essential for safe investigation requesting and is tested in the AKT. The unit of radiation dose is the millisievert (mSv). Background radiation in the UK is approximately 2.7 mSv per year. Approximate effective doses: CXR 0.02 mSv (equivalent to a few days background radiation); AXR 0.7 mSv (equivalent to approximately 3 months background); CT head 2.0 mSv (equivalent to approximately 8 months background); CT chest 7.0 mSv; CT abdomen and pelvis 10.0 mSv; PET-CT 14.1 mSv. The lifetime risk of fatal cancer from a single CT abdomen and pelvis is approximately 1 in 2000. The risk is higher in children due to greater radiosensitivity and longer life expectancy. Radiation in pregnancy: the embryo/fetus is more radiosensitive, particularly in the first trimester. Abdominal and pelvic CT during pregnancy delivers significant fetal radiation dose; alternatives (ultrasound, MRI — no ionising radiation) should be used where possible. However, if a pregnant woman has a life-threatening condition (e.g., suspected PE or trauma), CT should not be withheld.

IRMER Regulations

The Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations (IRMER) 2017 govern the use of ionising radiation in medical imaging in the UK. Three duty holders: the employer (the healthcare organisation — must have protocols and procedures); the referrer (the clinician requesting the investigation — must provide adequate clinical information to justify the request); and the practitioner (usually the radiologist — who justifies the referral and takes responsibility for the clinical indication). Key principle: every medical exposure must be individually justified — the benefit to the patient must outweigh the radiation risk. Referrers must provide adequate clinical information (history, relevant investigations, what question the imaging is designed to answer) to enable the practitioner to justify the exposure. Blanket or routine requests without clinical indication are not justifiable. The operator (usually the radiographer) is responsible for the practical conduct of the exposure.

Contrast Nephropathy Precautions

Iodinated contrast agents used in CT scanning can cause contrast-induced nephropathy (CIN), particularly in patients with pre-existing renal impairment, diabetes, dehydration, or heart failure, and in those taking nephrotoxic medications. Prevention: identify at-risk patients (eGFR below 60 mL/min/1.73m2, diabetics on metformin); stop metformin forty-eight hours before and for forty-eight hours after contrast administration (due to the risk of contrast-induced renal impairment causing metformin accumulation and subsequent lactic acidosis); IV hydration with 0.9% NaCl before and after contrast; use the lowest volume of iso-osmolar or low-osmolar contrast; withhold nephrotoxic medications where possible. N-acetylcysteine is no longer routinely recommended based on current evidence. Gadolinium-based contrast used in MRI: nephrogenic systemic fibrosis (NSF) is a rare but serious complication of gadolinium-based agents in patients with severe renal impairment (eGFR below 30 mL/min/1.73m2) — use gadolinium only if essential and inform the radiologist of renal function.

Common Emergency Imaging Decisions

The AKT frequently tests the appropriate investigation for specific clinical presentations. Key decision points: suspected PE — CTPA is first-line; V/Q scintigraphy if CTPA is contraindicated (contrast allergy, CKD, pregnancy where radiation to the chest is preferred over the abdomen). Suspected aortic dissection — CT angiogram of the thorax and abdomen (contrast-enhanced) is the investigation of choice; TOE is an alternative if CT is not immediately available or contraindicated. Acute pancreatitis — ultrasound within twenty-four hours to look for gallstones; CT with contrast at seventy-two hours if the diagnosis is uncertain or if there is clinical deterioration (to assess necrotising pancreatitis using the CT Severity Index). Head injury — NICE NG232 CT head criteria: GCS below 13 at any point, failure to reach GCS 15 within two hours, suspected open or depressed skull fracture, any sign of basal skull fracture (Battle's sign, periorbital haematoma without direct orbital trauma, CSF leakorrhea, haemotympanum), post-traumatic seizure, focal neurological deficit, or vomiting more than once. Suspected renal colic — CT KUB (unenhanced, low-dose) is the gold standard for renal calculi detection; ultrasound is the first-line modality in pregnancy and children to avoid radiation.

Frequently Asked Questions About Radiology in the UKMLA

How do I tell the difference between a pleural effusion and consolidation on a CXR?

A pleural effusion causes a homogeneous white opacity with a meniscus sign (curved upper border, highest laterally) and blunting of the costophrenic angle. The underlying lung structure is not visible. Consolidation causes a white opacity with air bronchograms visible within it (air in the bronchi outlined by the surrounding opaque consolidated lung), and the opacity tends to be more heterogeneous. The silhouette sign helps localise consolidation: if the right heart border is lost, the consolidation is in the right middle lobe (which normally touches the right heart border); if the right hemidiaphragm outline is lost, the consolidation is in the right lower lobe.

What is the difference between an extradural and subdural haematoma on CT?

An extradural haematoma (EDH) is biconvex (lens-shaped), hyperdense, and does not cross suture lines. It is typically caused by arterial bleeding (middle meningeal artery) following temporal head trauma. A subdural haematoma (SDH) is crescent-shaped, follows the brain surface, and crosses suture lines. It is caused by venous bleeding (bridging veins) and is more common in the elderly and those taking anticoagulants. An acute SDH is hyperdense on CT; a chronic SDH (more than two to three weeks old) is hypodense (appears dark) and can blend with the cortex, making it easy to miss.

When should MRI be preferred over CT for a neurological presentation?

MRI is preferred when superior soft tissue resolution is needed, when the posterior fossa is being assessed (CT has artefact here), when spinal cord pathology is suspected, when demyelinating disease (MS) is being evaluated, when a cerebral abscess needs characterisation (diffusion restriction), or when there is a need to avoid ionising radiation (pregnancy, children). CT remains preferred in acute presentations requiring rapid imaging (acute haemorrhage, trauma, PE, aortic dissection), as MRI is slower and has contraindications including pacemakers and some metallic implants.

What are the NICE criteria for CT head after head injury?

NICE NG232 recommends CT head within one hour of presentation for any of: GCS below 13 at initial assessment, GCS below 15 at two hours post-injury, suspected open or depressed skull fracture, any clinical signs of basal skull fracture, post-traumatic seizure, focal neurological deficit, or more than one episode of vomiting. CT within eight hours is recommended for those over sixty-five with any loss of consciousness or amnesia, a dangerous mechanism of injury (pedestrian or cyclist struck by vehicle, ejection from vehicle, fall above one metre), or amnesia for more than thirty minutes before the impact.

What does a Kerley B line look like and what does it mean?

Kerley B lines are short (1 to 2 cm), horizontal, white lines perpendicular to and touching the pleural surface, seen at the lateral lung bases on a CXR. They represent thickened interlobular septa due to interstitial oedema. They are a classic feature of cardiogenic pulmonary oedema and indicate an elevated pulmonary venous pressure. They can also be seen in lymphangitis carcinomatosa (tumour infiltrating the lymphatics) and rarely in other interstitial lung diseases.
