Rheumatology for the UKMLA: A Complete Revision Guide

Rheumatology is a specialty rich with clinical reasoning, pattern recognition, and guideline application — exactly what the UKMLA AKT tests. From autoimmune conditions with multi-system manifestations to degenerative joint disease management, this guide covers every major rheumatological condition on the UKMLA syllabus. This is one of the areas where understanding diagnostic criteria and distinguishing between similar conditions pays the highest dividends in the exam.

Before diving in, review our UKMLA overview and the full syllabus to understand how rheumatology fits within the broader exam blueprint.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic systemic inflammatory disease characterised by symmetrical polyarthritis affecting small joints, with extra-articular manifestations. It affects approximately 1% of the UK population, with a female:male ratio of 3:1 and peak onset in the 4th–5th decade.

2010 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria

The 2010 criteria replace the older 1987 criteria and are better at identifying early RA. A score ≥6/10 points classifies definite RA:

  • Joint involvement (0–5): 1 medium-large joint = 0; 2–10 medium-large = 1; 1–3 small joints = 2; 4–10 small joints = 3; >10 joints including at least 1 small joint = 5
  • Serology (0–3): Negative RF and anti-CCP = 0; low positive = 2; high positive = 3
  • Acute phase reactants (0–1): Normal CRP/ESR = 0; abnormal = 1
  • Duration of symptoms (0–1): <6 weeks = 0; ≥6 weeks = 1

Anti-CCP (anti-citrullinated protein antibodies) are more specific for RA than rheumatoid factor (RF) and are associated with more erosive, severe disease. RF is positive in ~70–80% of RA but also found in Sjögren syndrome, SLE, chronic infections, and normal elderly. Anti-CCP is positive in ~75–80% of RA with specificity ~95%.

DMARD Therapy

Methotrexate is first-line DMARD for RA. Weekly oral or subcutaneous administration (7.5–25mg weekly). Folic acid 5mg weekly (not same day as methotrexate) reduces side effects. Monitoring: FBC and LFTs every 2–3 months. Contraindicated in pregnancy, significant hepatic or renal impairment, immunodeficiency. Side effects: hepatotoxicity, myelosuppression, mucositis, interstitial lung disease (ILD), teratogenicity.

Other conventional DMARDs: hydroxychloroquine (antimalarial, safest — requires annual eye monitoring for maculopathy), sulfasalazine, leflunomide.

Combination DMARD therapy (typically methotrexate + sulfasalazine + hydroxychloroquine — "triple therapy") is recommended if single DMARD fails.

Biologic and Targeted Therapies

Indicated for moderate-to-severe RA failing conventional DMARDs (DAS28 >5.1 with inadequate response to 2 conventional DMARDs including methotrexate).

  • TNF inhibitors: Adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab, golimumab. Screen for latent TB before starting (tuberculin skin test or IGRA). Contraindicated in active infection, heart failure (NYHA III/IV), demyelinating disease, malignancy in last 5 years.
  • IL-6 inhibitors: Tocilizumab, sarilumab. Can mask infection (CRP suppressed).
  • JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib, baricitinib, upadacitinib. Oral targeted synthetic DMARDs. Black box warnings: VTE risk, malignancy, serious infections, MACE. Check lipids at initiation.
  • Rituximab: B-cell depletion, given in 2-cycle infusion, reserved for seropositive RA or where biologics failed/contraindicated.
  • Abatacept: T-cell costimulation blocker.

DAS28 Score

Disease Activity Score 28 assesses 28 joints for tenderness and swelling, plus ESR or CRP, and patient global assessment. Thresholds: remission <2.6; low disease activity 2.6–3.2; moderate 3.2–5.1; high >5.1. NICE uses DAS28 >5.1 as threshold for biologic eligibility.

Osteoarthritis

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterised by cartilage loss, subchondral bone changes, osteophyte formation, and synovial inflammation. It is the most common musculoskeletal condition globally. Risk factors: age, female sex, obesity, previous joint injury, repetitive loading, genetic predisposition.

NICE NG226 (2022) Management

NICE 2022 emphasises a biopsychosocial approach:

  1. Education and self-management: Explain OA is not just "wear and tear" and that exercise helps.
  2. Exercise: Aerobic and strengthening exercise is the cornerstone of OA management — reduces pain and improves function regardless of severity. Refer to physiotherapy or group exercise programme.
  3. Weight management: Particularly important for hip and knee OA — every 1kg weight loss reduces knee load by 4kg.
  4. Analgesia: Topical NSAIDs first (better safety profile than oral, especially for knee/hand OA). Paracetamol has limited evidence for sustained benefit — do not use as regular long-term treatment in isolation. Oral NSAIDs for short-term pain relief with lowest effective dose and PPI gastroprotection. Weak opioids only if other options inadequate. Capsaicin cream for hand/knee OA.
  5. Intra-articular injections: Corticosteroid injections for flares — short-term benefit. Hyaluronic acid injections: NICE does not routinely recommend but some evidence for knee OA.
  6. Surgery: Joint replacement for severe OA with significant pain and functional limitation not responding to conservative measures. Oxford score used to assess eligibility. Arthroscopic lavage and debridement not recommended.

Gout — NICE NG219 (2022)

Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in adults. Caused by monosodium urate crystal deposition in joints and soft tissues due to hyperuricaemia. Risk factors: male sex, obesity, CKD, hypertension, diuretics (particularly thiazides and loop diuretics), alcohol (especially beer and spirits), red meat and organ meat intake, fructose-rich drinks, ciclosporin, low-dose aspirin.

Acute Gout Management

Acute attack: intensely painful, hot, swollen joint — first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra) most classic, also ankle, knee, wrist.

  • NSAIDs (naproxen or indomethacin): First-line if no contraindication (not in CKD, peptic ulcer disease, anticoagulation). Give at maximum dose for 2–5 days.
  • Colchicine: Alternative to NSAIDs, particularly useful if renal impairment mild/moderate. 500 micrograms 2–4 times daily. Avoid in severe renal impairment (eGFR <10). GI side effects (diarrhoea) common.
  • Systemic corticosteroids: If NSAIDs and colchicine contraindicated. Prednisolone 30mg daily for 3–5 days or intra-articular steroid injection.
  • Do NOT start urate-lowering therapy during an acute attack (may prolong it). Do NOT stop ongoing urate-lowering therapy during an acute attack.

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT) — Prophylaxis

Indications: ≥2 attacks per year, tophaceous gout, gout with CKD, gout with renal calculi. NICE NG219 recommends offering ULT after first attack if CKD, hypertension, or other risk factors are present.

  • Allopurinol: Xanthine oxidase inhibitor — first-line ULT. Start at low dose (100mg daily, 50mg in CKD) at least 2–4 weeks after acute attack settles. Titrate monthly to achieve target serum urate <360 µmol/L (<300 µmol/L for tophaceous gout). Co-prescribe low-dose colchicine or NSAID for 6 months to prevent flares during initiation. HLA-B*5801 screening before allopurinol in those of Han Chinese, Korean, or Thai ethnicity (risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome).
  • Febuxostat: Alternative if allopurinol intolerant or ineffective. NICE: use with caution in cardiovascular disease (FAST trial showed non-inferiority to allopurinol on CV outcomes but slight excess all-cause mortality).
  • Lifestyle: Adequate hydration, reduce alcohol, reduce purine-rich foods (offal, red meat, shellfish, anchovies), avoid fructose-rich drinks.

Psoriatic Arthritis

Inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis (~30% of psoriasis patients). Can precede skin disease. Seronegative (RF negative). Classified by CASPAR criteria (must have inflammatory articular disease plus ≥3 points from: current psoriasis = 2 points; personal/family history psoriasis = 1; nail dystrophy = 1; negative RF = 1; current dactylitis or history = 1; juxta-articular new bone on X-ray = 1).

Five clinical subtypes: DIP joint involvement (characteristic — nail pitting, onycholysis), asymmetric oligoarthritis, symmetric polyarthritis (RA-like), spondylitis, and arthritis mutilans (most destructive — "pencil-in-cup" deformity on X-ray).

Features distinguishing from RA: nail involvement, DIP involvement, asymmetry, enthesitis (inflammation at tendon/ligament insertions — Achilles, plantar fascia), dactylitis ("sausage digit" — diffuse swelling of whole digit due to tenosynovitis), psoriatic skin/nail changes, RF negative.

Treatment: conventional DMARDs (methotrexate, leflunomide), TNF inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab — very effective for psoriasis and arthritis), IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab), JAK inhibitors.

Ankylosing Spondylitis (Axial Spondyloarthropathy)

Chronic inflammatory disease primarily affecting the axial skeleton (sacroiliac joints and spine). Male predominance (3:1). Age of onset typically 20–40 years. Strong association with HLA-B27 (present in ~90% of AS patients vs ~8% of general population).

Clinical features: Insidious onset of inflammatory back pain (worse at night and in morning, improves with activity, not rest), progressive stiffness, restricted spinal mobility (modified Schober test: mark L5 and 10cm above — normal increase ≥5cm on full forward flexion; <4cm suggests restriction), reduced chest expansion (<5cm), uveitis (25%), enthesitis (Achilles, plantar fascia), aortic regurgitation (late complication).

Investigations: X-ray shows sacroiliitis (erosions, sclerosis, eventual fusion), syndesmophytes (bony bridges between vertebrae — bamboo spine on X-ray is late sign). MRI is more sensitive for early sacroiliitis before X-ray changes (shows bone marrow oedema).

Management: Physiotherapy is cornerstone — exercises maintain spinal mobility. NSAIDs (regular, not just PRN — disease-modifying effect in early AS). TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab) for active disease failing NSAIDs (NICE criteria: BASDAI ≥4 on 2 occasions 4 weeks apart, failed 2 NSAIDs). IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) for axial disease. Conventional DMARDs (methotrexate) not effective for axial manifestations.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

SLE is a multisystem autoimmune condition characterised by autoantibody production and immune complex deposition. Female:male 9:1. Peak onset reproductive years. More common and severe in Black Caribbean and South Asian populations.

2019 EULAR/ACR Classification Criteria

ANA (antinuclear antibody) titre ≥1:80 is the entry criterion (sensitivity ~98%). Weighted scoring across seven domains: constitutional, haematological, neuropsychiatric, mucocutaneous, serosal, musculoskeletal, and renal. Score ≥10 classifies SLE.

Clinical Features

  • Mucocutaneous: Malar rash (butterfly rash — spares nasolabial folds), discoid rash (scarring, atrophic), photosensitivity, oral ulcers, alopecia
  • Musculoskeletal: Arthralgia/arthritis (non-erosive in SLE, unlike RA)
  • Serositis: Pleuritis, pericarditis
  • Renal: Lupus nephritis — commonest cause of serious morbidity/mortality. Class III/IV most severe (proliferative nephritis)
  • Neuropsychiatric: Seizures, psychosis, stroke (especially with antiphospholipid syndrome)
  • Haematological: Haemolytic anaemia, leucopaenia, thrombocytopaenia

Autoantibodies in SLE

  • Anti-dsDNA: Highly specific for SLE (~70% sensitivity, ~95% specificity). Titres correlate with disease activity, especially lupus nephritis.
  • Anti-Smith (Sm): Highly specific (~99%) but low sensitivity (~30%). Pathognomonic of SLE.
  • Anti-Ro (SSA) and anti-La (SSB): Associated with neonatal lupus (congenital heart block — screen all anti-Ro positive pregnant women) and Sjögren overlap.
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies (anticardiolipin, anti-β2-glycoprotein I, lupus anticoagulant): Present in ~30% of SLE; associated with thrombosis and miscarriage.
  • Complement: C3 and C4 consumed during active SLE (low levels indicate active disease).

Treatment

  • Hydroxychloroquine: All patients with SLE unless contraindicated. Reduces flares, damage accrual, and mortality. Annual ophthalmology review for maculopathy (dose-dependent).
  • NSAIDs: For musculoskeletal symptoms and serositis — use with caution given renal involvement.
  • Corticosteroids: For flares and organ-threatening disease.
  • Immunosuppressants: Azathioprine or mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) for maintenance. Cyclophosphamide (IV pulses) for severe lupus nephritis class III/IV — given with mesna uroprotection.
  • Belimumab: Anti-BLYS biologic for persistently active seropositive SLE despite standard therapy.
  • Anifrolumab: Type I interferon receptor antagonist — newer biologic for moderate-to-severe SLE.

Polymyalgia Rheumatica and Giant Cell Arteritis

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Inflammatory condition affecting adults >50 years (rarely <50). Female predominance. Presents with: bilateral shoulder and/or pelvic girdle pain and stiffness, worse in morning (>45 minutes), systemic symptoms (fatigue, weight loss, low-grade fever). ESR typically >50 mm/hr, CRP elevated, CK normal (distinguishes from myositis). Bursitis on USS (typically bicipital and subdeltoid bursae in shoulders, trochanteric bursae in hips).

Treatment: Prednisolone 15mg daily — dramatic response within days (failure to respond should prompt diagnostic reconsideration). Gradually taper over 18–24 months. Monitor for relapse (rising ESR/CRP, return of symptoms).

Giant Cell Arteritis (Temporal/Cranial Arteritis)

Large vessel vasculitis affecting branches of external carotid artery and aorta. Occurs almost exclusively over 50 years. Features: new severe headache (temporal, occipital), scalp tenderness (difficulty combing hair), jaw claudication (highly specific — pain on chewing), visual loss (ischaemic optic neuropathy — amaurosis fugax may precede permanent blindness), diplopia, constitutional symptoms. 50% coexist with PMR.

Investigation: ESR typically >80–100 mm/hr, CRP markedly elevated. Temporal artery biopsy (TAB): skip lesions mean negative biopsy does not exclude diagnosis — treat empirically if clinical suspicion high. PET-CT or MRA for large vessel involvement.

Treatment: Prednisolone 60mg daily IMMEDIATELY if visual symptoms — do not delay for biopsy. Biopsy remains positive for 2 weeks after starting steroids. For visual loss: IV methylprednisolone 500–1g for 3 days. Low-dose aspirin. Tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor) is licensed for GCA as steroid-sparing agent.

Reactive Arthritis

Previously called Reiter syndrome. Post-infectious arthritis developing 1–4 weeks after urogenital (Chlamydia trachomatis) or gastrointestinal (Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia) infection. Classic triad: urethritis, arthritis, conjunctivitis — "can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree." Asymmetric oligoarthritis, predominantly lower limbs. HLA-B27 associated. Usually self-limiting over weeks to months. NSAIDs for symptom control. DMARDs if chronic (>6 months).

Fibromyalgia

Chronic widespread pain syndrome without structural joint disease or inflammation. 2010/2016 ACR diagnostic criteria: Widespread Pain Index (WPI) ≥7 + Symptom Severity Score (SSS) ≥5, or WPI 4–6 + SSS ≥9, symptoms present >3 months, no other disorder explaining pain. Associated features: fatigue, non-restorative sleep, cognitive symptoms ("fibro fog"), headache, IBS, anxiety/depression.

Management: Structured exercise (most evidence — aerobic and strengthening). CBT to address pain catastrophising and improve coping. Sleep hygiene. Low-dose amitriptyline or duloxetine for sleep/pain. Pregabalin — licensed in US but not recommended by NICE for fibromyalgia in UK. Avoid opioids — no evidence of benefit, risk of dependency and worsening central sensitisation. Avoid polypharmacy. Patient education and self-management groups.

FAQ: Rheumatology for the UKMLA

What is the most specific antibody for SLE?

Anti-Smith (Sm) antibodies are the most specific (~99%) for SLE, though present in only ~30% of cases (low sensitivity). Anti-dsDNA antibodies are also highly specific (~95%) and are clinically useful because titres correlate with disease activity, particularly lupus nephritis. ANA is highly sensitive (~98%) but not specific — it is the entry criterion for the 2019 classification criteria.

What is the first-line treatment for an acute gout attack according to NICE NG219 2022?

NSAIDs (naproxen or indomethacin at maximum tolerated dose) are first-line for acute gout. Colchicine is an alternative, particularly when NSAIDs are contraindicated (renal impairment, GI history, anticoagulation). Corticosteroids are used if both NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated. Urate-lowering therapy should NOT be started during an acute attack but should not be stopped if already prescribed.

What distinguishes Graves ophthalmopathy and when does it affect treatment choice?

Graves ophthalmopathy (thyroid eye disease) is specific to Graves disease and is caused by TSH receptor antibody stimulation of orbital fibroblasts. Radioiodine can worsen ophthalmopathy — if active or severe Graves eye disease is present, radioiodine should be avoided or used with concomitant corticosteroids. Carbimazole is preferred in this setting. Active ophthalmopathy is treated with IV methylprednisolone and selenium supplementation; severe cases may require orbital decompression.

How is ankylosing spondylitis distinguished from mechanical back pain?

Inflammatory back pain (suggesting AS) has these features: onset <45 years, insidious onset, improves with exercise not rest, worse at night (wakes patient), morning stiffness >30–60 minutes, and good response to NSAIDs. Mechanical back pain is worse with activity, better with rest, no morning stiffness pattern. Positive family history of spondyloarthropathy, extra-articular features (uveitis, psoriasis, IBD), and HLA-B27 positivity further support the diagnosis.

When should DMARDs be started in rheumatoid arthritis?

DMARDs should be started as soon as the diagnosis of RA is confirmed — ideally within 3 months of persistent synovitis to prevent irreversible joint damage. Methotrexate is first-line. Rapid referral to rheumatology is essential. Treat-to-target strategy: aim for remission (DAS28 <2.6) or low disease activity (DAS28 <3.2) within 6 months. NICE recommends monthly monitoring of DAS28 until target is achieved, then 3–6 monthly in remission.

What investigations should be performed before starting TNF inhibitors?

Before initiating TNF inhibitors: IGRA (interferon-gamma release assay) or tuberculin skin test (TST) to screen for latent TB — treat latent TB before starting. Hepatitis B surface antigen and hepatitis C antibody (risk of reactivation). HIV testing if risk factors. Chest X-ray. Full blood count, renal and liver function. Assess for demyelinating disease history. Assess cardiovascular risk. Review vaccination status (live vaccines contraindicated once on biologics).

Conclusion

Rheumatology rewards candidates who can distinguish between similar presentations — RA versus psoriatic arthritis, SLE versus other connective tissue diseases, septic arthritis versus crystal arthropathy. The UKMLA AKT tests these discriminating features alongside management knowledge and guideline application.

Focus on the diagnostic criteria for RA (2010 ACR/EULAR), SLE (2019 EULAR/ACR), and gout management (NICE NG219 2022) — these are particularly high yield. Understanding the biologic therapy landscape and its contraindications is increasingly tested as these treatments enter routine clinical practice.
