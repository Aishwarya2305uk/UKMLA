The English language requirement for UKMLA India catches many Indian medical graduates off guard, precisely because it seems, on the surface, like it should not apply to them at all. Medical education across India — from the MBBS curriculum to clinical postings, ward rounds, and university examinations — is conducted almost entirely in English. Consequently, a large number of Indian doctors assume that this alone should satisfy the General Medical Council (GMC) that their English is good enough to practise safely in the NHS. It does not, and understanding why is the first step in planning a realistic UKMLA timeline.

This guide sets out, in detail, exactly what the GMC expects from Indian candidates on English language testing: the two accepted tests, the exact scores required, where to sit each test across India, how long a certificate remains valid, what limited exemptions exist, and how much the whole process typically costs in rupees. It also places this requirement in context alongside the rest of the UKMLA pathway — including GMC registration and EPIC verification for Indian doctors and the broader question of whether an Indian MBBS degree is valid in the UK — so that English testing is planned as part of a single coherent pathway rather than tackled in isolation.

Because English testing is one of the earliest formal steps most Indian candidates take, getting the sequencing and score requirements right from the outset avoids one of the most common and entirely avoidable causes of delay and extra cost on the route to becoming a doctor in the UK from India: sitting the test too early, too late, or with too narrow a preparation margin around the required score. Indeed, of all the administrative hurdles on the pathway, the English language requirement for UKMLA India is arguably the one most within a candidate's direct control — unlike EPIC verification timelines, which depend heavily on how quickly a medical school's registrar responds, the timing and outcome of an English test rests almost entirely on the candidate's own preparation and planning.

This matters because Indian doctors otherwise very well prepared clinically sometimes underestimate how much dedicated, test-specific preparation the English requirement demands, treating it as a box-ticking exercise rather than as an examination in its own right. As later sections set out, both accepted tests apply a strict all-components scoring rule, meaning genuinely excellent English can still fall short of the GMC's threshold in a single sub-skill if that sub-skill has not been specifically practised under exam conditions.

Quick Answer: Do Indian Doctors Need an English Test for the UKMLA?

Yes. The English language requirement for UKMLA India applies to almost every Indian medical graduate, regardless of the fact that their medical education was conducted entirely in English. The GMC requires documentary evidence of English language proficiency from the great majority of international medical graduates (IMGs), and this evidence must come from an approved, standardised test — not from a statement about the language of instruction at your medical school, however strong your spoken and written English genuinely is in practice.

Two tests are accepted for GMC registration purposes: IELTS Academic, where the required score is an overall 7.0, with no individual component (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) below 7.0, and OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, where the required score is a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking). Meeting either of these thresholds satisfies the GMC's English language evidence requirement as part of the wider registration application. This is a genuinely common misconception among Indian candidates specifically, since it mirrors a similar misunderstanding documented among Nigerian doctors applying for GMC registration, whose medical education is likewise conducted in English yet who are still required to sit a formal test.

The remainder of this guide works through each test in detail, compares them directly, explains certificate validity and sequencing, covers the narrow exemption criteria that do exist, and sets out realistic costs and preparation timelines specifically for candidates sitting these tests from India.

Why English Language Education Alone Does Not Exempt Indian Doctors

It is worth understanding the GMC's reasoning here, because it explains why this requirement is applied so consistently. The GMC's English language evidence requirement is not really a test of whether you can read a textbook or write an exam answer in English — Indian medical graduates clearly can do both. Instead, it is designed to confirm a specific, standardised, and independently verifiable level of communicative English across four separate skills: listening comprehension under real-world conditions, reading comprehension of dense professional material, written communication suitable for clinical documentation, and — critically — spoken fluency and clarity in the kind of unscripted, two-way conversation that defines a doctor-patient consultation.

Classroom and clinical training conducted in English does not, on its own, generate independently verifiable evidence of all four of these skills to a single, comparable international standard. Furthermore, the GMC applies this requirement uniformly across IMGs from dozens of source countries with widely varying language-of-instruction arrangements; carving out an exception for Indian-trained doctors specifically would undermine the consistency of the standard the GMC is trying to apply. As a result, Indian candidates should treat the English test not as a bureaucratic formality to be waived, but as a standard, expected stage of the pathway that deserves the same serious preparation as the UKMLA examinations themselves.

The GMC publishes the definitive, current version of this requirement directly, and it should always be checked before booking a test: see the GMC's evidence of your knowledge of English guidance. This page is updated periodically, and since scores, accepted tests, and evidentiary detail can shift, it should always take precedence over summaries such as this one.

IELTS Academic: Scores, Format, and Availability in India

IELTS Academic is the more widely recognised of the two accepted tests among Indian candidates, largely because it is also the standard test used for study-abroad and skilled migration applications, meaning most Indian test-takers already have some familiarity with its format before they ever consider it for GMC purposes.

Exact IELTS Score Requirements for GMC Registration

For UKMLA and GMC registration purposes, the required IELTS Academic score is an overall band score of 7.0, and — this is the detail that trips up otherwise strong candidates — no individual component may fall below 7.0. In other words, a candidate who scores 8.5 in Reading, 8.0 in Listening, and 7.5 in Writing, but only 6.5 in Speaking, does not meet the requirement, even though their overall average comfortably exceeds 7.0. Each of the four components — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking — must independently clear the 7.0 threshold. This all-components rule is the single most common reason Indian candidates need to re-sit the test, and it is worth planning preparation time around whichever of the four skills is genuinely weakest, rather than around the test as a whole.

IELTS Academic Test Format

IELTS Academic is structured across four sections, sat in a single day for the Listening, Reading, and Writing components, with Speaking either on the same day or scheduled separately within a seven-day window depending on the test centre:

  • Listening (approximately 30 minutes): four recorded sections of increasing difficulty, covering everyday conversations, monologues, and academic discussions, each followed by a set of comprehension questions.
  • Reading (60 minutes): three long academic passages drawn from journals, books, and periodicals, testing detailed comprehension, inference, and the ability to locate specific information quickly.
  • Writing (60 minutes): two tasks — a shorter report describing a graph, chart, or diagram (Task 1), and a longer discursive essay responding to a point of view or argument (Task 2).
  • Speaking (11 to 14 minutes): a face-to-face or video-call interview with a certified examiner, covering general questions about familiar topics, a two-minute individual long-turn on a given cue card, and a follow-up discussion exploring more abstract aspects of the same topic.

Consequently, IELTS Academic tests general academic English rather than healthcare-specific English, which means the subject matter in the Reading and Listening sections can range across topics as varied as environmental science, urban planning, or the history of art — content Indian medical graduates have not necessarily encountered since school, even though their command of English itself is strong.

Where Indian Candidates Can Sit IELTS Academic

IELTS is very widely available across India, with test centres operated by the British Council and IDP Education present in all major metropolitan areas — including Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Chennai, Hyderabad, Kolkata, Pune, and Ahmedabad — as well as a large number of tier-two cities. Both paper-based and computer-delivered formats are typically offered, with computer-delivered sittings generally available multiple times per week and results issued considerably faster (often within 3 to 5 days) than the paper-based format (13 days). This breadth of availability is one of IELTS Academic's genuine practical advantages for Indian candidates, since it usually means no long-distance domestic travel is required to sit the test, unlike some other stages of the UKMLA pathway.

Typical IELTS Preparation Timeline for Indian Candidates

Most Indian medical graduates with strong general English already in place need somewhere between four and twelve weeks of focused preparation to reliably clear 7.0 in all four components, though this varies considerably based on prior exposure to standardised English testing and — most importantly — which specific component is weakest. Candidates who have never taken a Speaking interview under timed, examiner-assessed conditions before often need disproportionately more practice time on that component than the raw complexity of the task would suggest, simply because the format itself is unfamiliar. Building a preparation plan around targeted practice of the specific sub-skill that is lagging, rather than generic overall revision, is consistently the more time-efficient approach.

A useful practical benchmark is to sit a full official practice test under strict timed conditions in the first week of preparation, before doing any further study. This establishes a genuine baseline band score across all four components and tells a candidate immediately whether their gap to 7.0 is a single-component problem — most commonly Speaking or Writing — or a broader issue affecting the whole test. Candidates who skip this diagnostic step and instead simply "revise generally" for several weeks often discover, only when their real result arrives, that they comfortably cleared three components but fell narrowly short in the fourth, at which point they must repeat the entire test rather than simply sharpening one skill.

OET Medicine: Scores, Format, and Availability in India

OET (Occupational English Test) is the second GMC-accepted route, and it is increasingly popular among Indian doctors specifically because its content is built entirely around real healthcare communication rather than general academic topics.

Exact OET Score Requirements for GMC Registration

For UKMLA and GMC registration purposes, OET Medicine requires a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. As with IELTS, this is an all-components requirement — a candidate scoring Grade A in three sub-tests but Grade C in one does not meet the GMC's standard, and would need to re-sit at least the sub-test that fell short (OET, unlike IELTS, does allow re-sitting individual sub-tests rather than the entire test in some circumstances, which can meaningfully reduce both cost and time for candidates who are close to the required grade in only one skill).

OET Medicine Test Format

OET's structure mirrors IELTS in broad shape — four sections covering the same four skills — but every task is built around genuine clinical scenarios relevant to practising healthcare professionals:

  • Listening (approximately 45 minutes): recordings of consultations and healthcare-related presentations, testing the ability to follow a doctor-patient conversation and extract clinically relevant detail.
  • Reading (60 minutes): healthcare-related texts, including a timed skimming/scanning task and detailed comprehension passages drawn from genuine medical and healthcare-policy material.
  • Writing (45 minutes, profession-specific): for the Medicine sub-test, candidates write a referral letter based on a set of case notes — a task that closely mirrors real clinical correspondence a junior doctor would produce in NHS practice.
  • Speaking (approximately 20 minutes, profession-specific): two role-played consultations with a trained interlocutor playing the part of a patient or relative, assessed against criteria including clinical communication, empathy, and structure — again, a close simulation of real consultation skills.

Because every task is grounded in clinical context, many practising doctors find OET considerably more intuitive than IELTS Academic. Writing a referral letter or conducting a role-played consultation draws directly on skills Indian doctors already use daily in clinical practice, whereas IELTS Academic's general-interest reading passages and discursive essay topics require a different, less familiar kind of preparation. Candidates who already think fluently in the register of clinical documentation and doctor-patient conversation — which describes most working Indian doctors — often report that OET preparation feels closer to revision than to learning an unfamiliar test format.

Where Indian Candidates Can Sit OET

OET is administered in India at test centres in major cities including Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Chennai, Hyderabad, Kochi, and several other locations, with both paper-based and computer-delivered on-screen sittings offered. Test dates are typically available multiple times per month, and computer-delivered sittings generally return results considerably faster than the paper-based format. Availability has expanded substantially in India over recent years as OET has grown in popularity among healthcare professionals applying to the UK, Australia, New Zealand, and Ireland, so candidates outside the largest metros should check current centre locations directly through OET's official booking system rather than assuming a particular city has a sitting available on a given date.

One further practical point worth noting: because OET offers profession-specific Writing and Speaking sub-tests, Indian candidates must ensure they book the Medicine version specifically, rather than the Nursing, Dentistry, or Pharmacy versions of the test, since the GMC's accepted score applies only to the Medicine sub-test content. This distinction is straightforward but occasionally overlooked by candidates booking through unfamiliar portals, and confirming the correct profession stream at the point of booking avoids a wasted sitting entirely.

Certificate Validity and Why Timing Matters

Both IELTS and OET certificates carry a defined validity period from the date of the test, and this detail matters enormously for how an Indian candidate sequences their overall UKMLA pathway. Sitting the English test too early — for example, immediately after graduation, well before EPIC verification is complete and before the AKT and CPSA are even booked — creates a real risk that the certificate will expire before the full registration application is actually submitted. Because GMC registration application timing depends on multiple parallel processes (credential verification, exam scheduling, document collection) that are not entirely within a candidate's control, a certificate sat too early can force an unplanned and entirely avoidable re-sit, adding both cost and delay at exactly the stage of the pathway when a candidate is trying to finish, not restart a step.

The safest practical approach is to treat English testing as a mid-pathway milestone rather than a first-day task: begin it once EPIC verification is underway and the AKT is being actively prepared for, so that the certificate's validity window comfortably covers the expected date of the full registration application, including a reasonable buffer for unexpected delays elsewhere in the process. The GMC's own guidance sets out the current validity period and evidentiary detail in full, and it should always be checked directly before finalising a test date: see the GMC's English language evidence requirements, since validity rules are exactly the kind of administrative detail that can be updated between the time this guide is written and the time a candidate reads it.

This sequencing question is not unique to Indian candidates — the same expiry risk is flagged for Nigerian doctors navigating the same English testing requirement — but it deserves particular attention from Indian applicants given how long primary source verification through EPIC can sometimes take, which is precisely the kind of delay that can quietly erode a certificate's remaining validity window if the test is sat too soon.

How English Testing Fits Into the Wider UKMLA Pathway From India

The English language requirement for UKMLA India does not sit in isolation — it is one stage within a longer sequence that also includes confirming basic eligibility, verifying an Indian MBBS degree through EPIC, sitting the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), and finally submitting a full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise. Understanding where English testing sits relative to these other stages is what allows a candidate to sequence it correctly, rather than treating it as a stand-alone task disconnected from the rest of the timeline.

In practice, most Indian candidates run English test preparation in parallel with the early stages of EPIC verification, since GMC registration and EPIC verification for Indian doctors can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months depending on how quickly a medical school's academic office responds to primary source verification requests. Sitting the English test during this window — rather than before EPIC has even begun, or after AKT preparation is already well underway — tends to produce the best balance between certificate validity and overall pathway efficiency, since it avoids both extremes: sitting so early that the certificate risks expiring, and sitting so late that it becomes a bottleneck immediately before the registration application.

Before committing time and money to English testing at all, however, it is worth confirming the more fundamental question of whether an Indian medical degree is accepted by the GMC in the first place. The guide to whether an Indian MBBS degree is valid in the UK sets out the specific eligibility criteria — accreditation status, World Directory of Medical Schools listing, and internship completion — that determine whether a given Indian qualification satisfies the GMC's basic threshold before English testing, EPIC verification, or exam booking become relevant at all.

Once eligibility is confirmed and English testing is under way, candidates should also begin structured preparation for the AKT itself, since the two workstreams draw on different skills and can run entirely in parallel without one delaying the other. The site's dedicated guide to UKMLA preparation for IMGs sets out how to build a study plan that covers the AKT's clinical content alongside — rather than after — English test preparation, which is the more time-efficient sequencing for most Indian candidates working towards a realistic overall pathway timeline.

Possible Exemptions From the English Language Test

The GMC does allow limited exemptions from the standard English language testing requirement in certain specific circumstances — for example, doctors who have recently and substantially practised medicine within another majority-English-speaking healthcare system may, in some cases, be able to rely on that experience instead of sitting a fresh test. However, exemption criteria of this kind are narrowly defined, assessed case by case, and can change over time as GMC policy is reviewed. It would be misleading to present a fixed, general list of exemption scenarios here, since doing so risks giving an Indian candidate false confidence about a route that may not actually apply to their specific circumstances, or that may have been updated since this guide was written.

Rather than assuming an exemption applies, Indian candidates should check the GMC's current exemption criteria directly and in detail before making any decision to skip test preparation or delay booking a test slot: see the GMC's official English language evidence page for the authoritative, current position. In the great majority of cases, an Indian medical graduate applying directly from India — without a period of substantial recent practice in a majority-English-speaking health system — should plan on sitting either IELTS Academic or OET Medicine as a standard part of the pathway, rather than pursuing an exemption that is unlikely to apply.

Preparation Tips for Indian Candidates

Indian test-takers, as a group, tend to show a fairly consistent pattern of relative strengths and weaknesses across both tests, and preparation time is generally best spent addressing the specific areas where this pattern tends to show up rather than revising uniformly across all four skills.

  • Speaking is the most common weak component. Indian medical education builds excellent reading and writing fluency through textbooks, case write-ups, and examinations, but offers comparatively little structured practice in extended, examiner-assessed spoken English on unfamiliar or abstract topics. Regular timed mock speaking sessions — ideally recorded and reviewed, or practised with a partner who can give honest feedback on fluency and coherence rather than just grammar — consistently produce the largest score improvements for Indian candidates.
  • Writing Task 2 (IELTS) or the referral letter (OET) rewards structure over vocabulary. Many Indian candidates already have strong vocabulary but lose marks on organisation, paragraphing, and directly addressing the exact question or clinical scenario asked. Practising with a clear, repeatable structural template for each task type improves scores faster than expanding vocabulary alone.
  • Listening under exam conditions differs from everyday listening comprehension. Indian candidates who consume a great deal of English-language media may still under-score on Listening because the exam format demands sustained concentration, rapid note-taking, and comprehension of varied native-speaker accents (including Australian, Scottish, and North American accents) within a single sitting, which is a different skill from passive listening.
  • Use official practice material first. Both the British Council/IDP (for IELTS) and Cambridge Boxhill Language Assessment (for OET) publish official practice tests and sample answers, and these should form the backbone of preparation before turning to third-party resources, since official material most accurately reflects actual scoring criteria.
  • Book a mock speaking session with a certified or experienced tutor close to the actual test date. Simulated speaking practice under realistic time pressure, ideally with feedback benchmarked against the real band descriptors or OET criteria, is consistently reported as the single most useful preparation activity by candidates who move from a borderline score to a comfortably passing one.
  • Practise topic breadth for IELTS specifically. Because IELTS Academic draws on general topics rather than clinical ones, Indian candidates should read broadly outside medical subject matter — current affairs, science journalism, and opinion writing — to build comfort with the kind of vocabulary and argument structures the Reading and Writing sections actually use.

These preparation strategies apply in addition to, not instead of, the wider exam preparation habits covered in the site's general guide to UKMLA preparation for IMGs, since English test preparation and AKT/CPSA preparation draw on overlapping but distinct skills and are best planned as parallel, rather than sequential, workstreams.

Cost Considerations: Test Fees and the Cost of a Re-Sit

Test fees in India for both IELTS Academic and OET are charged in rupees and are subject to periodic revision by the test providers, so the figures below should be treated as approximate and always cross-checked against the official current fee at the time of booking. As a general guide, IELTS Academic in India typically costs in the region of ₹17,000–₹18,000, while OET typically costs in the region of ₹19,000–₹20,500, with on-screen/computer-delivered sittings sometimes priced slightly differently from paper-based sittings at the same centre.

The more significant cost risk, however, is not the initial test fee itself but the cost of a re-sit for candidates who narrowly miss the required score in one component. Because both tests apply an all-components minimum — 7.0 across the board for IELTS, Grade B across the board for OET — a candidate who scores comfortably above the threshold in three skills but marginally below it in the fourth must, in most circumstances, pay and sit the test again in full (OET allows single-sub-test re-sits in certain circumstances, which can reduce this cost). Consequently, a realistic total budget for English testing should assume at least one possible re-sit, particularly for candidates whose weakest skill is Speaking, which data from both test providers consistently shows to be the most common component to fall short of the required band or grade among IMG candidates generally.

Beyond the test fee itself, Indian candidates should also budget for preparation costs — coaching classes, official practice material, and mock speaking sessions — which together commonly add a further ₹3,000–₹15,000 depending on how much structured coaching is used versus self-study. Treating English test preparation with the same seriousness (and budget) as AKT preparation, rather than as an afterthought, is one of the more reliable ways to avoid the added cost and delay of an unplanned re-sit.

It is also worth factoring in the indirect cost of a re-sit, which is often larger than the fee itself. A failed component typically adds four to eight weeks to the overall timeline once a new test slot is booked, preparation is refreshed, and results are issued — time that, for many Indian candidates, overlaps with other fixed deadlines such as an AKT booking window or a planned resignation date from a current post. Building a modest contingency of both time and money into the original plan, rather than assuming a first-attempt pass across all four components, is a more realistic approach than budgeting for the best-case outcome only. As with every fee mentioned in this guide, candidates should treat the rupee figures above as indicative rather than fixed, and should always confirm the live, current fee directly with IELTS or OET's official Indian booking portals before making a payment, since both providers revise fees periodically and without prior notice to third-party guides.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do Indian doctors need IELTS for UKMLA?

Yes, in almost all cases. The GMC requires documentary evidence of English language proficiency from the great majority of IMGs, including Indian medical graduates, regardless of the fact that Indian medical education is conducted in English. Indian candidates can satisfy this requirement with either IELTS Academic (overall 7.0, no component below 7.0) or OET Medicine (Grade B minimum in all four sub-tests) — IELTS is not the only accepted option, but it is one of the two standard routes.

Is OET accepted for GMC registration?

Yes. OET (Occupational English Test), Medicine version, is fully accepted by the GMC as an alternative to IELTS Academic, provided the candidate achieves a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. Many practising doctors, including Indian candidates, find OET more intuitive than IELTS Academic because its tasks are built around real clinical scenarios such as referral letters and patient consultations rather than general academic topics.

How long is an IELTS certificate valid for GMC registration?

IELTS and OET certificates both carry a defined validity period from the date of the test, and the GMC will not accept a certificate that has expired by the time a registration application is submitted. Because the exact current validity period is exactly the kind of detail the GMC updates from time to time, candidates should confirm it directly on the GMC's English language evidence page before finalising their test date, and should sequence their test so its validity comfortably covers their expected application date rather than sitting it too early in the pathway.

What IELTS score is needed for GMC registration from India?

Indian candidates need an overall IELTS Academic score of 7.0, with no individual component — Listening, Reading, Writing, or Speaking — scoring below 7.0. This is an all-components requirement, so a strong overall average does not compensate for a single component falling short of 7.0; each of the four skills must independently clear the threshold.

Can Indian doctors take OET instead of IELTS for the UKMLA pathway?

Yes. The GMC treats IELTS Academic and OET Medicine as equally valid routes to satisfying the English language evidence requirement, and Indian candidates are free to choose whichever test suits their strengths, provided they meet the required score in each of the four sub-tests (7.0 for IELTS, Grade B for OET). Neither test is preferred by the GMC over the other; the choice is entirely a matter of which format and content style suits the individual candidate.

Are there any exemptions from the English test for Indian doctors?

The GMC does allow limited exemptions in specific circumstances, such as recent, substantial practice within another majority-English-speaking healthcare system, but these criteria are narrowly applied, assessed case by case, and can change over time. Most Indian medical graduates applying directly from India should not assume an exemption applies and should instead check the GMC's current exemption criteria directly before deciding whether to sit a test.

How much does the IELTS or OET test cost in India?

As an approximate guide, subject to change, IELTS Academic in India typically costs around ₹17,000–₹18,000, while OET typically costs around ₹19,000–₹20,500. Because both tests apply an all-components minimum score, candidates should also budget for the realistic possibility of a re-sit if one component falls narrowly short, and should always confirm current fees directly with the relevant test provider before booking.

Which English test is easier for Indian doctors, IELTS or OET?

Neither test is objectively easier; the better fit depends on individual strengths. Indian doctors who are comfortable with formal academic writing and general-interest reading often do well with IELTS Academic, while those who are more comfortable with clinical documentation and consultation-style spoken English — which describes most practising doctors — often find OET Medicine's healthcare-specific scenarios more intuitive to prepare for.

When should Indian doctors sit their English test relative to the AKT?

Most Indian candidates get the best outcome by sitting their English test once EPIC verification is underway and AKT preparation has begun, rather than immediately after graduation or only once the AKT is already booked. This timing keeps the certificate's validity window comfortably ahead of the eventual GMC registration application, while still allowing English test preparation to run in parallel with, rather than delay, wider UKMLA preparation.

Conclusion

The English language requirement for UKMLA India is not a formality that Indian medical graduates can expect to have waived on the strength of an English-medium medical education; it is a standard, structured stage of the pathway that deserves dedicated preparation time, careful test choice between IELTS Academic and OET Medicine, and deliberate sequencing so a valid certificate is in hand when the full GMC registration application is submitted. Candidates who treat this stage with the same seriousness as AKT question bank preparation, budget realistically in rupees for both the test and a possible re-sit, and check the GMC's current requirements directly before booking consistently move through this part of the pathway faster and with fewer costly surprises.
