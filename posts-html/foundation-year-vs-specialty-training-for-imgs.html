The foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision is one of the most consequential choices an international medical graduate makes after clearing the UKMLA and securing GMC registration — and unlike most of the exam journey, there is no single right answer that applies to everyone. Some IMGs arrive in the UK with no postgraduate experience at all and genuinely need the structured, supervised grounding that FY1 and FY2 provide. Others arrive with five, ten, or fifteen years of specialist or GP practice already behind them, and re-entering a two-year foundation programme designed for brand-new graduates would waste time, income, and clinical seniority they have already earned elsewhere. Knowing which category a given candidate actually falls into — and what evidence is needed to prove it — is the single most important planning decision an experienced IMG makes when weighing foundation year vs specialty training IMG options in the months after passing the UKMLA.

This guide is deliberately narrow in scope. It assumes the reader already understands, in broad terms, what the UK Foundation Programme involves and what generally happens after passing the UKMLA and registering with the GMC. Its job is not to re-explain FY1 and FY2 from scratch, but to answer the harder question that follows immediately afterwards: given a specific IMG's background, should they apply for the Foundation Programme, or is direct entry to specialty, core, or GP training (CT1/ST1 or equivalent) realistically available to them instead? Framed another way, this is the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question in its most practical form — not an abstract policy debate, but a decision a specific candidate has to make with their own documents in hand. It sets out how the UK Foundation Programme Office (UKFPO) and the Royal Colleges actually assess prior experience, the structural criteria that separate the two routes, and the practical trade-offs — salary banding, competition, portfolio requirements, and visa sponsorship — that follow from each choice.

It is worth stating plainly, before going further, that this is genuinely a case-by-case decision rather than a formula that can be applied mechanically. The GMC registration type a doctor holds, the recency and recognisability of their prior clinical experience, the specific specialty they intend to enter, and the requirements of the relevant Royal College all interact to determine which route is realistically open. This guide explains the structural factors involved and hedges honestly wherever a specific number or threshold is reviewed annually and should be checked directly with the UK Foundation Programme Office or the relevant Royal College before a candidate commits to either path. Quick Answer: Foundation Year vs Specialty Training for IMGs

For most IMGs with no substantive postgraduate clinical experience, the Foundation Programme is not actually an available route at all — it is designed for, and overwhelmingly filled by, UK medical school graduates who progress into it directly from their final year. IMGs without prior postgraduate experience typically enter UK practice instead through Trust Grade or Clinical Fellow posts carrying FY1/FY2-equivalent responsibility, then build a portfolio towards specialty training over one to two years. IMGs who already hold substantial specialist or GP experience recognised as broadly equivalent by a Royal College may instead be able to apply directly into specialty, core, or GP training at CT1/ST1 level or above, skipping the foundation stage of UK training altogether. In short, the foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision usually resolves itself once prior experience and GMC registration type are established honestly.

The decision, in other words, is rarely a genuine binary choice made freely by the candidate. It is much more often a structural fact determined by prior experience, GMC registration type, and Royal College assessment of equivalence — the candidate's task is to work out honestly which category they fall into, rather than to simply pick whichever route sounds more appealing. Anyone comparing foundation year vs specialty training IMG options should treat this as a fact-finding exercise first and a preference exercise second. The remainder of this guide works through exactly how that determination is made, what evidence each route requires, and the practical consequences of each path once entered.

  • IMGs with no prior postgraduate clinical experience generally cannot apply for the standard UKFPO Foundation Programme allocation process, since it is structurally tied to UK medical school graduation; they instead enter via Trust Grade posts and build towards specialty training separately.
  • IMGs with substantial, recognisable postgraduate specialist or GP experience may be able to apply directly for CT1/ST1-level specialty, core, or GP training through Oriel, provided their GMC registration type and prior experience meet the relevant Royal College's entry criteria for that specialty.
  • Neither route is automatically easier or faster — each carries a different competition profile, a different salary starting point, and different portfolio expectations, all covered in detail below.

IMGs With No Prior Postgraduate Experience

An IMG who has just finished medical school overseas, with little or no independent postgraduate clinical practice, is in a structurally different position from an IMG who already has years of specialist experience — and the practical route available to them reflects that difference directly. For this group, the foundation year vs specialty training IMG comparison tends to have a fairly clear-cut answer.

Why the Standard Foundation Programme Route Is Usually Closed

The UKFPO's centrally coordinated Foundation Programme allocation process is built around the assumption that applicants are graduating from a UK medical school in the same academic cycle as their application. The overwhelming majority of Foundation Programme places are filled this way, and the process — Preference-Informed Allocation, application timelines tied to the UK academic year, white space questions calibrated to a final-year student's experience — is simply not structured around international graduates applying from overseas at an arbitrary point after qualification. This is a structural fact about how the scheme is run, not a judgement about an IMG's clinical competence; it means that, for the great majority of IMGs, the standard UKFPO Foundation Programme application route is not realistically available regardless of how well-prepared a candidate is clinically.

There are narrow exceptions — for instance, eligible re-applicants in specific circumstances the UKFPO defines — but these are genuinely limited categories, not a general route open to any international graduate who would like foundation-level training. Because eligibility criteria are reviewed and can change, any IMG who believes they might qualify as an eligible re-applicant should check the current criteria directly on the UK Foundation Programme Office's own website rather than assuming eligibility based on a general description here. For this group, the foundation year vs specialty training IMG comparison is really a comparison between the standard Foundation Programme route and the Trust Grade bridge described next, since standard specialty training entry is equally unavailable without prior clinical experience to point to.

The Realistic Alternative: Trust Grade Posts and Building a Portfolio

For the majority of IMGs without prior postgraduate experience, the realistic entry point after GMC registration is a Trust Grade or Clinical Fellow post carrying FY1/FY2-equivalent clinical responsibility, arranged directly with an individual NHS trust rather than through the centralised Foundation Programme allocation. These posts provide the same broad category of supervised clinical experience the Foundation Programme is designed to deliver — ward-based medicine, on-call exposure, prescribing practice, multidisciplinary teamworking — without requiring entry through the UKFPO's national scheme. A period in a Trust Grade post also serves a second, equally important purpose: it is how most IMGs accumulate the UK-specific clinical experience and portfolio evidence that later strengthens a specialty or core training application.

This is a genuinely common and sensible pattern, not a consolation route. An IMG typically spends one to two years in a Trust Grade post, uses that period to gather structured clinical evidence, secure UK-based references, and clarify a specialty preference, and then applies to core or specialty training through the same national Oriel application system used by UK Foundation Programme graduates progressing from FY2. In effect, the Trust Grade period functions as a self-arranged substitute for the foundation stage, rather than a permanent alternative career track.

What This Means for Portfolio-Building

IMGs without prior postgraduate experience should treat their first one to two years in a Trust Grade post as a deliberate portfolio-building exercise rather than simply a job. Core and specialty training applications, run through Oriel, typically score applicants against published person specifications that reward exactly the kind of evidence a well-used Trust Grade period generates: audits and quality improvement projects, taught or informal teaching experience, relevant courses and certifications, and structured references from UK-based consultants who can speak directly to the applicant's clinical performance in an NHS setting. Building this evidence base from the first week of a Trust Grade post — rather than scrambling to assemble it in the months before an application deadline — meaningfully strengthens a subsequent specialty training application.

IMGs With Substantial Specialist or GP Experience

The position is materially different for an IMG who arrives in the UK already holding several years of recognised specialist or GP practice, sometimes including a postgraduate qualification from their home country. For this group, the foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision is much more often genuinely live — direct entry to specialty or core training may be realistically available, provided the right evidence and registration type are in place. This is the audience for whom the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question is worth investigating seriously rather than assuming the foundation route is the only door open.

GMC Registration Type Is the First Gate

The single most important structural factor in any foundation year vs specialty training IMG assessment is the type of GMC registration a doctor holds. Provisional registration — the conditional registration newly qualified doctors hold during FY1 — does not itself grant eligibility to apply for specialty or core training; it is tied to supervised foundation-level practice. Full registration with a licence to practise is the baseline most core and specialty training programmes require of any applicant, IMG or UK graduate alike. Beyond that baseline, some experienced doctors may also be assessed for entry directly onto the GMC's Specialist Register or GP Register through a specialist application route, which is a different and generally higher bar than simply being eligible to apply for a specialty training national selection process. These are related but distinct questions, and an experienced doctor should be clear about which one actually applies to their situation before assuming either route is open.

It is also worth being explicit that most experienced IMGs, even highly senior ones, are not automatically exempt from sitting the UKMLA itself before any of this becomes relevant — as the site's separate guide on UKMLA exemptions for specialists and GPs sets out in detail, specialist or GP experience overseas does not, on its own, exempt a doctor from the licensing assessment. The foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision described in this guide is a question that arises only after UKMLA success and GMC registration are already secured — it does not change or shortcut the registration requirement itself.

How Royal Colleges Assess Prior Experience for Direct Entry

Core and specialty training programmes in the UK are run by individual Royal Colleges and specialty training committees, each publishing its own person specification for entry at CT1/ST1 level (and, in some specialties, higher entry points for doctors with more extensive relevant experience). These person specifications typically set out the minimum clinical experience, procedural competencies, and evidence expected of any applicant — UK Foundation Programme graduate or IMG alike — and an IMG's prior postgraduate experience is assessed against those same published criteria, not against a separate or more lenient IMG-specific standard.

This is where the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question is ultimately settled in individual cases. In practice, this means an experienced IMG applying directly to CT1/ST1 needs to demonstrate, through documented evidence, that their prior clinical practice covers broadly equivalent ground to what a UK Foundation Programme graduate would bring into the same application: a certain volume and breadth of relevant clinical experience, evidence of procedural competency where relevant to the specialty, and references that a UK panel can meaningfully interpret. Because exact competency thresholds, required evidence formats, and entry criteria differ by specialty and are reviewed periodically by each Royal College, any IMG considering this route should check the current person specification for their target specialty directly with the relevant college before assuming their experience qualifies, rather than relying on a general description.

Higher Specialty Training and Non-Standard Entry Points

Some experienced IMGs, particularly those with extensive specialist practice already recognised as broadly equivalent, may in principle be considered for entry at a training stage beyond CT1/ST1 in some specialties, though this is considerably less common and more tightly assessed than standard entry. Where this kind of non-standard entry is genuinely under consideration, it typically involves a more individualised assessment by the relevant college of the doctor's training history, competencies already achieved, and any gaps that would need to be addressed. This is not a route to assume applies without direct confirmation, and doctors exploring it should treat early, direct correspondence with the relevant Royal College as an essential first step rather than an optional extra.

How the UKFPO and Royal Colleges Actually Assess Prior Experience

Understanding the foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision requires understanding, at a structural level, how the two systems evaluate a candidate — since the Foundation Programme and specialty/core training are administered through genuinely different application systems with different underlying logic.

The UKFPO: Centrally Coordinated, Cohort-Based Allocation

The UK Foundation Programme Office (UKFPO) coordinates Foundation Programme applications, allocation, and Foundation School matching for the national cohort of applicants each year, principally UK medical school final-year students. Its processes — Preference-Informed Allocation, white space questions, Foundation School matching — are designed around a cohort applying at broadly the same point in their training and career, which is precisely why the system does not translate well to individual IMGs applying at arbitrary points in their career with varying amounts of prior experience. The UKFPO is the authoritative source for any question about eligibility as a re-applicant or about the very small number of circumstances in which an IMG might be considered for standard allocation, and its own published guidance should always be checked directly, since these criteria are reviewed periodically.

Oriel and Royal College Person Specifications: Individual, Criteria-Based Assessment

Core and specialty training, by contrast, is applied for through Oriel, the national online recruitment system used across specialty and GP training programmes in the UK, and is assessed against each specialty's individually published person specification rather than through a cohort-wide lottery-style allocation. This is a fundamentally different assessment logic: an applicant, whether a UK Foundation Programme graduate or an experienced IMG, is scored against explicit published criteria — clinical experience, procedural competencies, exam success where relevant, portfolio evidence, and references — rather than allocated through preference and chance. This is precisely why direct entry to specialty training is structurally available to experienced IMGs in a way that direct entry to the Foundation Programme generally is not: Oriel-based selection assesses experience and competency against a published standard, while Foundation Programme allocation assumes a specific graduating cohort.

The Role of the Portfolio in Both Systems

Whichever route applies, the underlying evidence an assessor is looking for is broadly similar in kind, even if the weighting differs: documented clinical experience, procedural competency evidence, teaching and quality improvement contributions, relevant postgraduate exam success, and structured references. An IMG who understands this early — and begins building the relevant evidence base from their very first UK clinical post, rather than only once a specific application deadline approaches — is better placed regardless of which route eventually turns out to be realistic for them.

Salary Banding, Competition, and Portfolio Trade-Offs in Practice

Beyond the structural comparison above, several practical trade-offs are worth understanding in more depth when weighing foundation year vs specialty training IMG options, since they materially affect the day-to-day experience of each route.

Salary Banding Considerations

Foundation doctor pay is set nationally and is broadly consistent for every FY1 and FY2 postholder, with additional banding supplements applied according to the intensity and pattern of the rota worked. Core and specialty trainee pay is set on a different national scale that generally reflects greater seniority and responsibility than Foundation-level pay, though the exact relationship between the two scales, and the specific banding supplements that apply to any given rota, are reviewed periodically by NHS Employers and the relevant pay review bodies. Because these figures change, this guide deliberately does not quote specific dates or amounts — any IMG comparing take-home pay between a Trust Grade post, a Foundation-equivalent post, and a specialty training post should check the current NHS pay circular directly, since the differences can be meaningful to a household budgeting decision, particularly for an IMG supporting dependants on a Health and Care Worker visa.

Competition Ratios Differ Sharply by Specialty and Change Year to Year

One of the most common planning mistakes experienced IMGs make is assuming that competition for direct specialty or core training entry is uniform across specialties. It is not. Some specialties attract very high applicant-to-post ratios in a given recruitment round, while others are considerably less competitive, and these ratios can shift meaningfully from one year to the next depending on training-post expansion, workforce planning changes, and the overall size of the applicant pool. Rather than relying on a fixed number that could be stale by the time of application, any IMG planning a direct specialty training application should check the current competition ratios published for their target specialty through Oriel and the relevant Royal College before finalising which specialty, and which application round, to target.

Portfolio Requirements: Front-Loaded vs Built Over Time

Perhaps the clearest practical difference between the two routes is when the portfolio-building work actually happens. A Foundation Programme applicant builds their portfolio during FY1 and FY2 itself, under structured educational supervision, with relatively modest expectations at the point of initial application. A direct specialty or core training applicant, by contrast, must generally have already assembled a substantial portfolio — evidence of relevant clinical experience, procedural competencies, audits, teaching, and strong references — before the application is even submitted. This front-loading is precisely why the one-to-two-year Trust Grade period described earlier matters so much for IMGs without prior experience: it is, in effect, the period during which that portfolio gets built, whether or not the doctor consciously frames it that way at the time.

Visa and Sponsorship Implications

Visa sponsorship considerations differ subtly but meaningfully between the two routes. A Foundation post and a core/specialty training post are both generally sponsorable under the Health and Care Worker visa route, provided the employing trust or deanery holds a valid sponsor licence, which the large majority of NHS employers do. The practical difference for most experienced IMGs is less about whether sponsorship is available in principle, and more about timing and continuity: a doctor moving from a Trust Grade post into specialty training via a successful Oriel application needs to ensure their Certificate of Sponsorship and visa conditions transfer or are reissued correctly for the new post, rather than assuming continuity is automatic. Any doctor uncertain about how a change of post affects their visa status should confirm directly with their current and prospective employer's HR or international recruitment team well before accepting a new offer, since visa administration errors at this transition point are a common and entirely avoidable source of delay.

How to Decide Which Route Applies to You

Given everything above, working out which side of the foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision actually applies to a specific candidate is best approached as a structured, honest self-assessment rather than a preference exercise. The steps below turn the foundation year vs specialty training IMG framework described so far into a concrete checklist.

Step 1: Confirm Your GMC Registration Type

Before considering either route seriously, confirm precisely what type of GMC registration you currently hold or are on track to receive — provisional, full with a licence to practise, or (in narrower cases) entry onto the Specialist or GP Register through the specialist application route. This single fact determines which applications are even structurally possible, and it should be established with certainty, directly through your GMC registration documentation, before any further planning. Doctors who are still assembling their original registration evidence, particularly those working through primary source verification, may find it useful to revisit the site's step-by-step guide to GMC registration and EPIC verification for doctors from India, since the same documentation discipline that gets registration approved the first time also underpins a credible foundation year vs specialty training IMG application later.

Step 2: Take an Honest Inventory of Prior Postgraduate Experience

This step is where most of the real work in a foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision actually happens.

List out, concretely, the postgraduate clinical experience already completed: years in independent or supervised practice, specific procedures performed regularly, any postgraduate qualifications held, and whether that qualification or registration was gained through a system the GMC or relevant Royal College is likely to recognise as broadly comparable. This inventory should be honest rather than optimistic — overstating equivalence to a Royal College or training committee wastes time on an application unlikely to succeed, while understating it can lead a genuinely experienced doctor to needlessly restart at a more junior level than necessary.

Step 3: Check the Current Person Specification for Your Target Specialty

Because entry criteria differ meaningfully by specialty and are reviewed periodically, identify the specific specialty you intend to pursue and check that specialty's current published person specification directly, rather than relying on generic assumptions about "specialty training" as a single undifferentiated category. This step alone resolves much of the uncertainty in the foundation-versus-specialty-training decision, since it tells you concretely what evidence a real application would need to demonstrate.

Step 4: If Direct Entry Looks Realistic, Start the Portfolio Immediately

If the person specification suggests direct entry is genuinely realistic, begin assembling the required evidence immediately rather than waiting until closer to an Oriel application window. References, audit evidence, and procedural competency documentation all take time to gather properly, and a rushed portfolio assembled in the final weeks before a deadline is rarely as strong as one built deliberately over months.

Step 5: If Direct Entry Is Not Realistic Yet, Plan the Trust Grade Bridge Deliberately

If the honest assessment in Steps 2 and 3 suggests direct entry is not currently realistic, plan a Trust Grade period as a deliberate bridge rather than a default fallback: pick posts and specialties that build directly towards the eventual target specialty's person specification, seek out audit and teaching opportunities proactively, and set a realistic timeline — commonly one to two years — for when a specialty training application will actually be submitted.

Step 6: Keep Revising and Stay Informed While You Decide

Because this decision can take weeks or months to resolve properly — gathering documentation, corresponding with Royal Colleges, and confirming registration details — many IMGs are still actively revising or preparing supporting materials for other parts of the pathway during this period. Candidates who are still working through UKMLA preparation as an IMG or maintaining exam-level knowledge with tools like an Anki deck and flashcard revision system should not treat career-route planning and continued revision as mutually exclusive — both can, and often should, run in parallel.

It is also worth knowing, while working through this decision, what happens administratively if personal circumstances change mid-process — for example, if a candidate needs to withdraw or amend a GMC registration application while still deciding between routes. The site's separate note on the GMC registration refund policy covers exactly this scenario and is worth reading before assuming any application, once submitted, is irreversible.

Common Scenarios Worked Through

Concrete scenarios often clarify the foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision faster than abstract rules. The following examples illustrate how the structural factors above combine in practice — they are illustrative only, not a substitute for checking current criteria directly.

Scenario: Recent Graduate, No Postgraduate Experience

This scenario is the clearest possible answer to the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question. A doctor who has just completed their primary medical qualification overseas, passed the UKMLA, and obtained full GMC registration, with no independent postgraduate clinical practice, will almost always find the Trust Grade route the realistic entry point. The standard Foundation Programme route is not structurally open to them, and they do not yet have the experience a direct specialty training application would require. Their planning priority should be securing a strong first Trust Grade post and beginning portfolio-building immediately.

Scenario: Three Years of General Medicine Experience, No Specialty Qualification

A doctor with several years of general postgraduate clinical experience, but no formal specialty membership qualification and no GMC-recognised specialist registration, sits in an intermediate position on the foundation year vs specialty training IMG spectrum. Direct entry to CT1/ST1 in some specialties may be realistic if their experience maps well onto the relevant person specification, but this needs to be checked specialty by specialty rather than assumed. A shorter Trust Grade period than a completely inexperienced graduate would need, focused specifically on closing any gaps the person specification identifies, is often the pragmatic middle path.

Scenario: Ten Years as a Specialist Consultant, Recognised Registration

This scenario sits at the opposite end of the foundation year vs specialty training IMG spectrum from the recent graduate above. A doctor with a decade of consultant-level specialist practice, whose registration and training are recognised by the GMC as broadly comparable through the Registrar route, is in the strongest position to explore direct entry — potentially including consideration for GMC Specialist Register entry rather than standard CT1/ST1 recruitment. This is exactly the kind of case that warrants direct, early correspondence with both the GMC and the relevant Royal College, since the applicable process differs meaningfully from standard specialty training recruitment.

Frequently Asked Questions About Foundation Year vs Specialty Training for IMGs

Can an IMG skip the Foundation Programme and go straight into specialty training?

Yes, in principle, provided the doctor holds the required type of GMC registration and can demonstrate, against the relevant Royal College's published person specification, sufficient prior clinical experience broadly equivalent to what the specialty expects at entry. This is genuinely more achievable for experienced specialists and GPs than for recent graduates with no independent postgraduate practice, and it should always be confirmed against the current person specification for the specific target specialty rather than assumed generally. In effect, this is the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question at its most direct, and the answer always comes back to documented prior experience rather than general seniority.

Does passing the UKMLA automatically qualify an IMG for the Foundation Programme?

No. Passing the UKMLA and obtaining GMC registration are prerequisites for UK medical practice generally, but they do not by themselves grant eligibility for the standard UKFPO Foundation Programme allocation process, which is structurally built around UK medical school final-year graduates. Most IMGs who pass the UKMLA enter UK practice through Trust Grade posts rather than the Foundation Programme itself, which is exactly why the foundation year vs specialty training IMG question needs a separate, honest answer rather than an assumption carried over from the exam stage.

How do Royal Colleges decide whether an IMG's overseas experience counts towards specialty training entry?

Each Royal College or specialty training committee publishes its own person specification setting out the clinical experience, competencies, and evidence expected of any CT1/ST1 applicant, and an IMG's prior experience is assessed against those same published criteria through the Oriel application and selection process, generally supported by documented evidence such as logbooks, audits, and references. Because criteria differ by specialty and are reviewed periodically, the current person specification for the specific target specialty should always be checked directly rather than relied on from memory or a general summary.

Does the type of GMC registration affect which training route is available?

Yes, significantly. Provisional GMC registration is tied to supervised foundation-level practice and does not itself support a specialty training application; full registration with a licence to practise is the baseline most core and specialty training programmes require; and a smaller group of experienced doctors may be assessed separately for direct entry onto the GMC's Specialist or GP Register through the specialist application route, which is a distinct and generally higher threshold than standard CT1/ST1 recruitment.

Is it better financially to go through Foundation-equivalent training or straight into specialty training?

This depends on the specific salary banding in place at the time, which is reviewed periodically by NHS pay bodies, so no fixed comparison can be stated reliably here — always check the current NHS pay circular for Foundation-level and core/specialty trainee banding before making a financial comparison. What can be said structurally is that direct specialty training entry generally reflects a higher starting point of recognised seniority than Foundation-level entry, since it is only realistically available to doctors who can already demonstrate meaningfully more prior clinical experience.

What happens to visa sponsorship if an IMG moves from a Trust Grade post into specialty training?

Sponsorship is generally available for both post types provided the employing trust or deanery holds a valid sponsor licence, but moving between posts requires the Certificate of Sponsorship and visa conditions to be correctly reissued or updated for the new role rather than assumed to continue automatically. Confirming this directly with the current and prospective employer's HR or international recruitment team before accepting a new offer avoids one of the more common and avoidable sources of delay at this transition.

Should an IMG keep revising for exams while deciding between these two routes?

Yes — the two activities are not in competition with each other. Deciding between Foundation-equivalent entry and direct specialty training can take weeks or months of correspondence with Royal Colleges and registration bodies, and many IMGs continue maintaining or building clinical knowledge in parallel using structured revision tools, since strong clinical knowledge remains relevant to both routes regardless of which one is ultimately taken.

Conclusion: Making the Foundation Year vs Specialty Training IMG Call With Confidence

The foundation year vs specialty training IMG decision is best understood as a structural question about registration type, prior experience, and Royal College criteria — not a simple preference between two equally available options. IMGs without prior postgraduate experience should plan realistically around a Trust Grade bridge into specialty training rather than expecting standard Foundation Programme entry; IMGs with substantial, recognisable specialist or GP experience should investigate direct CT1/ST1 entry seriously, starting with their GMC registration type and the relevant Royal College's current person specification. Whichever route applies, starting the self-assessment early, gathering evidence deliberately rather than reactively, and checking current criteria directly with the UKFPO, Oriel, and the relevant Royal College — rather than relying on assumptions carried over from a different country's training system — gives every IMG the clearest possible path forward.
