The Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 is now the single most-asked-about piece of legislation among international medical graduates planning a UK career, and much of the anxiety around it comes from a genuine misunderstanding of what it actually changes. In short: the Act reorders who gets offered postgraduate foundation and specialty training places in the NHS — it does not touch the UKMLA, GMC registration, or a doctor's basic right to come and work in the UK. Passed as emergency legislation in early 2026 after applications for specialty training posts roughly tripled in six years, the Act requires that UK medical graduates, certain other prioritised groups, and doctors already embedded in the NHS are offered training places ahead of the wider international applicant pool. For an IMG who has already sat, or is preparing to sit, the UKMLA, that distinction — training-post competition versus registration eligibility — is the single most important thing to understand before reading any further.

This guide is written for two overlapping audiences: international medical graduates who are building a UK career plan around the UKMLA, and UK medical students who will shortly be competing for the same Foundation Programme and specialty training numbers the Act now protects. It explains what the Act actually does, why the government introduced it, exactly which groups it prioritises and in what order, what it deliberately leaves unchanged, and what the practical implications are for an IMG's timeline, portfolio strategy, and expectations. Wherever a fact, date, or figure is stated, it is drawn from the official GOV.UK impact statement or NHS England's own guidance for applicants, both linked throughout, because this is exactly the kind of fast-moving policy area where secondary summaries drift out of date within weeks.

Nothing in this article should be read as discouraging a genuine, well-prepared IMG from pursuing a UK medical career. The Act changes the shape of competition for postgraduate training numbers; it does not close the door to the UK for international doctors, and it does not alter the route that gets a doctor onto the medical register in the first place. The primary source for the facts, figures, and dates in this guide is the government's own impact statement for the Medical Training (Prioritisation) Bill, supplemented by NHS England's published guidance for applicants, and readers are encouraged to check both directly whenever a decision hinges on the exact current wording of the rules.

Quick Answer: What Does the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 Actually Do?

The Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 is UK legislation, given Royal Assent on 5 March 2026, that requires NHS foundation and specialty training places to be offered first to UK medical graduates and a small number of other defined priority groups, ahead of the wider pool of applicants trained overseas. It was introduced to Parliament by the Health Secretary on 13 January 2026 and fast-tracked through both Houses in a matter of weeks specifically so it could take effect for training places starting in August 2026. The Act applies to the allocation of training places, not to who is permitted to sit the UKMLA or register with the GMC — those processes continue exactly as before.

In practical terms, the Act inserts a prioritisation step into two existing recruitment systems: the UK Foundation Programme Office's (UKFPO) allocation process, and the Oriel-based national recruitment system used for specialty and GP training. Rather than every eligible applicant competing on equal footing for a fixed number of posts, the Act requires that priority-group applicants are considered, and where suitable offered a place, before posts are opened up to the remaining applicant pool. For specialty training starting in 2026, because shortlisting was already underway when the Act took effect, prioritisation is applied at the offer stage rather than by re-running shortlisting from scratch.

  • What changed: the order in which UK foundation and specialty training places are offered, prioritising UK medical graduates and defined priority groups.
  • What did not change: UKMLA eligibility, GMC registration requirements, and a doctor's right to apply for non-training NHS posts such as Trust Grade roles.
  • Who it affects most directly: IMGs and UK medical students both competing for a capped number of foundation and specialty training numbers each recruitment cycle.

The remainder of this guide unpacks each of these points in turn, starting with the numbers that actually drove the government to legislate.

Why the Government Introduced the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026

The core driver behind the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 was a sharp, sustained rise in competition for a training-post pipeline that had not grown nearly as fast as demand for it. According to the government's own impact statement, in 2025 alone 15,723 UK-trained doctors and 25,257 overseas-trained doctors competed for just 12,833 round 1 and round 2 specialty training posts — meaning UK graduates were, for the first time at scale, at real risk of failing to secure a training place at all, in a system many had assumed was built primarily around them. Applications for specialty training overall had risen from roughly 12,000 in 2019 to nearly 40,000 by the time the Bill was introduced, a rate of growth the existing pipeline of foundation and specialty posts was never designed to absorb.

This surge followed the lifting of visa and immigration restrictions on overseas-trained doctors applying for UK training posts in 2020, which opened up direct competition between UK medical graduates and a much larger global pool of doctors, many of whom had already gained substantial NHS clinical experience through non-training routes. The government's stated concern was not a judgement on the clinical quality of internationally trained doctors — its own impact statement is explicit that many of the doctors competing for these posts are already contributing to the NHS — but a structural one: a growing share of finite training numbers were being filled by doctors recruited from overseas at exactly the moment a substantial pool of appointable, domestically trained or NHS-employed doctors already existed and, in the government's assessment, was more likely to remain in the NHS for the long term.

This sits inside a much wider workforce strategy. The NHS Long Term Workforce Plan commits to expanding medical training places by roughly 24% by 2028/29, and the government has stated an explicit ambition to reduce the proportion of internationally recruited NHS staff from around 34% today to under 10% by 2035. Read against that backdrop, the Act functions as a bridging measure: training-place expansion under the workforce plan takes years to fully deliver, while the competition pressure on this year's and next year's cohorts was, in the government's view, urgent enough to require immediate legislative intervention rather than waiting for supply-side growth alone to rebalance the market.

It is also worth being explicit about the political process here, since it explains why this Act moved so much faster than typical health legislation. The Bill was introduced to the House of Commons on 13 January 2026 by the Health Secretary, supported by the Prime Minister and Chancellor, and the government explicitly asked Parliament to fast-track it, with all Commons stages compressed into a single sitting day, 27 January 2026. After completing scrutiny in the House of Lords in February, the Bill received Royal Assent on 5 March 2026, becoming the Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026 in time to apply to the 2026 foundation and specialty training recruitment cycles, whose offer rounds were already underway.

What the Act Changes for UK Medical Students

For a UK medical student, the Act's practical effect is straightforward: it guarantees that, all else being equal, a UK primary medical qualification places an applicant ahead of the general international applicant pool when foundation and specialty training places are offered. This is a direct response to the concern, repeatedly raised by medical student bodies and Royal Colleges during the Bill's passage, that domestic graduates were increasingly failing to secure any training place at all in a system that had, for decades, been assumed to prioritise them implicitly.

It is worth being clear-eyed, though, about what this guarantee does and does not solve. The Act changes the order of allocation; it does not increase the number of training places available in any given year beyond whatever expansion is separately delivered under the NHS Long Term Workforce Plan. A UK graduate in a popular specialty with more UK applicants than posts will still face genuine competition against other UK graduates — the Act simply removes a layer of competition from overseas-trained doctors who do not also fall into one of the Act's other priority groups. Framed honestly, the Act redistributes competition pressure rather than eliminating it, and UK students should continue to treat portfolio-building, exam performance, and specialty choice with the same seriousness they always have.

UK students should also note that the Act's foundation-stage priority groups are not limited to UK graduates alone — applicants holding a primary medical qualification from an Irish medical school, or from Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, or Switzerland, are prioritised alongside UK graduates under existing international agreements. This means the "domestic" priority group is, in practice, a defined multi-country list rather than a strictly UK-only category, which is worth understanding before assuming every competitor in the priority tier trained in the UK specifically.

What the Act Changes for IMGs

For an international medical graduate, the Act's most important effect is on when and how easily a training-post application succeeds, not on whether the wider UK medical career route remains open. An IMG who has passed, or intends to pass, the UKMLA can still register with the GMC, hold a licence to practise, and work as a doctor in the UK under exactly the same rules as before the Act — see the site's detailed walkthrough of the route from UKMLA success to an NHS job for how that process works end to end. What has changed is that, when applying for a Foundation Programme place or a numbered specialty/GP training post through Oriel, an IMG without a qualifying priority-group status will now be considered after UK graduates, applicants from the small list of prioritised countries, and doctors who fall into the Act's NHS-experience-linked immigration-status category for 2026 offers specifically.

This is a meaningful shift in competition dynamics, and IMGs planning a UK career should plan around it rather than around the pre-2026 landscape. It does not, however, mean an IMG cannot ultimately reach a UK training post — it means the realistic timeline and strategy for getting there now more often runs through non-training NHS roles first, building exactly the kind of UK clinical experience, references, and portfolio evidence that strengthens a later training application. The site's comparison of foundation year versus direct specialty training entry for IMGs covers this Trust Grade-to-training-application pathway in detail, and it becomes considerably more relevant, not less, in the post-Act environment.

It is also worth stressing what does not change: the UKMLA itself, its content, its pass standard, and the GMC registration process that follows it are entirely untouched by this legislation. An IMG's country-specific route into UK practice — whether via the pathways described in the site's round-up of country-specific IMG routes to the UKMLA, or a dedicated national guide — is unaffected by the Act. The Act is legislation about training-post allocation, not about who may sit a licensing exam or hold GMC registration.

The Single Most Important Clarification

Because this is the point that causes the most anxiety, it is worth stating as plainly as possible, on its own, separate from everything else in this guide: the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 does not affect eligibility to sit the UKMLA, does not change GMC registration requirements, and does not restrict an IMG's right to work as a doctor in the UK. It affects competition for a specific, capped category of posts — Foundation Programme places and numbered specialty/GP training posts recruited through Oriel. Non-training NHS roles, such as Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow posts, sit entirely outside the Act's scope and remain open to IMGs on exactly the same basis as before.

Who Is in the Priority Groups, Exactly?

Precision matters here more than almost anywhere else in this guide, since vague summaries of "who gets priority" are exactly what has driven confusion since the Act passed. According to NHS England's own guidance for applicants, the priority groups differ slightly between the Foundation Programme and specialty/GP training, and specialty training carries an additional, time-limited category that only applies to offers made in 2026.

Foundation Programme Priority Groups

For Foundation Programme allocation, the Act requires places to be offered, in order, first to applicants with a UK primary medical qualification, then to other defined priority groups, before being offered to the remaining applicant pool. The other priority groups for foundation training specifically include applicants holding a primary medical qualification from an Irish medical school, and — reflecting the UK's existing international agreements — applicants with a primary medical qualification from Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, or Switzerland.

Specialty and GP Training Priority Groups

For specialty and GP training, the priority ordering is broader. It requires prioritisation, at the offer stage, of UK medical graduates, individuals in the same PMQ-based priority group used for foundation training, and individuals who have already completed the UK Foundation Programme or another relevant qualifying UK training programme — effectively recognising doctors already inside the UK training pipeline. For specialty training offers made specifically in 2026, the Act adds a further, one-off category: individuals with certain specified immigration statuses, used as a practical proxy for applicants who are most likely to already have substantial NHS clinical experience. NHS England's guidance specifies this includes those with British or Irish citizenship, the right of abode in the UK, indefinite leave to remain or enter, or residence rights secured under the EU Settlement Scheme following Brexit.

What This Means If You Do Not Fall Into a Priority Group

An IMG who does not hold a qualifying immigration status, a PMQ from one of the specified countries, or prior UK foundation training experience is not excluded from applying — they remain fully eligible to apply for foundation and specialty training posts through the normal Oriel and UKFPO systems. What changes is the order in which offers are made: non-priority applicants are considered once priority-group applicants for that recruitment round have been offered places, which in a tightly competitive specialty may mean materially fewer places remain by the time non-priority offers are made. This is precisely why building UK-specific clinical experience and a strong portfolio before applying, discussed further below, has become more consequential under the Act than it may have felt in previous recruitment cycles.

How the Profession Responded to the Act

Reaction to the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 among UK medical bodies was, broadly, supportive of the principle while raising practical concerns about implementation speed. Royal Colleges and NHS Employers publicly welcomed the underlying goal of ensuring domestic graduates are not squeezed out of the training pipeline they were trained to enter, while some professional bodies representing international and refugee doctors raised concerns about the fairness of applying new prioritisation rules mid-cycle, given that many overseas-trained doctors had already invested years of preparation, relocation planning, and NHS clinical experience on the assumption of the pre-Act rules. The government's own impact statement acknowledges this tension directly, framing the 2026 immigration-status priority category as a deliberate, time-limited compromise intended to recognise doctors who already have substantial NHS experience, rather than applying the full nationality-based priority order retroactively to doctors already deep into an NHS career.

Medical student representative bodies were, unsurprisingly, the strongest advocates for the Act during its passage through Parliament, citing the same competition figures discussed above as evidence that UK graduates faced a genuine and growing risk of qualifying with no realistic route into specialty training at all — a concern the site's own overview of the wider route to becoming a doctor in the UK now reflects as part of the current landscape. Several Royal Colleges issued member briefings in the weeks following Royal Assent clarifying how the new prioritisation order would interact with their existing person specifications, since the Act changes the order of offers rather than the underlying scoring criteria a college uses to assess an application. Any IMG or UK student wanting a specialty-specific view of how their own Royal College is implementing the Act should check that college's own published guidance directly, since implementation detail can differ meaningfully by specialty even though the Act's overarching structure is uniform.

Practical Implications for IMGs Planning a UK Career

Understanding the Act intellectually is one thing; adjusting a career plan around it is another. The practical implications below are aimed specifically at IMGs who are either mid-way through UKMLA preparation or already registered and thinking about their first UK clinical role, and they assume the reader has already absorbed the central point of this guide: the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 reorders training-post competition, it does not close off the UK career route itself.

Non-Training Clinical Experience Is Now More Valuable, Not Less

Because specialty and GP training priority for 2026 offers is partly linked to immigration status and prior UK training completion, an IMG's most reliable lever for strengthening a future training application is time spent in a genuine UK clinical role beforehand — typically a Trust Grade or Clinical Fellow post carrying FY1/FY2 or higher-level responsibility, arranged directly with an NHS trust rather than through the centrally coordinated Foundation Programme allocation. These posts sit entirely outside the Act's prioritisation rules and remain open to any IMG who meets an individual trust's requirements, and time spent in one continues to generate the audits, teaching experience, references, and clinical evidence that make a later Oriel-based training application competitive regardless of priority-group status.

This was already good advice before the Act existed; it is now considerably more load-bearing. An IMG who spends twelve to twenty-four months building a genuinely strong UK clinical portfolio before applying for training is, in effect, building the same kind of evidence base the Act's priority groups are partly designed to reward — recent, verifiable NHS experience — even without qualifying for the immigration-status category directly.

Timing Expectations Should Shift

IMGs planning their UK career around the assumption of entering specialty training within a year or two of GMC registration should revisit that timeline honestly. In a training environment where UK graduates and defined priority groups are now offered places first, an IMG without a qualifying status should expect the realistic path to a training post to run somewhat longer than it might have in previous cycles, particularly in the most competitive specialties. This is not a reason to abandon a UK career plan, but it is a reason to build a longer runway into that plan and to treat the intervening period as a deliberate portfolio-building phase rather than a delay to be minimised at all costs.

Portfolio Strength Matters More Under Increased Competition

Even before the Act, Royal College person specifications for specialty and GP training scored every applicant — UK graduate or IMG — against the same published criteria: documented clinical experience, procedural competency evidence, teaching and quality improvement contributions, relevant exam success, and structured references. With a larger, more competitive non-priority applicant pool now effectively competing for whatever places remain after priority-group offers are made, the quality and depth of that evidence matters more, not less. IMGs should treat every month of a non-training NHS post as an opportunity to accumulate exactly this kind of evidence rather than simply accruing time in post, and should keep their GMC registration documentation and CPD records organised from day one rather than reconstructing a paper trail retrospectively when an application deadline approaches.

Specialty Choice and Realistic Competition Levels

Because the Act's effect on competition intensity will not be uniform across every specialty, IMGs weighing specialty choice should factor in that historically oversubscribed specialties are likely to see the sharpest increase in effective non-priority competition, while historically undersubscribed specialties and less competitive regions may still offer a realistic route to training with a shorter effective wait. This is not a reason to choose a specialty purely on the basis of ease of entry, but it is a legitimate practical factor to weigh alongside genuine clinical interest when planning a multi-year UK career strategy.

Stay Current — This Is Genuinely Fast-Moving Policy

Because the Act was fast-tracked and its specialty-training immigration-status priority category is explicitly time-limited to 2026 offers, IMGs should treat this guide as a snapshot rather than a permanent statement of the rules. NHS England and the UKFPO continue to publish updated guidance as each recruitment cycle proceeds, and any IMG actively applying for training should check the current NHS England long-read and UKFPO guidance directly before finalising an application strategy, rather than relying solely on secondary summaries written shortly after Royal Assent.

Do Not Let the Act Distract From UKMLA Preparation Itself

It is worth stating a point that can get lost in all the policy discussion: none of the competition dynamics created by the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 are relevant to an IMG until they have actually passed the UKMLA and secured GMC registration. A candidate who is still preparing for the Applied Knowledge Test or the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment should treat exam preparation as the immediate priority, not the downstream training-post landscape they will only face a year or more later. The site's own guide to structuring UKMLA preparation as an international medical graduate remains the right starting point for anyone at this earlier stage, and getting the exam itself right the first time remains the single most time-efficient thing any IMG can do, regardless of how the training-post landscape eventually looks by the time they are ready to apply.

Budgeting and Salary Expectations During the Non-Training Phase

Because the practical implication of the Act, for many IMGs, is a longer period spent in non-training NHS roles before a training-post application becomes realistic, it is worth planning financially for that phase rather than assuming income will jump immediately to a training-grade salary. Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow posts are typically banded closer to Foundation or core-training pay scales depending on the role's seniority and responsibility, and the site's detailed UK doctor salary guide sets out realistic pay expectations at each career stage, alongside the currency context many IMGs planning a move from South Asia find useful. Building a personal budget around a realistic, possibly extended non-training phase — rather than the fastest theoretical route to a training-grade salary — tends to produce a far less stressful transition to UK practice.

How This Fits Alongside Other 2026 Policy Changes

The Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 did not arrive in isolation — it sits alongside several other 2026 policy shifts that IMGs planning a UK career should be tracking together, since they interact in practice even though each is a separate piece of policy. The government's decision to close the long-running Medical Training Initiative scheme, covered in the site's dedicated update on the Medical Training Initiative scheme closure in 2026, removes a separate route some IMGs previously used to gain UK clinical experience on a time-limited basis, which makes the Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow route discussed above even more central to a realistic IMG career plan. Meanwhile, changes to visa salary thresholds, detailed in the site's coverage of the 2026 UK visa salary threshold changes for doctors, affect the financial viability of specific NHS posts for sponsored doctors and should be factored into the same planning conversation as training-post competition.

Taken together, these changes point in a consistent direction: the UK government is deliberately tightening the training-post and sponsorship environment for internationally recruited doctors while continuing to expand overall training capacity over a longer timeframe. An IMG who understands all three changes as one connected policy shift, rather than three unrelated headlines, is far better placed to plan realistically than one reacting to each announcement in isolation.

What the Act Does Not Change: A Direct Recap

Given how much anxiety this legislation has understandably generated, it is worth recapping, directly and without qualification, everything that remains exactly as it was before the Act:

  • UKMLA eligibility criteria are set by the GMC and are entirely untouched by this Act.
  • The UKMLA's content, structure, and pass standard — the Applied Knowledge Test and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment — are unaffected.
  • GMC registration and licensing requirements, including primary source verification and English language testing, continue exactly as before.
  • The right to apply for non-training NHS roles, such as Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow posts, is entirely unaffected, since these roles are recruited directly by individual trusts outside the Oriel and UKFPO systems the Act governs.
  • Visa and immigration routes for doctors generally are governed by separate Home Office rules, not by this Act, though IMGs should still track related visa policy changes separately, as noted above.

The Act is, deliberately and narrowly, about the order in which a capped number of training places are offered. It is not a statement about who may become a doctor registered to practise in the UK.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 stop IMGs from working in the UK?

No. The Act affects the order in which Foundation Programme and specialty/GP training places are offered — it does not change UKMLA eligibility, GMC registration requirements, or an IMG's right to apply for non-training NHS roles such as Trust Grade or Clinical Fellow posts. An IMG can still register with the GMC and work as a doctor in the UK under exactly the same rules that applied before the Act.

When did the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 come into force?

The Bill was introduced to Parliament on 13 January 2026, fast-tracked through both Houses, and received Royal Assent on 5 March 2026, becoming the Medical Training (Prioritisation) Act 2026. It was designed to apply to foundation and specialty training places starting in August 2026, with prioritisation applied at the offer stage for the 2026 recruitment cycle since shortlisting was already underway.

Who is prioritised under the Act for specialty training in 2026?

For specialty and GP training, the Act prioritises UK medical graduates, applicants with a primary medical qualification from Ireland, Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, or Switzerland, and individuals who have completed the UK Foundation Programme or another relevant qualifying UK training programme. For 2026 offers specifically, it also prioritises applicants with certain UK immigration statuses, including British or Irish citizenship, indefinite leave to remain, right of abode, or EU Settlement Scheme status.

Can I still sit the UKMLA if I am not in a priority group under this Act?

Yes. UKMLA eligibility is set entirely by the GMC and has no connection to the Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026's priority groups. The Act governs competition for a specific category of training posts, not who may sit the licensing assessment or register with the GMC.

Why did the UK government introduce this Act?

The government introduced the Act after specialty training applications rose from around 12,000 in 2019 to nearly 40,000 by 2026, with 15,723 UK-trained doctors and 25,257 overseas-trained doctors competing for just 12,833 round 1 and 2 posts in 2025 alone. It sits alongside the NHS Long Term Workforce Plan's commitment to expand training places by roughly 24% by 2028/29 and a wider ambition to reduce internationally recruited NHS staff from around 34% to under 10% by 2035.

Does the Act affect Foundation Programme applications from UK graduates only?

UK primary medical qualification holders are the first priority group for Foundation Programme allocation, but the Act also prioritises applicants with a primary medical qualification from an Irish medical school, and from Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, or Switzerland, reflecting existing international agreements. Applicants outside these groups remain eligible to apply but are considered after priority-group applicants have been offered places.

What should IMGs do now to plan around the Act?

IMGs should focus on building genuine UK clinical experience through non-training routes such as Trust Grade or Clinical Fellow posts, which sit outside the Act's scope entirely, while assembling a strong portfolio of audits, teaching, and references ahead of applying for training. Building in a longer, more realistic timeline before expecting a training-post offer, and tracking related 2026 policy changes such as visa salary thresholds and the closure of the Medical Training Initiative scheme, are both sensible planning steps.

Conclusion

The Medical Training Prioritisation Act 2026 is a significant, fast-moving change to how UK foundation and specialty training places are allocated, but it is not the barrier to a UK medical career that early headlines sometimes suggested. It reorders competition for a capped number of training posts in favour of UK graduates and a small number of defined priority groups; it leaves the UKMLA, GMC registration, and the right to work as a doctor in the UK entirely untouched. For IMGs, the sensible response is not retreat but adjustment — building real UK clinical experience through non-training routes, strengthening a portfolio deliberately rather than passively, and planning around a realistic, somewhat longer timeline to a training post.
