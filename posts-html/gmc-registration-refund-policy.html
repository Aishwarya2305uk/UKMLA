The GMC registration refund policy is one of the least understood parts of the entire UK medical licensing journey, largely because most candidates never expect to need it — they assume that once a registration fee is paid, it simply disappears into the system regardless of what happens next. In reality, the General Medical Council operates a defined, published set of rules that determine exactly when a fee is returned, when it is partially retained, and when nothing comes back at all. Whether you are a UK medical graduate applying for provisional registration, an International Medical Graduate (IMG) who has just paid for full registration after passing the AKT and CPSA, or a doctor already on the register who is thinking about leaving the UK mid-year, understanding this policy protects you from losing money you didn't need to lose.

This guide is deliberately different from the site's general fee guides, which set out how much the AKT, CPSA and registration cost in the first place — see the 2026 cost guide for IMGs for that breakdown. Here, the focus is entirely on the mechanics of getting money back: what happens if you withdraw an application, what happens if the GMC refuses it, what the mysterious "scrutiny fee" actually is, and why leaving the register partway through the year can trigger a partial refund of your annual retention fee. We also cover, in plain terms, the handful of situations in which no refund is given at all, and why the GMC treats those cases differently.

Fee refund rules, like every other part of GMC fee policy, are reviewed periodically and can change with notice. Every figure in this guide reflects the position set out in the GMC's own application fee refunds and fees we keep guidance as of 2026, cross-checked against the Registration Fee Regulations 2026, and we link directly to both primary sources throughout this guide so you can verify the current position before you apply, withdraw, or request a refund under the GMC registration refund policy.

The Quick Answer: What the GMC Refunds and What It Keeps

In short: the GMC registration refund policy works like this — if a registration application is closed, refused, or withdrawn, the GMC may refund the registration fee minus a non-refundable scrutiny fee of £110, which covers the administrative work already carried out on your application. Where a refund is due, the GMC's stated aim is to process it automatically, within 5 working days, back to the original payment method — the same card for a card payment, or the bank account on file for a Direct Debit payment.

That single rule underpins almost everything else in the GMC registration refund policy, so it is worth restating in full before we go further. The scrutiny fee is not a penalty — it is a fixed administration charge that reflects the fact that GMC staff have already reviewed documents, checked identity, verified qualifications, or processed part of your file by the time an application closes, is refused, or is withdrawn. Whatever remains of your registration fee after that £110 is deducted is what gets refunded, provided your account is in credit and there is nothing else outstanding.

Separately, doctors who are already fully registered and who choose to leave the register voluntarily partway through a year may be entitled to a refund of the unused portion of their annual retention fee — a different mechanism from the scrutiny fee, covered in detail further down this guide. And there is a third, equally important category the GMC registration refund policy addresses directly: situations where the GMC keeps the entire fee and issues no refund whatsoever, most commonly when a doctor's registration is removed for failing to pay a fee, failing to keep in contact with the Registrar, or failing to meet a regulatory requirement. Each of these three scenarios — withdrawal/refusal, voluntary removal, and non-refundable removal — is unpacked separately below.

Understanding the Registration Fee Lifecycle

Before diving into the refund mechanics themselves, it helps to understand the shape of the registration fee lifecycle as a whole, because the point at which you are in that lifecycle determines which part of the GMC registration refund policy applies to you.

Stage One: Paying the Initial Registration Fee

For most IMGs, the registration fee is paid after passing both the AKT and CPSA, once the application for full registration with a licence to practise is submitted through GMC Online. UK medical graduates pay a comparable, generally lower, fee when applying for provisional registration on graduation. At this point, the fee sits with the GMC while your application is assessed — checking your identity documents, your primary qualification, your English language evidence, and any declarations you have made about health, criminal record, or fitness to practise.

Stage Two: The Application Is Assessed

During assessment, GMC staff carry out what is formally described as scrutiny of the application — verifying documents, cross-checking data, and in some cases requesting further evidence. This is the work the £110 scrutiny fee exists to cover, and it is incurred regardless of the eventual outcome. Whether your application ultimately succeeds, is refused, or is withdrawn by you partway through, this scrutiny work has already happened and the GMC does not waive its cost.

Stage Three: An Outcome Is Reached

From here, one of several things happens. The application may be approved, in which case registration is granted and no refund question arises. Alternatively, the application may be closed by the GMC (for example, because required information was never supplied), refused (because the GMC was not satisfied the applicant met the requirements for registration), or withdrawn (because the applicant themselves decided not to proceed). It is this second group of outcomes — closed, refused, or withdrawn — that triggers the refund-minus-scrutiny-fee rule described in the quick answer above, and each is worth examining individually because the practical triggers differ.

What Happens If You Withdraw Your Application: The Refund Policy in Practice

Withdrawing a registration application is the most common scenario in which IMGs and UK graduates alike encounter the GMC registration refund policy directly, because it is entirely within the applicant's control. Candidates sometimes withdraw an application because their circumstances change — a visa is refused, a job offer falls through, or they decide to pursue further training before registering. Others withdraw because they realise partway through the process that they will not be able to satisfy a requirement, such as an outstanding document that cannot be obtained in time.

How the GMC Registration Refund Policy Works on Withdrawal

If you formally withdraw your application, the GMC will refund your registration fee minus the £110 scrutiny fee, provided your account is in credit. This reflects the fact that even a withdrawn application has typically already had some scrutiny work carried out on it. The refund itself, where due, is generally processed automatically rather than requiring a separate manual request — but as covered later in this guide, it is still worth confirming your bank details and payment method are correctly recorded so nothing delays the transfer.

Timing Matters Less Than You Might Expect

Unlike exam booking cancellations, where the refund amount depends heavily on how many days before the exam you cancel, the registration fee refund policy does not operate on a sliding scale tied to how far into the assessment process you are when you withdraw. Whether you withdraw a day after submitting or several months in, the same principle applies: registration fee back, minus the £110 scrutiny fee. This is a meaningfully different structure from the AKT and CPSA cancellation policy, and candidates who assume the two work the same way are sometimes surprised. If your plans are changing mid-application — for example, if you are reconsidering whether to pursue a foundation year route versus specialty training before committing to registration — it is worth making that decision as early as possible, since delaying a withdrawal doesn't reduce what the GMC retains, but it does mean more of your time and planning is tied up in an application you may not complete.

Practical Reasons IMGs Withdraw Mid-Process

Under the GMC registration refund policy, the reason for withdrawal has no bearing on the refund calculation itself — but in practice, the most common withdrawal triggers among IMGs are: a change in visa sponsorship arrangements that makes taking up a UK post impractical in the short term; a decision to first complete additional clinical experience or further exams in the home country; a realisation that additional primary source verification will take longer than the application window allows; or simply a change of heart about relocating to the UK at that particular time. None of these reasons affect the refund calculation — the scrutiny fee is retained regardless of the reason for withdrawal, because the fee reflects work already done, not the applicant's motivation for stopping.

What Happens If Your Application Is Refused

A refusal is different from a withdrawal in one important respect — it is a decision made by the GMC rather than the applicant — but under the GMC registration refund policy, the refund mechanics are the same. If the GMC refuses an application for registration, for example because it is not satisfied that the applicant's primary qualification, English language evidence, or fitness-to-practise declarations meet the required standard, the registration fee is refunded minus the same £110 scrutiny fee.

Common Reasons Applications Are Refused

Refusals are relatively uncommon compared with successful applications, but they do happen. Typical reasons include: primary qualification verification that cannot be completed or comes back with unresolved discrepancies; English language test results that do not meet the required score thresholds by the time of assessment; incomplete or contradictory identity documentation; or a fitness-to-practise concern raised during the application that the GMC decides cannot be resolved in the applicant's favour at that time. In many of these cases, particularly where the issue is a fixable documentation gap, applicants are given the opportunity to provide further evidence before a final refusal decision is reached — refusal is generally a last resort rather than an immediate response to any queried document.

A Refusal Is Not Necessarily Final

It is worth noting that a refused application does not always mean the door is permanently closed. Depending on the reason for refusal, an applicant may be able to address the underlying issue — retaking an English language test, obtaining outstanding verification, or resolving a fitness-to-practise concern — and submit a new application later. Because a new application carries its own registration fee and its own scrutiny fee, candidates who anticipate a possible refusal are often better served by resolving documentation issues before submitting in the first place, rather than submitting early and risking a second full fee cycle. This is one of several reasons the site consistently recommends working through the registration guide pillar page and the detailed step-by-step GMC registration guide for IMGs in full before submitting, rather than treating the application as a formality to be corrected after the fact.

Voluntary Removal from the Register: Retention Fee Refunds

The scrutiny fee rule applies to registration applications — the process of getting onto the register in the first place. Once you are registered, a separate mechanism governs refunds relating to your annual retention fee, the fee registered doctors pay each year to remain on the register.

What Happens If You Leave the Register Partway Through the Year

If a doctor chooses to come off the register voluntarily partway through the annual retention cycle — for example, because they are leaving medical practice, moving to a jurisdiction where UK registration is no longer needed, or retiring earlier than the next renewal date — they may be due a refund of the unused portion of that year's annual retention fee. In other words, if you have already paid your annual retention fee for the coming year but decide to leave the register only a few months in, the GMC does not simply keep the full year's fee for a period of registration you didn't use.

Why This Differs from the Scrutiny Fee Rule

This is a genuinely distinct mechanism from the registration application side of the GMC registration refund policy described above, and it is worth being clear about the difference so you know which rule applies to your situation. Under the GMC registration refund policy, the scrutiny fee deduction applies specifically to registration applications that are closed, refused, or withdrawn — it is a one-off administrative charge tied to the work of processing an application. The retention fee refund, by contrast, applies to fully registered doctors who are already on the register and choose to leave it before the year they have paid for has run its course — it is a proportionate refund of time not used, not a scrutiny-related deduction. A doctor voluntarily removing themselves from the register partway through the year is not typically charged a further £110 scrutiny fee on top, since no new application scrutiny is being carried out; the retention fee refund is simply calculated against the unused portion of the year already paid for.

Who Typically Uses Voluntary Removal

Under this arm of the GMC registration refund policy, doctors request voluntary removal for a wide range of reasons: relocating permanently to practise in another country, taking an extended career break, moving into a non-clinical role that does not require registration, or simply deciding to retire. IMGs who complete a period of UK training or service and then return home sometimes choose voluntary removal rather than continuing to pay the annual retention fee for a registration they no longer intend to use — and in doing so, may be entitled to a partial refund for the portion of the year not yet served.

What the GMC Registration Refund Policy Does Not Cover — and Why

Just as important as knowing when a refund applies is knowing when it categorically does not. This is the part of the GMC registration refund policy that catches the most people off guard, because the GMC is explicit that no refund is given in a defined set of circumstances, all of which relate to registration being removed for reasons connected to the doctor's own compliance, rather than a voluntary or administrative closure.

Failure to Pay a Required Fee

If a doctor's entry is removed from the register because they failed to pay a fee that was due — most commonly the annual retention fee — no refund is given. This is a meaningful distinction from voluntary removal: a doctor who proactively asks to come off the register partway through a paid year may get a partial refund of the unused portion, but a doctor who simply stops paying and is removed for non-payment receives nothing back, because there is no "unused portion already paid for" to refund in the first place, and because the removal in this case is a regulatory consequence of the doctor's own default, not a request the doctor initiated.

Failure to Maintain Contact with the Registrar

All registered doctors are required to keep the GMC informed of a current contact address and to respond to correspondence from the Registrar. If a doctor is removed from the register because they have failed to maintain contact — for example, GMC correspondence about renewal or revalidation goes unanswered over an extended period — no refund is given for that removal. This underlines a point that is easy to overlook, particularly for IMGs who relocate frequently: keeping your GMC Online contact details current is not just an administrative courtesy, it is a condition that protects your right to any refund you might otherwise be entitled to.

Failure to Comply with Regulatory Requirements

Similarly, if removal follows a failure to comply with other regulatory requirements — for instance, requirements connected to revalidation, or conditions attached to a doctor's registration — no refund is due. The unifying principle across all three non-refund scenarios is the same: refunds are available where a doctor or applicant proactively withdraws, is refused through no fault connected to non-compliance, or voluntarily and properly requests removal partway through a paid period. Refunds are not available where the register entry is removed as a consequence of the doctor's own failure to meet a basic ongoing obligation.

Why the Distinction Exists

This structure within the GMC registration refund policy is not arbitrary. It exists to preserve an incentive for doctors to engage properly with their regulatory obligations — paying fees on time, keeping contact details current, and complying with revalidation and other requirements — rather than treating non-compliance as a cost-free way to exit the register with money back. From an applicant's perspective, the practical lesson is simple: if you know you want to come off the register, request voluntary removal yourself rather than letting your registration lapse through non-payment or non-contact, since the former can produce a partial refund and the latter guarantees none.

How Refunds Are Actually Processed

Understanding the GMC registration refund policy on paper is only half the picture — it also helps to know what actually happens mechanically once a refund becomes due, since this is the part candidates ask about most once they realise they are entitled to money back.

Automatic Processing When Your Account Is in Credit

Where a refund is due and the applicant's GMC Online account is in credit, the GMC's standard practice is to process the refund automatically, without the applicant needing to submit a separate refund request form. The GMC aims to complete this within 5 working days. This is a genuinely fast turnaround by the standards of most public-body fee refunds, and it reflects the fact that the GMC's payment systems are designed to route a credit balance back out automatically once an application is closed, refused, or withdrawn, rather than requiring manual reconciliation for every case.

Refunds Go Back to the Original Payment Method

A detail that trips up a meaningful number of applicants: refunds are returned to the original payment method used, not to a new card or account you might prefer to use instead. If you paid your registration fee by debit or credit card, the refund is returned to that same card. If you paid by Direct Debit, the refund goes to the bank account details the GMC holds on file for that Direct Debit instruction. This matters in two practical ways. First, if the card you paid with has since expired, been cancelled, or been replaced, the refund may be delayed while the transaction is resolved with your card issuer rather than the GMC — it is worth keeping an old card active, where possible, until any pending refund has cleared. Second, if your bank account details have changed since you set up a Direct Debit, you should update those details with the GMC promptly, since an outdated account on file is one of the more common causes of a delayed retention fee refund.

What to Do If a Refund Doesn't Arrive

If more than 5 working days have passed since your application was closed, refused, or withdrawn, and your account was confirmed to be in credit, the sensible next step is to log in to GMC Online and check your account balance and transaction history first, since in many cases the refund has processed but simply hasn't been reflected in your own banking app or statement cycle yet due to normal card-processing delays on the receiving end. If the GMC Online account itself still shows an outstanding credit balance, or if the transaction history shows no refund initiated, contact the GMC's registration and fees team directly, quoting your GMC reference number, the date your application was withdrawn, refused, or closed, and the amount you are expecting back.

How and When to Request a Refund

Although the GMC registration refund policy is designed to process many refunds automatically, there are situations in which it is sensible — or necessary — to proactively raise the matter with the GMC rather than simply waiting.

When to Contact the GMC Directly

You should get in touch directly if: your application was closed, refused, or withdrawn more than 5 working days ago and no refund has appeared; you believe you are due a partial retention fee refund following voluntary removal from the register and have not received confirmation of the amount; your payment method has changed since you paid the original fee and you need to update your bank details before a refund can be issued; or you are unsure whether your particular circumstances fall under the standard refund policy or one of the non-refund exceptions described above.

What Information to Have Ready

Before contacting the GMC about a refund query, gather the following so the query can be resolved as quickly as possible: your GMC reference number; the date you submitted the original application or payment; the date and method by which you withdrew the application, were notified of a refusal, or requested voluntary removal; confirmation of the payment method originally used (card or Direct Debit); and, if your bank or card details have changed, the updated details you want the refund sent to. Having this information ready in a single message or call, rather than provided piecemeal over several contacts, meaningfully speeds up resolution.

Documentation IMGs Specifically Should Keep

IMGs are in a materially different position from UK graduates when it comes to navigating the GMC registration refund policy in practice, because the registration fee they pay is larger, the application process involves more moving parts (primary source verification, English language evidence, sometimes EPIC records), and — for many candidates — the process is being managed from outside the UK, which can make phone-based resolution slower and more expensive. For these reasons, IMGs should keep the following documentation from the moment they begin a registration application, not just from the moment a refund issue arises: a copy of the payment confirmation or receipt for the registration fee, showing the amount, date, and payment method used; any written confirmation from the GMC of the application being closed, refused, or withdrawn, including the specific date this took effect; correspondence relating to any request to withdraw the application, particularly if this was communicated by email rather than through a GMC Online action, since email correspondence provides a clear timestamped record; bank or card statements showing the original outgoing payment, which may be needed if a card refund needs to be traced or disputed with your bank; and, where relevant, records of any EPIC verification fees paid separately, since these are handled by a different body and fall outside the GMC's own refund policy entirely — EPIC fees are not covered by the GMC's scrutiny fee refund mechanism at all. For IMGs whose registration touches on EPIC verification specifically, our dedicated step-by-step guide to GMC registration and EPIC verification is worth reading alongside this one, since verification fees and registration fees are handled by different systems with different refund rules.

Keep Records Even After a Successful Application

It is easy to assume that once an application succeeds, the GMC registration refund policy no longer has any relevance and documentation can be discarded. In practice, keeping a clear record of your original registration payment remains useful even after successful registration, because it establishes a clean paper trail should you ever need to request a partial retention fee refund on voluntary removal in future, or should any dispute arise later about which fees were paid, when, and by which method.

How This Compares to Exam Fee Refunds

It is worth briefly distinguishing the GMC registration refund policy from the separate cancellation and refund rules that apply to the AKT and CPSA exam fees, since candidates sometimes conflate the two. Exam fee refunds are governed by a sliding scale tied to how many days before the exam date you cancel, with no refund at all available inside 14 days of the exam. Registration fee refunds, by contrast, are not time-sensitive in the same way — the £110 scrutiny fee deduction applies whenever an application is closed, refused, or withdrawn, regardless of how long the application had been open. If you are weighing up whether to withdraw a registration application versus deferring an upcoming exam sitting, or you are managing both processes at once, it is worth reading the exam fee rules and the GMC registration refund policy as entirely separate systems rather than assuming one mirrors the other. Candidates who need to resit either exam after an unsuccessful attempt should also see the rules, limits, and repeat costs covered in our resits guide, since resit fees follow the standard exam fee structure, not the registration refund rules discussed here.

Why Cost Transparency Matters Beyond the GMC Itself

One underappreciated benefit of the GMC registration refund policy is how transparent it is by design. The scrutiny fee is a fixed, published figure; the refund timeline is a stated number of working days; and the circumstances under which no refund applies are set out explicitly rather than left to case-by-case discretion. This level of transparency is a useful benchmark to apply when evaluating other costs in your UKMLA journey — particularly paid preparation providers, whose refund and cancellation terms are often far less clearly stated. Before committing significant money to any paid preparation product, including an intensive crash course aimed at doctors preparing for the exam, it is worth asking the provider the same basic questions the GMC answers plainly on its own website: what exactly is refundable, what administrative fee (if any) is retained, and how quickly a refund is processed if you change your mind or your circumstances change. A provider that cannot answer these questions as clearly as the GMC does for its own fees is a signal worth taking seriously.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where can I find the official GMC registration refund policy?

The official GMC registration refund policy is published on the GMC's own website under "application fee refunds and fees we keep," and the underlying legal basis sits in the GMC's Registration Fee Regulations, updated annually. Because fee amounts and administrative charges are reviewed periodically, always check the current version on the GMC's website directly before relying on any specific figure, including those quoted in this guide.

What is the GMC scrutiny fee and why isn't it refunded?

The scrutiny fee is a fixed £110 administration charge that covers the work the GMC has already carried out assessing a registration application — checking documents, verifying identity, and reviewing evidence — regardless of the application's outcome. It is retained when an application is closed, refused, or withdrawn because that assessment work has already happened and cannot be "undone," which is why it sits outside the portion of the fee that gets refunded.

How long does a GMC registration refund take?

Where a refund is due and your GMC Online account is in credit, the GMC aims to process it automatically within 5 working days, returning the money to the original payment method — the same card for a card payment or the bank account on file for a Direct Debit. If more than 5 working days have passed with no refund and your account shows a credit balance, it is reasonable to contact the GMC's fees team directly with your reference number.

Do I get a full refund if I withdraw my GMC registration application?

No. If you withdraw a registration application, the GMC refunds the registration fee minus the £110 scrutiny fee, provided your account is in credit. This applies regardless of how far into the assessment process your application had progressed before you withdrew it.

What happens to my registration fee if my application is refused?

If your application for GMC registration is refused, the same rule applies as for a withdrawal: the registration fee is refunded minus the £110 scrutiny fee. A refusal does not automatically bar you from reapplying later, but a new application would involve paying the registration fee — and the scrutiny fee — again.

Can I get a refund if I leave the GMC register partway through the year?

Yes, in a specific sense. If you voluntarily come off the register partway through the year after already paying your annual retention fee, you may be due a refund of the unused portion of that fee for the remainder of the year. This is a different mechanism from the registration-application scrutiny fee rule and typically does not involve a further £110 deduction, since no new application scrutiny is being carried out.

Will I get a refund if the GMC removes me from the register for not paying my fee?

No. If your entry is removed from the register because you failed to pay a required fee, failed to maintain contact with the Registrar, or failed to comply with a regulatory requirement, no refund is given. These are treated as regulatory consequences of non-compliance rather than a voluntary, proactive removal, and the GMC's policy explicitly excludes them from any refund.

Does EPIC verification fee refund policy work the same way as the GMC's?

No. EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) verification fees are charged and administered separately from GMC registration fees and are not covered by the GMC's own scrutiny fee refund policy at all. IMGs who have paid for EPIC verification should keep separate records of that payment and check EPIC's own terms directly, since a GMC registration refund does not automatically extend to any EPIC charges paid alongside it.

Should I request voluntary removal myself rather than let my registration lapse?

Yes, if you know you want to come off the register. Requesting voluntary removal proactively can make you eligible for a partial refund of the unused portion of your annual retention fee, whereas allowing your registration to be removed through non-payment or loss of contact with the Registrar guarantees no refund at all. Taking the proactive route is the only way to preserve any entitlement to money back.

Conclusion

The GMC registration refund policy is more structured, and in most cases fairer, than candidates initially assume — but only if you understand which of the three broad categories your situation falls into. Withdraw, get refused, or have your application closed, and you'll typically see your registration fee back minus the £110 scrutiny fee, usually within 5 working days. Leave the register voluntarily partway through a paid year, and you may be entitled to a partial retention fee refund for the time not used. But let your registration lapse through unpaid fees, lost contact, or non-compliance, and no refund applies at all. Because fee amounts and processes are reviewed periodically, always confirm the current scrutiny fee and refund timeline directly on the GMC's website before applying, withdrawing, or requesting voluntary removal — and keep clear documentation of every payment and communication along the way, particularly if you are managing the process as an IMG from outside the UK.
