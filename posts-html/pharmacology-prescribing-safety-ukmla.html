UKMLA pharmacology revision sits at the centre of the Applied Knowledge Test, and no candidate can afford to treat it as a side topic. Pharmacology and safe prescribing is one of the most consistently high-yield content areas across the whole exam, since it is tested both as standalone pharmacology questions and woven into clinical management questions across every organ system. A cardiology station is rarely just about diagnosing heart failure — it usually also asks which drug class to start, which one to avoid, and what to monitor. Consequently, a candidate who has genuinely mastered pharmacology gains marks in domains that, on the surface, look unrelated to drugs at all.

This guide is a revision-focused companion to the site's dedicated piece on prescribing errors and incident reporting, which covers the patient-safety and professionalism angle of things going wrong. Here, the focus is different: this is a structured tour of the drug classes, mechanisms, and safe-prescribing principles that the MLA content map expects every candidate to know before sitting the exam. Whether a reader is a UK medical student consolidating five years of pharmacology teaching, or an international medical graduate (IMG) meeting the British National Formulary (BNF) for the first time, the aim of this article is the same: to map out what topic areas and question styles to expect, and how to revise them efficiently.

Quick Answer: How Important Is Pharmacology for the UKMLA?

Pharmacology and prescribing safety is one of the highest-yield revision areas in the entire UKMLA AKT, because it is examined twice over — once as dedicated pharmacology questions (mechanism of action, drug class, interactions) and again indirectly, embedded inside almost every clinical scenario across cardiovascular, respiratory, endocrine, psychiatric, and infectious disease medicine. In practice, this means a well-organised pharmacology and prescribing safety revision plan pays off far beyond the questions that are explicitly labelled as "pharmacology."

The AKT does not test pharmacology in isolation from clinical reasoning; instead, it typically presents a patient scenario and asks the candidate to choose, adjust, or avoid a particular drug based on the clinical picture. Therefore, revising drug classes purely as a list of names and mechanisms is a weaker strategy than revising them alongside the clinical contexts in which they appear. A candidate preparing for how the AKT pass mark and pass rate are calculated should understand that pharmacology questions are distributed widely enough across the paper that a weak grasp of this single content area can meaningfully depress an overall score, even for candidates who are clinically strong elsewhere.

For IMGs in particular, UKMLA pharmacology revision carries an additional dimension: it is not only about learning UK clinical practice, but about learning to think and prescribe within the specific conventions of the BNF, which may differ noticeably from the national formulary or textbook a candidate trained with originally. The remainder of this guide works through the drug classes most likely to appear, the BNF itself as a reference tool, how interaction and contraindication questions are typically constructed, prescribing considerations in special populations, and a practical revision strategy for this content area.

Readers who are still mapping out their overall study timetable may find it useful to read this guide alongside the site's broader UKMLA preparation guide, since safe-prescribing revision fits most naturally into a study plan once the overall exam structure, timeline, and question styles are already clear. Pharmacology and prescribing safety revision is not a topic to leave until the final weeks before the exam; because it recurs across so many clinical domains, it rewards being built up steadily, alongside systems-based revision, from early in a candidate's preparation.

High-Yield Drug Classes to Know Cold

A relatively small number of drug classes account for a disproportionate share of AKT pharmacology questions. Building genuine familiarity with the mechanism, main indication, main contraindication, and one or two "trap" facts for each of these classes is a far more efficient use of revision time than attempting to memorise the entire BNF cover to cover. Cardiovascular Drugs: ACE Inhibitors, Beta-Blockers, and Anticoagulants

Cardiovascular pharmacology is tested more heavily than almost any other system, in part because heart failure, hypertension, atrial fibrillation, and ischaemic heart disease all appear repeatedly across the AKT blueprint. ACE inhibitors are a cornerstone of heart failure and hypertension management, and the AKT typically expects candidates to recognise the class by its "-pril" suffix, understand its blood-pressure-lowering and cardioprotective mechanism, and know the main reasons a prescriber would pause or avoid it — most notably in pregnancy, in bilateral renal artery stenosis, and where renal function or potassium levels need close monitoring after starting treatment. Angiotensin receptor blockers are frequently tested as the natural alternative when an ACE inhibitor causes an intolerable dry cough.

Beta-blockers are similarly high-yield, since they appear across cardiology, but also carry important safety caveats elsewhere in the curriculum. The AKT often asks candidates to recognise situations where a beta-blocker would usually be helpful (heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, post-myocardial infarction, rate control in atrial fibrillation) versus situations that demand caution, such as significant reactive airway disease. In addition, beta-blockers can blunt the usual adrenergic warning signs of hypoglycaemia, which is a classic cross-system question linking cardiovascular and endocrine pharmacology.

Anticoagulants deserve particular attention because the AKT tests both the underlying pharmacology and the practical decision-making around them. Candidates should be comfortable distinguishing vitamin K antagonists from direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) and from heparins, understanding broadly why renal function influences the choice and dosing of several agents in this class, and recognising the clinical situations — active bleeding, certain valve replacements, specific comorbidities — where one agent is preferred over another. Because anticoagulants carry a genuinely high risk of harm when prescribed incorrectly, this is one of the clearest areas where the AKT rewards a cautious, checklist-driven approach over confident guesswork.

Respiratory Drugs: Bronchodilators and Corticosteroids

Asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) management questions are a near-certainty on the AKT, and both conditions are built around a stepwise pharmacological approach. Short-acting beta-2 agonists provide rapid symptomatic relief, whereas long-acting beta-2 agonists and long-acting antimuscarinic agents are added for maintenance control once symptoms are not adequately managed on relief therapy alone. The AKT typically expects candidates to know the broad order in which treatments are escalated, rather than exact dosing, and to recognise when a patient's presentation suggests the current step of treatment has failed and that escalation — or a specialist review — is warranted.

Corticosteroids appear in both inhaled and systemic forms, and, similarly to bronchodilators, the distinction between the two is a frequent exam theme. Inhaled corticosteroids are generally well tolerated with a relatively low systemic side-effect burden when used as directed, whereas systemic corticosteroids carry a much broader range of adverse effects with prolonged use, including bone density loss, adrenal suppression, and metabolic disturbance. Consequently, the AKT often tests whether a candidate recognises the need for a steroid treatment card, the importance of not stopping long-term systemic steroids abruptly, and the "sick day" principle of adjusting steroid doses during intercurrent illness or before surgery.

Endocrine Drugs: Insulin Regimens and Oral Hypoglycaemics

Diabetes pharmacology is tested extensively, and this content area overlaps closely with the site's dedicated endocrinology and diabetes revision guide, which is worth reading alongside this section for the clinical detail behind the drug choices summarised here. On the insulin side, the AKT expects familiarity with the broad categories of insulin — rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting — and with common regimen structures such as basal-bolus dosing, rather than exact unit calculations. Recognising and managing hypoglycaemia, and understanding the sick-day adjustments needed around insulin therapy, are recurring themes.

In addition, oral hypoglycaemic agents are equally well represented. Metformin remains the usual first-line option in type 2 diabetes for most patients, and the AKT often tests the situations in which it needs to be used cautiously or avoided, particularly related to renal function. Other classes — including sulfonylureas, SGLT2 inhibitors, DPP-4 inhibitors, and GLP-1 receptor agonists — are increasingly tested for their differing effects on weight, cardiovascular risk, and hypoglycaemia risk, since NICE guidance increasingly steers drug choice based on a patient's individual comorbidity profile rather than a single universal algorithm.

Analgesia: The WHO Pain Ladder and Opioid Safety

Pain management questions are built around the World Health Organization's analgesic ladder, which moves from non-opioid analgesics, through weak opioids, to strong opioids as pain severity increases and lower steps prove insufficient. The AKT expects candidates to apply this framework to clinical scenarios — recognising, for example, when a patient's pain is inadequately controlled on a lower step and a logical next step is required, rather than memorising the ladder as an abstract diagram.

Opioid safety is a particularly rich exam theme because it combines pharmacology with patient safety principles. Candidates should understand that opioid dose requirements vary between routes and preparations, that opioids require dose caution in renal and hepatic impairment because of altered metabolite clearance, and that opioid toxicity has a recognisable clinical picture involving reduced consciousness, reduced respiratory rate, and pinpoint pupils. Furthermore, safe opioid prescribing includes considering laxative co-prescription for constipation, anticipating and counselling on tolerance and dependence risk, and following the additional legal requirements that apply to controlled drug prescriptions in the UK. The related theme of recognising and escalating a prescribing error involving an opioid — rather than the pharmacology of opioids itself — is covered in more depth in the site's dedicated prescribing errors and patient safety guide.

Antimicrobials: Appropriate Choice and Stewardship Principles

Similarly, antimicrobial questions on the AKT rarely ask for an obscure spectrum-of-activity fact in isolation; instead, they usually present an infection scenario and ask which antibiotic class is the appropriate first-line choice, or whether antibiotics are indicated at all. Consequently, candidates benefit far more from understanding the broad first-line approach to common infections — community-acquired pneumonia, urinary tract infection, cellulitis, and similar presentations — than from attempting to memorise an exhaustive spectrum chart for every antimicrobial in the BNF.

Antimicrobial stewardship is an explicit UKMLA theme in its own right. The exam expects candidates to recognise the harms of unnecessary or overly broad-spectrum antibiotic use, including the contribution to antimicrobial resistance, and to understand why a narrow-spectrum, evidence-based choice is usually preferred over a broad-spectrum "just in case" prescription. In addition, the AKT tests allergy-checking as a core safe-prescribing habit — recognising, for instance, that a documented penicillin allergy changes the appropriate first-line choice for many common infections, and that the nature of a reported allergy (a mild rash versus anaphylaxis) meaningfully changes the risk calculation around related drug classes.

Psychiatric Medication: Antidepressants, Antipsychotics, and Monitoring

Psychiatric pharmacology is tested more heavily on the UKMLA than many candidates expect, reflecting the significant weight given to mental health content across the MLA content map. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are typically the first-line antidepressant class for most presentations of depression and many anxiety disorders, and the AKT expects candidates to know the broad adverse-effect profile, the need for gradual dose changes rather than abrupt stopping, and situations requiring particular caution, such as the combination of multiple serotonergic drugs.

Meanwhile, antipsychotic pharmacology is tested with a strong emphasis on monitoring. Candidates should understand that antipsychotics, particularly certain second-generation agents, require regular monitoring of metabolic parameters such as weight, lipid profile, and blood glucose, alongside monitoring for movement-related side effects associated with some agents. Lithium — used as a mood stabiliser rather than strictly as an antipsychotic — is a perennial AKT favourite because of its narrow therapeutic index and its interactions with several commonly prescribed drug classes, including certain diuretics and anti-inflammatory drugs. As with all specific dosing and monitoring intervals, exact thresholds and frequencies should always be confirmed against current BNF and NICE guidance rather than memorised from a revision article, since these details are reviewed and updated periodically.

Taken together, these ten drug classes are the backbone of any efficient UKMLA pharmacology revision plan. However, they should not be revised as an isolated list; rather, each class is best learned alongside the specific clinical presentations it is most often paired with in question banks, since that is exactly how the AKT itself presents the material. A candidate who can name a drug class's mechanism but cannot recognise the clinical scenario in which it becomes the correct or incorrect answer has only completed half of the necessary preparation.

The British National Formulary: The UK's Standard Prescribing Reference

The British National Formulary, universally known as the BNF, is the standard prescribing reference used throughout UK clinical practice, and fluency with it is an explicit, examinable UKMLA skill rather than a background convenience. The BNF is jointly published by the British Medical Association and the Royal Pharmaceutical Society, and it organises drug information by therapeutic class, with consistent sections covering indications, contraindications, cautions, interactions, dosing, and monitoring for each entry.

For IMGs, the BNF is frequently the single biggest early stumbling block in UKMLA pharmacology revision, and this is worth naming explicitly rather than glossing over. Many national formularies elsewhere in the world use different structural conventions, different default drug naming, and sometimes different first-line choices for the same condition, based on local prescribing guidelines and drug availability. The BNF's use of International Non-proprietary Names (INNs) — the standardised generic drug names recognised internationally by the World Health Organization — can also be a source of early confusion, since a drug a candidate learned by a brand name or a locally used generic name in their training country may appear under an unfamiliar INN in the BNF.

In addition, the BNF's conventions for expressing frequency, route, and dose differ in some respects from formularies used in other countries, and this is precisely the kind of formatting detail the AKT likes to test through prescription-chart-style questions. Therefore, IMGs preparing for the UKMLA should treat active, hands-on time with the BNF — ideally the same digital interface used in UK clinical practice — as a core part of their revision, not an optional extra. Even experienced doctors who have prescribed confidently for years elsewhere need to build this specific UK-facing fluency, a point also relevant to readers checking the site's guide on UKMLA exemptions available to specialist and GP doctors, since exemption from parts of the exam does not remove the need to know current UK prescribing conventions in daily practice.

Key features of the BNF worth deliberately practising during revision include:

  • Navigating by drug class versus navigating by indication, and knowing when each approach is faster
  • Reading the interactions section for a given drug quickly and accurately
  • Locating renal and hepatic impairment guidance for a specific drug
  • Understanding the separate BNF for Children (BNFC) and why paediatric dosing is never simply extrapolated from adult dosing
  • Recognising the black triangle symbol used for newer medicines under enhanced safety monitoring

Drug Interactions and Contraindications as an AKT Question Style

One of the most recognisable AKT question formats presents a patient already established on one or more medications, then asks whether a newly proposed drug is safe to add — testing, in effect, whether the candidate spots a dangerous interaction or contraindication before it reaches the patient. This scenario-based format rewards genuine clinical reasoning over rote memorisation, since the same underlying pharmacological principle can be dressed up in many different clinical costumes across the exam.

A few interaction and contraindication patterns recur often enough to deserve deliberate revision attention. Drugs that affect renal potassium handling — including ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, and certain diuretics — are frequently combined in exam scenarios to test recognition of a cumulative hyperkalaemia risk. Similarly, drugs metabolised through shared liver enzyme pathways are often paired in questions to test whether a candidate recognises that one drug can raise or lower the blood level of another, sometimes to a dangerous degree. Anticoagulants combined with other drugs that increase bleeding risk are another classic combination, as are drugs with a narrow therapeutic index — such as lithium and certain antiepileptics — paired with anything that alters their clearance.

Contraindication questions typically test whether a candidate can match a patient's existing condition to a drug that should not be started, or should be started only with caution. Common exam pairings include reactive airway disease with non-selective beta-blockers, significant renal impairment with renally cleared drugs that accumulate, and pregnancy with any of a well-recognised list of medications considered unsafe at that life stage. In addition, allergy status is treated as a contraindication in its own right, and the AKT expects candidates to check it as reflexively as they check renal function.

The most effective way to prepare for this question style is not to memorise long interaction tables in isolation, but to practise applying a consistent mental checklist to every prescribing scenario: what is this patient already taking, what does this patient's renal and hepatic function look like, is this patient pregnant or breastfeeding, and does this patient have a documented allergy. Candidates who build this checklist habit during revision generally find that AKT interaction questions become pattern-recognition exercises rather than memory tests.

Prescribing in Special Populations

Cross-cutting prescribing questions — where the same drug decision changes depending on the patient in front of you — are one of the UKMLA's favourite ways to test whether pharmacology knowledge is genuinely applied rather than memorised in isolation. Four populations come up repeatedly enough to deserve dedicated revision time.

Renal Impairment

Many drugs are cleared partly or wholly by the kidneys, and reduced renal function can cause a drug or its active metabolites to accumulate to harmful levels if the dose is not adjusted. The AKT expects candidates to recognise which broad drug categories most commonly require renal dose adjustment or avoidance — including several antibiotics, anticoagulants, oral hypoglycaemics, and analgesics — and to know that renal function should always be checked before prescribing a renally cleared drug in a patient with known or suspected kidney impairment. Exact dose-adjustment figures and eGFR thresholds change between BNF editions and should always be confirmed against current guidance at the point of prescribing, rather than recalled from memory.

Hepatic Impairment

The liver is the primary site of metabolism for a very large proportion of prescribed drugs, and hepatic impairment can reduce a patient's ability to clear or activate certain medications safely. The AKT tests broad recognition of drug classes that require particular caution in significant liver disease, alongside an understanding that hepatic impairment can also affect clotting and albumin levels, which in turn changes how some drugs behave in the body. As with renal impairment, the exam rewards recognising the pattern and knowing to check the BNF, rather than recalling specific numerical dose adjustments.

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

Prescribing in pregnancy and breastfeeding is tested frequently, and the underlying principle is straightforward even though the list of individual drugs is long: a range of well-recognised medications carry teratogenic risk or other fetal harm and should generally be avoided or substituted in pregnancy, while a separate but overlapping set of considerations applies to breastfeeding, where the relevant question is how much of a drug passes into breast milk and what effect that could have on an infant. The AKT expects candidates to recognise commonly tested examples of drugs considered unsafe in pregnancy and to know that specialist or BNF guidance should always be checked before prescribing to a pregnant or breastfeeding patient, rather than relying on general impressions of a drug's safety.

Paediatric and Elderly Prescribing

At the opposite ends of the age spectrum, prescribing decisions change substantially. Paediatric prescribing is weight-based and uses the BNF for Children rather than simple extrapolation from adult doses, reflecting real differences in drug metabolism and clearance between children and adults. Elderly prescribing, meanwhile, is complicated by polypharmacy, age-related decline in renal and hepatic function, and increased sensitivity to certain drug classes, particularly those acting on the central nervous system. The AKT expects familiarity with the general principle that a good prescriber actively reviews an elderly patient's full medication list for drugs that may no longer be appropriate, rather than simply adding new medications on top of an existing long list.

Recognising Common AKT Pharmacology Question Patterns

Beyond individual drug classes, UKMLA pharmacology revision benefits enormously from recognising the small number of question structures the AKT reuses again and again. Once a candidate has seen a pattern a handful of times in question-bank practice, an unfamiliar drug name inside that same pattern becomes far less intimidating, because the underlying logic of the question is already familiar.

The "which drug should be started" pattern. A clinical vignette establishes a diagnosis, then asks which of five drug options is the most appropriate first choice. These questions usually hinge on a single differentiating clinical detail — a comorbidity, an allergy, a pregnancy status, or a renal function result — that rules out several otherwise-plausible options. Reading the vignette twice, once for the diagnosis and once specifically hunting for that differentiating detail, is a reliable exam technique.

The "which drug should be stopped or avoided" pattern. Here the vignette presents a patient already on a stable medication regimen, then introduces a new clinical development — an acute kidney injury, a new diagnosis, an upcoming surgical procedure — and asks which existing drug now needs to be withheld, substituted, or dose-adjusted. This pattern rewards the same interactions-and-contraindications checklist described later in this guide.

The "what should be monitored" pattern. A patient is started on, or has been taking, a drug with a recognised monitoring requirement — lithium, methotrexate, an anticoagulant, an antipsychotic — and the question asks which parameter should be checked, or how often monitoring should occur in principle. Because exact monitoring intervals shift between guideline updates, the safest revision approach is to know which broad parameter matters for each drug class (renal function, liver function, full blood count, therapeutic drug level) rather than memorising a specific number of weeks or months.

The "identify the toxicity" pattern. A patient presents with a symptom cluster, and the correct answer is the drug responsible for that toxicity syndrome — digoxin toxicity, lithium toxicity, opioid toxicity, and serotonin syndrome are recurring examples. These questions reward pattern recognition of symptom clusters linked to specific drug classes, which is precisely why building a personal summary sheet organised by drug class, described in the revision strategy section below, pays off so directly on exam day.

Prescribing Safety and Error-Reporting: A Related but Distinct Topic

It is worth being explicit about how this guide relates to the site's other major prescribing-focused article. This piece has focused on the pharmacology itself — the drug classes, mechanisms, and safe-prescribing principles that sit within the AKT content map as a knowledge domain. The site's dedicated patient safety and prescribing errors guide covers a genuinely different angle: the professionalism and systems-safety side of prescribing, including how prescribing errors are classified, how near misses and incidents are reported through systems such as Datix, and how the duty of candour applies when something goes wrong.

Both topics matter, and they are frequently tested together in the same clinical scenario. However, they call for different revision approaches. UKMLA pharmacology revision, as covered in this article, rewards building a genuine working knowledge of drug classes and how they behave in different patients. The prescribing-errors and patient-safety angle, by contrast, rewards familiarity with professional frameworks, reporting pathways, and communication skills — content that is tested as much through professionalism and CPSA-style reasoning as through pure clinical knowledge. Treating these as two related but separate revision streams, rather than one blended topic, tends to produce more efficient preparation for both.

Revision Strategy for Pharmacology and Prescribing Safety

An effective UKMLA pharmacology revision plan combines three complementary habits, and candidates who use all three together generally report far more confidence with this content area than those who rely on passive reading alone.

Target question-bank practice by pharmacology tags. Most question banks allow filtering by topic, and pharmacology-tagged questions are usually available as a discrete category alongside system-based tags such as cardiovascular or respiratory. Working through a dedicated block of pharmacology-tagged questions, then reviewing every incorrect answer carefully, surfaces personal knowledge gaps far faster than working through mixed blocks alone. It is also worth deliberately revisiting pharmacology questions embedded inside system-based question blocks, since this mirrors how the real exam actually mixes the two.

Use the BNF actively during practice, not just as a memorisation source. Rather than trying to memorise long lists of drug facts in isolation, candidates benefit from getting into the habit of looking up an unfamiliar drug in the BNF whenever one appears in a practice question, reading the relevant sections on interactions, cautions, and renal or hepatic dosing, and noting anything genuinely new. Over the course of a revision period, this active-lookup habit builds durable, retrievable knowledge far more effectively than passive reading, and it also builds the exact BNF fluency that the exam itself expects a safe prescriber to have.

Build a personal high-yield drug-class summary sheet. Rather than relying solely on a textbook's organisation, many successful candidates create their own condensed summary covering each major drug class: its mechanism in a sentence, its main indication, its one or two most commonly tested cautions or contraindications, and any "trap" fact the candidate has personally found confusing. Because the act of building this sheet forces active recall and personal synthesis, it tends to be far more memorable than a sheet copied from someone else, even if the final content looks similar.

Finally, it is worth revising pharmacology alongside related content areas rather than in isolation. The site's guide to NICE guidance and how it is tested in the AKT is a natural companion to this article, since a large proportion of prescribing decisions tested on the exam are directly derived from current NICE guidelines rather than from pharmacology textbooks alone. Similarly, reviewing this guide's content against the official MLA content map helps confirm that no major drug class or clinical context has been missed during revision.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much pharmacology is on the UKMLA AKT?

There is no single fixed percentage published for pharmacology as an isolated category, because pharmacology questions are woven throughout the paper rather than confined to one section. However, because prescribing decisions form part of most clinical management questions, candidates who have not revised pharmacology thoroughly typically find it affects their performance across many different clinical topics, not just questions explicitly labelled as pharmacology.

Do I need to memorise exact drug doses for the AKT?

Generally, the AKT places far more emphasis on understanding drug classes, mechanisms, indications, and safe-prescribing principles than on recalling exact numerical doses. Where a question does involve a specific figure, it usually tests whether a candidate recognises a dose is clearly inappropriate for the clinical context, rather than requiring recall of an exact number, and any real prescribing decision should always be checked against the current BNF rather than memory.

What is the best way to learn the BNF as an IMG?

The most effective approach is active, repeated use rather than passive reading — looking up every unfamiliar drug encountered during question-bank practice, reading its interactions and cautions sections, and deliberately noting where UK naming or conventions differ from a candidate's original training country. Over a revision period of several weeks, this habit builds genuine fluency with the BNF's structure and with International Non-proprietary Names far more effectively than reading the formulary cover to cover.

Are psychiatric drugs really tested as heavily as cardiovascular drugs?

Yes, psychiatric pharmacology is tested substantially more than many candidates initially expect, reflecting the significant weight given to mental health content across the MLA content map. Antidepressants, antipsychotics, and mood stabilisers such as lithium appear regularly, with particular attention paid to monitoring requirements and to recognising situations calling for caution or specialist input.

Is prescribing safety the same topic as pharmacology on the UKMLA?

They overlap but are not identical. Pharmacology, as covered in this guide, refers to the underlying knowledge of drug classes, mechanisms, and safe-prescribing principles, while prescribing safety and error-reporting — covered in the site's separate patient safety and prescribing errors article — refers to the professionalism and systems side of what happens when prescribing goes wrong. Strong candidates revise both, but recognise them as distinct content streams.

Which drug classes should I prioritise if I am short on revision time?

If time is genuinely limited, prioritise the classes covered in depth in this guide: cardiovascular drugs including ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and anticoagulants; respiratory bronchodilators and corticosteroids; insulin and oral hypoglycaemics; opioid analgesia and the WHO pain ladder; core antimicrobial choices and stewardship principles; and the main classes of antidepressant and antipsychotic medication. These classes appear across the widest range of clinical scenarios and consistently reward focused revision time.

Does experience as a qualified doctor reduce how much pharmacology revision I need?

Clinical experience helps considerably with understanding mechanisms and clinical reasoning, but it does not remove the need to learn UK-specific prescribing conventions, BNF navigation, and current NICE-guided first-line choices, which can differ from practice elsewhere. Even experienced specialists and GPs exploring the site's guide to UKMLA exemptions still need to become fluent with the BNF and UK prescribing norms before practising independently in the UK.

Conclusion

Pharmacology and safe prescribing rewards structured, active revision more than almost any other UKMLA content area, precisely because it appears everywhere rather than in one contained section. A candidate who builds genuine fluency with high-yield drug classes, learns to navigate the BNF confidently, practises spotting interactions and contraindications, and understands how prescribing decisions shift across special populations will find that this preparation pays dividends across the entire AKT, not just in questions explicitly labelled as pharmacology. As with all exam preparation content, remember that this guide describes topic areas and question styles to expect — actual clinical prescribing decisions must always follow the current BNF, NICE guidance, and local hospital protocols, never a revision article.
