Public health UKMLA questions sit at the intersection of population-level thinking and individual clinical decision-making, and they appear far more often in the Applied Knowledge Test than most candidates expect walking in. Where clinical medicine asks "what is wrong with this patient," public health asks "how do we stop this happening to the next hundred thousand people," and the UKMLA increasingly tests whether Foundation doctors can move fluently between the two. This guide sets out exactly what you need to know: the UK's national screening programmes, the classical principles that determine whether a screening test is worth running at all, the structure of the NHS immunisation schedule, the three (or five) levels of prevention, and the core epidemiological measures — incidence, prevalence, sensitivity, and specificity — that underpin every public health question you will meet.

This guide is best read as the population-health companion to your wider AKT revision plan, since screening, prevention, and epidemiology recur across specialty-specific content rather than sitting in isolation. Both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AKT need this material for slightly different reasons.

UK students will usually have encountered the NHS screening pathways during community placements and public health teaching blocks, but often haven't consolidated the underlying principles into exam-ready form. IMGs, by contrast, may have excellent epidemiological grounding from their home training but limited exposure to the specific UK screening programmes, their exact eligibility ages, and NHS-specific terminology — this guide is written to close both gaps in a single pass.

Public health and screening questions on the AKT are rarely abstract. They are usually embedded in a clinical vignette: a 52-year-old woman asks why she hasn't been invited for a mammogram yet, a new father wants to know which vaccines his baby is due, or a question stem gives you a table of test results and asks you to calculate sensitivity. Understanding the structure behind these scenarios — rather than memorising isolated facts — is what allows you to answer confidently even when the exact numbers in a question are unfamiliar.

Public Health UKMLA: The Quick Answer

Public health UKMLA content covers four connected areas: the NHS's national screening programmes (breast, cervical, bowel, abdominal aortic aneurysm, and antenatal/newborn screening), the Wilson and Jungner criteria that define what makes a screening programme worthwhile, the structure of the routine NHS immunisation schedule, and the levels of disease prevention (primary, secondary, and tertiary) alongside basic epidemiological measures such as incidence, prevalence, sensitivity, and specificity. Foundation doctors are expected to know which screening programmes exist, roughly who is eligible and how often they are invited, why a test that seems clinically sensible can still fail to make a good screening programme, and how to interpret basic population-level statistics when counselling a patient or interpreting a results table.

None of this is tested as isolated trivia. The AKT is designed around what a newly qualified Foundation doctor plausibly needs to explain to a patient, act on in a GP or ward setting, or reason through when interpreting data — so the practical framing throughout this guide mirrors how the exam actually presents public health content.

The UK's National Screening Programmes

Screening is the process of identifying apparently healthy people who may be at increased risk of a disease or condition, enabling earlier treatment or informed decision-making. In the UK, national screening programmes are commissioned and overseen by the UK National Screening Committee (UK NSC), an independent body that advises ministers and the NHS on which conditions should be screened for, based on evidence that the benefits outweigh the harms and costs. Not every disease that can be tested for is screened for nationally — the UK NSC's evidence review process is deliberately conservative, precisely because a poorly designed screening programme can do more harm than good at a population level.

The major adult national screening programmes that Foundation doctors are expected to know are breast screening, cervical screening, bowel (colorectal) screening, and abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) screening, alongside the antenatal and newborn screening pathways offered during pregnancy and in the first days and weeks of a baby's life. Each programme has its own eligible age range, invitation frequency, and screening modality, summarised in the table below before being discussed individually. This section is the factual backbone of public health UKMLA revision, since exact eligible ages and invitation intervals are tested directly and repeatedly.

Breast Screening

The NHS Breast Screening Programme invites women (and anyone registered as female with a GP) aged 50 up to their 71st birthday for a mammogram every three years. Some areas participate in an age-extension trial offering screening from 47 to 73, so exact local eligibility can vary slightly, but 50–70 on a three-yearly cycle is the figure to know for the AKT. Women over 70 are not automatically invited but can self-refer for continued screening every three years if they wish.

Mammography detects breast cancer at an earlier, often asymptomatic stage, when treatment is more likely to be successful and less extensive. Women at significantly increased risk — for example, those with a strong family history or a known pathogenic variant in BRCA1, BRCA2, or a related gene — may be referred to a family history or genetics clinic for more intensive surveillance starting at a younger age, outside the standard population programme. This is a useful example for the AKT of how population-level screening and individualised risk-based surveillance sit alongside each other rather than being interchangeable.

Cervical Screening

Cervical screening tests a sample of cells from the cervix for high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV), the virus responsible for the vast majority of cervical cancers. In England, Scotland, and Wales, women and people with a cervix aged 25 to 49 are invited every three years, and those aged 50 to 64 are invited every five years (Wales moved to five-yearly screening for all eligible ages from 25 in recent years, so candidates should be aware minor jurisdictional variation exists, though the England-based three-year/five-year split by age band is the version most consistently tested).

The programme's modern approach is HPV primary screening: a sample is first tested for high-risk HPV, and cytology (looking at the cells themselves under a microscope) is only performed if HPV is detected. This is more efficient and more sensitive for detecting the women genuinely at risk of developing cervical abnormalities than the older cytology-first approach, because a woman who is HPV-negative has an extremely low near-term risk of cervical cancer regardless of her cytology, so testing for the causative virus first appropriately reduces unnecessary follow-up for HPV-negative women. This is a good example of a screening pathway being redesigned around a better understanding of disease mechanism, and it connects directly to the topic of gynaecological cancer risk factors and presentations, which candidates should review alongside this section since screening and clinical presentation questions are often tested together in the same AKT block.

Bowel (Colorectal) Screening

Bowel screening in the UK uses the faecal immunochemical test (FIT), a home test that detects tiny amounts of blood in a stool sample invisible to the naked eye. FIT has replaced the older guaiac-based faecal occult blood test (gFOBT) across the UK because it is more sensitive for colorectal neoplasia and requires only a single sample rather than multiple samples across different days, improving uptake.

Eligibility has expanded over recent years: the NHS Bowel Cancer Screening Programme now invites men and women aged 50 to 74 to complete a FIT kit every two years, sent by post and returned by post — a design deliberately chosen to maximise participation compared with requiring an in-person clinic visit. People with a positive FIT result are referred for further investigation, typically colonoscopy. People with a strong family history of colorectal cancer, or with conditions such as ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease affecting the colon, or Lynch syndrome, are managed through separate, more intensive surveillance pathways outside the population screening programme, since their baseline risk is too high for a population-level test interval to be appropriate.

Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA) Screening

The AAA screening programme is a single, one-off ultrasound scan of the abdominal aorta offered to men in the year they turn 65. Men over 65 who have not previously been screened can self-refer at any time. Women are not routinely invited, because AAA is roughly four to six times more common in men, reflecting differences in smoking prevalence history and vascular risk factor distribution between the sexes — this sex-specific eligibility is itself a useful AKT teaching point about how screening programmes are targeted at the population subgroup where the harm/benefit balance is most favourable.

An aortic diameter of 3.0 cm or greater defines an aneurysm on screening ultrasound. Depending on the measured diameter, men are either discharged (no aneurysm), placed on annual ultrasound surveillance (small aneurysm, 3.0–4.4 cm), placed on three-monthly surveillance (medium aneurysm, 4.5–5.4 cm), or referred for vascular surgical assessment for consideration of elective repair (large aneurysm, ≥5.5 cm, or rapid growth). Unrepaired AAA carries a significant risk of rupture, which is frequently fatal — the screening programme exists precisely because a ruptured AAA rarely offers a second chance, making early, asymptomatic detection unusually valuable in this specific condition.

Antenatal and Newborn Screening

Pregnancy and the newborn period involve several distinct screening pathways, each covering a different category of condition. Antenatally, pregnant women are offered screening for infectious diseases (HIV, hepatitis B, and syphilis), for sickle cell disease and thalassaemia (particularly relevant where family origin suggests higher carrier prevalence), and combined or quadruple screening for Down's syndrome (trisomy 21), Edwards' syndrome (trisomy 18), and Patau's syndrome (trisomy 13), usually alongside the dating and anomaly ultrasound scans performed at around 11–14 weeks and 18–21 weeks respectively.

After birth, every baby in the UK is offered the newborn blood spot test (commonly known as the "heel prick" test), usually performed on day five of life. This tests for nine conditions across the UK nations, including congenital hypothyroidism, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, and a panel of inherited metabolic disorders such as phenylketonuria (PKU) and medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase deficiency (MCADD). Separately, the newborn hearing screening programme tests every baby's hearing, usually within the first few weeks of life, and the newborn and infant physical examination (NIPE) — a top-to-toe clinical examination performed within 72 hours of birth and again at 6–8 weeks — screens for congenital abnormalities of the eyes, heart, hips (developmental dysplasia of the hip), and testes (in male infants).

What Makes a Good Screening Test: The Wilson and Jungner Principles

Not every disease that can be detected early should be screened for at a population level, which is one of the most conceptually important ideas in public health UKMLA revision because it explains why a test that looks clinically sensible can still make a poor population screening programme. In 1968, James Maxwell Glover Wilson and Gunnar Jungner published a set of criteria, commissioned by the World Health Organization, that has become the classical framework for deciding whether a screening programme is worthwhile. The UK NSC's modern evidence review criteria are a direct, more detailed descendant of this original list, and the AKT expects candidates to understand the underlying logic even if the exact modern wording differs slightly from the 1968 original.

The Wilson and Jungner principles can be grouped into three broad categories: criteria about the condition itself, criteria about the test used to screen for it, and criteria about the treatment and system available once someone screens positive.

Criteria About the Condition

  • The condition should be an important health problem, in terms of either its frequency or its severity (or both).
  • The natural history of the condition, including its progression from latent to declared disease, should be adequately understood.
  • There should be a recognisable latent or early symptomatic stage — a screening programme is pointless for a condition that either never has a detectable pre-symptomatic phase or progresses so slowly that early detection confers no advantage.

Criteria About the Test

  • There should be a suitable test or examination — one that is simple, safe, precise, and validated, with a distribution of test values in the target population that is known and an agreed cut-off level defined.
  • The test should be acceptable to the population being screened — a technically excellent test that patients refuse to undergo achieves nothing at a population level.
  • There should be an agreed policy on further diagnostic investigation of individuals with a positive result, and on the choices available to those individuals.

Criteria About Treatment and the System

  • There should be an effective treatment or intervention for patients identified through screening, with evidence that early treatment leads to better outcomes than treatment started after symptomatic presentation.
  • Facilities for diagnosis and treatment should be available, and the whole screening programme (including diagnosis and treatment) should be a clinically, socially, and ethically acceptable use of resources.
  • The cost of case-finding — including diagnosis and treatment of patients diagnosed — should be economically balanced in relation to possible expenditure on medical care as a whole; in other words, screening should be cost-effective, not simply technically feasible.
  • Case-finding should be a continuous process, not a "once and for all" project — screening is an ongoing programme with defined recall intervals, not a single population sweep.

Two concepts worth understanding in more depth because they explain why a test can look impressive in isolation but still make a poor screening programme are lead-time bias and length-time bias. Lead-time bias occurs when screening appears to prolong survival simply because the disease is diagnosed earlier, even if the actual date of death is unchanged — the patient simply knows about their disease for longer, without living any longer in absolute terms. Length-time bias occurs because screening preferentially detects slower-growing, less aggressive disease (since it is present and detectable for longer), while faster-growing, more aggressive disease is more likely to present clinically between screening rounds — this can make screen-detected disease look like it has a better prognosis than it really does, purely as an artefact of which cases screening is more likely to catch. Both biases are classic AKT distractors: a question may present survival data that looks like clear evidence a screening programme works, when the correct answer recognises that the apparent benefit could be explained by lead-time or length-time bias rather than a genuine reduction in mortality.

NHS Immunisation Schedule Basics

Immunisation is one of the most effective forms of primary prevention available in modern medicine, and it is a recurring theme in public health UKMLA questions. The UKMLA expects Foundation doctors to understand the overall structure and logic of the NHS routine vaccination schedule rather than memorising every exact date down to the week. The schedule is designed around key ages where a child's immune system is developed enough to respond well to vaccination, balanced against the age at which natural protection (for example, from maternal antibodies) is waning and the child becomes vulnerable to a given infection.

The Overall Shape of the Schedule

The routine childhood schedule is concentrated most heavily in the first year of life, when infants are most vulnerable to serious infection and have the least existing immunity. Multiple vaccines are combined into single injections wherever possible (for example, a hexavalent "6-in-1" vaccine covering diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, polio, Haemophilus influenzae type b, and hepatitis B) to reduce the total number of injections a child receives — this combination approach is itself a useful exam point, since candidates are sometimes asked why several diseases are vaccinated against in a single dose rather than separately.

Key vaccination milestones to know conceptually, rather than by exact week, include:

  • 8, 12, and 16 weeks of age — the primary course of infant vaccinations, including the 6-in-1 vaccine, rotavirus vaccine, MenB vaccine, and pneumococcal conjugate vaccine, given across these three visits.
  • Around 1 year of age — the first dose of the MMR (measles, mumps, and rubella) vaccine, along with booster doses of Hib/MenC, MenB, and pneumococcal vaccine.
  • Around 3 years 4 months (pre-school booster) — a second dose of MMR and a pre-school booster of the 4-in-1 (diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, polio).
  • Around 12–13 years — the HPV vaccine, offered to all adolescents to protect against HPV-related cancers, including cervical cancer, and other HPV-related disease.
  • Around 14 years — a further teenage booster of the 3-in-1 (tetanus, diphtheria, polio) alongside a MenACWY vaccine.

Beyond childhood, adult immunisation continues through targeted programmes rather than a universal schedule: an annual influenza vaccine offered to people aged 65 and over, people with certain long-term health conditions, pregnant women, and healthcare workers; a shingles (herpes zoster) vaccine offered to older adults; a pneumococcal vaccine for adults aged 65 and over and for at-risk groups; and pertussis (whooping cough) vaccination offered to pregnant women in each pregnancy to protect the newborn during the vulnerable first weeks of life before the infant schedule begins, via passive transfer of maternal antibodies. Candidates wanting a deeper dive into how prescribing and medication safety questions are tested should treat that post as a companion to this section.

Herd Immunity

Herd immunity (sometimes called community or population immunity) describes the indirect protection given to unvaccinated or non-immune individuals when a sufficiently high proportion of the surrounding population is immune, because sustained transmission chains become mathematically less likely. The exact vaccination coverage threshold required to achieve herd immunity varies by disease, driven principally by how transmissible that disease is — highly transmissible diseases such as measles require a very high vaccination coverage (in the region of 95%) to reliably interrupt transmission, whereas less transmissible diseases require somewhat lower coverage. This concept underpins why vaccine hesitancy in even a minority of a population can allow outbreaks of highly transmissible diseases like measles to occur, disproportionately endangering those who cannot be vaccinated themselves — for example, infants too young for their first MMR dose, or immunosuppressed patients for whom live vaccines are contraindicated.

For the AKT, the key exam-relevant takeaway is conceptual fluency, not date-perfect recall: know roughly which vaccines cluster in infancy, that MMR is given as two doses (around age 1 and around age 3 years 4 months), that HPV vaccination happens in early adolescence, that pregnant women receive a pertussis vaccine to protect their newborn, and that adult programmes (flu, shingles, pneumococcal) are targeted at older adults and at-risk groups rather than the whole population.

Health Promotion and the Levels of Disease Prevention

Public health interventions are conventionally organised into levels of prevention, describing the stage of the disease process at which an intervention acts. The UKMLA regularly tests whether candidates can correctly classify a given intervention into the right level, and correct classification depends on understanding the underlying logic rather than memorising a list of examples. Getting comfortable with this classification is one of the most efficient uses of time in public health UKMLA preparation, since the same three-level framework recurs across dozens of otherwise unrelated question stems.

Primary Prevention

Primary prevention aims to prevent disease before it ever occurs, by acting on completely healthy individuals or populations to reduce the incidence of new cases. This is prevention in the truest sense — stopping the disease process from starting at all. Examples include childhood immunisation programmes, health education around smoking cessation and diet, water fluoridation, folic acid supplementation before and during early pregnancy to reduce neural tube defects, and legislative or fiscal measures such as minimum unit alcohol pricing or tobacco taxation. Because primary prevention acts before disease onset, its target population is, by definition, currently healthy.

Secondary Prevention

Secondary prevention aims to detect disease early, in a pre-symptomatic or early stage, so that treatment can begin before the disease causes significant harm — this reduces the prevalence of advanced or complicated disease, even though it does not reduce the underlying incidence of the condition arising in the first place. National screening programmes are the clearest example of secondary prevention: breast, cervical, bowel, and AAA screening all aim to detect disease (or a pre-cancerous change) in people who have no symptoms, at a stage when intervention is more effective. The NHS Health Check, offered to adults aged 40–74 without a pre-existing relevant condition every five years to assess cardiovascular risk, is another example of secondary prevention operating at population level.

Tertiary Prevention

Tertiary prevention aims to reduce the impact of disease that has already been diagnosed and is established, by limiting complications, disability, and further deterioration, and by supporting rehabilitation and quality of life. Examples include cardiac rehabilitation programmes following a myocardial infarction, structured diabetes education programmes to prevent complications in patients already diagnosed with diabetes, and pulmonary rehabilitation for patients with established chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Tertiary prevention does not aim to prevent the disease itself — that ship has already sailed — but rather to prevent its consequences from becoming worse.

Some modern frameworks add two further categories worth being aware of, even though the classical primary/secondary/tertiary triad remains the version most consistently examined. Primordial prevention refers to action taken even before primary prevention — addressing the broad social, economic, and environmental conditions that give rise to risk factors in the first place, rather than addressing an already-established risk factor in an individual. Quaternary prevention refers to protecting patients from unnecessary or excessive medical intervention — avoiding overdiagnosis, overtreatment, and iatrogenic harm from well-intentioned but disproportionate care. Both concepts extend the classical model rather than replacing it, and candidates should recognise them if they appear but should default to the primary/secondary/tertiary framework as the safest first response to an ambiguous question stem.

Understanding prevention levels also connects naturally to the wider conceptual territory around how social and person-centred factors shape health outcomes, since many primary prevention interventions work precisely by addressing the material and social conditions in which people live, rather than acting on biology alone. Candidates revising public health for the AKT should treat these as two related but distinct content areas: this guide focuses on screening programmes and prevention mechanics, while the social determinants material focuses on why health outcomes vary systematically between population groups in the first place.

Basic Epidemiological Measures for Public Health Practice

A recurring AKT question format presents a data table or short vignette and asks the candidate to calculate or interpret a basic epidemiological measure. Four concepts account for the overwhelming majority of these questions in public health UKMLA papers: incidence, prevalence, sensitivity, and specificity.

Incidence vs Prevalence

Incidence is the number of new cases of a disease occurring in a defined population over a specified period of time — it measures the rate at which new disease is developing. Prevalence is the total number of existing cases (new and pre-existing) in a defined population at a given point in time (point prevalence) or over a given period (period prevalence) — it measures the overall burden of disease present in a population, regardless of when each case first arose.

The relationship between the two is captured by the approximate formula prevalence ≈ incidence × average duration of disease. This relationship explains several scenarios that are common AKT distractors. A disease can have a rising prevalence without any change in incidence, if improved treatment means patients are living longer with the condition (increasing average duration) — for example, improved treatment of HIV infection has substantially increased its prevalence in the UK, even where new infection rates (incidence) have fallen, because people are living for decades with the condition rather than dying from it relatively quickly. Conversely, a disease with a very short duration — because it either resolves quickly or is rapidly fatal — can have a high incidence but comparatively low prevalence at any single point in time, since cases do not accumulate in the population.

For screening programmes specifically, understanding this distinction matters because screening reduces the prevalence of advanced, late-stage disease (secondary prevention, as covered above) but does not, on its own, reduce the incidence of the underlying condition arising — a screening programme does not stop someone developing colorectal cancer in the first place, it detects it earlier once it has already begun to develop.

Sensitivity and Specificity in Screening

Sensitivity is the proportion of people who truly have the condition who are correctly identified as positive by the test — it answers the question "of everyone who genuinely has the disease, what fraction does this test catch?" A highly sensitive test produces few false negatives, making it well suited to ruling a condition out when the result is negative (sometimes remembered by the mnemonic SnNout: a highly Sensitive test, when Negative, rules the condition out).

Specificity is the proportion of people who truly do not have the condition who are correctly identified as negative by the test — it answers the question "of everyone who genuinely does not have the disease, what fraction does this test correctly clear?" A highly specific test produces few false positives, making it well suited to ruling a condition in when the result is positive (mnemonic SpPin: a highly Specific test, when Positive, rules the condition in).

National screening programmes are deliberately designed to prioritise high sensitivity, because the harm of missing a genuine case of cancer or a serious congenital condition is generally judged to be worse than the harm of a false positive result that triggers further, more specific diagnostic testing. This is precisely why a positive screening result (for example, a positive FIT test or a screen-positive combined Down's syndrome test) is never itself a diagnosis — it is an invitation to further, more specific diagnostic investigation, such as colonoscopy or amniocentesis/chorionic villus sampling, which carries its own separate risk-benefit calculation that must be discussed with the patient.

The following simplified 2x2 table format is the standard way sensitivity and specificity questions are presented on the AKT, and candidates should be comfortable populating it from a written vignette rather than needing it pre-drawn.

Disease PresentDisease Absent
Test PositiveTrue positive (a)False positive (b)
Test NegativeFalse negative (c)True negative (d)

From this table, sensitivity = a / (a + c) — true positives divided by all people who actually have the disease — and specificity = d / (b + d) — true negatives divided by all people who do not have the disease. Two related measures worth distinguishing are positive predictive value (PPV), which is a / (a + b) and answers "if this patient tests positive, what is the probability they actually have the disease," and negative predictive value (NPV), which is d / (c + d) and answers "if this patient tests negative, what is the probability they are genuinely disease-free." Crucially, unlike sensitivity and specificity, PPV and NPV are directly affected by the prevalence of the disease in the population being tested — the same test applied to a low-prevalence screening population and a high-prevalence symptomatic clinic population will produce a markedly different PPV in each setting, even though its sensitivity and specificity remain unchanged. This distinction is a frequent source of correct-sounding wrong answers on the AKT, so it is worth internalising rather than treating PPV/NPV and sensitivity/specificity as interchangeable.

How This Fits Into Your Wider AKT Revision

Public health UKMLA content rarely appears as a stand-alone, clearly labelled block of questions on the AKT — it is woven through vignettes across almost every specialty, since population-level thinking is a genuinely cross-cutting theme of the exam rather than a niche topic area. A cardiology question might ask you to interpret NHS Health Check data; an obstetrics question might test your knowledge of antenatal screening pathways; a general practice question might ask you to counsel a patient about why they were or weren't invited for a particular screening programme.

This is also one of several areas where the GMC's 2026 update to the MLA content map is directly relevant, since population health and prevention themes sit alongside — and are increasingly tested in combination with — the newly expanded content areas in that update. Candidates building a revision plan around the current content map should treat public health, screening, and basic epidemiology as core, examinable content in their own right, not background reading. For candidates who have not yet reviewed how frailty and multimorbidity are assessed in older patients, it is worth noting the direct overlap with this guide's content: several UK screening programmes (bowel, AAA) have upper or lower age boundaries chosen specifically because the harm-benefit balance shifts as frailty and life expectancy change with age, and questions increasingly test this intersection directly.

Because public health questions frequently require interpreting numerical data — incidence and prevalence figures, sensitivity and specificity calculations, or trial results presented as relative risk or odds ratios — this content area also connects closely with the statistical reasoning skills tested elsewhere on the AKT. Candidates who find the calculations in this guide unfamiliar should treat that as a signal to revisit how basic statistical concepts are tested on the AKT as a companion topic, since the two areas are examined together more often than either is examined in isolation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What screening programmes does the NHS offer, and at what ages?

The NHS offers breast screening for women aged 50–70 every three years, cervical screening for women/people with a cervix aged 25–64 (three-yearly from 25–49, five-yearly from 50–64), bowel screening using a home FIT kit for adults aged 50–74 every two years, and a one-off abdominal aortic aneurysm ultrasound scan for men in the year they turn 65. Antenatal and newborn screening pathways run alongside these adult programmes and cover infectious diseases, chromosomal conditions, and a panel of inherited and congenital conditions in every pregnancy and newborn baby.

What are the Wilson and Jungner criteria for screening?

The Wilson and Jungner criteria, first published in 1968, are a set of principles for deciding whether a condition is suitable for population screening. They require the condition to be an important health problem with a well-understood natural history and a detectable early or latent stage, the test to be simple, safe, accurate, and acceptable to the population, and an effective treatment plus adequate diagnostic and treatment facilities to be available, with the whole programme representing a cost-effective, continuous, and ethically acceptable use of resources.

What is the difference between incidence and prevalence?

Incidence measures the number of new cases of a disease arising in a population over a defined period, while prevalence measures the total number of existing cases (new plus pre-existing) present in a population at a given point or period in time. A condition's prevalence can rise even without any increase in incidence if patients are surviving with the condition for longer due to improved treatment, since prevalence is approximately equal to incidence multiplied by the average duration of disease.

What is the difference between sensitivity and specificity?

Sensitivity is the proportion of people who truly have a condition who are correctly identified as positive by a test, making a highly sensitive test useful for ruling a condition out when negative. Specificity is the proportion of people who truly do not have the condition who are correctly identified as negative, making a highly specific test useful for ruling a condition in when positive. National screening tests are usually designed to prioritise high sensitivity, since missing a genuine case is generally considered more harmful than generating a false positive that prompts further diagnostic testing.

What are the three levels of disease prevention?

Primary prevention acts on healthy individuals or populations to stop disease occurring in the first place, such as childhood immunisation. Secondary prevention detects disease early, before it causes significant harm, such as through national screening programmes. Tertiary prevention reduces the impact of already-established disease and supports rehabilitation, such as cardiac rehabilitation after a myocardial infarction.

Do international medical graduates need to know exact NHS screening ages for the UKMLA?

Yes — IMGs should know the approximate eligible ages and invitation frequencies for the major NHS screening programmes (breast, cervical, bowel, and AAA screening) covered in this guide, since these are UK-specific figures that may differ from screening protocols in an IMG's home country and are directly and repeatedly tested on the AKT. Precise recall of the exact age bands and frequencies in the summary table above is one of the highest-yield, most learnable pieces of UK-specific public health knowledge for the exam.

Why do some UK screening programmes only apply to one sex, such as AAA screening for men?

Screening programmes are targeted at the population subgroup where the balance of benefit to harm is most favourable, based on underlying disease prevalence in that group. Abdominal aortic aneurysm is around four to six times more common in men than women, reflecting higher historical smoking rates and other vascular risk factors, so the NHS AAA screening programme invites only men, in the year they turn 65, rather than the whole population.

How much of the NHS immunisation schedule do I need to memorise for the AKT?

You need conceptual fluency rather than exact-date recall: know that the primary infant vaccination course is concentrated at 8, 12, and 16 weeks, that MMR is given as two doses (around 1 year and around 3 years 4 months), that the HPV vaccine is offered in early adolescence, that pregnant women receive a pertussis vaccine to protect their newborn, and that adult programmes such as influenza, shingles, and pneumococcal vaccination target older adults and at-risk groups rather than the whole population.

Conclusion

Public health UKMLA content rewards candidates who understand the underlying logic — why a screening programme exists, why it is targeted at a specific age band or sex, and what a sensitivity or prevalence figure actually represents — rather than those who have only memorised isolated facts. Build your revision around the national screening programmes table, the Wilson and Jungner principles, the three levels of prevention, and the four core epidemiological measures covered in this guide, and you will be well placed to handle public health content wherever it appears across the AKT, since it is woven through clinical vignettes in almost every specialty rather than confined to a single question block. Always verify current screening ages, frequencies, and vaccination schedule details against the NHS's own screening programme pages before an exam sitting, since eligibility criteria are periodically reviewed and updated by the UK National Screening Committee.
