GMC registration requirements for international doctors come down to five conditions that apply regardless of which country a doctor trained in: confirmed eligibility, completed primary source verification of the primary medical qualification, accepted evidence of English language proficiency, passes in both components of the UKMLA, and a successfully processed application through GMC Online. Only once every one of these conditions is satisfied does the General Medical Council grant registration with a licence to practise — the legal authorisation that allows a doctor to work as a doctor anywhere in the United Kingdom. Passing the UKMLA alone does not confer this authorisation; it is one of five pieces that all have to be in place together.

This guide sets out the GMC registration process for doctors trained outside the UK in generic, country-agnostic terms, since the underlying framework — eligibility, EPIC verification, English testing, the exam, and the registration application itself — is identical whether a doctor qualified in India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Egypt, the Gulf, or anywhere else on the World Directory of Medical Schools. What differs by country is the fine detail: which documents a specific national regulator issues, how quickly a particular medical school typically responds to a verification request, and where the nearest test centre for exams or English tests happens to be. Consequently, this article deliberately avoids country-specific procedural detail and instead points to the site's dedicated country posts wherever that nuance matters, so international medical graduates (IMGs) from any background can use this as their master reference and then drill into the post written for their own country.

Because registering with the GMC as an IMG sits inside a much larger journey — one that also includes exam preparation, visa planning, and eventually finding NHS employment — this guide focuses specifically on the registration and verification mechanics rather than repeating ground already covered elsewhere on the site. The Five Requirements for GMC Registration With a Licence to Practise

Full GMC registration with a licence to practise is granted only once eligibility is confirmed, primary source verification is complete, English language evidence is accepted, and both UKMLA components are passed — after which the registration application itself is submitted and processed. These five requirements apply in exactly the same order and to the same standard regardless of a doctor's nationality or country of qualification; the GMC does not operate a faster or slower track based on where a candidate trained, only different supporting evidence depending on the qualifying country's own documentation and regulatory structures.

Understanding this five-part structure matters because it reframes what "GMC registration" actually means in practice. It is not a single form submitted at the end of the process; it is the final administrative step that follows naturally once four separate, largely independent workstreams have already been completed. In addition, three of those four prerequisite workstreams — eligibility confirmation, primary source verification, and English language testing — do not depend on exam results at all, and therefore can and should be started well before a candidate feels "ready" to sit the AKT. Doctors who treat GMC registration requirements for international doctors as a checklist to begin only after the exams are behind them routinely add many months to their overall timeline for no good reason.

The five requirements, at a glance, are as follows:

  • Eligibility — a primary medical qualification from a school and programme that meets the GMC's recognition standards.
  • Primary source verification — independent confirmation of the qualification through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials).
  • English language evidence — an accepted IELTS or OET certificate meeting the minimum score thresholds.
  • UKMLA success — passes in both the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA).
  • The registration application — submission via GMC Online, identity verification, and payment of the registration fee.

Each of these is covered in turn below, with the country-agnostic mechanics explained in full and country-specific detail deliberately left to the site's dedicated posts for individual nationalities. The GMC's own overview of the full pathway — Before You Apply: A Guide for Doctors — is the authoritative source for all five stages and should be treated as the definitive reference whenever this guide and the GMC's own published guidance appear to differ, since GMC requirements are reviewed and updated periodically.

Eligibility: Does Your Medical Qualification Qualify?

Eligibility for GMC registration depends on holding a primary medical qualification from a medical school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) for the year in which the qualification was awarded, and on that programme meeting the GMC's minimum standards for duration and curriculum content. This is the very first gate in the entire pathway, and it is worth confirming deliberately rather than assuming, since not every medical school and not every qualification automatically satisfies it.

The World Directory of Medical Schools is the internationally recognised register — maintained jointly by the World Federation for Medical Education and the Foundation for Advancement of International Medical Education and Research — that regulators including the GMC use to confirm a medical school was legitimately accredited to award medical degrees at the time a candidate graduated. Consequently, the relevant question is not simply "is my medical school on WDOMS today," but "was my medical school listed on WDOMS for the specific year I graduated," since accreditation status can change over time and the GMC checks against the historical listing relevant to your own qualification date. Before progressing further, it is worth reviewing the site's full eligibility criteria for GMC registration, which sets out these qualification-recognition rules alongside the health and character declarations that apply to every applicant.

Beyond the WDOMS listing itself, the GMC also expects the underlying programme to have met certain minimum standards — broadly, a full-time programme of an appropriate minimum duration, covering the breadth of clinical and pre-clinical training expected of a primary medical qualification, rather than an abbreviated or non-standard pathway. Most standard medical degree programmes worldwide satisfy this comfortably; however, candidates with an unusual academic history — for example, an accelerated programme, a qualification obtained through a non-standard route, or training interrupted and resumed at a different institution — should confirm their specific circumstances directly with the GMC rather than assume eligibility.

Because the detailed eligibility rules include several specific edge cases — recognised qualifications, dual-accreditation programmes, and how the GMC treats postgraduate qualifications obtained after the primary degree — the full detail is covered separately in the site's dedicated guide to UKMLA eligibility and who can sit the exam. Reading that guide before investing time in EPIC verification or exam booking is the safest way to avoid discovering an eligibility problem only after other steps are already under way.

Primary Source Verification Through EPIC

EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) is the online platform the GMC uses to carry out primary source verification of an international medical graduate's qualifications — meaning the degree, the transcripts, and typically the internship record are confirmed directly with the institution that issued them, rather than accepted on the strength of a certified copy alone. This step is mandatory for every IMG regardless of country of qualification, and it cannot be skipped, expedited through a higher fee, or substituted with a notarised or apostilled document.

The distinction between primary source verification and a certified copy is the single most common point of confusion for first-time applicants of any nationality. A certified copy confirms only that a photocopy matches an original document a candidate physically holds; it says nothing about whether the awarding institution itself confirms, from its own records, that the qualification was genuinely granted. EPIC verification, by contrast, involves the GMC's verification partner contacting the medical school directly and asking the school to confirm the degree, the graduation date, and the programme details from its own institutional records — a materially higher evidentiary bar, and the reason this step takes weeks rather than days for every applicant, everywhere.

Creating an EPIC Account and Submitting Documents

The process begins with creating an EPIC account, accessed via a link within GMC Online once basic eligibility has been confirmed. Within EPIC, a candidate registers their medical school, degree details, and graduation year, and then submits the required supporting documents for that qualification. EPIC then routes a verification request to the medical school's designated liaison or academic office, which is asked to confirm the details directly with EPIC rather than with the candidate.

Because the request goes to the institution rather than to the applicant, a candidate's own responsiveness stops being the limiting factor once the request has been submitted correctly — the medical school's administrative speed becomes the deciding variable instead. This is an important adjustment for candidates accustomed to processes where personal effort directly shortens the timeline; once an EPIC application has been submitted properly, the most productive action available is periodic, polite follow-up with the issuing institution, rather than assuming nothing further can be done.

The GMC publishes full, authoritative detail on exactly which documents EPIC requires and how the resulting verification feeds into the wider registration decision on its own website; see the GMC's guidance referenced within Before You Apply: A Guide for Doctors for the current, definitive requirements. Reading this page directly before creating an EPIC account is worthwhile, since it is updated periodically and takes precedence over any general third-party summary.

How Long EPIC Verification Typically Takes

Response times from medical schools to EPIC verification requests vary enormously, and this variability is one of the single biggest sources of unpredictability in the entire GMC registration process for international doctors. In broad, indicative terms, well-resourced institutions with a dedicated international affairs or examinations office often respond within a small number of weeks to a couple of months, whereas institutions handling verification requests informally, or relying on paper records rather than digitised ones, can take several months or longer. These ranges differ meaningfully by country and even by individual institution within the same country, so they should be read as broad planning bands rather than a fixed promise for any specific applicant.

Because EPIC response speed depends almost entirely on factors outside any individual candidate's control — whether the request lands during an academic term or an examination period, whether the relevant graduating cohort's records are digitised, and how the responsible administrative staff member manages competing duties — the single most effective planning decision available to any IMG, regardless of nationality, is to start EPIC verification as early as possible, ideally well before serious AKT preparation begins. Every week spent delaying the start of EPIC verification is simply a week added to the overall timeline that could otherwise have run quietly in the background while exam preparation continued in parallel.

What EPIC Verification Costs

EPIC verification carries its own fee, separate from GMC registration fees, exam fees, and English testing costs, and this fee is generally payable by the candidate at the point of submitting the EPIC application. Because fees across the entire pathway are reviewed periodically, candidates should confirm the current amount directly on the EPIC platform or through GMC Online rather than budgeting from a figure quoted in any third-party guide, including this one. Building the EPIC fee into an overall pathway budget from the outset, alongside AKT and CPSA costs and the eventual registration fee, avoids an unwelcome surprise partway through the process.

English Language Evidence for GMC Registration

Acceptable English language evidence for GMC registration means either an IELTS Academic score of 7.0 overall with no individual component below 7.0, or an OET Medicine result of Grade B or higher in all four sub-tests (Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking). These thresholds apply uniformly to IMGs of every nationality, including doctors from countries where English is an official or widely used language of medical education, since the GMC's requirement is evidenced through a specific approved test rather than through self-declaration or prior exposure to English-medium training.

Both IELTS and OET certificates carry a validity window from the date of the test, and this window is one of the most common, entirely avoidable causes of delay across every nationality. Candidates who sit their English test too early relative to the rest of their pathway sometimes find their certificate has expired by the time they are ready to submit a complete registration application, forcing them to sit the test again at additional cost and with additional lead time. Sequencing the English test deliberately — neither so early that it expires before the rest of the pathway catches up, nor so late that it becomes the final bottleneck — therefore matters just as much as achieving the required score in the first place.

Because English testing logistics, test centre availability, and the specific scoring nuances that trip up candidates preparing in a non-native-English-speaking environment are covered in far greater depth elsewhere on the site, readers should consult the dedicated guide to the English language requirement for the UKMLA for the full breakdown of scoring bands, test formats, and preparation strategy. That guide is written with India-specific examples in its title and framing, but the underlying English language requirement, test formats, and score thresholds it describes are identical for every nationality — the mechanics of IELTS and OET do not change based on a candidate's country of origin.

The UKMLA: AKT and CPSA as the Licensing Assessment

The UKMLA (United Kingdom Medical Licensing Assessment) is the licensing examination component of GMC registration, made up of two separate parts that must both be passed: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), a computer-based knowledge examination, and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), a practical, scenario-based clinical skills examination. Passing both components demonstrates the level of medical knowledge and clinical competence the GMC requires before granting registration, and — together with eligibility, EPIC verification, and English language evidence — it forms one of the five pillars covered in this guide.

Because detailed exam format, syllabus coverage, scoring, booking logistics, and preparation strategy for the AKT and CPSA are already covered extensively elsewhere on the site, this guide deliberately does not repeat that detail here. Instead, this section exists only to place the UKMLA correctly within the wider registration pathway: it is the assessment component, sitting alongside — not instead of — eligibility confirmation, EPIC verification, and English language evidence, all of which are typically progressing at the same time as exam preparation rather than only afterwards.

One planning point is worth stating plainly here, since it recurs across every nationality: passing the UKMLA does not, by itself, grant the right to work as a doctor in the UK. Registration is only granted once the UKMLA passes are combined with completed EPIC verification, accepted English language evidence, and a successfully processed registration application. Consequently, candidates should never treat exam success as the finish line — it is one of five conditions, and treating it as the only one is a common, avoidable planning mistake.

The Registration Application: From GMC Online to a Licence to Practise

Once eligibility, EPIC verification, English language evidence, and both UKMLA passes are all confirmed, the registration application itself follows a defined, largely administrative sequence through GMC Online. This final stage is where every strand of the preceding pathway comes together into a single submitted application, and it is generally the fastest-moving stage of the whole process provided the supporting evidence is complete and internally consistent.

The sequence typically runs as follows for candidates of any nationality:

  1. Create or return to a GMC Online account. This account, generally established at the very start of the pathway, is where EPIC tracking, exam results, English language evidence, and eventually the registration application itself all come together in one place.
  2. Confirm all supporting evidence is complete. Before submitting, candidates should check that EPIC verification shows as complete, that the English test certificate remains valid and correctly uploaded, and that both AKT and CPSA results are recorded against the account.
  3. Submit the registration application itself, including standard declarations regarding health, criminal record, and professional conduct — requirements that apply identically to every applicant regardless of nationality, and which should be answered fully and accurately.
  4. Complete identity verification. The GMC requires robust proof of identity as part of registration, which many overseas candidates complete in conjunction with a UK visit — often the same trip used for the CPSA — or through an approved remote identity-verification route where available.
  5. Pay the registration fee and submit the completed application for processing.
  6. Await the outcome. Processing times vary, but applications with complete, unambiguous supporting documents typically move considerably faster than those where the GMC needs to raise a query.

Processing times for a complete, query-free application are generally measured in a small number of weeks; however, this figure should be treated as an indicative range rather than a guarantee, since the GMC's own processing capacity and the complexity of an individual file both affect the actual outcome for any given applicant. Registration fees are payable online through GMC Online at the point of submission, and the GMC accepts standard international payment methods from overseas applicants. As with every fee referenced in this guide, the exact current amount should always be confirmed directly on the GMC's website immediately before paying, rather than budgeted from a figure quoted here or in any other third-party source.

What Happens If Your Application Is Queried

A query from the GMC is not automatically a sign that something has gone seriously wrong; it is a routine part of the process for a meaningful proportion of applicants across every nationality, and it typically means one specific piece of evidence needs clarifying or replacing rather than the whole application being in doubt. Common reasons a query is raised include an ambiguous or incomplete transcript, a name discrepancy across documents that has not been formally explained, uncertainty over which national regulatory body's confirmation applies to a candidate's specific registration history, or a translation that does not meet the GMC's required certification standard.

When a query is raised, the GMC specifies exactly what additional information or document is required, and the most effective response is a prompt, complete, and precisely targeted reply — providing exactly what has been asked for, in the format requested, rather than resubmitting a broader set of documents in the hope one of them resolves the issue. Most queries, once answered completely, do not significantly extend the overall timeline; however, an incomplete or vague response can trigger a second query, which does meaningfully add to processing time. Keeping organised copies of every document submitted, together with dates and reference numbers for each communication, makes responding to a query considerably faster than reconstructing a submission history from scratch.

Identity Verification in Practice

Identity verification deserves particular attention because it is a step some overseas candidates plan for too late, regardless of nationality. Given that many IMGs remain resident outside the UK at the point their EPIC verification and exam results come through, coordinating identity verification — which may require an in-person element — alongside a planned UK trip for the CPSA is usually more efficient than treating it as a separate, later journey. Confirming exactly which identity verification route applies to a candidate's own circumstances, directly through GMC Online guidance, well before finalising UK travel plans, avoids an unnecessary second trip. This travel planning naturally overlaps with visa arrangements, which is why it is worth reading alongside the site's UK visa guide for international doctors, since visa timing and identity verification travel frequently need to be planned together rather than as two unrelated tasks.

Common Mistakes Across All Nationalities

Several mistakes recur across IMGs from every country, and recognising them in advance is one of the simplest ways to prevent a minor administrative delay from becoming a multi-month setback.

  • Starting EPIC too late. Because EPIC verification depends on institutional response speed rather than personal effort, delaying its start until after exam preparation is under way — or worse, until after exams are passed — routinely adds months to the overall timeline for no benefit whatsoever.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. IELTS and OET certificates carry a validity window, and candidates who sit the test too early relative to the rest of their pathway sometimes discover their certificate has lapsed by the time they are ready to submit a complete registration application, forcing an unnecessary and costly resit.
  • Underestimating document translation and certification time. Any document not already issued in English requires certified translation to a standard the GMC accepts, and sourcing a recognised professional translation service, then allowing time for the translation itself, is frequently underestimated by candidates planning their timeline.
  • Assuming exam success alone is sufficient. Passing both the AKT and CPSA is necessary but not sufficient; registration is only granted once EPIC verification and English language evidence are also complete, and treating the exams as the final hurdle rather than one of five is a persistent, avoidable misunderstanding.
  • Name mismatches across documents. Degree certificates, passports, and national regulator registration records are sometimes issued at different points in time using slightly different name formats, and resolving this with formal evidence before submission avoids an otherwise easily preventable query.
  • Treating registration travel and visa planning as unrelated tasks. Identity verification, CPSA attendance, and visa arrangements frequently need to happen within the same UK trip, and planning them separately rather than together often results in avoidable extra travel and cost.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the minimum GMC registration requirements for international doctors?

The minimum requirements are a primary medical qualification from a school listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools for the relevant graduation year, completed primary source verification through EPIC, accepted English language evidence, and passes in both the AKT and CPSA components of the UKMLA. Only once all four are satisfied can the registration application itself be submitted and processed through GMC Online.

Do GMC registration requirements differ by country of qualification?

The underlying five-part framework — eligibility, EPIC verification, English language evidence, UKMLA passes, and the registration application — is identical for every nationality. However, the specific documents required, typical medical-school response times, and local test centre availability vary by country, which is why the site maintains dedicated posts covering the procedural detail for individual countries.

How long does the GMC registration process take for an international doctor?

Most candidates who plan carefully and avoid major setbacks should expect somewhere in the region of eighteen months to three years from beginning EPIC verification to holding full GMC registration, though individual circumstances — particularly EPIC response speed and exam performance — can move this substantially in either direction. This range should be treated as a planning tool rather than a precise prediction, since no general guide can account for a specific medical school's administrative speed or the GMC's processing capacity at any given time.

Can I start GMC registration before passing the UKMLA?

Full registration cannot be granted until both UKMLA components are passed, but the earlier stages — confirming eligibility, creating an EPIC account, and sitting an English language test — should all begin well before the exams, since they run on independent timelines. Starting these workstreams early, in parallel with exam preparation, is the single most effective way to shorten the overall pathway.

What documents do I need for EPIC verification?

Generic requirements include a degree certificate, full academic transcripts, evidence of internship or equivalent clinical training completion, and confirmation of current registration status from the relevant national medical regulator, alongside identity documents such as a passport. Because exact document names and issuing bodies vary by country, readers should consult the dedicated country post for their own nationality or confirm directly with EPIC for a precise list.

Is a certified copy of my medical degree enough for GMC registration?

No. A certified copy only confirms that a photocopy matches an original document a candidate holds; it does not confirm, from the issuing institution's own records, that the qualification was genuinely awarded. Primary source verification through EPIC is mandatory for every international medical graduate and cannot be substituted with a certified or notarised copy, however official that copy appears.

What English test score do I need for GMC registration?

The GMC accepts either an IELTS Academic score of 7.0 overall with no component below 7.0, or an OET Medicine result of Grade B or higher in all four sub-tests. Both certificates carry a validity window, so timing the test so the certificate remains valid through to registration application submission requires deliberate sequencing rather than sitting it at the first convenient opportunity.

Conclusion

GMC registration requirements for international doctors follow the same five-part structure everywhere in the world — eligibility, EPIC verification, English language evidence, UKMLA success, and the registration application itself — even though the fine detail of documents, response times, and test centres varies meaningfully by country. Treating EPIC verification and English testing as early, parallel tasks rather than a final checklist, keeping documents complete and internally consistent, and consulting the dedicated country-specific posts for procedural nuance are the practical habits that separate a registration that proceeds smoothly from one that stalls for months.
