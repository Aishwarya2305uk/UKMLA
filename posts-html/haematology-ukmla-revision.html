Haematology for the UKMLA AKT: A Complete Revision Guide

Haematology spans from common anaemias encountered in primary care to rare and life-threatening blood dyscrasias requiring specialist management. The UKMLA AKT tests haematology extensively — expect questions on diagnosis, investigation, and management of anaemias, leukaemias, lymphomas, coagulation disorders, and transfusion medicine. This guide provides complete coverage of the haematology syllabus with an emphasis on clinical reasoning and guideline-based management.

Anaemia: Classification and Approach

Anaemia is defined as haemoglobin below the lower limit of normal: <130 g/L in adult males, <120 g/L in adult non-pregnant females, <110 g/L in pregnancy. The most useful initial classification is by MCV (mean corpuscular volume).

Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide and the most common cause of anaemia in primary care. Symptoms: fatigue, dyspnoea, palpitations, pale skin, angular cheilitis, koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), atrophic glossitis, Plummer-Vinson syndrome (iron deficiency + oesophageal web + dysphagia).

Investigation

Serum ferritin is the single most useful test for diagnosing iron deficiency — levels <30 µg/L are diagnostic (in the absence of inflammation, which raises ferritin as an acute phase reactant). In inflammatory states, transferrin saturation <20% and serum iron <10 µmol/L with raised TIBC support iron deficiency. Blood film: microcytic hypochromic red cells, pencil cells, anisocytosis, poikilocytosis.

Investigation for Underlying Cause

The key clinical principle for the UKMLA: iron deficiency in any adult without an obvious cause (e.g., heavy menstruation, inadequate dietary intake) requires investigation for gastrointestinal blood loss. Upper and lower GI endoscopy should be arranged. NICE NG12 (suspected cancer recognition): refer urgently (2-week wait) for upper GI endoscopy if iron deficiency anaemia with no other obvious cause in any man, or postmenopausal woman.

Treatment: oral ferrous sulfate 200mg three times daily (contains 65mg elemental iron per tablet). Take on empty stomach for best absorption. Vitamin C enhances absorption. GI side effects (constipation, nausea) common — take with food to reduce, or switch to ferrous fumarate/gluconate. IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose) for intolerance, malabsorption, or need for rapid repletion. Haemoglobin should rise ~10 g/L per 1–2 weeks. Continue iron for 3 months after Hb normalises to replenish stores.

Vitamin B12 and Folate Deficiency

Both B12 and folate are required for DNA synthesis — deficiency causes megaloblastic anaemia (large, hypersegmented neutrophils; megaloblasts in bone marrow). Clinically indistinguishable haematologically, but B12 deficiency causes neurological complications that folate does not.

B12 Deficiency

Causes: Pernicious anaemia (autoimmune gastric atrophy — antibodies to intrinsic factor and parietal cells; associated with other autoimmune conditions, slightly increased risk of gastric carcinoma), gastrectomy, terminal ileum resection (B12 absorbed in terminal ileum), strict vegan diet (B12 only in animal products), malabsorption (coeliac, Crohn's), metformin (reduces B12 absorption — check B12 annually), proton pump inhibitors (reduce acid required for B12 release from food).

Neurological features of B12 deficiency: Subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — demyelination of dorsal columns (loss of proprioception, vibration sense) and corticospinal tracts (UMN signs: spasticity, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars). Also: peripheral neuropathy (glove-and-stocking), optic atrophy, dementia. Critical: these neurological features can occur without anaemia and can be irreversible if treatment delayed. Do NOT give folate alone in B12 deficiency (can precipitate or worsen neurological damage).

Treatment: IM hydroxocobalamin 1mg on alternate days for 2 weeks (total 6 doses), then maintenance every 3 months for life (if cause is pernicious anaemia or other irreversible cause). Oral cyanocobalamin or hydroxocobalamin 1000 µg daily if cause is dietary or malabsorption managed.

Folate Deficiency

Causes: Poor diet (elderly, alcoholics, eating disorder), malabsorption (coeliac), increased requirements (haemolysis, pregnancy, psoriasis, dialysis), drugs (methotrexate, phenytoin, trimethoprim — all inhibit dihydrofolate reductase). Folic acid 5mg daily for 4 months. In pregnancy: 400 µg folic acid pre-conception until 12 weeks (5mg if high risk: previous neural tube defect, diabetes, BMI >30, anti-epileptic drugs).

Haemolytic Anaemia

Premature red cell destruction. Evidence of haemolysis: elevated LDH, elevated unconjugated bilirubin, reduced haptoglobin (binds free haemoglobin, consumed in intravascular haemolysis), elevated reticulocyte count (regenerative response), urobilinogen in urine. Intravascular haemolysis (within blood vessels): haemoglobinuria (dark urine), haemoglobinaemia, haemosiderinuria. Extravascular (spleen/liver phagocytosis): jaundice.

Inherited Causes

Sickle Cell Disease — NICE CG143: Autosomal recessive — HbSS. Substitution of glutamic acid for valine at position 6 of beta-globin chain. Under deoxygenation, sickling → vascular occlusion → ischaemia/infarction. Presentations: painful crises (acute vaso-occlusive crisis — precipitated by cold, dehydration, infection, hypoxia), acute chest syndrome (fever + new pulmonary infiltrate on CXR + chest pain/respiratory symptoms — life-threatening), stroke (cerebral infarction), splenic sequestration, aplastic crisis (parvovirus B19 infection → transient red cell aplasia → precipitous drop in Hb), avascular necrosis, chronic organ damage (renal, retinal).

Management: All patients — penicillin prophylaxis (encapsulated organism risk due to functional hyposplenism), pneumococcal/meningococcal/Hib vaccines, folic acid 5mg daily. Hydroxycarbamide (hydroxyurea): increases HbF production, reduces sickling frequency and severity — NICE recommends for frequent crises. Transcranial Doppler USS annually in children to detect cerebral vasculopathy (elevated velocity >200 cm/sec → prophylactic exchange transfusion). Exchange transfusion for acute chest syndrome, stroke, pre-operatively.

Thalassaemia: Reduced or absent globin chain synthesis. Alpha thalassaemia (HBA1/HBA2 gene deletion) — from silent carrier (one gene) to haemoglobin Bart's hydrops fetalis (all four genes — fatal in utero). Beta thalassaemia (HBB gene mutation) — trait (minor): one mutation, mild microcytic anaemia, normal life expectancy; intermedia: moderate; major (two mutations): severe transfusion-dependent anaemia from 6 months (when HbF replaced by HbA). Management of thalassaemia major: regular blood transfusions every 3–4 weeks, iron chelation (desferrioxamine SC infusion or oral deferasirox) to prevent iron overload (causes cardiomyopathy, liver cirrhosis, endocrine failure). Bone marrow transplant curative in selected cases. Gene therapy approaching availability.

G6PD Deficiency: X-linked recessive (predominantly male, common in Mediterranean, Middle East, Africa — malaria-endemic regions — heterozygous female protective against malaria). Deficiency of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase → reduced NADPH → oxidative haemolysis on exposure to triggers. Heinz bodies (denatured Hb) on blood film. Triggers: infections, fava beans, oxidising drugs (primaquine, dapsone, rasburicase, high-dose aspirin, nitrofurantoin). Avoid triggers; supportive care during haemolytic episodes.

Hereditary Spherocytosis: Autosomal dominant (mostly) spectrin/ankyrin gene mutations → spherical, fragile red cells → splenic destruction. Spherocytes on blood film. Negative DAT (distinguishes from AIHA). Osmotic fragility increased. Splenectomy effective but delayed until after 5–6 years (infection risk — require vaccinations beforehand and lifelong penicillin).

Acquired Haemolytic Anaemia

Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA): Direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs test) positive — antibody on red cell surface. Warm AIHA (70%): IgG antibodies, complement activation, extravascular haemolysis. Causes: idiopathic, SLE, CLL, methyldopa. Treatment: prednisolone, rituximab, splenectomy. Cold AIHA: IgM antibodies, cold agglutinins, complement-mediated. Causes: Mycoplasma pneumoniae, EBV, lymphoma. Treatment: avoid cold, rituximab, chlorambucil.

Lymphoma

Hodgkin Lymphoma

Bimodal age distribution (15–35 and over 55). Associated with EBV infection. Classical Hodgkin lymphoma: Reed-Sternberg cells (large binucleate cells with owl-eye nucleoli) in a background of inflammatory cells. B symptoms: unexplained fever (>38°C), night sweats, unintentional weight loss >10% in 6 months. Staging: Ann Arbor system (I–IV based on lymph node regions involved, with A/B suffix for absence/presence of B symptoms; E for extranodal extension). Treatment: ABVD chemotherapy (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) ± radiotherapy. Excellent prognosis — 80–90% curable.

Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Diverse group. Follicular lymphoma: indolent B-cell NHL; nodal, bone marrow involvement common; translocation t(14;18) — BCL-2 overexpression; responds to treatment but incurable with standard therapy; watch-and-wait for low tumour burden; rituximab ± bendamustine. Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL): most common aggressive NHL; treatment: R-CHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, hydroxydaunorubicin/doxorubicin, oncovin/vincristine, prednisolone) — 60–70% curable. Burkitt lymphoma: highly aggressive, associated with EBV and malaria, t(8;14) — c-MYC overactivation, jaw tumour in endemic form; emergency treatment with intensive chemotherapy.

Multiple Myeloma

Malignant proliferation of plasma cells in bone marrow, producing monoclonal immunoglobulin (M-protein, paraprotein). Median age 70. CRAB criteria for end-organ damage requiring treatment:

  • Calcium elevated (>2.75 mmol/L)
  • Renal impairment (creatinine >177 µmol/L or eGFR <40)
  • Anaemia (Hb <100 g/L)
  • Bone lesions (osteolytic lesions, osteoporosis with fractures, or pathological fracture)

Additional features: fatigue, recurrent infections (hypogammaglobulinaemia — normal immunoglobulins suppressed), bone pain (diffuse, particularly spine/pelvis), hyperviscosity (visual disturbance, confusion, bleeding), Bence-Jones proteinuria (free light chains in urine — detected by Bence-Jones test or urine protein electrophoresis), rouleaux formation on blood film (red cells stacking like coins due to high paraprotein). Bone marrow biopsy: >10% clonal plasma cells. Radiological: whole-body MRI or PET-CT preferred over skeletal survey (more sensitive). Bisphosphonates for bone protection.

Treatment: proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib), immunomodulatory agents (lenalidomide, thalidomide), anti-CD38 monoclonal antibody (daratumumab), dexamethasone, autologous stem cell transplant in fit patients.

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

DIC is simultaneous activation of coagulation and fibrinolysis, leading to microvascular thrombosis and consumption of clotting factors/platelets with bleeding. Causes: sepsis (most common), obstetric emergencies (placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, PPH), malignancy (especially APML — most DIC-prone cancer), major trauma, massive transfusion.

Investigations: elevated PT and APTT, low fibrinogen (<1.5 g/L — most specific), elevated D-dimer, low platelets, schistocytes on blood film. Treatment: treat underlying cause (most important). Replace clotting factors (FFP), fibrinogen (cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen <1.5 g/L), platelets if <50 with active bleeding.

Venous Thromboembolism (VTE) — NICE NG158

DVT Diagnosis

Wells score for DVT: 1 point each for: active cancer, paralysis/immobilisation of lower limb, bedridden >3 days or major surgery in 12 weeks, localised tenderness along deep venous system, entire leg swollen, calf swollen ≥3cm more than other side, pitting oedema greater in symptomatic leg, collateral superficial veins, previously documented DVT. Subtract 2 points if alternative diagnosis at least as likely. Score ≥2 = DVT likely → proceed to USS; score <2 = unlikely → D-dimer; if D-dimer positive → USS.

PE Diagnosis

Wells score for PE: clinical signs of DVT (3 points), PE most likely diagnosis (3 points), heart rate >100 (1.5), immobilisation >3 days or surgery in last 4 weeks (1.5), previous DVT/PE (1.5), haemoptysis (1), malignancy (1). Score >4 = PE likely → CTPA. ≤4 = PE unlikely → D-dimer; if positive → CTPA. PERC rule (8 criteria) can be used to exclude PE without D-dimer in very low-risk patients.

V/Q scan: preferred in pregnancy (lower fetal radiation dose) and renal impairment, or when CTPA technically difficult. Echo in massive PE (right heart strain — RV dilation, interventricular septal flattening, TR).

Anticoagulation

DOACs (direct oral anticoagulants) are first-line for most DVT and PE: apixaban or rivaroxaban (dose-intensive regimen in first 7–21 days then maintenance). Dabigatran and edoxaban used after initial parenteral treatment. Duration: 3 months for provoked VTE; extended/indefinite for unprovoked (consider patient preference, bleeding risk — HAS-BLED score); indefinite if malignancy (LMWH or DOAC).

HIT (Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopaenia)

Type 1 HIT: non-immune, self-limiting, mild thrombocytopaenia within 48 hours, not clinically significant. Type 2 HIT: immune-mediated — IgG antibodies against heparin-PF4 complex → platelet activation → paradoxical thrombosis. Suspect if platelets fall >50% from baseline 5–14 days after heparin (or within 24 hours if recent heparin exposure). 4T score assesses probability. Diagnosis: ELISA for anti-PF4 antibodies, functional assay (serotonin release assay — gold standard). Management: STOP heparin immediately (all formulations including flushes). Start alternative anticoagulation (argatroban IV or fondaparinux SC or bivalirudin). Do NOT transfuse platelets. Do NOT switch to warfarin until platelet count >150 (warfarin alone causes skin necrosis in HIT).

Blood Transfusion

ABO compatibility is essential — ABO-incompatible transfusion causes fatal acute haemolytic reaction (anti-A or anti-B IgM → complement activation → intravascular haemolysis → renal failure, DIC, death). Group O negative is universal donor.

SHOT (Serious Hazards of Transfusion) reporting: UK haemovigilance scheme for adverse events. All serious adverse events must be reported.

FAQ: Haematology for the UKMLA

What is the key distinction between TTP and HUS?

TTP is caused by ADAMTS13 deficiency (acquired or congenital), while HUS is caused by Shiga toxin from E. coli O157:H7 (typical HUS). TTP typically presents with prominent neurological features and less renal impairment; HUS has more severe renal failure and follows a diarrhoeal prodrome. In both conditions, platelet transfusion is contraindicated as it can worsen microvascular thrombosis. TTP is treated with emergency plasma exchange; typical HUS is managed supportively.

What is the Philadelphia chromosome and why is it important?

The Philadelphia chromosome is a translocation t(9;22) creating the BCR-ABL fusion gene, which encodes a constitutively active tyrosine kinase driving uncontrolled cell proliferation. It is found in virtually all CML patients (defining the disease) and in 25% of adult ALL (poor prognostic factor). Imatinib (Gleevec), the first targeted TKI, revolutionised CML treatment, converting a disease with 5-year survival of ~30% to >90%.

Why must B12 deficiency not be treated with folate alone?

Giving folic acid alone to a patient with B12 deficiency corrects the haematological abnormality but does not correct — and may actually unmask or worsen — the neurological damage (subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord). The pathophysiology involves methionine synthase requiring both B12 and methyltetrahydrofolate; folate supplementation bypasses the B12 block metabolically but cannot enter the neurological pathway that B12 regulates. Always treat both if in doubt.

When should you suspect HIT and what is the initial management?

Suspect HIT Type 2 if platelet count falls >50% from baseline 5–14 days after starting any heparin product (LMWH included), or if new thrombosis develops despite anticoagulation. Use the 4T score (thrombocytopaenia magnitude, timing, thrombosis or other sequelae, alternative causes) to assess probability. Management: stop heparin immediately (all sources), start a non-heparin anticoagulant (argatroban, bivalirudin, fondaparinux), avoid platelet transfusion, avoid warfarin until platelet count recovers, send HIT antibody tests.

What are the B symptoms in lymphoma and why are they important?

B symptoms in the Ann Arbor staging of lymphoma are: unexplained fever above 38°C, drenching night sweats, and unexplained weight loss of more than 10% of body weight in the preceding 6 months. The presence of B symptoms (designated by the suffix "B" in staging) indicates systemic disease activity and generally confers a worse prognosis, potentially influencing treatment intensity. Their absence is designated "A."

What is TACO and how does it differ from TRALI?

TACO (Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload) is pulmonary oedema from fluid overload during transfusion — features include hypertension, raised JVP, raised BNP, bilateral crackles, hypoxia. Most at risk: elderly patients and those with pre-existing cardiac/renal disease. TRALI (Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury) is a non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema caused by donor anti-HLA or anti-neutrophil antibodies activating recipient neutrophils in the pulmonary vasculature. TRALI is associated with hypotension (not hypertension), normal filling pressures, and usually occurs within 6 hours. TRALI is a medical emergency requiring ITU-level respiratory support; treatment is supportive.

Conclusion

Haematology in the UKMLA rewards systematic thinking — starting with MCV to classify anaemia, applying the 4T score to assess HIT probability, using CRAB criteria to recognise myeloma, or knowing when NOT to transfuse platelets in TTP and HUS. These clinical rules are tested repeatedly in SBA format.

Master the high-yield topics: iron deficiency anaemia investigation and management, sickle cell disease crises, leukaemia classification, TTP versus HUS distinction, and transfusion reactions.
