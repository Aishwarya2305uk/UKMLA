The UKMLA AKT pass mark is one of the most searched, and most misunderstood, figures in the whole examination. Candidates often want a single number — "what percentage do I need?" — but the honest answer is that the UKMLA AKT does not use a fixed percentage that stays the same every sitting. Instead, the exact number of marks needed to pass is set individually for each sitting through a formal process called Angoff standard setting, which means the cut score can move slightly from one diet to the next depending on that sitting's specific mix of questions. This post takes the practical angle: what the pass mark actually means for you, how to read your own result against it, what is honestly known about pass rates, and which preparation factors genuinely move the needle on your chances of meeting the standard.

Before going further, it is worth being clear about what this post does not do. The detailed mechanics of how the pass mark is calculated — the expert panels, the Minimally Competent Candidate concept, the item-by-item ratings — are covered in full in our dedicated piece on Angoff standard setting. Here, the focus stays on interpretation: what your sitting's pass mark tells you, how it compares with other sittings, and how to use published pass-rate patterns sensibly rather than anxiously.

Quick Answer: What Is the UKMLA AKT Pass Mark?

The UKMLA AKT pass mark is not a fixed percentage such as 60% that applies to every sitting. It is a criterion-referenced cut score, expressed as a specific number of marks out of the total available, that is recalculated for each individual sitting using the Angoff method. Consequently, the exact mark you need to pass in your sitting may differ slightly from the mark a candidate needed in the previous or following sitting, because each sitting uses a different set of questions with a different overall difficulty profile.

This is a genuinely important distinction for candidates to internalise early in their preparation. A candidate who fixates on a remembered "60% target" from a forum post or a well-meaning senior colleague is anchoring to the wrong number — that figure may have applied to a completely different sitting with a different difficulty profile. In addition, because the AKT cut score is set before most candidates even sit the paper (the standard-setting panel does its work independently of live candidate performance, subject to later psychometric adjustment), there is no meaningful sense in which "most people pass around X%" translates into a target for you to aim for.

The GMC publishes the specific pass mark for each sitting alongside results, rather than a single number that applies indefinitely. Therefore, the only pass mark that matters to you is the one published for your own sitting, communicated through your results report. Aiming to comprehensively demonstrate competency across the full MLA content map, rather than chasing a remembered percentage, is the only strategy that is robust to this variation.

It also helps to be clear about what kind of number the AKT cut score actually is. It is not a percentile rank, it is not a curve fitted to how the cohort happened to perform, and it is not adjusted afterwards to produce a target pass rate. It is a raw mark, out of the total number of scored items in that sitting, derived from expert judgement about what a borderline-competent candidate should be able to achieve. Once you accept that the number itself is sitting-specific, several common candidate anxieties resolve themselves fairly quickly. Comparing your predicted score against a friend's remembered pass mark from a different diet, for example, is simply comparing two different measurements that were never meant to be equivalent in the first place.

Why the Pass Mark Varies Between Sittings

The UKMLA AKT pass mark varies sitting-to-sitting because each sitting is built from an entirely different set of questions, and each of those question sets is independently judged for difficulty by a panel of clinical and educational experts before the exam is sat. This is not an oversight or an inconsistency in the system — it is a deliberate design feature that exists specifically to keep the exam fair when question difficulty inevitably fluctuates between diets.

In plain terms, here is what happens behind the scenes:

  • A new bank of questions is assembled for each sitting, drawn from a much larger item pool.
  • A panel of experienced clinicians, educators, and psychometricians reviews that specific question set and estimates, item by item, how a borderline-competent candidate would be expected to perform.
  • Those item-level judgements are summed to produce the pass mark for that sitting alone.
  • If that sitting's questions happen to be, on the whole, more demanding than the previous sitting's, the resulting pass mark will typically be lower — and conversely, an easier question set produces a higher pass mark.

Consequently, the pass mark is best understood as a reflection of that sitting's specific difficulty, not as a moving average or a random fluctuation. The full mechanics of this process — including the Minimally Competent Candidate concept, the rating rounds, and the psychometric adjustments applied afterwards — are explained step by step in our companion post on Angoff standard setting explained, and it is worth reading if you want the "why" behind the number rather than just the practical implications covered here.

Why This Is Fairer Than a Fixed Percentage

It might initially feel more reassuring to have one unchanging number to aim for. However, a fixed percentage pass mark would actually be less fair to candidates, not more, because it assumes every sitting is equally difficult — an assumption that is empirically untrue for any exam drawing from a large, rotating item bank. If a fixed 60% pass mark were applied regardless of difficulty, candidates unlucky enough to sit a harder paper would be penalised twice: once by facing harder questions, and again by being held to the same raw score as candidates who sat an easier paper.

By contrast, a variable, expert-calibrated pass mark corrects for this automatically. A harder sitting produces a lower cut score; an easier sitting produces a higher one. As a result, a candidate's chance of passing depends on their competency relative to the clinical standard, rather than on the luck of which sitting they happened to be allocated. This is precisely the same logic used by other major English-language licensing examinations, including PLAB 1 and the Australian Medical Council's MCQ examination, both of which also use Angoff-based, criterion-referenced standard setting rather than a static percentage.

There is also a subtler fairness argument worth making explicit. A fixed percentage pass mark effectively outsources the definition of competency to whichever question writers happened to draft that year's paper, since an unusually easy paper would let more borderline candidates through at 60%, while an unusually hard paper would fail candidates who were genuinely competent but simply encountered tougher items. An expert-calibrated, sitting-specific pass mark instead keeps the definition of competency constant and lets the raw number move instead — which is precisely what should happen if the goal is a stable standard rather than a stable-looking number.

How This Compares With Norm-Referenced Grading

It is worth briefly contrasting this criterion-referenced approach with norm-referenced grading, since many candidates are more familiar with the latter from undergraduate coursework, where marks are sometimes adjusted to fit a target grade distribution across a cohort. Under a norm-referenced system, a fixed proportion of candidates would pass regardless of how competent the whole cohort actually was — meaning a uniformly weak cohort could see most candidates pass, while a uniformly strong cohort could see many candidates fail simply because they were compared against unusually capable peers. The UKMLA AKT deliberately avoids this model. Your result depends on your own performance against the clinical standard, not on how the rest of your specific cohort happened to perform.

Where to Find Your Sitting's Pass Mark and Your Own Result

Candidates find out their own sitting's pass mark, and how their own result compares to it, through the official results reporting channel for their route into the profession — GMC Online for most candidates, with UK medical schools typically also communicating results through their own examination boards for finalists sitting the AKT as part of their degree. The results report does not simply tell you "pass" or "fail" in isolation; it situates your score against that sitting's specific cut score, so you can see exactly how far above or below the threshold you fell.

When your results become available, a few things are worth checking carefully:

  • The specific pass mark that applied to your sitting, rather than any percentage you may have seen quoted for a previous diet.
  • Your own raw score, expressed against that same scale, so the comparison is meaningful.
  • Any domain-level or content-area breakdown provided, which can indicate where your performance was strongest and weakest relative to the standard.

Reading a Borderline Result Correctly

Candidates who score very close to the pass mark — whether just above or just below — often find this the hardest result to interpret emotionally, even though it is one of the most statistically explicable outcomes in the whole system. No examination measures competency with perfect precision; every test score carries a margin of measurement error, meaning a candidate's true underlying competency could plausibly have produced a slightly higher or slightly lower observed score on a different day, even with no change in their actual knowledge. A result within a few marks of the cut score therefore sits in a genuinely uncertain zone, and it is worth resisting the urge to over-interpret a narrow pass as proof of mastery or a narrow fail as proof of inadequacy. Both outcomes call for the same sensible response: identify the specific content or question-style gaps visible in the results feedback report, and address them directly.

What Is Known About UKMLA AKT Pass Rates

Published pass-rate statistics for the UKMLA are released periodically by the GMC, often in collaboration with the Medical Schools Council where UK final-year students are concerned, since final-year students sit the AKT as an integrated part of their medical degree rather than as a standalone licensing step. It is important to be precise here: this post deliberately avoids quoting a specific pass-rate percentage, because that figure changes between cohorts and sittings, and any number written into a static blog post risks becoming stale or misleading almost immediately.

What can be said honestly, in qualitative terms, is that the pattern of results tends to differ meaningfully between the two main candidate groups:

  • UK final-year medical students sitting the AKT as a compulsory part of their degree generally benefit from structured, longitudinal preparation embedded in their curriculum, cohort-wide mock exams, and institutional exam-technique support built up over several years of training within the same assessment culture the AKT was designed to sit alongside.
  • IMG candidates sitting the AKT as their route to GMC registration typically come from a much wider variety of undergraduate training backgrounds, educational systems, and prior assessment styles, and often prepare independently, without an institution running structured mock sittings or dedicated exam-technique teaching aligned specifically to the UKMLA's format.

As a result of these differing preparation contexts, published aggregate figures have generally shown a different pass-rate pattern between the two groups, with IMG cohorts tending to show a lower aggregate pass rate than UK final-year cohorts. This is not a reflection of clinical ability; rather, it reflects the fact that IMG candidates are, on average, sitting an examination that is calibrated to a specific national curriculum and NHS-based clinical-guideline style that they may not have been immersed in during undergraduate training, and are frequently doing so without the same institutional scaffolding that UK final-year students receive by default.

Factors That Genuinely Influence Your Chance of Passing

While the pass mark itself is outside any individual candidate's control, several preparation factors are firmly within it, and these are the practical levers most worth focusing your energy on. Understanding the mechanics of standard setting is useful context, but it does not, by itself, get you across the line — deliberate preparation does.

Systematic Coverage of the Official Content Map

The single most reliable predictor of AKT success is comprehensive, systematic coverage of the official MLA content map, rather than an ad hoc revision approach built around whichever topics feel most comfortable or most recently taught. Because the exam is blueprinted against this content map — meaning questions are drawn from it in proportion to clinical importance and frequency — candidates who work through it presentation by presentation, rather than skipping around, are directly aligning their preparation with how the exam is actually constructed. Furthermore, treating high-frequency, high-stakes presentations (chest pain, breathlessness, sepsis, and similarly common acute scenarios) as non-negotiable revision priorities reflects the fact that these areas are judged, by the standard-setting panel, to be ones a minimally competent candidate should reliably get right.

In practice, systematic coverage means working from a checklist rather than a feeling of familiarity. Many candidates unconsciously gravitate towards topics they already find interesting or that were taught particularly well at their medical school, while quietly avoiding presentations they find dull or that were covered only briefly during undergraduate training. Over a long revision period, this creates exactly the kind of uneven coverage the content map is designed to catch, since the AKT samples proportionally across the whole breadth of UK clinical practice rather than concentrating on any single specialty. Working through the content map methodically, marking off each presentation as genuinely revised rather than merely recognised, is a simple but effective safeguard against this bias.

It is equally important not to interpret "systematic" as "exhaustive to the point of diminishing returns." Some rare, highly specialised presentations receive comparatively little weight in the standard-setting panel's judgements precisely because they are rare, and spending disproportionate revision time on them at the expense of common acute presentations is a poor use of a candidate's limited preparation time. A sensible approach allocates revision time roughly in proportion to clinical frequency and stakes, mirroring how the exam itself is blueprinted.

Exposure to UK-Specific NHS and NICE-Guideline Question Style

For many IMG candidates specifically, the gap between undergraduate clinical knowledge and AKT performance is not really a knowledge gap at all — it is a question-style gap. Candidates trained under different national guidelines may know the underlying clinical science perfectly well, yet still select the "wrong" answer on an AKT-style single-best-answer question because the correct option reflects a specific NHS pathway or a NICE guideline recommendation rather than a more generic international standard of care. This distinction matters enormously, because it means the fix is not "study harder" in a generic sense but rather deliberate, targeted exposure to NHS-based and NICE-guideline-based scenarios until that specific style of clinical reasoning becomes familiar. Practising with a large volume of UK-guideline-aligned single-best-answer questions, and consciously noting where your instinctive answer diverges from the NICE-recommended one, is one of the most effective ways to close this gap.

High-yield areas where this style gap shows up particularly often include prescribing decisions and drug-safety scenarios. Our post on pharmacology and prescribing safety is a good example of the kind of high-yield, NHS-specific content area that rewards this systematic, guideline-literate style of revision rather than generic pharmacology knowledge alone.

This question-style gap also tends to show up in areas such as referral pathways, safety-netting advice, and first-line management choices, where several answer options may all be clinically defensible in a general sense, but only one matches the specific pathway a NICE guideline or NHS protocol recommends. Candidates who have trained exclusively in health systems with different referral thresholds, different first-line drug choices, or different escalation criteria can find these questions disproportionately difficult relative to their actual clinical knowledge, simply because the "best" answer is defined by a specific national guideline rather than by general clinical reasoning. Recognising this pattern early in preparation, rather than mid-exam, allows candidates to deliberately seek out NICE-guideline-referenced question banks and revision resources rather than relying solely on general international clinical textbooks.

Realistic Mock Exam Practice Under Timed Conditions

Timed, full-length mock papers that replicate real sitting conditions are an essential preparation tool, both for content revision and for exam-technique reasons. Candidates who only practise questions in short, untimed bursts often discover on the actual exam day that time pressure changes their decision-making — rushed second-guessing, panic at unfamiliar question stems, or simply running out of time on the final block of questions. Building stamina and pacing through realistic mock conditions well before the real sitting therefore addresses a genuinely controllable variable in your performance.

Exam-day anxiety itself is also a real and controllable factor, and it is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as unavoidable nerves. Because performance anxiety can meaningfully suppress a candidate's ability to demonstrate knowledge they genuinely have, our guide on managing UKMLA exam anxiety is directly relevant here — worrying excessively about where the pass mark will land, rather than focusing on your own preparation and technique, is exactly the kind of unproductive anxiety that guide addresses.

It is also worth understanding the practical exam-day format itself before sitting a mock, since unfamiliarity with logistics (timing per block, navigation between questions, flagging for review) can cost marks unrelated to clinical knowledge. Our AKT format and preparation guide covers this background in detail and is a useful companion to mock-exam practice.

Mock practice is most valuable when it is treated as a diagnostic tool rather than purely a confidence exercise. Simply completing a mock paper and noting an overall score tells you very little on its own; reviewing every incorrect answer, understanding precisely why the correct option was preferred over your choice, and tracking recurring error patterns across multiple mocks is what actually converts practice into improvement. Candidates who sit several mocks without ever returning to review their mistakes in depth often plateau well before the real exam, because they are repeating the same errors under slightly different clinical scenarios each time.

For Repeat Candidates: Targeted Analysis of a Previous Results Report

Candidates resitting the AKT face a specific, and very common, trap: treating a resit as an opportunity to simply "revise everything again, but harder." This is rarely the most efficient approach, and it often fails to address the actual reason the previous attempt fell short. Instead, a resitting candidate's first step should be a careful analysis of their previous results feedback report, identifying specific domains or content areas where performance was genuinely weak relative to the standard, rather than assuming the shortfall was evenly spread across the whole syllabus.

In practice, this means asking targeted questions of your own previous result: which content areas or presentation types showed weaker relative performance? Was there a pattern suggesting a question-style issue (for example, consistently choosing clinically plausible but non-NICE-aligned answers) rather than a pure knowledge gap? Answering these questions honestly, using your own data rather than generic advice, is what separates an efficient resit preparation plan from a repeat of the same mistakes.

What Happens If You Don't Meet the Pass Mark

If your score falls below your sitting's published pass mark, this is a meaningful signal that additional preparation is needed before entering independent practice — not a verdict on your long-term potential as a clinician, and importantly, not something that happens in isolation, since resitting is a well-established, structured pathway used by many successful doctors before them. The practical process — how many resit attempts are permitted, how soon you can sit again, and how to plan a resit strategically — is covered in full in our dedicated post on UKMLA resits and appeals, so it is not repeated in depth here.

The one point worth emphasising in the context of the pass mark specifically is this: a narrow miss, particularly one within a few marks of the published cut score for your sitting, reflects the genuine statistical uncertainty inherent in any examination rather than a dramatic shortfall in competency. Consequently, candidates in this position are often well served by a targeted review of their weaker domains rather than a wholesale re-revision of everything, exactly as described in the previous section.

It is also worth resisting two common but unhelpful reactions to a below-pass-mark result. The first is to assume the sitting's pass mark itself was set unfairly high — a belief that, however understandable emotionally, is not usually supported by the evidence, since the standard-setting process is applied consistently and independently of any individual candidate's outcome. The second is to over-correct by abandoning a structured revision plan altogether in favour of frantic, unfocused re-reading of everything at once. Neither response addresses the specific gap that produced the result. A calmer, evidence-based approach — reviewing the feedback report, identifying two or three genuinely weak domains, and building a focused plan around them — is consistently the more productive path towards a successful resit.

Candidates should also remember that a below-pass-mark result on one sitting does not retroactively change how that sitting's pass mark was calculated, nor does it affect the pass mark of any future sitting. Each sitting is assessed entirely on its own terms, which means a disappointing result is genuinely a fresh starting point rather than a permanent mark against a candidate's record within the examination system itself.

Common Misconceptions About the UKMLA AKT Pass Mark

A number of persistent myths circulate among candidates about how the pass mark works, often passed along informally between cohorts without ever being checked against the official methodology. Addressing these directly is useful, since believing them can lead to genuinely counterproductive preparation decisions.

Myth: "The pass mark is always around 60%." This figure may have applied to a specific past sitting, but treating it as a permanent benchmark misunderstands how the cut score is generated. Because the mark is recalculated for each sitting based on that sitting's specific question difficulty, a remembered historical figure tells you very little about what your own sitting will require.

Myth: "If more people fail, the exam board must lower the pass mark afterwards to fix it." The pass mark for a given sitting is fundamentally set by the standard-setting panel's judgement about question difficulty, subject to defined psychometric adjustments such as equating and the removal of flawed items — not by adjusting the outcome to hit a target pass rate after the fact. A high fail rate in a particular sitting is not, by itself, evidence that the standard was set incorrectly; it may simply reflect that a genuinely harder set of questions still produced a defensible standard.

Myth: "IMGs are held to a different, harder pass mark than UK students." This is inaccurate. Both groups sitting the same version of the AKT in the same sitting are held to the same pass mark for that sitting, since the standard is set against the examination content itself, not against the candidate's background or route into the profession. The pattern of differing aggregate pass rates described earlier in this post reflects differences in preparation context and guideline familiarity, not a differently calibrated standard applied to different groups.

Myth: "Scoring well above the pass mark in a question bank guarantees a pass on the real AKT." As covered above, question banks and the real examination often have different difficulty distributions and question-writing conventions, so a comfortable margin in a question bank does not translate directly into an equivalent margin on the actual AKT. Question bank performance remains a useful trend indicator, but it should not be treated as a precise forecast of your result against the sitting-specific pass mark.

Myth: "A single very difficult question means the whole paper's pass mark will be unreasonably high." The pass mark is derived from the sum of expert judgements across every question in the paper, not from any single item in isolation. A handful of difficult questions are typically balanced by others judged more accessible to a borderline-competent candidate, and the overall cut score reflects that full balance rather than the hardest or easiest individual question.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the pass mark for the UKMLA AKT?

The UKMLA AKT does not have one fixed pass mark that applies to every sitting. Instead, the pass mark is calculated individually for each sitting using the Angoff standard-setting method, meaning it can vary slightly depending on that sitting's specific question difficulty. The GMC publishes the exact pass mark for each sitting alongside candidates' results, so the only figure that applies to you is the one attached to your own sitting.

Does the pass mark change every sitting?

Yes, the pass mark can change from one sitting to the next, because each sitting uses a different set of exam questions that is independently assessed for difficulty by an expert panel before results are calculated. A sitting with a harder overall question set will typically produce a slightly lower pass mark, while an easier sitting will typically produce a slightly higher one, so that the standard being applied stays consistent even though the raw number moves.

Is the UKMLA AKT pass rate different for IMGs and UK students?

Published aggregate data has generally shown a different pattern between UK final-year medical students, who benefit from institutional exam-technique support and longitudinal preparation embedded in their degree, and IMG candidates, who often prepare independently from more varied educational backgrounds. This does not reflect a difference in underlying clinical ability, but rather a difference in exposure to the specific NHS and NICE-guideline-based question style the exam uses. Exact current figures are published by the GMC and should be checked directly rather than relied upon from secondhand sources.

How do I find out my own sitting's pass mark?

Your own sitting's pass mark is communicated as part of your official results report, typically through GMC Online or your medical school's examination board if you are a UK final-year student. This report situates your score directly against that sitting's specific cut score, rather than against a generic percentage, so you can see precisely how your result compares to the standard that applied to you.

Why doesn't the UKMLA just use a fixed percentage like 60%?

A fixed percentage pass mark would assume every sitting's questions are equally difficult, which is not a realistic assumption for a large, rotating exam question bank. Using expert judgement to calibrate the pass mark to each specific sitting's difficulty, instead of a static number, protects candidates from being unfairly disadvantaged simply because they were allocated a harder sitting than someone else.

What should I do if I don't meet the pass mark?

A result below the published pass mark for your sitting means further preparation is needed before you can demonstrate the required standard, and resitting is a well-established, structured route that many candidates use successfully. The most effective next step is usually a careful review of your previous results feedback report to identify genuine weak areas, rather than a generic repeat of your original revision plan, alongside understanding the formal resit process itself.

Does question bank performance predict my AKT pass mark accurately?

Question bank percentages are a useful directional guide to your progress, but they are not a precise prediction of your AKT outcome, because question banks and the real AKT often differ in their difficulty distribution and question style. It is more useful to track whether your performance is trending upward and where your specific content or domain weaknesses lie, rather than treating any single question bank percentage as equivalent to your likely AKT score.

Conclusion

The UKMLA AKT pass mark is not a number you can simply memorise in advance from a forum post or a previous cohort's result — it is calculated freshly for each sitting, reflecting that sitting's specific question difficulty as judged by an expert panel, and published to you individually alongside your own result. Rather than chasing a remembered percentage, the most reliable strategy is thorough, systematic preparation across the full content map, deliberate exposure to UK-specific guideline-based question style, realistic timed mock practice, and, for resitting candidates, a targeted review of genuine weak areas from a previous results report. Understood this way, the pass mark stops being a source of anxiety and becomes simply the standard your preparation needs to meet.
