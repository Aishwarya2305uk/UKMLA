The UKMLA AKT (Applied Knowledge Test) is the written component of the UK Medical Licensing Assessment and the first of two examinations every doctor must pass before receiving a GMC licence to practise in the UK. Whether you are a UK medical student approaching final year or an International Medical Graduate (IMG) sitting via the PLAB 1 route, the AKT represents a carefully structured test of your ability to apply clinical knowledge to realistic patient scenarios at the level expected of a Foundation Year 1 doctor. Passing the AKT on your first attempt is the gateway to booking the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), and a strong performance provides the confidence and momentum that carry through into clinical exam preparation.

The AKT differs from the rote-recall model of traditional medical examinations. Questions do not ask you to list the causes of a condition from memory — they present a patient in a specific clinical context and ask you to select the most appropriate next step in investigation, diagnosis, or management. This applied reasoning approach is deliberate: it tests the thinking process a Foundation doctor would actually use at the bedside, not the performance of recalling lists under pressure. Understanding this distinction is the first step in preparing effectively.

This guide covers the exact format, question anatomy, domain breakdown, standard-setting methodology, best preparation resources, common candidate mistakes, and a detailed day-of-exam strategy. By the end, you will have a complete picture of what the UKMLA AKT involves and a structured plan for passing it.

UKMLA AKT Format at a Glance

The AKT consists of 200 Single Best Answer (SBA) questions delivered on-screen, completed over a three-hour (180-minute) session. This gives candidates an average of 54 seconds per question — a pace that rewards both clinical knowledge and efficient decision-making. The examination is delivered through Pearson VUE test centres worldwide for IMGs (via PLAB 1) and at designated sites for UK medical students as coordinated by the Medical Schools Council.

The 200 questions are not split across multiple papers on separate days in the current IMG/PLAB 1 format — all 200 questions are completed in a single session. Some UK medical school versions of the AKT may have slightly different structural arrangements depending on how they integrate the national examination with their institution's own assessment calendar; students should check their medical school's specific guidance for the exact format they will encounter.

There is no negative marking in the AKT. Every question left unanswered scores zero; every question answered incorrectly also scores zero. This means there is never a rational reason to leave a question blank — always select your best guess, even if you are uncertain. A guessed answer on a 5-option question gives a 20% chance of being correct; a blank guarantees zero.

What the UKMLA AKT Tests: Domain Breakdown

The AKT is mapped against the MLA Content Map across all four pillars (clinical presentations, clinical conditions, professional behaviours, and practical skills) and all three underpinning themes (patient safety, safeguarding, prescribing). SBA Question Anatomy: How AKT Questions Are Written

Every SBA question in the AKT follows a standard structural template. Understanding this template is essential for efficient, accurate answering. The standard AKT question consists of three parts:

The Clinical Vignette

The vignette presents a patient scenario. It typically includes: patient demographics (age, sex, sometimes occupation or social context); the presenting complaint; a brief history; relevant examination findings; and, often, investigation results (blood tests, imaging, ECG findings). UKMLA AKT vignettes are longer and more detailed than pre-MLA PLAB 1 vignettes, reflecting the shift toward applied reasoning rather than recall. A typical vignette is 60–120 words long. Candidates who read slowly or who try to hold every detail in working memory during reading will struggle with time management.

The Stem Question

The stem is the question itself. Common formats include: What is the most likely diagnosis? / What is the most appropriate next investigation? / What is the most appropriate initial management? / What is the most likely causative organism? / What is the most appropriate course of action regarding this patient's capacity? The stem tells you the cognitive skill being tested — diagnosis, investigation selection, management, or professional behaviour. Reading the stem before the vignette can help some candidates focus their reading on the most relevant information.

The Five Answer Options

Five answer options are provided, labelled A to E. One is the single best answer; four are distractors. In well-written SBA questions, two or three distractors will be plausible — the correct answer is not necessarily the only defensible answer, but it is the best according to current UK clinical guidelines (primarily NICE, BNF, and GMC guidance). High-scoring candidates know not just why the correct answer is right, but exactly why each distractor is less correct than the chosen option.

AKT Scoring and Standard Setting

The AKT pass mark is not fixed at a predetermined percentage. It is determined for each sitting using a process called criterion-referenced standard setting, specifically the Angoff method. In this process, a panel of practising clinicians (typically Foundation Programme-level doctors and educators) reviews each question before the sitting and estimates the probability that a minimally competent Foundation Year 1 doctor would answer it correctly. The sum of these probabilities across all questions generates the expected raw score of the minimally competent candidate — and this becomes the pass mark for that sitting.

The practical implication is that the pass mark varies between sittings, typically falling in the range of 53–68% of items correct. A particularly difficult question set will generate a lower pass mark; a more straightforward set will generate a higher one. This system is fairer than a fixed percentage because it adjusts for variation in question difficulty between sittings rather than penalising candidates who happen to sit on a difficult day.

Results are typically released three to four weeks after sitting. The result report shows a pass or fail outcome together with a domain-level performance profile. Candidates who fail receive feedback showing their performance relative to the pass mark across clinical domains, which is invaluable for targeting re-sit preparation.

AKT vs Traditional Written Medical Exams: Key Differences

Understanding how the AKT differs from the types of written examinations most medical graduates experienced during their training helps candidates adjust their preparation strategy. Many IMG candidates in particular have extensive experience with recall-based examinations from their home country systems, and may need to consciously shift their revision approach.

  • Applied reasoning vs recall: The AKT tests what you would do, not what you can list. Preparation should focus on decision-making frameworks, not fact memorisation.
  • Longer, more detailed vignettes: AKT vignettes are typically 60–120 words compared to 20–40 words in older-style PLAB questions. Speed reading and selective information extraction are important skills.
  • UK guideline alignment: All management questions are answered according to NICE guidelines, BNF, and current UK clinical practice. Candidates from non-UK backgrounds must explicitly study UK management pathways, which may differ from their home country standards.
  • No negative marking: Unlike some international examinations, the AKT does not penalise incorrect answers. Always answer every question.
  • Criterion-referenced pass mark: The pass mark adjusts per sitting, unlike percentage-threshold systems. This means comparisons of raw scores between sittings are not meaningful.

UKMLA AKT Preparation Strategies

Effective AKT preparation requires a systematic, content-map-led approach over a sustained period. Most candidates benefit from a preparation period of three to six months for a first attempt, though candidates with recent clinical experience in a UK context may require less, and candidates preparing from scratch may need more.

Build Your Foundation with the MLA Content Map

Download the current MLA content map from the GMC website and use it as your primary organising document. Work through the conditions list systematically, rather than by specialty interest or perceived difficulty. For each condition, study the typical presentation, differential diagnosis, initial investigations, immediate management, and key complications at Foundation-level depth. Avoid going deeper than Foundation level — you are preparing for an entry-level licensing examination, not a specialist membership exam.

Use a Quality Question Bank as Your Primary Learning Tool

Question banks are the most effective single preparation tool for the AKT. High-quality question banks aligned to the MLA content map include detailed explanations that teach the reasoning behind each answer, not just the correct option. When using a question bank, do not simply read the explanation for the question you got wrong — read the explanations for all five options of every question, including the ones you answered correctly. Understanding why each distractor is less correct than the right answer is a higher-level cognitive skill that the AKT specifically rewards.

Popular AKT question banks include resources from MedRevisions, Pastest, UKMLA Question Bank, AiMedQs, and Quesmed, among others. Subscription costs range from approximately £40 to £200. Using multiple question banks with different question writing styles helps expose you to the variety of approaches you will encounter in the real examination.

Simulate Exam Conditions Regularly

Use timed mock papers to simulate the pacing demands of the real AKT. Set a 180-minute timer, work through 200 questions without interruption, and resist the urge to look up answers mid-session. Time pressure is a genuine challenge in the AKT — the 54-second average gives very little room for extended deliberation on any single question. Practise the habit of spending no more than 90 seconds on a flagged question before making your best guess and moving on.

Focus on UK Guidelines

NICE guidelines are the primary reference for AKT management questions. Key guidelines to know well include those for hypertension, diabetes, asthma, COPD, heart failure, AKI, CKD, depression, schizophrenia, dementia, and common cancers. The BNF (British National Formulary) is essential for prescribing questions. The GMC's Good Medical Practice is the reference document for all professional behaviour and ethical questions. Candidates from non-UK backgrounds who are familiar with different national guidelines (WHO, SIGN, non-UK national guidelines) must actively overwrite these with UK-specific pathways for exam purposes.

AKT Booking for IMGs (PLAB 1): Logistics

IMGs sit the AKT through the PLAB 1 booking system on the GMC website. To book, you must have an active GMC Online account and have your eligibility to sit PLAB confirmed (including having your medical qualification recognised and, if required for your nationality, a valid visa or appropriate immigration status to access the test centre country). Bookings are made at Pearson VUE test centres using the GMC's booking portal; you select your preferred country, city, and date. Payment of £283 is required at the time of booking.

Popular test centres in high-demand countries (India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Egypt, the Philippines) fill quickly. It is advisable to book two to three months in advance to secure your preferred date and location. If your first-choice date is not available, consider nearby cities or neighbouring countries with available slots. Test centre experience is standardised — every Pearson VUE centre delivers the same software interface, equipment, and security protocols regardless of location.

Frequently Asked Questions About the UKMLA AKT

How many times can I sit the AKT if I fail?

There is no limit on the number of AKT attempts for IMGs. UK medical students' re-sit opportunities are governed by their medical school's academic regulations, which vary by institution. Each IMG re-sit requires a fresh booking and payment of £283. Most candidates who invest in targeted re-preparation after a fail — using their domain-level feedback report to prioritise weak areas — are successful on their next attempt. The AKT feedback report clearly shows performance across clinical domains, making it a powerful guide for focused second-attempt preparation.

Do I need to travel to the UK to sit the AKT?

No. IMGs sit the AKT (via PLAB 1) at Pearson VUE test centres in over 180 countries worldwide. You do not need to be in the UK to sit the AKT. This is a significant logistical and financial advantage compared to the CPSA, which must be taken in Manchester. You can complete the entire AKT stage of the UKMLA pathway from your home country.

What score do I need to pass the AKT?

The AKT pass mark is criterion-referenced and set separately for each sitting using the Angoff method, so there is no single fixed score. The pass mark typically falls in the range of 53–68% of the 200 questions answered correctly (106–136 questions). You will not know the exact pass mark for your sitting until results are released; focus on maximising your raw score rather than targeting a specific percentage threshold.

How long are AKT results valid?

AKT results (for IMGs) are valid for five years from the date of the sitting. You must pass the CPSA and complete GMC registration within this five-year window, or you may need to re-sit the AKT. This validity period gives IMGs substantial flexibility in the timing of their CPSA preparation without risking the expiry of their AKT result.

Is the AKT the same as PLAB 1?

From August 2024 onward, PLAB 1 is assessed against the MLA Content Map and meets the requirements of the AKT. In practical terms, if you book and sit PLAB 1, you are sitting an examination that meets the MLA AKT standard. The booking portal, fee, and logistics remain labelled as PLAB 1, but the content, question style, and standard are now equivalent to the UKMLA AKT sat by UK medical students.

Conclusion

The UKMLA AKT is a rigorous but very achievable examination for candidates who prepare systematically, understand the applied-reasoning question style, and align their revision with the MLA Content Map and UK clinical guidelines. The key insight that separates high-scorers from those who struggle is the shift from passive information absorption to active clinical reasoning practice — every question in your preparation should be an exercise in thinking through the clinical problem as a Foundation doctor would, not an exercise in checking whether you can recall a fact.

Start your preparation with the MLA Content Map as your organising framework, build solid clinical knowledge across all domains, practise extensively with high-quality SBA question banks, simulate timed exam conditions regularly, and focus on UK-specific guidelines and management pathways. With this approach, passing the AKT on your first attempt is a realistic and achievable goal for the vast majority of well-prepared candidates.
