The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes represent the most significant revision to the MLA content map since the exam launched, and every candidate sitting from September 2026 onwards needs to understand exactly what has moved, what has stayed the same, and how to adjust a revision plan accordingly. In October 2025, the General Medical Council (GMC) published an updated MLA content map alongside a "You said, we did" summary explaining how candidate and medical school feedback shaped the revision. The update does not take effect immediately — it applies to all MLA exams and assessments sat from September 2026 onwards, with the original, pre-2026 content map remaining the valid reference for anyone sitting up to and including August 2026.

The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes matter for a wider group of people than the phrase "content map update" might suggest. It affects final-year UK medical students who are already mid-revision and wondering whether their question bank is current. It affects first- and second-year students building a longer-term study plan around a syllabus that will look different by the time they sit. And, distinctly, it affects International Medical Graduates (IMGs) taking the PLAB route, because from September 2026 PLAB sittings are assessed against the very same updated content map — a structural convergence that has been building since PLAB and the UKMLA were first aligned to a common standard.

This guide sets out exactly what changed in the 2026 update, why the GMC made these changes, what has not changed (the exam format and structure remain the same — only the content scope has moved), and a practical framework for adapting your revision plan depending on where you are in your preparation timeline. Because the GMC has described the new list as "indicative and non-exhaustive" and reserves the right to revise it further, this guide also flags exactly where you should always check the GMC's live content map page before finalising a revision timetable. If you want the fuller, non-2026-specific explainer on how the content map works as a whole, the general blueprint guide to the MLA content map is the companion piece to this one; this article is deliberately focused on what is changing for 2026 specifically.

What Changed in the UKMLA Syllabus for 2026? The Quick Answer

The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes centre on four things: the content map's condition list grew from approximately 311 conditions to 430 conditions; the mapping grid that previously restricted which conditions could be tested in which specialty context has been removed entirely; four new topic areas — transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance, and social determinants of health — have been formally integrated into the map; and women's health has received the single largest content increase, with conditions that used to be grouped together now broken out individually with more expected clinical detail. None of this changes the format of the exam itself: the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) remain structurally the same. What has changed is the scope of content that can appear within that structure.

The GMC has also made the full updated document available as a downloadable PDF content map, which is useful for offline, systematic revision planning alongside the live page.

What This Means for UK Medical Students

If you are a UK medical student, the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes will interact with your revision timeline differently depending on when you are due to sit. The GMC's transition rule is straightforward in principle: the original, pre-2026 content map remains the reference document for any MLA exam or assessment sat up to and including August 2026, while the updated map applies to everything sat from September 2026 onwards. In practice, this creates two distinct groups of students with quite different planning needs.

Students Sitting Before September 2026

The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes do not apply retroactively. If your AKT or CPSA sitting falls before September 2026, the original content map remains your reference document, and you do not need to restructure your revision around the expanded 430-condition list. It would be inefficient — and potentially counterproductive given your limited revision time — to try to absorb the newly integrated topic areas in depth if your actual sitting predates their formal introduction. That said, it is worth being aware of the direction of travel, particularly around social determinants of health and updated sepsis guidance, since good clinical practice in these areas was already implicit in earlier professional behaviour and patient safety themes even before the update made them explicit.

Students Sitting From September 2026 Onwards

If any part of your MLA pathway — whether your first AKT attempt, a re-sit, or your CPSA — falls in or after September 2026, you are assessed against the updated content map in full. This includes students who may have started revision using older materials built around the pre-2026 map. The practical implication is that your revision plan needs an audit: work through your current question bank, revision notes, and study group materials, and check whether they have been explicitly updated to reflect the expanded conditions list and the four newly integrated topic areas. Materials published or last revised before October 2025, when the updated map was released, are the most likely to be out of date.

Students already mid-revision when the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes were announced face a particular planning challenge. Rather than discarding months of prior work, the sensible approach is targeted supplementation: keep your existing foundation in core specialties, then deliberately layer in focused revision on the newly integrated areas and the expanded women's health content, since these are the areas where a pre-2026 resource is most likely to have a genuine gap rather than simply lighter coverage.

What This Means for IMGs and the PLAB Pathway

For International Medical Graduates, the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes carry an additional layer of significance beyond the content itself, and the changes should not be read as a UK-students-only story: from September 2026, PLAB sittings are also based on the same updated content map used for the UKMLA. This completes a convergence process that has been underway for some time, and it means that UK medical graduates and IMGs are now assessed against precisely the same standard and the same content map, regardless of which route — undergraduate UK training or PLAB — they took to reach the assessment.

Why This Convergence Matters

Before this alignment, IMGs preparing for PLAB sometimes worked from materials that framed the exam as having its own distinct syllabus, separate from the MLA content map used for UK medical students. That framing is now fully obsolete. Any preparation resource, course, or question bank that still describes a "PLAB syllabus" as something separate from the MLA content map is using outdated terminology, and IMGs should treat that as a signal to look for more current materials. The practical upshot for IMGs is that the same audit exercise recommended for UK students above applies with equal or greater force: check whether your PLAB 1 (AKT) and PLAB 2 (CPSA) preparation materials have been updated to reflect the 430-condition list, the removal of the specialty mapping grid, and the four newly integrated topic areas. For a fuller comparison of how the two routes to licensure relate to one another beyond this specific content update, the detailed breakdown of how PLAB and the UKMLA compare covers the wider structural relationship between the two pathways.

Timing Considerations for IMGs

IMGs often plan their PLAB pathway around travel logistics, visa timelines, and financial planning for the exam fees involved, which means the September 2026 transition point deserves particular attention when booking sittings. Because the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes apply on a sitting-by-sitting basis rather than to a candidate's overall pathway as a whole, it is entirely possible for an IMG's PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 attempts to fall on opposite sides of the transition date. An IMG who books a PLAB 1 sitting for August 2026 is assessed against the original content map; the same candidate booking for October 2026 is assessed against the updated map. If your overall pathway spans this boundary — for example, sitting PLAB 1 in mid-2026 and PLAB 2 several months later — check carefully which map applies to each component, since it is entirely possible for one part of your pathway to fall under the old map and the other under the new one. This is a genuinely practical planning point, not just an administrative detail, because it affects which revision resources are the right ones to use for each stage.

The Structural Change: Why Removing the Mapping Grid Matters

Of all the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, the removal of the specialty mapping grid is arguably the one with the biggest effect on how candidates should revise, even though it receives less attention than the headline jump from 311 to 430 conditions. Many candidate discussions about the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes focus almost entirely on the condition count, which risks under-preparing for this structural shift. Under the pre-2026 framework, the content map included a grid that restricted which conditions could be tested within which clinical or specialty context. A condition like sepsis, for instance, might have been primarily mapped to emergency medicine or acute care contexts, and a candidate could reasonably expect not to encounter it framed as, say, a community pharmacy or primary care scenario.

From September 2026, that grid no longer exists. Any condition can now be tested in any specialty setting. This is a genuine structural change, not a cosmetic one, and it has direct consequences for how question-writers can construct AKT stems and how CPSA stations can be scripted.

Why the GMC Removed the Grid

The GMC's "You said, we did" summary, published alongside the updated map in October 2025, explains that this change reflects direct feedback from candidates and medical schools. Real clinical practice does not respect the boundaries of a specialty mapping grid — a Foundation doctor genuinely can encounter a cardiological presentation in a GP setting, a psychiatric presentation on a surgical ward, or an endocrine emergency in an emergency department. By removing the grid, the GMC is asserting that the exam should test the kind of flexible, cross-contextual clinical reasoning that reflects real Foundation-level practice, rather than rewarding candidates who have memorised which conditions "belong" to which specialty silo.

The Practical Revision Implication

This is the single change most likely to catch out a candidate who revises from an outdated mental model. If you have been taught, or have assumed, that certain conditions only appear in certain specialty-themed question blocks — for example, assuming that a cardiology-only stem will always be framed as a cardiology scenario — that assumption is no longer safe. Under the updated map, a condition traditionally associated with one specialty can legitimately appear framed within a completely different clinical context. Practically, this means your revision should deliberately practise applying knowledge across contexts rather than revising specialty-by-specialty in isolation. When you work through a condition, ask not only "what is the typical presentation and management" but also "in what different clinical settings might a Foundation doctor plausibly encounter this."

The Expanded Conditions List: From 311 to 430

The headline number most people associate with the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes is the expansion of the named conditions list from approximately 311 to 430 conditions — an increase of well over a third. It is worth being precise about what this figure does and does not mean for your revision.

What "430 Conditions" Actually Represents

The GMC's content map lists named conditions that are considered examinable within the scope of Foundation-level clinical practice. The expansion to 430 reflects both genuinely new content areas (transgender health, genetics) and a more granular breakdown of content that was previously grouped more broadly, particularly in women's health (covered in detail in the next section). Crucially, the GMC describes this list as "indicative and non-exhaustive." That phrasing is deliberate and important: it means the 430 named conditions are not a hard ceiling on what could appear in an AKT question or CPSA station. A question can still test clinical reasoning around a presentation or condition that is closely related to, but not verbatim identical to, a named entry on the list, provided it sits within the same scope of Foundation-level practice.

How to Use the Expanded List Without Over-Indexing on It

The practical guidance here is to treat the 430-condition list as a structured checklist for breadth of coverage, not as a literal, closed universe of everything that could be asked. Work through the list systematically to identify genuine gaps in your existing knowledge, particularly around the newly integrated topic areas, but do not assume that mastering exactly 430 named conditions in isolation, without also practising applied clinical reasoning across contexts, is sufficient preparation. The removal of the specialty mapping grid (discussed above) reinforces this point: it is the combination of broader content and unrestricted context that defines the real scope of the updated exam, not the condition count on its own.

New Topic Areas Integrated Into the 2026 Map

Among the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, four topic areas have been formally integrated into the updated MLA content map: transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance, and social determinants of health. Each represents a genuine area of new or substantially expanded expected knowledge, and each is worth addressing specifically in your revision plan rather than assuming it is adequately covered by general clinical knowledge.

Transgender Health

Transgender health is now integrated as a distinct area within the content map, reflecting the reality that Foundation doctors across all specialties may encounter transgender patients and need to provide safe, competent, person-centred care. This includes awareness of gender-affirming care pathways, appropriate consultation approaches, and avoiding assumptions that can undermine trust or lead to unsafe or exclusionary care. Candidates should ensure their revision includes this area specifically rather than assuming it is implicitly covered by general communication skills content.

Genetics

Genetics has moved from being scattered lightly across other topic areas to being explicitly integrated as its own content area. This reflects the increasing relevance of genetic and genomic medicine to mainstream clinical practice — not just specialist genetics clinics, but everyday scenarios involving genetic counselling conversations, recognising inherited condition patterns, and understanding the basics of how genetic test results inform clinical decisions. Candidates whose undergraduate genetics teaching was relatively light should treat this as a genuine gap to close rather than an area that can be skipped.

Updated Sepsis Guidance

Sepsis recognition and management guidance has been updated within the 2026 content map to reflect current best-practice frameworks. Sepsis has always been a high-yield area for the UKMLA given its direct relevance to patient safety, and candidates should ensure their revision materials reflect the current guidance rather than an earlier version. Because clinical guidance in this area evolves, this is one of the clearest examples of why outdated preparation materials — even those that are otherwise broadly comprehensive — can create a real knowledge gap if they were compiled before the update.

Social Determinants of Health

Social determinants of health — the ways that housing, poverty, employment, education, and social circumstances shape health outcomes — have moved from being an implicit theme within professional behaviours to an explicitly listed, examinable content area. This reflects a broader recognition that person-centred care requires understanding the social context in which a patient's health problem exists, not just the biomedical presentation in isolation. For a fuller treatment of how this topic area is likely to be tested and what it means for person-centred care in the UKMLA specifically, candidates preparing for questions in this area should look at how social factors are woven into person-centred clinical scenarios rather than treating this purely as background context.

Women's Health: The Largest Single Content Increase

Among all the areas affected by the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, women's health represents by far the largest single increase in content, and it deserves more attention than it typically receives in candidate discussion of the update. The pre-2026 content map grouped many obstetric and gynaecological conditions together under broader headings; the updated map breaks these out into individually named, more specific conditions, with correspondingly more detailed clinical expectations attached to each.

What "Broken Out Individually" Means in Practice

Where a pre-2026 candidate might have revised a broader grouped heading covering several related obstetric or gynaecological presentations together, the updated map expects familiarity with the individual conditions within that grouping at a more granular level. This does not necessarily mean an entirely new universe of women's health knowledge — much of the underlying clinical content was always relevant to safe Foundation-level practice — but it does mean the content map now expects candidates to demonstrate more specific, differentiated knowledge rather than broader pattern-recognition across a grouped category.

Revision Implications for Women's Health

Given that this is the single largest area of content increase within the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, candidates — particularly those revising from pre-2026 materials — should treat women's health as a priority area for a fresh audit. Do not assume that a pre-existing revision framework built around broader groupings will automatically translate into adequate coverage of the more granular conditions now named individually. Where possible, work through the updated content map's women's health section condition-by-condition rather than relying on a summary that may still reflect the older, grouped structure.

What Has Not Changed: Exam Format and Structure

It is just as important to be clear about what the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes do not affect as it is to understand what they do affect. The exam format and structure are not changing — only the content scope has changed. The AKT remains a computer-based test of applied clinical knowledge and decision-making, and the CPSA remains a station-based clinical and professional skills assessment. Candidates do not need to prepare for a different exam format, different question types, different station structures, or a different overall assessment philosophy. The change is entirely about what can be tested within the existing structure, not how it is tested.

The underpinning themes that run across the content map — patient safety, safeguarding, and safe prescribing — are also unchanged in principle, though the removal of the specialty mapping grid means prescribing-themed questions can now be woven into a wider range of clinical contexts than before. Prescribing safety was already one of the most heavily tested underpinning themes prior to 2026, and nothing about the update reduces its weight. If your revision plan does not yet include a dedicated pass through how prescribing and medication safety questions are typically tested, this is a good moment to add one, since prescribing scenarios are exactly the kind of content that benefits from the cross-context practice the updated map now rewards.

This distinction matters for revision planning because it means your existing familiarity with AKT question formats — for example, how to approach a clinical stem systematically — remains just as relevant as before. What needs updating is the breadth and specificity of the content knowledge you bring to that familiar format, not the exam-technique skills themselves. Candidates should resist the temptation to over-interpret the update as a wholesale overhaul of the assessment; it is a content scope revision layered onto an unchanged assessment structure.

How to Adapt Your Revision Plan for the 2026 Content Map

Translating the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes into a practical, workable revision plan depends heavily on where you currently stand in your preparation timeline. The following framework addresses the most common scenarios.

If You Are Just Starting Your Revision

Candidates starting revision from scratch, particularly those with a sitting date from September 2026 onwards, should build their study plan directly around the updated content map from day one. Download the current version from the GMC's website, and structure your revision systematically around it rather than adapting an older framework. This is the most straightforward scenario because there is no need to reconcile old and new materials — simply build on the current foundation.

If You Are Mid-Revision With Existing Materials

Candidates who are partway through a revision plan built on older materials, but whose sitting falls from September 2026 onwards, need a more surgical approach. Rather than discarding existing work, conduct a gap audit: compare your current revision notes and question bank against the updated content map, and specifically check coverage of the four newly integrated topic areas (transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance, social determinants of health) and the expanded women's health section. These are the areas most likely to contain genuine gaps rather than simply lighter treatment, precisely because they represent either new inclusions or a more granular breakdown of previously grouped content.

If You Use Question Banks or Flashcard Decks

Because the content map expansion changes what is examinable, any revision resource — a commercial question bank, a locally produced set of revision notes, or a flashcard deck — needs to reflect the new 430-condition map to remain fully current. This is particularly relevant for spaced-repetition tools, since flashcard decks are often built once and used over an extended period without systematic review. If you are relying on a flashcard-based revision system, check specifically whether the deck's card set has been reviewed and expanded since October 2025 to reflect the updated content areas, rather than assuming a well-regarded deck is automatically current simply because it was comprehensive under the old map.

If You Are Preparing Intensively Close to Your Sitting

Candidates in an intensive, compressed preparation window — for example, those following a structured short-course approach in the months before sitting — should confirm directly with their course provider or preparation programme that course materials have been updated to reflect the 2026 content map, particularly if the sitting date falls at or after the September 2026 transition point. An intensive revision window leaves little room to discover content gaps late in the process, so this verification step is worth doing early rather than assuming currency.

Cross-Context Practice, Not Just Content Breadth

Given the removal of the specialty mapping grid, revision plans should also build in deliberate cross-context practice — working through practice questions that test the same underlying condition or presentation framed in different clinical settings, rather than only revising specialty-by-specialty in strict silos. This directly addresses the structural change discussed earlier and is arguably as important as absorbing the expanded conditions list itself.

How UK Medical Schools Are Expected to Respond

UK medical schools do not set the content map independently — they build their curricula to prepare students for the GMC's standard, and the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes require corresponding curriculum adjustments at the institutional level across the country. Schools with strong existing processes for tracking GMC content map revisions are generally well placed to update teaching schedules, clinical placements, and internal assessment content to reflect the expanded scope, particularly around the newly integrated topic areas. For context on how individual UK medical schools generally structure their approach to delivering MLA-aligned teaching and internal assessment, it is worth understanding how institutions build the MLA framework into their programmes more broadly, since the same institutional mechanisms that handled the original MLA rollout will be responsible for incorporating this update into curricula going forward.

Students should not assume, however, that their institution's teaching will automatically and immediately reflect every element of the updated content map on the exact date it takes effect. Curriculum change takes time to implement fully, and there may be a transitional period during which formal teaching lags slightly behind the GMC's published content map. This is precisely why independent verification against the GMC's own materials — rather than relying solely on institutional teaching schedules — remains good practice for any candidate serious about full coverage.

Common Misconceptions About the 2026 Syllabus Changes

Several misunderstandings about the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes tend to circulate whenever a major content map revision is announced, and it is worth addressing them directly given how consequential a wrong assumption could be for revision planning.

"The Exam Itself Is Becoming Harder or Longer"

This is not accurate. Among the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, the exam format and structure are unchanged — the number of questions, the station format of the CPSA, and the overall assessment approach remain the same. What has changed is the scope of content that can be tested within that unchanged structure, which is a different thing from the exam becoming inherently harder or longer.

"430 Conditions Means I Must Memorise Exactly 430 Discrete Entries"

As covered above, the GMC explicitly describes the list as indicative and non-exhaustive. This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes: treating the list as a rigid, closed checklist misses the point of the underlying clinical reasoning the exam is designed to test, and risks a narrow, rote-memorisation approach that performs poorly against applied clinical scenarios.

"This Only Affects IMGs, Not UK Students, or Vice Versa"

Both assumptions are wrong. The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes apply to all MLA exams and assessments from September 2026 — both the AKT and CPSA sat by UK medical students and, from the same date, PLAB sittings taken by IMGs. This is precisely the convergence discussed earlier: a single content map now governs assessment regardless of route to licensure.

"If I Already Passed Part of the Assessment, I Need to Re-Sit Under the New Map"

This is also incorrect. The UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes apply prospectively — they govern which map applies to assessments sat from September 2026 onwards. They do not retroactively affect the validity of a component you have already passed under the original content map. If you have already passed the AKT, for example, that result stands; only your remaining components, if sat from September 2026, fall under the updated map.

A Practical Checklist for the Transition Period

Bringing the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes together into an actionable plan, candidates navigating the transition to the updated content map should work through the following checklist as a starting point, always cross-referencing against the GMC's own published materials for the most current detail.

  • Confirm which content map applies to each remaining component of your pathway, based on the actual sitting date rather than the date you started revising.
  • Audit your existing question bank, notes, and flashcard decks for a review date — anything last substantially updated before October 2025 should be checked carefully against the new topic areas.
  • Specifically review the four newly integrated topic areas: transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance, and social determinants of health.
  • Give women's health a dedicated review pass, since it represents the largest single content increase and involves conditions that may have previously been revised only as part of a broader grouped category.
  • Deliberately practise applying knowledge across different clinical contexts, reflecting the removal of the specialty mapping grid, rather than revising strictly specialty-by-specialty.
  • If you are an IMG on the PLAB route, confirm with your preparation provider that materials have been explicitly updated for the same content map now shared with UK medical students.
  • Periodically re-check the GMC's live content map page, since the GMC may issue further clarifications or revisions before or after the September 2026 transition date.

Frequently Asked Questions About the UKMLA Syllabus 2026 Changes

The following questions address the specific, practical points candidates most often raise about the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes, phrased as they are typically searched.

When do the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes actually take effect?

The updated MLA content map applies to all MLA exams and assessments sat from September 2026 onwards. If you sit any part of your pathway up to and including August 2026, the original, pre-2026 content map remains the valid reference document for that sitting. The specific date that matters is your actual exam date, not the date the update was published (October 2025) or the date you began revising.

How many conditions are in the updated 2026 content map?

The updated content map lists approximately 430 named conditions, up from approximately 311 in the previous version. The GMC describes this list as indicative and non-exhaustive, meaning it is not a strict, closed ceiling on everything that could be examined, but rather a structured guide to the scope of examinable content.

Does the removal of the specialty mapping grid mean questions are now unpredictable?

Not unpredictable, but broader in scope. Previously, a mapping grid restricted which conditions could be tested within specific specialty or clinical contexts. That grid has been removed from September 2026, meaning any condition can now be tested in any specialty setting. This rewards candidates who can apply clinical reasoning flexibly across contexts, rather than those who have only prepared for conditions appearing in their traditionally associated specialty framing.

Do PLAB candidates need to worry about the 2026 content map changes?

Yes, directly. From September 2026, PLAB sittings are assessed against the same updated content map used for the UKMLA, completing the convergence between the two pathways. IMGs should treat this update with the same seriousness as UK medical students and should specifically verify that their PLAB preparation materials have been updated to reflect the expanded content and the new topic areas.

I am already mid-revision using older materials — do I need to start over?

No, but you should conduct a targeted gap audit rather than starting from scratch. Retain the foundation you have already built, then specifically review the newly integrated topic areas (transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance, social determinants of health) and the expanded women's health section, since these are the areas most likely to be genuinely under-covered by pre-2026 materials.

Is the exam format changing along with the content?

No. The exam format and structure are not changing — the AKT and CPSA retain their existing structure and assessment approach. Only the scope of examinable content has changed. Candidates should not expect a different question format, station structure, or overall assessment philosophy.

Where can I find the official, current version of the content map?

The authoritative source is the GMC's own content map page, which the GMC updates directly and which should always be checked before finalising a revision timetable, since the GMC may revise the map further. The GMC has also published the full content map as a downloadable PDF document, which can be used alongside the live page for detailed, systematic revision planning.

Conclusion

Taken together, the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes mark a genuine turning point in how the exam's content scope is defined, not just an incremental update. The expansion from roughly 311 to 430 named conditions, the removal of the specialty mapping grid, the formal integration of transgender health, genetics, updated sepsis guidance and social determinants of health, and the substantial deepening of women's health content all apply to every MLA exam and assessment sat from September 2026 onwards — while the original content map remains valid for anything sat up to and including August 2026. For IMGs, the update also completes the long-running convergence between PLAB and the UKMLA onto a single shared standard.

What has not changed across the UKMLA syllabus 2026 changes is just as important: the exam format and structure remain the same, so your existing exam technique and familiarity with how the AKT and CPSA work continues to apply. The task ahead is to update the content scope of your revision, not to relearn how the exam works. Because the GMC describes the updated list as indicative and non-exhaustive and retains the ability to revise it further, always check the GMC's live content map page for the current version before you finalise a revision timetable, rather than relying on any single secondary summary — including this guide.
