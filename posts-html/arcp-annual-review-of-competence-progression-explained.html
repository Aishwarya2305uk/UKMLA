The Annual Review of Competence Progression — almost universally shortened to ARCP — is the formal, typically once-a-year process by which a doctor in a UK postgraduate training programme has their progress checked against the requirements of their curriculum for that stage. Whether a doctor reached that training post as a UK medical graduate moving through Foundation Year 1 and Foundation Year 2 into specialty or GP training, or as an international medical graduate (IMG) who sat the UKMLA, secured GMC registration and then competed for a training number, the same underlying review sits at the centre of every training year: a panel looks at a portfolio of evidence and decides whether that year's training has been satisfactorily completed. Understanding how the Annual Review of Competence Progression actually works — not just that it exists — is one of the most practically useful things a trainee can do in their first few months in post, because by the time the formal review itself arrives, most of the work that determines its outcome has already been done or left undone months earlier.

This guide sets out what the Annual Review of Competence Progression actually reviews, what kind of evidence Annual Review of Competence Progression panels expect to see, the broad categories of outcome a trainee can receive, how those outcomes eventually feed into the award of a Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT), and — most usefully — what a trainee can do throughout the year to walk into their review with a strong, low-stress portfolio rather than a last-minute scramble. It is written for both audiences who encounter this process: UK graduates moving through Foundation and then specialty or GP training, and IMGs who have joined a UK training programme via the standard national recruitment routes. Wherever the two groups' experience of the process differs in a meaningful way, this guide says so explicitly rather than leaving either group to guess which parts apply to them.

Quick Answer: What Is the Annual Review of Competence Progression?

The Annual Review of Competence Progression is the formal, typically annual process by which a doctor's training progress is reviewed against the curriculum requirements for their current training stage, based on a portfolio of evidence built up over the year. A panel — usually convened by the trainee's deanery, local education and training board, or the relevant NHS England-successor body responsible for postgraduate medical education in that part of the UK — considers this evidence and issues a formal outcome. That outcome determines whether the trainee progresses to the next training year, needs additional time or support to meet the requirements, or, in more serious cases, is referred into a process that considers whether they should remain in the training programme at all.

The review is deliberately built around evidence the trainee has already generated during ordinary clinical practice, rather than a fresh assessment sat on the day itself. That evidence typically includes structured entries in the trainee's e-portfolio, completed workplace-based assessments, records of relevant courses and examinations passed, and supporting documents such as the educational supervisor's structured report. Because the panel is reviewing a year's accumulated evidence rather than testing the trainee afresh, the single most important determinant of a smooth Annual Review of Competence Progression is not a burst of preparation in the final weeks before the review, but consistent, honest record-keeping across the whole training year.

What the Annual Review of Competence Progression Actually Reviews

At its core, the Annual Review of Competence Progression exists to answer one question for each trainee, once a year: has this doctor progressed through their curriculum at the expected rate, with the expected level of competence, based on the evidence available? Everything about the process — the timing, the panel composition, the documents requested — flows from that single underlying purpose. It is worth trainees, especially those new to UK postgraduate training, understanding this framing early, because it reshapes how the whole process should be approached. The review is not primarily a test of how well a trainee performs in a single meeting; it is a structured audit of a year's worth of already-generated evidence.

Curriculum Requirements as the Yardstick

Every UK training programme, whether Foundation, a medical or surgical specialty, or general practice, is built around a curriculum published by the relevant Royal College or, for Foundation training, the UK Foundation Programme Office. That curriculum sets out the capabilities, knowledge and practical competencies a trainee is expected to demonstrate by particular points in their training. An ARCP panel's central task is to check the trainee's portfolio against that specific curriculum, for that specific stage of training — not against a generic standard of "good doctoring," but against the concrete, published requirements attached to the training year the trainee has just completed. This is why the exact expectations reviewed at an ARCP differ meaningfully between, say, a Foundation Year 1 doctor, a core surgical trainee, and a GP registrar in their final year — each is being measured against a different curriculum document, even though the review format looks broadly similar across all three.

For a trainee preparing for their first Annual Review of Competence Progression, the practical implication is straightforward: the curriculum document for the current training year is not background reading to skim once and forget — it is the actual checklist the panel will use. Trainees who periodically map their accumulated evidence back against the specific capabilities listed in their curriculum, rather than relying on a vague sense that "things are going fine," tend to identify gaps early enough to close them, rather than discovering a gap for the first time when the panel does.

The Role of the Educational Supervisor

Each trainee is assigned a named educational supervisor for the training year — usually a senior doctor working in the same specialty or a closely related one — whose role includes overseeing the trainee's progress, meeting with them at defined points across the year, and producing a structured educational supervisor's report ahead of the formal review that eventually becomes part of the Annual Review of Competence Progression. This report is one of the most influential single documents in the entire portfolio, because it represents the considered judgement of someone who has actually worked with and observed the trainee across the year, rather than a panel encountering the trainee's evidence cold. A supervisor's report that clearly, honestly and specifically addresses the trainee's progress against the curriculum carries far more weight than a portfolio of raw assessment scores with no narrative context around them.

Because the supervisor's report matters so much, the relationship between trainee and educational supervisor deserves active management throughout the year, not just at the two or three meetings that are formally mandated. A trainee who meets their supervisor only at the minimum required checkpoints, and who arrives at each meeting without having reflected on their own progress in advance, gets far less out of the relationship than one who treats the educational supervisor as an ongoing resource — someone to flag concerns to early, to ask for informal feedback from, and to involve in shaping the coming months' learning objectives.

Who Sits on the Panel and What They Look For

The ARCP panel is typically convened by the trainee's deanery or the relevant NHS England-successor body responsible for postgraduate medical education in that nation or region of the UK, and usually includes a training programme director, one or more senior trainers from the relevant specialty, and often a lay representative. The panel's task is deliberately evidence-based rather than interview-based: in most straightforward cases, the trainee does not appear before the panel in person at all — the panel reviews the completed portfolio, the educational supervisor's report, and any other required documentation, and reaches a decision based on what is in front of them. A trainee is more likely to be asked to attend in person, or have a more detailed conversation with the panel, where the evidence is incomplete, where there are specific concerns to explore, or where an unsatisfactory outcome is being considered.

This evidence-first structure is precisely why portfolio quality throughout the year matters so much more than performance on the day of the review itself. A trainee cannot straightforwardly "have a good day" in front of an ARCP panel the way they might perform well in an objective structured clinical assessment such as the UKMLA's CPSA — because in most cases there is no live, on-the-day performance for the panel to observe at all. What the panel sees is what was recorded, honestly and consistently, across the preceding months.

The Portfolio of Evidence: What Panels Actually Review

The portfolio a trainee presents to their Annual Review of Competence Progression is built from several distinct categories of evidence, most of which accumulate naturally through ordinary clinical work if the trainee keeps their records up to date as they go. Understanding each category — and, crucially, understanding roughly how much of each a panel expects to see by the point of review — is the foundation of good preparation.

E-Portfolio Entries

Every UK training programme now runs on an electronic portfolio system, in which trainees log their clinical activity, reflections, and supporting evidence throughout the year. The specific platform varies by specialty and nation, but the underlying purpose is consistent: to create a running, timestamped, reviewable record of a trainee's development that a panel can inspect months later without needing the trainee to reconstruct it from memory. Readers who are earlier in their training journey, or who are still preparing for the exams that lead into a training post, may find it useful to read a companion guide covering the NHS e-portfolio system in more detail before their first placement begins, since familiarity with the platform itself reduces one avoidable source of last-minute stress.

E-portfolio entries typically include reflective pieces on clinical encounters, records of teaching delivered or attended, logs of procedures performed, and structured entries linking specific pieces of evidence back to the named curriculum capabilities they demonstrate. A portfolio built up steadily across the year — a handful of entries added most weeks, rather than dozens added in the final fortnight — reads very differently to a panel than one where the timestamps cluster suspiciously close to the review deadline. Panels are experienced at spotting the difference, and a portfolio that shows genuine, ongoing engagement with reflective practice is treated as stronger evidence than one that merely ticks the same boxes at the last minute.

Workplace-Based Assessments

Workplace-based assessments (WBAs) are structured, in-the-moment assessments completed by a senior colleague — often a consultant, sometimes a more senior trainee — observing or discussing a specific piece of clinical work with the trainee. Common formats include direct observation of a clinical procedure or consultation, case-based discussions in which the trainee talks a senior colleague through their clinical reasoning on a real case, and multi-source feedback exercises gathering structured input from a range of colleagues who have worked alongside the trainee. Each curriculum specifies a minimum expected number and type of these assessments across the training year, and the Annual Review of Competence Progression panel checks not just that the minimum number exists, but that they are spread across the year and across different clinical contexts, rather than being clustered into a handful of assessment days that do not reflect ordinary practice.

The most common, avoidable failure mode here is leaving workplace-based assessments until the final weeks before the review, then asking multiple senior colleagues to complete several assessments in a compressed window. Beyond the practical difficulty of finding enough willing assessors in a short window, assessments completed this way tend to be rushed, superficial, and less genuinely reflective of the trainee's rounded performance across the year — exactly the pattern an experienced panel is trained to notice. Assessments spaced naturally across the year, arising from real clinical encounters as they happen, are both easier to arrange and more credible as evidence.

Courses, Examinations and Other Supporting Evidence

Alongside e-portfolio entries and workplace-based assessments, panels expect to see evidence of relevant courses completed, mandatory examinations passed where the curriculum requires them at that stage, and any other supporting evidence relevant to the trainee's specific circumstances — for example, evidence relating to a period of less-than-full-time training, a period of leave, or a documented reasonable adjustment. Trainees who are also working through the Multi-Specialty Recruitment Assessment as part of applying into or progressing within certain specialty routes should keep a clear, dated record of that process too, since recruitment milestones can sometimes need to be referenced alongside the evidence reviewed at the Annual Review of Competence Progression, particularly around transitions between training stages.

Examination results deserve particular care because many training programmes attach explicit deadlines to specific postgraduate examinations — a trainee who has not passed a required examination by a defined point in training is very likely to receive an unsatisfactory outcome regardless of how strong the rest of their portfolio is, since the examination requirement is usually a hard curriculum gate rather than a general expectation. Trainees should know their own curriculum's examination deadlines from the very start of a training stage, not discover them only when a deadline is imminent.

ARCP Outcome Categories: What Can Happen at the Review

Once the panel has reviewed the portfolio, it issues a formal outcome. At a conceptual level, ARCP outcomes fall into a small number of broad categories, and understanding the shape of these categories — rather than memorising specific outcome codes — is what matters most for a trainee trying to understand what is at stake. Because the exact numbering and precise wording of outcome codes can be revised between editions of the governing guidance, this article deliberately does not quote specific outcome-code numbers; trainees should always check their deanery's or relevant NHS England-successor body's current published guidance, and the current edition of the Gold Guide, for the exact, up-to-date definitions that apply to their review.

A Satisfactory Outcome

The most common outcome, by a wide margin, for a trainee who has engaged consistently with their curriculum and kept their portfolio up to date across the year is a satisfactory one: the panel confirms that progress has been made at the expected rate, and the trainee moves forward into the next stage of training. This is the outcome the overwhelming majority of well-prepared trainees receive at their Annual Review of Competence Progression, and it should be understood as the expected, default result of a training year handled attentively — not as a rare achievement reserved for exceptional trainees. A trainee who keeps their e-portfolio current, attends their scheduled supervisor meetings, completes workplace-based assessments across the year rather than in a last-minute burst, and raises any concerns as they arise rather than sitting on them, gives themselves the best possible chance of a straightforward, satisfactory review.

Outcomes Requiring Additional Training Time

Where a panel finds that some curriculum requirements have not yet been met, but the gap is addressable, a common category of unsatisfactory outcome involves being given additional time — sometimes described as an extension to the current training period — to complete the outstanding requirements, often accompanied by a clear, specific list of what remains to be achieved and by when. This is not, in itself, a career-ending outcome, and trainees who receive it should not treat it as a personal or professional catastrophe: it is, in essence, the panel's mechanism for saying "you are not quite there yet, but you can get there with defined additional time and support." What matters most for a trainee in this position is engaging fully and promptly with whatever plan accompanies the outcome, rather than treating the extra time as an extension of the same pattern of behaviour that produced the shortfall in the first place.

Outcomes Involving a Remediation Plan

A related category of unsatisfactory Annual Review of Competence Progression outcome involves the panel setting a structured remediation plan — a defined set of actions, support and, usually, a shorter-interval review to check progress against specific objectives before the next full annual review. Remediation plans are typically used where the panel has identified specific, describable concerns (for example, a particular clinical skill needing further development, or a pattern of incomplete assessments) rather than a broad, undifferentiated sense that things are not going well. Because remediation plans are specific by design, a trainee who receives one should read it closely, ask their educational supervisor and, where appropriate, their training programme director to clarify anything ambiguous, and treat the plan as the single clearest description available of exactly what "satisfactory" will look like at the next review.

More Serious Outcomes and Referral Processes

In a smaller number of Annual Review of Competence Progression cases, where a panel has significant, unresolved concerns about a trainee's progress, engagement or, in rare cases, fitness to practise, the outcome can involve referral into a more formal process — potentially including a review of whether the trainee should continue in the training programme at all. These outcomes are relatively uncommon and are generally reserved for situations involving either a sustained, unaddressed pattern of underperformance despite earlier support, or serious concerns that go beyond straightforward gaps in curriculum coverage. A trainee facing this kind of outcome, or genuinely worried they might be heading towards one, should seek advice promptly — from their educational supervisor, their training programme director, their medical or dental defence organisation, and, where relevant, the British Medical Association or an equivalent professional body — rather than facing the process without support.

Because the exact definitions, numbering and procedural detail attached to each outcome category can and do change between editions of the governing guidance, and can also vary slightly by nation within the UK, trainees should always treat their deanery's or the relevant NHS England-successor body's current published guidance, together with the current edition of the Gold Guide, as the authoritative source for the exact outcome definitions that apply to their specific review — not a summary written for general orientation such as this one.

Why the Outcome Categories Exist at All

It is worth pausing on why the Annual Review of Competence Progression uses this structured, categorised system of outcomes in the first place, rather than a simple pass/fail judgement. Training a doctor safely and well takes years, and progress is rarely perfectly uniform — a trainee can be excelling in most areas of a curriculum while genuinely needing more time in one specific domain, or can encounter a difficult year for reasons entirely outside their control, such as illness, caring responsibilities, or a period of less-than-full-time training. A graduated system of outcomes, ranging from straightforwardly satisfactory through to more structured forms of support, allows the training system to respond proportionately to genuinely different situations, rather than forcing every trainee whose year did not go perfectly into the same binary outcome as a trainee with serious, unaddressed concerns. This proportionality is, in the end, a patient-safety feature as much as a trainee-welfare one: it allows problems to be identified and addressed early, in a structured way, rather than either being ignored until they become serious or triggering an outcome disproportionate to the actual concern.

How ARCP Outcomes Feed Into the Certificate of Completion of Training

For trainees on a run-through or fixed-length specialty or GP training programme, the sequence of Annual Review of Competence Progression outcomes across the full length of training is what ultimately determines eligibility for the Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT) — the document confirming that a doctor has completed an approved UK training programme and is eligible to be added to the GMC's specialist or GP register. Put simply, a satisfactory outcome at the trainee's final ARCP, following a run of satisfactory (or appropriately remediated) outcomes across every preceding year, is the trigger that allows the relevant Royal College and the GMC to process the CCT award. A companion guide covering what the Certificate of Completion of Training actually involves sets out that end-of-programme process in detail; the point worth emphasising here is simply that CCT is not a separate, disconnected event bolted onto the end of training — it is the direct downstream consequence of a trainee's accumulated run of ARCP outcomes, stage by stage, across the whole programme.

This connection is exactly why a single difficult training year need not be catastrophic for a trainee's overall trajectory, provided it is handled properly at the time. An outcome requiring additional time, addressed promptly and fully, simply extends the pathway to CCT by the additional period required — it does not, by itself, prevent CCT from eventually being awarded. What genuinely jeopardises the pathway to CCT is not a single difficult year, but an unaddressed, worsening pattern across multiple years, or a failure to engage with the support attached to an earlier unsatisfactory outcome. Trainees sometimes assume, incorrectly, that any unsatisfactory outcome represents a permanent black mark; in practice, the training system is explicitly designed to allow recovery from an isolated difficult year, provided the trainee engages fully with whatever plan the panel sets.

The Cumulative Nature of the Training Record

Because each year's ARCP outcome sits within a cumulative training record that follows the trainee through to their eventual CCT application, it is worth trainees understanding that the record being built is not discarded at the end of each training year — it persists, and can be referenced again at a later review if a pattern needs to be understood in context. This is another reason honest, accurate portfolio-keeping throughout every year matters more than managing the appearance of any single review in isolation: a portfolio that reflects genuine, ongoing engagement across the whole training programme, including honest documentation of any difficulties encountered and how they were addressed, tends to serve a trainee far better over the full length of a training programme than one where problems were minimised or hidden in any single year's review.

Practical Advice for Preparing a Strong ARCP Portfolio

Almost everything that determines whether an Annual Review of Competence Progression goes smoothly happens months before the formal review itself. The advice in this section is deliberately practical and can be adopted from the very first week of a training post, regardless of specialty or nation.

Keep the E-Portfolio Continuously Updated

The single highest-leverage habit a trainee can build is treating e-portfolio entries as a routine part of clinical work, completed within days of the relevant event, rather than a backlog to be cleared in the weeks before the review. A short, honest reflective entry written the same week as the clinical encounter it describes is both easier to write and more credible to a panel than a longer entry reconstructed from memory months later. Trainees who block out a small, protected slot of time most weeks — even fifteen or twenty minutes — specifically for portfolio maintenance tend to arrive at their review with a complete, evenly distributed body of evidence, rather than facing the realisation, a fortnight before the deadline, that several months of entries are still outstanding.

Seek Regular Educational Supervisor Meetings

Beyond the minimum number of formal meetings a curriculum requires ahead of the Annual Review of Competence Progression, trainees benefit from treating their educational supervisor as an ongoing resource throughout the year, not a checkpoint to be passed at fixed intervals. Requesting an additional, informal check-in when something is unclear, when a rotation has been unusually challenging, or simply to get a senior colleague's read on overall progress costs little and can surface concerns — on either side — early enough to address them constructively. A trainee who has had several honest conversations with their educational supervisor across the year gives that supervisor far more to work with when it comes to writing a specific, well-evidenced structured report, compared with a trainee who has been largely invisible outside the mandatory minimum contact.

Address Concerns Early Rather Than Waiting for the Formal Review

Perhaps the single most consequential piece of practical advice for anyone preparing for the Annual Review of Competence Progression is this: if a trainee becomes aware of a genuine concern — a gap in a required assessment type, a rotation that has not provided enough exposure to a particular curriculum domain, a personal circumstance affecting capacity to train — the right time to raise it is as soon as it becomes apparent, not at the formal review itself. Training programmes have far more flexibility to adjust a placement, arrange additional targeted experience, or put reasonable support in place with several months' notice than they do in the final weeks before a review, when most of the year's opportunities to generate fresh evidence have already passed. Raising a concern early is not an admission of failure; it is, in effect, exactly the kind of proactive, reflective behaviour the whole system is designed to reward.

Treat Workplace-Based Assessments as Genuine Learning Opportunities

Workplace-based assessments work best, both as evidence and as a genuine developmental tool, when they are requested naturally as part of ordinary clinical work rather than being batch-processed in a rushed final push. Trainees who ask a supervising colleague to complete an assessment shortly after a relevant clinical encounter — while the detail is still fresh for both parties — tend to receive more specific, useful feedback than trainees requesting the same assessment retrospectively, weeks later, from memory. Building this into routine practice, rather than treating assessments purely as a box-ticking exercise for the ARCP, also tends to produce better clinical development overall, since the feedback loop between doing the work and reflecting on it stays tight.

Map Progress Against the Curriculum Periodically, Not Just Once

Rather than waiting until shortly before the Annual Review of Competence Progression to check accumulated evidence against curriculum requirements, trainees benefit from doing this mapping exercise several times across the year — for example, at the point of each formal supervisor meeting. This turns curriculum-checking into an ongoing habit rather than a single, high-stakes audit performed under time pressure, and it means any gaps identified are found with enough of the training year still remaining to address them through ordinary clinical exposure, additional targeted experience, or a conversation with the educational supervisor about how best to close them.

Understand How Working Patterns and Post Type Interact With Training Evidence

Trainees should also be aware that the working arrangements around a post can shape how straightforward it is to generate certain kinds of ARCP evidence. Doctors working patterns compliant with the working-time and rest-rules framework that governs doctors' hours should still ensure that rota patterns leave realistic time for portfolio maintenance and supervisor meetings, rather than assuming these will simply happen around a demanding schedule. Similarly, trainees considering how locum and substantive NHS posts differ should understand that formal ARCP evidence is generated through substantive training posts within an approved training programme, not through locum work undertaken outside that structure — a distinction worth understanding clearly before assuming that any clinical work automatically counts towards training progression.

Know Where to Find the Authoritative, Current Rules

Because outcome definitions, exact evidence requirements and procedural detail are periodically revised, trainees should treat this article as an orientation to the shape and logic of the process, and always check their own deanery's or relevant NHS England-successor body's current guidance — together with the current edition of the Gold Guide, published by the Conference of Postgraduate Medical Deans — for the specific, up-to-date rules that apply to their training programme and nation. Trainees who are earlier in the pipeline, still working towards GMC registration itself, will encounter ARCP only once they are established in a training post, but understanding the process in advance — as part of general orientation to how UK postgraduate training works — helps set realistic expectations about what training in the UK actually involves once registration and recruitment are behind them.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Annual Review of Competence Progression?

The Annual Review of Competence Progression is the formal, typically annual process by which a doctor in a UK training programme has their progress reviewed against their curriculum's requirements for that stage, based on a portfolio of evidence including e-portfolio entries, workplace-based assessments and an educational supervisor's report. A panel reviews this evidence and issues a formal outcome, which determines whether the trainee progresses to the next training year.

How often does ARCP happen?

ARCP is typically conducted once per training year for most trainees, though trainees working less than full time, those with performance or progression concerns, or those requesting accelerated progression may have reviews scheduled more frequently. Exact scheduling is set by the trainee's deanery or the relevant NHS England-successor body and can vary by specialty and nation, so trainees should confirm the specific timetable that applies to their own programme.

What evidence do I need for my ARCP?

Trainees typically need up-to-date e-portfolio entries, a sufficient number and range of completed workplace-based assessments spread across the training year, evidence of any required courses or examinations passed, a structured report from their educational supervisor, and any relevant supporting documentation such as records of leave or less-than-full-time training arrangements. The exact requirements are set by the specific curriculum for the trainee's specialty and stage, so trainees should check their own curriculum document directly rather than assuming requirements are identical across specialties.

What happens if I get an unsatisfactory ARCP outcome?

An unsatisfactory outcome generally results in either additional training time being granted to complete outstanding requirements, or a structured remediation plan with a defined set of actions and a shorter-interval follow-up review. In a smaller number of more serious cases, an unsatisfactory outcome can lead to referral into a more formal process. The exact outcome definitions are set out in the trainee's deanery guidance and the current edition of the Gold Guide, and trainees facing an unsatisfactory outcome should seek support promptly from their educational supervisor, training programme director, and, where appropriate, their defence organisation.

Does an unsatisfactory ARCP outcome stop me getting a CCT?

An isolated unsatisfactory outcome, addressed properly through the additional time or remediation plan set by the panel, does not by itself prevent an eventual Certificate of Completion of Training — it typically extends the pathway by the additional time required to meet the outstanding requirements. What genuinely jeopardises eventual CCT award is an unresolved, worsening pattern across multiple years or a failure to engage with the support offered after an earlier unsatisfactory outcome, not a single difficult training year handled properly.

Do IMGs go through the same ARCP process as UK graduates?

Yes — once an international medical graduate has secured a place on a UK specialty or GP training programme, they go through the same Annual Review of Competence Progression process, against the same curriculum, as a UK graduate at the equivalent training stage. The process itself does not differ by how a trainee entered the training programme; what can differ is the point at which a trainee first encounters it, since IMGs typically enter after GMC registration and a period of successful recruitment via national selection processes.

How can I prepare for a strong ARCP outcome?

The most effective preparation is continuous rather than concentrated: keep e-portfolio entries current throughout the year rather than compiling them shortly before the review, request workplace-based assessments naturally as part of ordinary clinical work, meet regularly and honestly with your educational supervisor, and raise any concerns about gaps in training experience as soon as they become apparent rather than waiting for the formal review to surface them.

Where can I find the exact ARCP outcome definitions that apply to me?

The authoritative source for exact, current outcome definitions is the current edition of the Gold Guide, published by the Conference of Postgraduate Medical Deans, together with your own deanery's or relevant NHS England-successor body's published guidance for your specific specialty and nation. Because outcome codes and procedural detail are periodically revised, always check the current published version rather than relying on general summaries, including this one.

Conclusion

To summarise, the Annual Review of Competence Progression is best understood not as a single high-stakes event but as the formal, once-a-year audit of a much longer, continuous process of training, reflection and supervision. Trainees who keep their e-portfolio genuinely current, engage regularly and honestly with their educational supervisor, and raise concerns as soon as they arise rather than waiting for the formal review, give themselves the strongest possible chance of a straightforward, satisfactory outcome year after year — and, over the full length of a training programme, that steady accumulation of satisfactory outcomes is precisely what carries a trainee through to their eventual Certificate of Completion of Training. For doctors still working towards the exams and registration that lead into a UK training post in the first place, understanding this process now is a useful part of planning realistically for the years of training that follow.
