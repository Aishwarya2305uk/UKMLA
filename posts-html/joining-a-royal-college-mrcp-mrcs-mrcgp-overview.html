Joining a Royal College after UKMLA is the milestone that comes into view once a foundation doctor — whether a UK graduate or an international medical graduate who entered training directly — starts looking beyond day-to-day clinical work towards a recognised specialty career. Passing the UKMLA and securing GMC registration opens the door to practising in the UK; completing the Foundation Programme, as covered in the site's guide to what the Foundation Programme involves after the UKMLA, builds the generalist clinical grounding every doctor needs. But neither of those steps, on its own, gets a doctor into a recognised medical or surgical specialty, or into general practice, on a permanent footing. That requires membership of one of the medical Royal Colleges — MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP being the three a foundation doctor is overwhelmingly most likely to hear about first.

This guide is deliberately an orientation, not a study guide. It will not walk through the exact number of papers in MRCP(UK) Part 2, the passmark for MRCS Part A, or the structure of the MRCGP's workplace-based assessment — those details change periodically, are set and published by the colleges themselves, and are genuinely out of scope for a site focused on the UKMLA. What this guide does instead is answer the questions that come up naturally at this stage of a career: what a Royal College membership actually is, why it matters, which of the three most common memberships applies to which kind of specialty, roughly when in a career each one becomes relevant, and how the choice of Royal College pathway is tied directly to the choice of specialty itself. For readers earlier in the pathway, the companion guide on foundation year vs specialty training for IMGs covers the step immediately before this one — whether an IMG enters the Foundation Programme at all or goes straight into specialty training — and is worth reading first if that decision has not yet been made.

Because joining a Royal College after UKMLA success is a genuinely different kind of milestone from the exam itself — a professional and career decision rather than a single pass/fail hurdle — this guide takes a wider, more explanatory approach than a typical UKMLA post. It is written for both audiences the site serves: UK medical graduates progressing through the Foundation Programme in the ordinary way, and IMGs who have passed the UKMLA and are now weighing up, often for the first time, what postgraduate membership actually means for their career in the UK.

Quick Answer: What Does Joining a Royal College After UKMLA Involve?

In short, joining a Royal College after UKMLA success means sitting and passing that college's postgraduate membership examination — a separate, specialty-specific qualification from the UKMLA — as part of progressing through UK specialty, surgical, or GP training. A Royal College membership is both a professional body membership and an academic postgraduate qualification: it confirms that a doctor has met a defined standard of specialty-level knowledge and clinical competence, and in most specialties it is a formal requirement for completing specialty training and being awarded a Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT) — a milestone explained in full in the site's separate guide to the Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT).

The three memberships a foundation doctor is most likely to encounter are MRCP(UK) (Membership of the Royal Colleges of Physicians of the United Kingdom, for medical specialties such as cardiology, respiratory medicine, gastroenterology, and general internal medicine), MRCS (Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons, for surgical specialties such as general surgery, trauma and orthopaedics, and ENT), and MRCGP (Membership of the Royal College of General Practitioners, for GP training). Each is tied to a different training pathway and a different specialty group, and — critically — a doctor typically only needs to pursue the one that matches the specialty they intend to enter, not all three.

  • MRCP(UK) is generally relevant to doctors heading into core medical training and onward into a physician-based medical specialty.
  • MRCS is generally relevant to doctors heading into core surgical training and onward into a surgical specialty.
  • MRCGP is generally relevant to doctors who have secured a place on GP specialty training and are working towards independent general practice.

The remainder of this guide expands on each of these three, explains why the choice of Royal College membership is inseparable from the choice of specialty, and sets out a realistic timeline for when this milestone typically comes into focus relative to the Foundation Programme and specialty training.

What a Royal College Membership Actually Is

Before comparing MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP individually, it is worth being clear about what a Royal College membership is in general terms, since the concept itself is often unfamiliar to doctors encountering it for the first time — particularly IMGs whose home training systems may not have a directly equivalent structure.

A Professional Body and an Academic Qualification, Combined

Each medical Royal College in the UK is, first and foremost, a professional membership body — an organisation that represents doctors within a particular branch of medicine, sets and maintains professional and educational standards for that branch, and provides ongoing professional development, guidance, and representation for its members throughout their careers. The Royal College of Physicians, the Royal College of Surgeons, and the Royal College of General Practitioners each perform this role for their respective specialty groupings, much as equivalent professional bodies exist in other countries, though the specific structures and names differ internationally.

Alongside this professional body function, each Royal College also sets and administers a postgraduate membership examination — MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP respectively — which functions as an academic and clinical competence qualification. Passing the relevant examination, and meeting the college's other membership requirements, confers the postgraduate letters (MRCP, MRCS, or MRCGP) that a doctor can then use after their name, and which signal to employers, patients, and other doctors that a defined specialty-level standard has been met. In other words, a Royal College membership is simultaneously a credential a doctor earns and an organisation a doctor joins — the two are bound together rather than being separate steps.

Why Royal College Membership Matters for Career Progression

In most UK medical and surgical specialty training pathways, and in GP training, passing the relevant Royal College membership examination is a formal requirement for progressing through specialty training and, ultimately, for being awarded a Certificate of Completion of Training. Without the relevant membership, a doctor in specialty training generally cannot progress to the more senior stages of that training programme, regardless of how strong their clinical performance is in other respects. This is why the membership examination is not an optional add-on for career-minded doctors — for the great majority of medical, surgical, and GP training pathways, it is a structural gateway that must be passed through, in the same way the UKMLA is a structural gateway into UK practice itself.

It is worth being explicit that a Royal College membership examination tests something meaningfully different from the UKMLA. The UKMLA is a generalist licensing assessment, confirming that a doctor can practise safely across the breadth of UK clinical practice at the point of first registration — a distinction explored in detail in the site's separate guide on UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors, which explains why even doctors who already hold an overseas specialty qualification still generally need to sit the UKMLA. A Royal College membership examination, by contrast, tests deep, focused knowledge and competence within a specific specialty area, at a stage of training well beyond first registration. The two assessments sit at different points in a doctor's career and serve different regulatory purposes; neither substitutes for the other.

Membership Is Not the Same as Full College Fellowship

It is also worth clarifying, briefly, a point of terminology that sometimes causes confusion: membership (MRCP, MRCS, MRCGP) is a distinct status from fellowship (FRCP, FRCS, and so on), which several colleges award later in a doctor's career, often by election or through a separate application process, as a mark of more senior standing within the profession. This guide is concerned specifically with the membership examinations relevant to doctors joining a Royal College after UKMLA success and Foundation Programme completion, since fellowship is a later-career milestone that sits well beyond the scope of a UKMLA-focused site.

MRCP(UK): Membership of the Royal Colleges of Physicians of the United Kingdom

MRCP(UK) is the shared postgraduate membership examination administered jointly by the Royal College of Physicians of London, the Royal College of Physicians of Edinburgh, and the Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Glasgow, under an umbrella body sometimes referred to as the Federation of the Royal Colleges of Physicians of the UK. It is relevant to doctors pursuing training in medical — as opposed to surgical — specialties.

Which Specialties MRCP(UK) Applies To

For doctors joining a Royal College after UKMLA success in a medicine-based specialty, MRCP(UK) is the qualification most closely associated with core medical training and internal medicine training, and onward progression into medical specialties such as cardiology, respiratory medicine, gastroenterology, renal medicine, endocrinology, neurology, rheumatology, geriatric medicine, and general internal medicine, among others. Broadly speaking, if a doctor's intended specialty sits within what is generally described as "physician-based" or "medical" practice — treating patients predominantly through non-surgical, medical management — MRCP(UK) is the membership examination most likely to apply to their training pathway. Some medical specialties additionally require a further specialty-specific examination on top of MRCP(UK) later in training, reflecting the additional depth expected at that more senior stage, though the specifics of any such additional examination are set by the relevant specialty training body rather than by MRCP(UK) itself.

General Purpose and Typical Timing

In general terms, MRCP(UK) becomes relevant once a doctor has completed the Foundation Programme and entered core medical training or internal medicine training — the training stage that follows FY2 for doctors heading towards a medical specialty. Doctors typically begin working towards MRCP(UK) during this stage of training, rather than during the Foundation Programme itself, since the examination assumes a level of clinical exposure and knowledge that builds on, rather than repeats, foundation-level competence. For doctors who entered training directly at specialty level rather than through the standard Foundation Programme route — a pathway discussed in the site's guide on foundation year vs specialty training for IMGs — the same general logic applies: MRCP(UK) becomes relevant once training has progressed to the point where medical specialty training itself has begun, rather than at the point of GMC registration.

Full membership of MRCP(UK) is, for the great majority of medical specialty training pathways, required before a doctor can progress into higher/registrar-level specialty training in a medical specialty. This guide deliberately does not set out the specific examination structure, number of parts, format, or passmark for MRCP(UK), since these are precisely the kind of detail that is set, published, and periodically reviewed by the Federation of the Royal Colleges of Physicians itself, and any doctor planning towards this examination should consult the current, authoritative detail directly via the Royal College of Physicians' official MRCP(UK) examination page rather than relying on a secondary summary that may become outdated.

MRCS: Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons

MRCS — Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons — is the postgraduate membership examination relevant to doctors pursuing surgical, rather than medical, specialty training. In the UK, the MRCS examination is intercollegiate, meaning it is set and recognised jointly across the UK's surgical Royal Colleges (including the Royal College of Surgeons of England, the Royal College of Surgeons of Edinburgh, the Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Glasgow, and the Royal College of Surgeons in Ireland for candidates training within its remit), rather than being administered by a single college in isolation.

Which Specialties MRCS Applies To

For doctors joining a Royal College after UKMLA success in a surgical specialty, MRCS is the qualification most closely associated with core surgical training and onward progression into surgical specialties such as general surgery, trauma and orthopaedic surgery, urology, otolaryngology (ENT), neurosurgery, plastic surgery, cardiothoracic surgery, paediatric surgery, and oral and maxillofacial surgery, among others. Some surgical specialties recognise a specialty-specific variant of the intercollegiate examination — for example, a version tailored to ENT training — reflecting the more focused clinical content relevant to that specific surgical field, though again, the exact structure of any such variant is a matter for the relevant college to publish and update, not something this guide attempts to summarise in detail.

General Purpose and Typical Timing

MRCS generally becomes relevant once a doctor has completed the Foundation Programme and entered core surgical training — the stage that follows FY2 for doctors heading towards a surgical specialty. As with MRCP(UK), doctors typically begin preparing for MRCS once they are established within core surgical training, rather than during the Foundation Programme itself, since the examination assumes clinical and operative exposure that builds on foundation-level experience. Passing MRCS is, for the great majority of surgical specialty training pathways, a requirement for progressing into higher/registrar-level surgical specialty training, in much the same structural role that MRCP(UK) plays for medical specialties.

For the full, current, and authoritative detail on MRCS — its structure, eligibility requirements, and examination diets — doctors should refer directly to the Royal College of Surgeons of England's official Intercollegiate MRCS page, since this guide is intentionally limited to explaining MRCS's general purpose and role rather than its detailed examination format.

MRCGP: Membership of the Royal College of General Practitioners

MRCGP — Membership of the Royal College of General Practitioners — is the postgraduate membership qualification specific to GP training in the UK. Unlike MRCP(UK) and MRCS, which sit within broader medical or surgical specialty training structures with multiple onward specialty destinations, MRCGP is tied to a single, defined training outcome: independent general practice.

Which Doctors MRCGP Applies To

For doctors joining a Royal College after UKMLA success with general practice in mind, MRCGP applies to doctors who have secured a place on a GP specialty training programme, generally entered after the Foundation Programme (or, for some IMGs, after an equivalent period of prior experience recognised for direct entry, as discussed in the companion guide on foundation year vs specialty training decisions for IMGs). GP training in the UK is itself a structured specialty training programme, typically lasting several years, that combines hospital-based placements with practice-based general practice training, and MRCGP sits within — rather than alongside — that programme as its formal assessment framework.

General Purpose and Typical Timing

MRCGP is generally described by the Royal College of General Practitioners as an integrated assessment system: rather than being a single, isolated examination sat once towards the end of training, it comprises multiple components assessed at different points across GP specialty training, together confirming that a trainee has satisfactorily met the standard required for independent general practice. Successful completion of all of MRCGP's components is required for a doctor completing GP specialty training to be awarded a Certificate of Completion of Training in general practice, and — critically for international readers — is the milestone that ultimately confers the licence to practise independently as a GP within the NHS.

Because MRCGP's components, structure, and requirements are reviewed and updated periodically by the Royal College of General Practitioners, this guide deliberately does not attempt to summarise its detailed assessment format. Doctors planning towards MRCGP should refer to the Royal College of General Practitioners' official MRCGP exams page for current, authoritative detail on eligibility, components, and timing.

This is precisely why the choice between them is not really a choice made in isolation at all, a point the next section explores directly.

Choosing a Royal College Pathway Is Choosing a Specialty

Perhaps the single most important point for a foundation doctor to understand about joining a Royal College after UKMLA success is that the decision is not really a standalone decision at all. A doctor does not typically choose MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP the way they might choose an elective module — the choice is a direct consequence of, and is made simultaneously with, the choice of specialty and specialty training pathway itself.

The Specialty Decision Comes First

In practice, a foundation doctor decides which broad specialty area they wish to pursue — medicine, surgery, general practice, or one of the other training routes not covered in detail by this guide — and applies for the corresponding specialty, core, or GP training programme through the national recruitment process. The Royal College membership that then becomes relevant follows automatically from that specialty choice: a doctor entering core medical training will be working towards MRCP(UK); a doctor entering core surgical training will be working towards MRCS; a doctor entering GP training will be working towards MRCGP. There is very little scope, once a specialty training programme has been entered, to substitute one Royal College membership pathway for another without effectively changing specialty altogether.

This has a practical implication worth stating plainly: the specialty-choice decision that foundation doctors are encouraged to start thinking about during FY2 — informed by rotation experience, personal interest, and realistic self-assessment of competitiveness within different specialties — is, in effect, also the Royal College membership decision. A doctor genuinely undecided between a medical and a surgical specialty during FY2 is, whether they frame it this way or not, also undecided between working towards MRCP(UK) and working towards MRCS, and would be well advised to treat the two questions as one and the same rather than deferring the membership question to a later date.

Some Specialties Sit Outside This Three-College Framework

It is worth being honest that MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP, while the three most commonly encountered by foundation doctors, do not cover every specialty training pathway in UK medicine. Other Royal Colleges and faculties administer their own membership examinations for their respective specialties — psychiatry, obstetrics and gynaecology, anaesthetics, radiology, paediatrics, pathology, ophthalmology, emergency medicine, and public health each have their own college or faculty and their own membership or fellowship examination, distinct from all three covered in detail here. This guide focuses specifically on MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP because they are the three a foundation doctor is statistically most likely to encounter first and ask about, given the size of medical, surgical, and GP training relative to other specialty groups — not because they are the only Royal College pathways that exist. A foundation doctor whose specialty interest lies outside medicine, surgery, or general practice should research the specific college or faculty relevant to their intended specialty directly, since the general principles in this guide — membership as a professional-body-plus-qualification, tied to specialty training progression — apply broadly across all of them, even though the specific examination names and colleges differ.

Why This Matters for Planning, Not Just for Exam Preparation

Understanding that the Royal College choice is inseparable from the specialty choice has practical consequences well before any membership examination preparation begins. A foundation doctor who is seriously undecided between two different broad specialty areas during FY2 should treat that indecision as reason to seek further exposure, career guidance, and honest self-reflection during the time genuinely available — rather than delaying the decision until a specialty training application deadline forces a choice. Since the relevant membership pathway only becomes clear once the specialty training application is submitted and a training number is secured, doctors benefit from resolving genuine uncertainty about specialty direction as early as realistically possible, rather than treating the Royal College membership question as something to defer entirely until training has already begun.

How This Fits Into the Wider Career Pathway After UKMLA

It is worth stepping back to place Royal College membership within the fuller sequence of milestones a doctor passes through after the UKMLA, since seeing the whole sequence helps make sense of why this particular milestone sits where it does.

The Sequence for UK Medical Graduates

For a UK medical graduate, the typical sequence runs: pass the UKMLA during final year, obtain provisional GMC registration, complete FY1 and gain full GMC registration, complete FY2 while deciding on a specialty direction, apply for and enter core medical training, core surgical training, or GP training, and then begin working towards the relevant Royal College membership examination — MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP — as an integral part of that specialty training programme. Full membership is generally required before progression to higher or registrar-level training, and successful completion of the whole training programme, including the relevant membership, leads eventually to the award of a CCT.

The Sequence for IMGs

For an IMG, the same broad sequence applies, but the entry point can differ depending on prior experience, as set out in detail in the companion guide on foundation year vs specialty training for IMGs. An IMG without prior postgraduate experience typically follows a path closely resembling the UK graduate's sequence, via a Trust Grade bridging period rather than the Foundation Programme itself, before entering specialty, core, or GP training and beginning work towards the relevant Royal College membership. An IMG with substantial, recognised prior specialist or GP experience may, in some cases, enter specialty or core training more directly — but even then, the relevant Royal College membership examination generally still applies, since it is a training-programme requirement tied to the specialty training itself, not something typically waived on the basis of prior overseas experience alone, in much the same way that the UKMLA itself is not waived for the great majority of experienced specialists, as explained in the site's guide on UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors.

Practical and Personal Considerations Alongside Career Planning

It is also worth acknowledging that this stage of a career often coincides with significant personal and family decisions for IMGs in particular — many doctors are, by the time they reach specialty training, considering whether and how to bring family members to the UK on a long-term basis. The site's separate guide on bringing family to the UK on a dependant visa covers this practical dimension in detail and is worth reading alongside career planning at this stage, since visa and family planning decisions often run in parallel with specialty and Royal College membership decisions rather than following neatly after them.

Timing Is Approximate and Varies by Individual Circumstance

Every timeline given for joining a Royal College after UKMLA success in this guide is necessarily general. Actual timing varies by specialty, by individual training programme, by whether a doctor is training full-time or less than full-time, and by any periods of research, career break, or out-of-programme experience a doctor takes during training. Doctors should treat every timing reference in this guide as an orientation to the general sequence of milestones, not a fixed personal timetable, and should consult their own training programme director, deanery, or the relevant Royal College directly for guidance specific to their own circumstances.

Preparing to Choose: Practical Steps for Foundation Doctors

While this guide deliberately does not cover examination preparation, it is worth setting out a few practical, non-exam-specific steps a foundation doctor can reasonably take while approaching this milestone.

Use FY2 Rotations Deliberately

FY2 rotations offer a foundation doctor one of the last structured opportunities to sample different specialty areas before a formal specialty choice is required. Doctors genuinely undecided between medicine, surgery, and general practice should use FY2 placements, taster weeks, and informal shadowing opportunities deliberately to gather first-hand evidence about which specialty area genuinely suits their interests and working style, rather than relying solely on assumptions formed during medical school.

Speak to Doctors Already on Each Pathway

Doctors who have already gone through joining a Royal College after UKMLA success, and who are currently working towards or already hold MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP, are generally the best source of first-hand, current guidance on what each pathway genuinely involves day to day — the working patterns, the training culture, and the realistic demands of preparing for the relevant membership examination alongside a full clinical workload. Speaking directly to trainees and trainers already on a given pathway is consistently more useful at this planning stage than general online research, since training experiences and college requirements can vary meaningfully by deanery and specialty.

Check Official College Guidance Directly, Not Second-Hand Summaries

Because each Royal College reviews and updates its own membership examination requirements, structure, and eligibility criteria periodically, any doctor seriously planning towards MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP should treat the relevant college's own official website as the authoritative source for current detail, rather than relying on forum posts, informal summaries, or guides — including this one — for anything beyond general orientation. This guide's purpose is to explain what these memberships are and how they fit into the broader career pathway, not to serve as a substitute for the colleges' own current, detailed guidance.

Do Not Rush the Specialty Decision to Avoid Discomfort

Because the Royal College membership choice is inseparable from the specialty choice, doctors sometimes feel pressure to settle on a specialty earlier than they are genuinely ready to, simply to reduce uncertainty. This is understandable but not necessarily advisable. A specialty decision made hastily, without adequate exposure or reflection, can lead to a doctor beginning specialty training — and working towards a specific Royal College membership — in a specialty that does not ultimately suit them, which is a considerably more costly outcome than taking a little longer to decide during FY2, or, where genuinely necessary, taking a deliberate F3 year to gain further clarity before committing.

Frequently Asked Questions About Joining a Royal College After UKMLA

Do I need to join a Royal College immediately after passing the UKMLA?

No. Royal College membership becomes relevant once a doctor has entered specialty, core, or GP training — a stage that comes after the UKMLA, GMC registration, and, for most UK graduates, completion of the Foundation Programme. There is no requirement, and generally no opportunity, to begin working towards MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP immediately upon passing the UKMLA itself.

Can I hold more than one Royal College membership after UKMLA?

In principle a doctor can hold more than one Royal College membership over the course of a career, particularly if their career path changes direction between specialty groups, but this is uncommon in practice, since most doctors work towards the single membership relevant to the specialty training programme they are actually following. Doctors considering a genuine change of specialty direction partway through training should seek guidance from their training programme director and the relevant college directly, since the practical and administrative implications vary by individual circumstance.

Is MRCP(UK), MRCS, or MRCGP harder than the UKMLA?

This is not a meaningful comparison, since the two types of assessment test fundamentally different things at fundamentally different career stages. The UKMLA is a generalist licensing assessment sat at the point of first GMC registration; MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP are specialty-specific postgraduate membership examinations sat well into specialty training, assuming a level of clinical exposure and knowledge the UKMLA does not assume. Doctors should prepare for each on its own terms rather than assuming performance in one predicts performance in the other.

Does passing the UKMLA count towards Royal College membership requirements?

No. The UKMLA and Royal College membership examinations are entirely separate assessments administered by different bodies for different purposes — the UKMLA by the GMC for licensing purposes, and MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP by their respective Royal Colleges for specialty membership purposes. Passing the UKMLA does not exempt a doctor from any part of a Royal College membership examination, and the two should not be treated as related steps in the same assessment process.

Do IMGs need to complete the UK Foundation Programme before working towards a Royal College membership?

Not necessarily. As explained in the companion guide on foundation year vs specialty training for IMGs, some IMGs with substantial, recognised prior specialist or GP experience may enter specialty, core, or GP training more directly, without completing the standard UK Foundation Programme. In these cases, the relevant Royal College membership becomes relevant once specialty training itself begins, in the same way it does for a doctor who has completed the Foundation Programme in the standard way.

Which Royal College should I choose if I am still undecided about my specialty?

There is no single answer, since the Royal College choice follows directly from the specialty choice rather than preceding it. A doctor genuinely undecided between medicine, surgery, and general practice should focus on resolving that underlying specialty decision — through further clinical exposure, career guidance, and honest self-reflection during FY2 or equivalent — rather than trying to choose a Royal College membership pathway in isolation from the specialty it is tied to.

Where can I find official guidance on joining a Royal College after UKMLA and Foundation training?

The authoritative source for each is the relevant college's own website: the Royal College of Physicians (jointly with the Royal Colleges of Physicians of Edinburgh and Glasgow) for MRCP(UK), the Royal College of Surgeons of England (and the other intercollegiate surgical colleges) for MRCS, and the Royal College of General Practitioners for MRCGP. Because examination structure, eligibility, and requirements are reviewed periodically, doctors should always check current detail directly with the relevant college rather than relying on a general overview.

Conclusion: Royal College Membership Is the Next Major Milestone, Not an Immediate One

Joining a Royal College after UKMLA success is best understood as the specialty-level milestone that follows the UKMLA, GMC registration, and the Foundation Programme (or its equivalent for IMGs entering training directly) — not a step that needs to be planned for in detail immediately after the exam itself. MRCP(UK), MRCS, and MRCGP each attach to a different specialty group — medicine, surgery, and general practice respectively — and the choice between them is, in practice, the same decision as choosing a specialty in the first place. Foundation doctors and IMGs alike are best served by using the time available during FY2 or equivalent to genuinely explore specialty options, speaking directly to doctors already on each pathway, and consulting the relevant Royal College's own official guidance once a specialty direction becomes clear, rather than treating this milestone as something to rush.
