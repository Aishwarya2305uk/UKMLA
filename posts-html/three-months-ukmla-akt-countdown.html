Three-Month UKMLA Study Plan: A Week-by-Week AKT and CPSA Preparation Schedule

The United Kingdom Medical Licensing Assessment is a high-stakes examination, and the difference between a structured twelve-week preparation and a rushed last-minute cram is not merely a matter of effort — it is a matter of how the brain encodes and retrieves medical knowledge. This guide provides a comprehensive, evidence-informed week-by-week plan covering both the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), with specific content targets, daily schedules, and condensed alternatives for candidates with less time available.

Why Twelve Weeks? The Evidence Behind the Timeline

Before diving into the plan itself, it is worth understanding why twelve weeks — not six, not two — is the optimal preparation window. The rationale is rooted in cognitive science, not convention.

The Spacing Effect and Ebbinghaus's Forgetting Curve

Hermann Ebbinghaus demonstrated in the nineteenth century that memory decays exponentially after a single learning event. Without review, roughly 50% of new information is forgotten within 24 hours, and up to 80% within a week. However, material reviewed across multiple spaced sessions — spaced repetition — is retained far better than the same volume of material studied in a single massed session. Twelve weeks allows you to return to cardiovascular pharmacology, for example, in week one, week five (via questions), and again in week eleven (via weak-area targeting) — three genuinely spaced encounters with the same material.

Interleaving: The Counterintuitive Advantage

Blocked practice — studying all of cardiology, then all of respiratory, then all of gastroenterology — feels efficient because performance within a session is high. But interleaving — mixing topics across sessions — produces superior long-term retention, even though it feels harder in the moment. A twelve-week plan naturally creates interleaving because week five's MSK questions will inevitably connect to week two's systemic inflammation knowledge, and week seven's haematological malignancies will loop back to week three's lymph node examination skills.

Retrieval Practice: Questions Beat Re-Reading

The testing effect is one of the most robust findings in educational psychology. Every time you retrieve information from memory, the memory trace strengthens. Reading your notes again does almost nothing to consolidate memory. Answering a single best answer (SBA) question about the same content creates a stronger, more durable memory trace — especially when you answer incorrectly, review the explanation, and are tested again later. This is why every phase of this plan front-loads active retrieval rather than passive content absorption.

Three Passes Through the Content Map

The MLA Content Map lists 430 core conditions across all systems. Twelve weeks gives you time to make three meaningful passes through this material:

  • First pass (Weeks 1–4): Content coverage — establishing the knowledge scaffolding for each system
  • Second pass (Weeks 5–8): Question-led active recall — testing what you actually know versus what you merely recognise on the page
  • Third pass (Weeks 9–11): Targeted weak-area sprint — addressing the gaps revealed by your mocks and question-bank analytics

The Danger of Starting Too Late

Candidates beginning preparation with fewer than six weeks remaining face a serious structural problem: there is only time for a single pass through the content map. This means no opportunity to consolidate, no time for spaced repetition to work, and — critically — inadequate CPSA preparation time. The CPSA assesses clinical communication and examination skills that cannot be crammed in a weekend. Communication frameworks, breaking bad news, and clinical management discussions require repeated practice with partners and self-review. Six weeks is the absolute minimum viable preparation window; twelve weeks is the target.

Pre-Plan Setup: What to Do Before Week 1

The week before your twelve-week plan begins should not be spent on revision. It should be spent on honest self-assessment and organisational preparation. The following four steps will determine how you allocate your time across the plan.

Step 1: Sit a Diagnostic Mock Without Revising First

Sit a full timed diagnostic mock — either a Passmedicine timed test or the official MSC sample questions — without revising anything beforehand. The purpose is to reveal your true baseline, not to perform well. The results will tell you which systems you are genuinely strong in and which represent significant knowledge gaps. If you revise before this baseline test, you will overestimate your starting competence and misallocate your twelve weeks.

Step 2: RAG-Rate the MLA Content Map

Download the MLA Content Map and work through all 430 core conditions, applying a traffic-light rating to each:

  • Red: Significant weakness — you could not answer a basic question on this topic reliably
  • Amber: Some knowledge — you know the broad picture but would struggle with nuanced or high-yield details
  • Green: Confident — you could answer most SBA questions on this topic accurately

This RAG rating system becomes your dynamic planning tool. Update it at the end of every week based on question-bank performance.

Step 3: Identify Your Three Weakest Systems

From your diagnostic mock and RAG map, identify your top three weakest systems. These systems will receive additional time during Phase 2 (Weeks 5–8) and will be the primary focus of your targeted sprint in Week 10. Be honest: most candidates overestimate their strength in familiar-feeling topics (common presentations seen in clinical placements) and underestimate their weakness in areas they have never been tested on formally.

Step 4: Set Up Your Systems and Block Your Calendar

Before week one begins, set up your Anki deck (or physical flashcard system) with the system-level tags you will need, and choose your primary question bank. Commit to one question bank as your primary source — switching between banks mid-plan wastes time and fragments your analytics. Block your calendar for the twelve weeks: six hours per day is the ideal preparation commitment, with four hours as the minimum viable day. Mark in advance which days will include CPSA simulation sessions — these require a practice partner and cannot be done solo, so they need advance coordination.

Phase 1 — Foundation (Weeks 1–4): Content Coverage

Phase 1 is about building the knowledge scaffolding. You are not trying to achieve mastery in this phase — you are ensuring that every system on the content map has at least an Amber-level foundation before you enter the question-led phase. One system (or system pair) per week keeps the workload manageable while ensuring breadth of coverage.

Week 1: Cardiovascular and Respiratory

These are the two highest-yield systems on the AKT and among the most heavily represented in clinical practice. Depth of knowledge here pays dividends across multiple question types.

Cardiology priorities:

  • Hypertension: NICE NG136 thresholds (clinic BP ≥140/90, ABPM/HBPM ≥135/85), treatment stepwise (ACE-I or ARB → add calcium channel blocker → add thiazide-like diuretic → consider further agents), target organ damage assessment
  • Heart failure: quadruple therapy (ACE-I/ARB/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT-2 inhibitor), HFrEF vs HFpEF distinction, BNP role in diagnosis
  • ACS: STEMI pathway (primary PCI within 120 minutes, dual antiplatelet therapy, secondary prevention) vs NSTEMI pathway (risk stratification, GRACE score, timing of angiography), troponin interpretation
  • Atrial fibrillation: CHA2DS2-VASc scoring for stroke risk, DOAC selection and contraindications, rate vs rhythm control decision, cardioversion principles
  • ECG interpretation: identifying STEMI, LBBB, AF, complete heart block, VT — pattern recognition, not just theoretical knowledge

Respiratory priorities:

  • Asthma: BTS/SIGN step-up ladder (SABA → add ICS → add LABA → increase ICS dose → consider add-on therapy), acute severe vs life-threatening features, management of acute attack
  • COPD: GOLD criteria (FEV1/FVC ratio <0.7 post-bronchodilator), symptom and exacerbation-based inhaler stepwise, smoking cessation as the single most effective intervention, pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Pneumonia: CURB-65 scoring (Confusion, Urea, Respiratory rate, BP, age ≥65 — score 0–1 home, 2 hospital consider, 3+ hospital), community vs hospital-acquired organisms, empirical antibiotic selection per local guidelines
  • Pulmonary embolism: Wells score for pre-test probability, CTPA as investigation of choice, LMWH bridging to DOAC, massive PE management (thrombolysis), direct oral anticoagulants for treatment
  • Lung cancer: CXR findings (hilar mass, pleural effusion, Pancoast tumour, Horner's syndrome), staging principles, paraneoplastic syndromes (SIADH, hypercalcaemia, Eaton-Lambert)

Daily schedule for Week 1: Two hours content review (notes, Oxford Handbook, or equivalent resource), one hour question practice (minimum 40 SBAs on the day's content), one hour question review and error logging, one hour Anki card creation and review, one hour CPSA skill practice (history taking station frameworks).

Week 2: Gastroenterology and Hepatology

Gastroenterology priorities:

  • GORD and peptic ulcer disease: H. pylori testing (urea breath test or stool antigen) and treat approach, triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin), indications for endoscopy (alarm features: dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, haematemesis, iron deficiency anaemia in men or post-menopausal women)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: distinguishing UC (continuous, rectal involvement, bloody diarrhoea, pseudopolyps) from Crohn's (skip lesions, transmural, any GI segment, cobblestone mucosa, fistulae, granulomas), treatment stepwise (5-ASA → steroids → immunomodulators → biologics), extraintestinal manifestations
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: Rome IV criteria, reassurance and lifestyle as first-line, symptom-based pharmacological options
  • Coeliac disease: anti-TTG IgA antibody as screening test, HLA-DQ2 (90%) and HLA-DQ8 associations, duodenal biopsy confirmation, gluten-free diet as treatment, complications (lymphoma, osteoporosis)
  • GI bleeding: upper GI (haematemesis/melaena — Rockford-Henderson endoscopy, variceal vs non-variceal management) vs lower GI (PR bleeding — colonoscopy, causes by age group), colorectal cancer screening (Lynch syndrome/HNPCC — MSH2/MLH1 mutations, Amsterdam criteria)

Hepatology priorities:

  • LFT interpretation: isolated raised ALP (bone, cholestatic), raised transaminases with ALP (hepatocellular), synthetic function tests (albumin, PT/INR as true markers of liver function)
  • Viral hepatitis: hepatitis B serology (HBsAg, anti-HBs, HBeAg interpretation — acute vs chronic vs immune), hepatitis C (RNA testing, direct-acting antivirals achieving >95% cure rates)
  • Cirrhosis and its complications: portal hypertension leading to varices (propranolol prophylaxis, banding vs sclerotherapy for acute bleeds, terlipressin), ascites (sodium restriction, spironolactone, paracentesis for tense ascites), spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP — neutrophil count >250/mm³, empirical cefotaxime), hepatic encephalopathy (lactulose, rifaximin), hepatorenal syndrome (vasoconstrictors, transplant only definitive treatment)
  • Jaundice: classification into pre-hepatic (haemolysis — unconjugated bilirubin raised, urobilinogen in urine), hepatic (hepatocellular damage — mixed picture), and post-hepatic (obstructive — conjugated bilirubin raised, pale stools, dark urine)

Week 3: Neurology and Psychiatry

Neurology priorities:

  • Stroke: FAST recognition, thrombolysis window (4.5 hours from onset, contraindications), mechanical thrombectomy (large vessel occlusion, up to 24 hours in selected cases), TIA management (ABCD2 score, dual antiplatelet for 21 days then monotherapy, carotid Doppler, statin, anticoagulation if AF), DVLA rules (1 month no driving after TIA, 1 month after minor stroke)
  • Headache: migraine (prodrome, aura, unilateral, photophobia — triptans as acute treatment, propranolol/topiramate/amitriptyline prophylaxis), tension-type (bilateral, non-pulsating, no nausea), cluster (severe unilateral periorbital, autonomic features — high-flow O2 and subcutaneous sumatriptan acute, verapamil prophylaxis), subarachnoid haemorrhage ("thunderclap" headache, sentinel bleed, CT then LP if negative, CT angiography)
  • Epilepsy: focal vs generalised classification, first-line drugs (sodium valproate — caution in women of childbearing age, carbamazepine for focal, lamotrigine as alternative), DVLA (seizure-free for 6 months for car licence, 5 years for HGV/PSV), sodium valproate pregnancy warning (MHRA 2024 guidance — PREVENT programme)
  • Multiple sclerosis: relapsing-remitting as most common type, optic neuritis (painful loss of vision, RAPD on examination), Uhthoff's phenomenon (worsening with heat), MRI white matter lesions, disease-modifying therapies
  • Parkinson's disease: cardinal features (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability), dopaminergic pathways (substantia nigra → striatum), L-dopa/carbidopa (gold standard), non-motor features (constipation, depression, dementia, REM sleep disorder), caution with dopamine antagonists (metoclopramide, haloperidol)
  • Meningitis: LP timing (do not delay if no papilloedema or focal neurology — do not await CT), empirical antibiotics (ceftriaxone IV) plus aciclovir (if encephalitis/HSV suspected), dexamethasone (bacterial meningitis — reduces complications), meningococcal prophylaxis for contacts (ciprofloxacin)

Psychiatry priorities:

  • Depression: PHQ-9 screening, severity classification (mild/moderate/severe), SSRI as first-line pharmacological treatment, CBT as first-line psychological treatment, monitoring at 2 and 4 weeks after starting antidepressant, lithium augmentation for treatment-resistant depression
  • Anxiety: GAD-7 for generalised anxiety, CBT as first-line, SSRI/SNRI for pharmacological treatment, stepped care model
  • Psychosis and schizophrenia: positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder) vs negative symptoms (flat affect, social withdrawal, alogia, avolition), first-generation (typical) vs second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics, metabolic monitoring (weight, glucose, lipids), clozapine monitoring (weekly FBC for 18 weeks due to agranulocytosis risk)
  • Bipolar disorder: mood stabilisers (lithium, valproate, olanzapine, lamotrigine — note lamotrigine for bipolar depression), lithium therapeutic range 0.4–1.0 mmol/L (narrow therapeutic index — toxicity signs: coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion), monitoring (renal and thyroid function every 6 months)
  • Mental Health Act: Section 2 (28-day assessment), Section 3 (6-month treatment), Section 5(2) (72-hour emergency holding power by doctor), Section 136 (police power — 24 hours in place of safety), Community Treatment Order, Nearest Relative rights
  • Mental Capacity Act: two-stage test (impairment of mind or brain, AND inability to understand/retain/use/communicate decision), five principles (presumption of capacity, supported decision-making, right to make unwise decisions, best interests, least restrictive option), Lasting Power of Attorney, Court of Protection

Week 4: Endocrinology and Renal

Endocrinology priorities:

  • Type 1 DM: insulin regimes (basal-bolus as standard, mixed/biphasic as alternative), DKA management (insulin infusion, fluid resuscitation with 0.9% NaCl, potassium replacement, bicarbonate rarely indicated), distinguishing DKA from HHS (HHS — no significant ketosis, extreme hyperglycaemia, older T2DM patient, slower rehydration)
  • Type 2 DM: NICE stepwise (metformin first-line → add SGLT-2 inhibitor, GLP-1 receptor agonist, DPP-4 inhibitor, or sulfonylurea based on individual patient factors → add insulin if HbA1c not at target), SGLT-2 inhibitor cardiovascular and renal benefits, GLP-1 agonist weight loss benefit, HbA1c targets (48 mmol/mol on lifestyle, 53 mmol/mol on one drug, 58 mmol/mol on combination)
  • Thyroid: hypothyroidism (levothyroxine dose titrated to TSH, start low in elderly and cardiac disease), hyperthyroidism causes (Graves' — TSH receptor antibodies, toxic nodular goitre, thyroiditis), treatment (carbimazole — agranulocytosis monitoring, propylthiouracil in pregnancy first trimester, radioiodine, surgery), thyroid storm (propranolol + Lugol's iodine + carbimazole + dexamethasone)
  • Adrenal conditions: Addison's disease (primary adrenal insufficiency — short Synacthen test for diagnosis, hydrocortisone replacement, sick day rules), Conn's syndrome (primary hyperaldosteronism — hypertension + hypokalaemia, low renin, aldosterone:renin ratio, adrenal CT), Cushing's syndrome (ACTH-dependent vs ACTH-independent, dexamethasone suppression test, 24-hour urinary free cortisol)
  • Calcium: hypercalcaemia causes (bones — metastases, osteoporosis; groans — constipation, nausea; stones — renal calculi; psychic moans — depression, confusion), management (IV fluids, bisphosphonates, treat underlying cause), SIADH (euvolaemic hyponatraemia, low urine output, fluid restriction) vs diabetes insipidus (cranial vs nephrogenic, desmopressin test, water deprivation test)

Renal priorities:

  • AKI: KDIGO staging (Stage 1 — creatinine ×1.5 or rise ≥26 µmol/L in 48h; Stage 2 — ×2; Stage 3 — ×3 or <0.3 mL/kg/h urine output for 24h), prerenal (dehydration, haemorrhage, cardiac failure — FENa <1%) vs intrinsic (ATN, glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis) vs postrenal (obstruction — ultrasound), urgent referral criteria (anuria, severe electrolyte disturbance, fluid overload, need for RRT)
  • CKD: eGFR staging (G1–G5), complications (anaemia — EPO deficiency, bone disease — vitamin D deficiency and hyperphosphataemia, hypertension, hyperkalaemia), ACE-I/ARB use for proteinuric CKD, contraindications and monitoring, referral thresholds to nephrology
  • Nephrotic vs nephritic: nephrotic (heavy proteinuria >3.5g/day, hypoalbuminaemia, oedema, hyperlipidaemia — minimal change disease in children, membranous in adults) vs nephritic (haematuria, hypertension, oliguria, proteinuria — IgA nephropathy most common worldwide, post-streptococcal, anti-GBM/Goodpasture's)
  • Renal replacement therapy: haemodialysis (hospital or home, 3–5 sessions/week, arteriovenous fistula), peritoneal dialysis (continuous ambulatory, home-based, contraindicated in extensive abdominal surgery), transplant (optimal for eligible patients — living donor preferred, calcineurin inhibitor immunosuppression)

Phase 2 — Active Consolidation (Weeks 5–8): Question-Led Revision

Phase 2 shifts the balance. Content review time reduces; question practice and review time expands. The target is a minimum of 50 SBA questions per day, with thorough review of every incorrect and uncertain answer. Your error log becomes your most valuable revision document.

Week 5: MSK, Dermatology, and Ophthalmology/ENT

MSK priorities: Distinguishing OA (age, insidious onset, Heberden/Bouchard nodes, joint space narrowing) from RA (symmetrical, MCP/PIP involvement, morning stiffness >1 hour, anti-CCP and RF positive, periarticular osteoporosis on X-ray) from gout (first MTP joint, negatively birefringent urate crystals, allopurinol for prophylaxis, colchicine or NSAIDs acute) from pseudogout (knee/wrist, positively birefringent calcium pyrophosphate crystals). DMARDs (methotrexate — monitoring LFTs, FBC, renal function; sulfasalazine; hydroxychloroquine; leflunomide), biologics (TNF-alpha inhibitors — TB screening before starting). Septic arthritis as a medical emergency requiring same-day joint aspiration and IV antibiotics. Back pain red flags using the TUNA mnemonic: Trauma (significant mechanism), systemic Upset (weight loss, night sweats, fever, known malignancy), Neurological compromise (bladder/bowel dysfunction, bilateral symptoms, saddle anaesthesia), Age extremes (>50 or <20 with back pain).

Dermatology priorities: Eczema (emollient backbone, topical steroids by potency, step-up to tacrolimus/ciclosporin), psoriasis (topical steroids and vitamin D analogues, phototherapy, systemic agents including methotrexate and biologics). Skin cancer: BCC (pearly nodule with rolled edges, sun-exposed skin, rarely metastasises, 3mm excision margin), SCC (indurated, crusted, risk of metastasis, 4–6mm margin), melanoma (ABCDEs — Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Colour variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving — wide local excision margin based on Breslow thickness). Drug rashes: Stevens-Johnson syndrome/toxic epidermal necrolysis (stop offending drug, specialist dermatology, supportive care). Cellulitis vs erysipelas (erysipelas — superficial dermis, sharp border, raised, face common).

Week 6: Obstetrics, Gynaecology, and Paediatrics

Obstetrics priorities:

  • Pre-eclampsia: BP >140/90 plus new-onset proteinuria after 20 weeks, severe features (BP >160/110, severe headache, visual disturbance, epigastric pain, rapidly rising creatinine), eclampsia treatment (magnesium sulphate IV — 4g loading then infusion, antihypertensives: labetalol first-line, nifedipine alternative, hydralazine IV for acute severe), delivery as definitive treatment
  • Ectopic pregnancy: risk factors (PID, previous ectopic, IUD, IVF), beta-hCG interpretation (serum level, doubling every 48h in viable intrauterine), transvaginal ultrasound, management (methotrexate for unruptured stable vs laparoscopic salpingectomy for ruptured)
  • Miscarriage: threatened (closed os, bleeding, viable pregnancy on scan), inevitable (open os, bleeding), complete (tissue passed, empty uterus), incomplete (partial tissue remaining — surgical/medical management), missed (embryo non-viable, no symptoms), recurrent miscarriage investigation (3+ losses — thrombophilia screen, antiphospholipid syndrome most important treatable cause)
  • PPH: HAEMOSTASIS mnemonic (Haematinics/Help, Assess vital signs, Ergometrine/Ecbolics, Massage uterus, Oxytocin infusion, Shift to theatre, Tamponade, Apply compression sutures, Systematic devascularisation, Interventional radiology, Subtotal/total hysterectomy)
  • Group B Streptococcus: intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis (IAP) criteria — previous GBS-affected baby, GBS bacteriuria in current pregnancy, maternal fever in labour, prolonged membrane rupture

Gynaecology priorities: PCOS (Rotterdam criteria — oligomenorrhoea + hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries on USS, 2 of 3 needed, insulin resistance, metformin, clomifene for ovulation induction), endometriosis (cyclical pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, dyschezia, subfertility — COCP or GnRH analogues, laparoscopic excision), cervical screening (HPV primary testing — age 25–49 3-yearly, age 50–64 5-yearly, HPV positive then cytology triage, colposcopy if abnormal), menorrhagia (tranexamic acid and mefenamic acid for symptom control, COCP, levonorgestrel intrauterine system as most effective medical option, surgical options).

Paediatrics priorities: Febrile convulsion (simple vs complex, NICE traffic light for feverish child assessment), meningitis in children (non-blanching rash = meningococcal septicaemia until proven otherwise — immediate IM benzylpenicillin if >30 minutes to hospital), developmental milestones (gross motor, fine motor, language, social — know the key ages and red flags), safeguarding (CAAF, child protection procedures, mandatory reporting principles), CAMHS referral pathways.

Week 7: Haematology, Oncology, and Infection

Haematology priorities: Anaemia classification (microcytic — iron deficiency, thalassaemia, sideroblastic; normocytic — chronic disease, haemolysis, bone marrow failure; macrocytic — B12 or folate deficiency, liver disease, hypothyroidism, haemolytic), haematinics replacement (IV iron for IBD/malabsorption, IM B12 for pernicious anaemia), blood transfusion reactions (ABO incompatibility — stop immediately, febrile non-haemolytic — paracetamol and slow transfusion, TRALI, TACO). Coagulopathies: haemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency), haemophilia B (Factor IX deficiency), von Willebrand disease (most common inherited bleeding disorder). Haematological malignancies: leukaemia types (ALL — children/B symptoms/blasts on film, AML — adults, CLL — mature lymphocytes, CML — Philadelphia chromosome BCR-ABL), lymphoma (Hodgkin's — Reed-Sternberg cells/B symptoms/younger patients, Non-Hodgkin's), myeloma (CRAB criteria — hyperCalcaemia, Renal failure, Anaemia, Bone pain/lesions).

Infection priorities: Antibiotic mechanisms (beta-lactams — cell wall, aminoglycosides — protein synthesis 30S, tetracyclines — 30S, macrolides/chloramphenicol — 50S, quinolones — DNA gyrase, metronidazole — DNA strand breakage). Sepsis: NEWS2 score triggers, qSOFA criteria, Sepsis 6 within 1 hour (take Blood cultures, give Broad-spectrum antibiotics, take Blood lactate, give IV Fluids, measure urine output, give high-flow Oxygen — mnemonic: BBBFFiO). HIV: staging (WHO or CDC — CD4 count and AIDS-defining illnesses), opportunistic infections by CD4 count (<200 — PCP pneumonia, <100 — toxoplasmosis/CMV retinitis/cryptococcal meningitis), combination antiretroviral therapy (cART) — U=U (undetectable = untransmittable). TB: DOTS (directly observed therapy short-course), latent TB (IGRA testing, 3HP or 6H treatment), active TB (RIPE — Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol for 2 months, then RI for 4 months), BCG indications. Antimicrobial stewardship principles: right drug, right dose, right route, right duration, de-escalation when sensitivities available.

Week 8: Emergency Medicine and First Full Timed AKT Mock

Emergency Medicine priorities: ABCDE structured assessment as universal framework (Airway — adjuncts and positioning; Breathing — respiratory rate, SpO2, examination; Circulation — HR, BP, CRT, IV access; Disability — GCS, pupils, blood glucose; Exposure — temperature, rashes, injuries). Anaphylaxis: IM adrenaline 1:1000 0.5 mg (0.5 mL) into anterolateral thigh as first priority, position supine with legs raised, IV fluid resuscitation, chlorphenamine and hydrocortisone as secondary measures, repeat adrenaline after 5 minutes if no improvement.

Emergency management high-yield topics: MI (STEMI pathway — aspirin 300mg + P2Y12 inhibitor + anticoagulant + primary PCI), stroke (thrombolysis if within 4.5 hours and no contraindications), PE (massive — thrombolysis; submassive/low-risk — anticoagulation), DKA (0.9% NaCl resuscitation, fixed-rate insulin infusion 0.1 units/kg/h, potassium monitoring), HHS (slower rehydration than DKA, avoid hypotonic fluids, LMWH), hypoglycaemia (conscious — 15-20g fast-acting carbohydrate; unconscious — IV glucose 20% or IM glucagon). Status epilepticus protocol (IV/buccal/IM lorazepam first-line → IV levetiracetam or phenytoin → RSI and ICU if refractory). Toxicology: paracetamol overdose (Rumack-Matthew nomogram, N-acetylcysteine within 8 hours ideally — but give even if late presentation), opioid toxicity (naloxone 400 micrograms IV/IM, short half-life — infusion may be needed), TCA overdose (sodium bicarbonate for cardiac toxicity), burns (Parkland formula: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA in 24 hours, half in first 8 hours).

End of Week 8: Sit your first full timed AKT mock — 180 questions over 3 hours under exam conditions. No phone, no notes, timed strictly. After completing it, review every incorrect and uncertain answer in detail using your question bank explanations. Update your RAG system based on performance. Calculate your per-domain score and compare to baseline. This data drives Phase 3.

Phase 3 — Peak Performance (Weeks 9–11)

Week 9: Professional, Ethical, and Legal Frameworks + Public Health

This is often under-revised because it does not feel like "clinical" medicine — but it is heavily tested on the AKT and forms the backbone of CPSA communication scenarios.

GMC Good Medical Practice 2024: Four domains (Knowledge, skills and development; Patients, partnership and communication; Colleagues, culture and safety; Trust and professionalism). Know the specific duties under each domain rather than just the broad headings. Duties to patients vs duties to society — when these conflict and how to navigate them.

Consent: Valid consent requires capacity + information + voluntariness. Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board (2015) — material risks are those the patient would consider significant, not just what the clinician considers clinically significant. Document consent discussions, not just the signed form.

Confidentiality: The general rule is confidentiality; the exceptions are specific. Key exceptions: DVLA notification duty (patient with relevant medical condition who refuses to inform DVLA — doctor may disclose after informing patient), public interest disclosure (serious crime prevention, significant risk of harm to identifiable others), child safeguarding (duty to refer, information sharing appropriate), court orders. Caldicott Principles.

Duty of candour: Statutory (organisational) and professional (individual) duty. When something goes wrong that has or may have caused moderate harm or above — you must tell the patient, apologise, and document. Being open and honest applies even when causation is uncertain.

End-of-life care: DNACPR as a medical decision (clinician decision in consultation with patient/family), Treatment Escalation Plans (TEP), goals of care conversations, advance care planning, advance decisions to refuse treatment (ADRT — legally binding if valid and applicable), palliative care principles (Cicely Saunders — total pain concept, symptom control, psychological/social/spiritual care).

Public health: Social determinants of health (employment, housing, education, social support, early childhood), Marmot Review principles, health inequalities — explaining variation in outcomes by deprivation. Public health frameworks: Dahlgren and Whitehead rainbow model, Lalonde health field model. Surveillance, screening criteria (Wilson and Jungner), vaccination schedules, communicable disease notification (notifiable disease list — including TB, meningitis, measles, food poisoning).

Week 10: Second Full Mock and Targeted Weak-Topic Sprint

Sit your second full timed mock at the start of this week. Compare your score and per-domain breakdown to your Week 8 mock — the trajectory matters as much as the absolute score. A rising score confirms that your preparation strategy is working. A plateau suggests you need to change approach (more questions, different timing, additional spaced repetition).

Spend the remainder of the week on targeted 30–50 question sets on your lowest-scoring domains. Do not spread this time across all topics — concentration of effort on your specific weaknesses generates the highest marginal return in this late phase. Use rapid-fire one-hour sessions: 50 questions on a single weak topic, then immediate review of every answer. If haematology was your lowest domain, run five such sessions across the week. If professional/ethics was weak, return to the GMC framework and run scenario-based questions.

Week 11: CPSA Simulation and Communication Skills

The CPSA cannot be revised from notes alone. It must be practiced with another person, filmed for self-review, and repeated. Aim for a minimum of three simulated CPSA sessions this week, each covering a different station type.

Finding practice partners: BMA student societies, medical school revision groups, online platforms connecting candidates, your own cohort of UKMLA candidates. Reciprocal practice — you play the doctor, they play the patient, then switch — is more valuable than solo role-play.

Communication frameworks to master:

  • Calgary-Cambridge model: initiating the session → gathering information (open then closed questioning, ICE — Ideas, Concerns, Expectations) → physical examination → explanation and planning → closing the session
  • SPIKES for breaking bad news: Setting (private, seated, tissues), Perception (what does the patient already know?), Invitation (how much do they want to know?), Knowledge (give information in small chunks, avoid jargon), Emotions (acknowledge and respond empathically), Strategy and Summary
  • Motivational interviewing: reflective listening, rolling with resistance, developing discrepancy, supporting self-efficacy — useful in smoking cessation, alcohol reduction, medication adherence stations
  • SBAR for handover: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — used in escalation and referral scenarios

Clinical examination scenarios: Focused physical examination (know the cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, and neurological examination sequences by heart — these are the most common CPSA examination scenarios), requesting and interpreting investigations in a clinical reasoning framework, clinical management discussions (presenting your differential and management plan clearly and systematically).

Phase 4 — Final Week (Week 12): Consolidation Only

The most important rule of Week 12 is this: introduce no new content. Your brain needs time to consolidate and retrieve existing knowledge, not to acquire new material that will compete with — and potentially displace — well-established memories. Candidates who start new topics in the final week consistently report feeling less confident than those who spent the week on consolidation.

  • Run a 30-minute Anki session daily — mature cards only, no new card creation. This keeps consolidated knowledge accessible without overloading your working memory.
  • Complete one half-mock (90 questions) mid-week — this is for pacing, confidence, and reinforcing exam technique, not for learning new content. Review it briefly, noting any patterns in your error log.
  • Review your personal error log — look for patterns (do you misread question stems? Do you change your first answer too frequently? Are there specific content areas that keep appearing?). Address patterns in behaviour, not just content.
  • Exam-day logistics: confirm travel arrangements, check the examination centre location and rules, ensure your ID documentation is in order, plan food and hydration for the exam day, and identify a calming pre-exam routine that works for you.
  • Sleep eight hours every night this week. Sleep is not passive — memory consolidation, including procedural and declarative memories, occurs during sleep. Sacrificing sleep in the final week to do more revision is counterproductive and is supported by no evidence in the cognitive science literature.

Daily Schedule Template

The following schedule assumes a six-hour structured study day. It can be adjusted for part-time workers (see below) while preserving the ratio of active retrieval to passive review.

Time Block Activity Duration Notes
09:00 – 11:00 Content review 2 hours System-specific notes, guidelines, NICE summaries. Phase 1 only — reduce to 1h in Phase 2 and 3
11:00 – 12:00 Question practice 1 hour Minimum 40–50 SBAs. Timed. No looking up answers mid-session.
12:00 – 13:00 Lunch break 1 hour Away from screen. Walk if possible — physical activity aids memory consolidation.
13:00 – 14:00 Question review and error log 1 hour Review every incorrect and uncertain answer. Log error patterns. Update RAG ratings.
14:00 – 15:00 Anki / spaced repetition 1 hour Due cards first. Create new cards only from today's content review. Cap new cards at 20/day.
15:00 – 16:00 CPSA prep / clinical frameworks 1 hour Weeks 1–7: history-taking and examination frameworks. Weeks 8–11: simulated CPSA stations with partner.

Adjustments for Part-Time Workers and Candidates With DSA Accommodations

Part-time workers (4-hour minimum viable day): Cut content review time to 1 hour but preserve questions and review time — these are the highest-return activities and should be protected. The daily minimum should be: 1h content review + 1h questions + 45min review + 45min Anki. CPSA practice moves to weekends. Accept that Phase 1 may need to be extended by one to two weeks if you are starting twelve weeks out, or that some system pairs will need to be combined.

Candidates with DSA or accessibility adjustments: If you have additional time in the examination itself, factor this into your mock practice — always practice under your adjusted time conditions, not standard time. For ergonomic considerations, ensure your study setup is appropriate for sustained work (chair height, screen position, regular breaks). If you use assistive technology, ensure it is set up and tested before your mocks.

Condensed Plans: When Twelve Weeks Is Not Available

Six-Week Plan

A six-week plan is survivable but requires significant compromises. Combine systems: CVS and Respiratory together in week 1, Gastroenterology and Hepatology and Renal in week 2, Neurology and Psychiatry in week 3, Endocrinology and MSK in week 4. If you have recently qualified or just completed finals revision, skip Phase 1 deep content review entirely and jump to question-led revision from day one — your recent clinical knowledge means that content coverage is less necessary, and your time is better spent identifying AKT-specific question patterns. Aim for only one full mock. CPSA skills must still receive weekend time; do not cut this entirely.

Four-Week Plan

A four-week plan is an emergency option only, and the strategy is fundamentally different. There is no time for phased content coverage. The approach is: question bank only, 100 SBAs per day, every day. Review every incorrect answer immediately. CPSA skills are limited to weekends only — two sessions per weekend. Do not attempt phase-by-phase organisation; use question analytics to identify your weakest areas and weight your questions accordingly. This plan sacrifices breadth for intensive retrieval practice and relies on you having adequate baseline clinical knowledge. It is not recommended for candidates who feel significantly unprepared across multiple systems.

Final Thoughts: What the Evidence Actually Says

A twelve-week structured preparation plan is not about working harder than other candidates. It is about working in alignment with how human memory actually functions. The candidates who perform best on the AKT are not those who read the most pages of notes — they are those who retrieved information most frequently, spaced their practice appropriately, and spent the most time in active question practice rather than passive review.

Build your plan around these principles. Trust the structure when motivation is low — the plan carries you when willpower does not. Use your mock data honestly, update your RAG system weekly, and treat every incorrect question not as a failure but as the single most useful piece of information your preparation has generated. That question just told you exactly where your knowledge needs work, with twelve weeks still available to fix it.

Start on day one with your diagnostic mock. Everything else follows from there.
