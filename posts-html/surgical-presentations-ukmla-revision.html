Surgical presentations UKMLA questions test the knowledge a foundation doctor needs from day one on the wards: recognising acute abdominal emergencies, managing perioperative risk, understanding postoperative complications, and knowing when to call for urgent surgical review. This topic spans primary surgical conditions such as appendicitis and bowel obstruction through to the practical management of drains, wounds, fluid balance, and DVT prophylaxis. This guide covers every major surgical presentations UKMLA area with data tables, clinical algorithms, and the high-yield details most frequently tested in the AKT and CPSA.

Consent for Surgery

Valid consent requires three elements: the patient must have capacity, be adequately informed, and consent voluntarily (without coercion). Adults are presumed to have capacity under the Mental Capacity Act 2005 unless demonstrated otherwise. Capacity assessment: can the patient understand the information, retain it, weigh it up, and communicate their decision? For consent to be informed, the patient must be told about the nature of the procedure, its purpose, benefits, risks (including common risks and rare but serious risks), alternatives (including no treatment), and what happens without treatment. The Montgomery v Lanarkshire judgment (2015) requires disclosure of any risk that a reasonable patient in their position would want to know — not just what a reasonable doctor would disclose. Consent for a competent adult cannot be given by a relative. For an incapacitous patient, proceed in their best interests following MCA principles; check for Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) for health and welfare.

WHO Surgical Safety Checklist

The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist (introduced by the NHS in 2009) is completed at three stages: Sign In (before anaesthetic induction — confirm patient identity, site, procedure, consent, allergies, anaesthetic machine check, pulse oximeter); Time Out (before skin incision — team introductions, confirm procedure, site, antibiotic prophylaxis given, imaging available); Sign Out (before patient leaves theatre — confirm procedure performed, instrument count, specimen labelling, postoperative instructions). The checklist has been shown to reduce surgical complications by ~36% and mortality by ~47% in high-income countries.

DVT Prophylaxis in Surgical Patients

All surgical inpatients must be assessed for VTE risk (Caprini score or local equivalent). Mechanical prophylaxis: anti-embolic stockings (TEDS, correctly fitted) and intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) devices — started preoperatively and continued until mobile. Pharmacological prophylaxis: LMWH (e.g. dalteparin or enoxaparin) for moderate-high risk surgical patients — started 6–12 hours post-operatively (earlier timing for laparoscopic surgery). Duration: 28 days for cancer surgery (NICE NG89). Contraindications to LMWH: active bleeding, severe thrombocytopaenia (<50 Ã— 10â¹/L), HIT. Fondaparinux is an alternative if HIT suspected. Early mobilisation is mandatory for all patients.

Complications of hernias: irreducibility (cannot be pushed back), obstruction (bowel lumen obstructed), strangulation (blood supply compromised — surgical emergency requiring immediate repair). Femoral hernias have the highest risk of strangulation — all femoral hernias should be repaired. A strangulated hernia presents with severe pain, erythema over the hernia, systemic sepsis, and may progress to bowel gangrene and perforation.

Wound Healing

Primary intention: wound edges approximated (sutures, staples, glue) — clean surgical wounds. Secondary intention: wound left open to granulate — infected wounds, pressure ulcers. Tertiary intention (delayed primary closure): wound initially left open (to control infection), then closed days later. Four phases of wound healing: haemostasis (immediate — platelet plug, clot); inflammation (0–3 days — neutrophils, macrophages clear debris); proliferation (day 3–21 — fibroblasts, granulation tissue, angiogenesis, epithelialisation); remodelling (21 days–2 years — collagen type III replaced by type I; maximum tensile strength 80% of original). Factors impairing wound healing: malnutrition (low zinc, vitamin C); diabetes; steroids; immunosuppression; poor perfusion; infection; obesity; haematoma; necrotic tissue. Surgical site infection (SSI): most common healthcare-associated infection. Incisional SSI: cellulitis Â± abscess at wound site. Deep SSI: involves fascia/muscle. Organ space SSI: involves cavity opened during surgery. Management: wound swab; IV antibiotics for cellulitis; open and drain abscess; debridement of necrotic tissue; negative-pressure wound therapy (VAC) for complex wounds.

Vascular Emergencies and Urological Presentations

Vascular Presentations

Acute limb ischaemia (6 Ps): Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paraesthesia, Paralysis, Perishing with cold (poikilothermia). This is a surgical emergency — call vascular surgery immediately. Causes: embolism (from AF, recent MI — sudden onset, no prior claudication, contralateral pulses present) vs thrombosis (sudden deterioration of chronic ischaemia — prior claudication, atrophic skin changes, absent contralateral pulses). Management: heparin anticoagulation immediately; angiography Â± embolectomy (Fogarty catheter), bypass, or thrombolysis. Amputation if non-salvageable. Critical limb ischaemia (chronic): rest pain, ulceration, or gangrene for >2 weeks; ABPI <0.5. Requires urgent vascular assessment. AAA management: screening programme in England (all men aged 65 invited for one-time USS). AAA <5.5 cm: surveillance (3-monthly/annual USS depending on size). AAA ≥5.5 cm: elective repair (EVAR preferred if suitable anatomy; open repair otherwise). Ruptured AAA: immediate resuscitation and emergency vascular surgery (mortality >50%).

Urological Presentations

Renal colic: sudden severe loin-to-groin pain (colicky); haematuria; nausea/vomiting; patient unable to lie still (distinguishes from peritonitis). Investigation: CT KUB (gold standard for stone diagnosis, size, and site — non-contrast); urine dipstick (blood); USS (less sensitive than CT but avoids radiation, used in pregnancy). Management: NSAIDs (diclofenac) first-line analgesia; IV opioids if severe; alpha-blocker (tamsulosin) to facilitate passage of distal ureteric stones ≤10 mm; urological referral if stone >10 mm, urosepsis, complete obstruction, single kidney, or failure to pass spontaneously. Lithotripsy (ESWL), ureteroscopy (laser), or PCNL depending on stone size and location. Testicular torsion: surgical emergency (salvage rate drops from >90% if <6h to <10% if >24h from onset). Sudden onset severe scrotal pain; nausea/vomiting; high-riding tender testis; loss of cremasteric reflex. Management: do NOT delay for investigations — immediate surgical exploration and bilateral fixation (orchidopexy) or orchidectomy if non-viable. Urinary retention: acute retention — painful inability to void. Causes: BPH (most common in men), urethral stricture, constipation, post-op, drugs (anticholinergics, opioids, TCAs). Management: urethral catheterisation; trial without catheter (TWOC) after 24–48h Â± alpha-blocker (tamsulosin); transurethral resection of prostate (TURP) if fails TWOC. Haematuria: painless haematuria in an adult is bladder cancer until proven otherwise — 2WW referral. Frank haematuria at any age or microscopic haematuria in age >45: cystoscopy and upper tract imaging (CT urogram).

Breast Conditions and Surgical Investigations

Breast Triple Assessment

Any breast lump or suspicious symptom should undergo triple assessment: (1) Clinical examination (E1–5); (2) Imaging — USS (<35 years, USS preferred; ≥35 years mammogram Â± USS); (3) Histology (core biopsy preferred over FNA for definitive diagnosis). Each component scored 1–5 (1 = normal, 3 = equivocal, 4 = suspicious, 5 = malignant). Discordant triple assessment (e.g. clinical suspicious but biopsy benign) requires repeat biopsy or excision. 2WW referral for breast symptoms (NICE NG12): unexplained breast lump in age ≥30; lump in age ≥50 with skin changes, nipple discharge, nipple retraction; unilateral nipple changes in age ≥50. Breast cancer risk factors: age, family history (BRCA1/2 — lifetime risk up to 85%), early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, HRT (combined), obesity, alcohol, previous breast radiation.

Mastitis vs breast abscess: mastitis presents in breastfeeding women (day 3–21 postpartum) with tender, red, warm breast Â± flu-like symptoms. Management: continue breastfeeding, analgesia, flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS for 10–14 days. Breast abscess: fluctuant tender lump developing from untreated mastitis. Management: USS-guided aspiration (preferred — avoid incision if possible) or formal drainage; send pus for MC&S; antibiotics. Non-puerperal mastitis (periductal/plasma cell) in non-breastfeeding women: associated with smoking; periareolar; treat with antibiotics Â± surgery (excision of affected ducts).

Surgical Drains, ERAS, and Perioperative Nutrition

Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols reduce length of stay and complications by: preoperative carbohydrate loading (clear carbohydrate drink up to 2h before surgery for elective patients — reduced insulin resistance); multimodal analgesia (avoiding opioids where possible — NSAIDs, paracetamol, regional blocks, local anaesthesia); early oral feeding (within hours of surgery for colorectal, upper GI); early mobilisation; restrictive IV fluid administration; VTE prophylaxis. Surgical drains: closed suction (Jackson-Pratt, Redivac) — remove when <30 mL/day drainage; open drain (corrugated, Penrose) — for infected spaces; chest drain — for pneumothorax/pleural effusion (see respiratory section). Nasogastric (NG) tube placement confirmation: chest X-ray (gold standard) — tip should be below diaphragm, to the left of midline, in stomach. Aspiration of 0.5 mL confirms pH ≤5.5 (do NOT use blue litmus alone). NG tubes are used for decompression (post-op ileus, obstruction) or enteral feeding (nasojejunal preferred for proximal GI surgery).

Fluid Management and Electrolyte Prescribing in Surgical Patients

Fluid prescribing is one of the most commonly tested surgical presentations UKMLA topics and a core foundation doctor skill. The NICE NG29 guideline (2013) provides the framework. Maintenance fluids for a 70 kg adult: approximately 25–30 mL/kg/day water, 1 mmol/kg/day sodium, potassium, and chloride, plus 50–100 g/day glucose to prevent starvation ketosis. This equates to roughly 2–2.5 litres per day of fluid in total. Standard maintenance regimen: 1 litre sodium chloride 0.9% over 8 hours, then 1 litre glucose 5% over 8 hours with 20 mmol KCl, then 1 litre glucose 5% over 8 hours with 20 mmol KCl — reassess regularly and adjust based on fluid balance, renal function, and clinical status.

Resuscitation fluids for hypovolaemia: use crystalloids (sodium chloride 0.9% or Hartmann's/Ringer's lactate) in 250–500 mL boluses and reassess. Hartmann's solution is physiologically closer to plasma and preferred for large-volume resuscitation (less risk of hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis than normal saline). Avoid glucose solutions for resuscitation. Colloids (albumin, gelatin solutions) are not recommended for routine resuscitation. Blood products are required for haemorrhagic shock once crystalloid exceeds 2 litres — activate major haemorrhage protocol and transfuse packed red cells, FFP, and platelets in a 1:1:1 ratio (damage control resuscitation). Signs of fluid overload: pulmonary oedema, rising JVP, peripheral oedema, increasing oxygen requirement — reduce or stop IV fluids, consider furosemide. Signs of dehydration: tachycardia, hypotension, reduced urine output (target above 0.5 mL/kg/hour), raised urea and creatinine, concentrated urine (SG above 1.020).

Electrolyte abnormalities in surgical patients: hypokalaemia (K+ below 3.5 mmol/L) — causes: vomiting, NG losses, diarrhoea, diuretics; replace orally (Sando-K) if mild, or IV at no more than 10 mmol/hour via peripheral line (risk of cardiac arrhythmia at higher rates); monitor ECG. Hyponatraemia (Na+ below 135 mmol/L) — commonest inpatient electrolyte abnormality; causes: SIADH (post-op, pneumonia, drugs), excess hypotonic fluid, hypothyroidism, Addison's; correct slowly (no faster than 8–10 mmol/L in 24 hours) to avoid central pontine myelinolysis. Hypercalcaemia — causes: malignancy (PTHrP, bone metastases), primary hyperparathyroidism; symptoms: bones (pain), stones (renal calculi), groans (abdominal), psychic moans (depression, confusion); management: IV fluids, bisphosphonates (zoledronate), treat underlying cause.

Colorectal Surgery and Enhanced Recovery

Colorectal surgery is a major component of surgical practice relevant to foundation doctors on general surgical attachments. Elective sigmoid colectomy, right hemicolectomy, and anterior resection are performed for colorectal cancer, diverticular disease, and IBD. Stoma types: colostomy (end-stoma using colon — effluent is formed; usually left iliac fossa); ileostomy (effluent is liquid; usually right iliac fossa; higher risk of dehydration and electrolyte imbalance); loop stoma (temporary; two limbs visible); Hartmann's procedure (sigmoid resection with end colostomy, rectal stump oversewn — used in emergency perforation or obstruction). Stoma complications: high output (above 2 litres/day — risk of dehydration; manage with loperamide, codeine, dietary advice), prolapse, retraction, parastomal hernia, skin excoriation. Foundation doctors must be able to identify stoma complications, assess fluid status in high-output stomas, and prescribe appropriate fluid replacement.

Appendicitis management: diagnosis is clinical (Alvarado score: migration of pain to RIF, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, RIF tenderness, rebound tenderness, elevated temperature, leucocytosis, left shift — score 7–10 = high probability). CT abdomen is used when diagnosis uncertain (sensitivity 94%, specificity 95%); USS is preferred in children and pregnant women to avoid radiation. Management: laparoscopic appendicectomy is standard (lower wound infection rate, shorter hospital stay than open). Antibiotics alone for uncomplicated appendicitis are increasingly used (APPAC trial) — acceptable for carefully selected patients who prefer non-surgical management with informed consent. Perforated appendicitis: laparoscopic washout Â± appendicectomy; postoperative antibiotics; risk of appendix mass (treated conservatively with antibiotics, interval appendicectomy at 6–8 weeks if mass resolves).

Trauma, Shock Classification, and ATLS Principles

Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) provides the framework for managing trauma patients — tested in UKMLA surgical presentations questions. Primary survey follows ABCDE: Airway (with cervical spine control), Breathing, Circulation (with haemorrhage control), Disability (neurological status — GCS, pupils), Exposure (full exposure with environmental control). Resuscitative measures happen simultaneously with assessment. Haemorrhage control: direct pressure, tourniquets for extremity bleeding, pelvic binder for pelvic fractures, REBOA (resuscitative endovascular balloon occlusion of the aorta) or emergency laparotomy for internal haemorrhage. Massive transfusion protocol (MTP): ratio-based resuscitation (1:1:1 packed red cells:FFP:platelets) to prevent dilutional coagulopathy; tranexamic acid 1 g IV within 3 hours of injury (CRASH-2 trial).

Frequently Asked Questions About Surgical Presentations in the UKMLA

What are the 6 Ps of acute limb ischaemia and what is the immediate management?

The 6 Ps are: Pain (severe, sudden onset), Pallor (white/mottled limb), Pulselessness (absent distal pulses), Paraesthesia (tingling — indicates early nerve ischaemia), Paralysis (fixed, weak foot — indicates advanced ischaemia, poor prognosis for limb), and Perishing cold (poikilothermia). Immediate management: call vascular surgery; IV heparin bolus (5000 units) to prevent thrombus propagation; analgesia; DO NOT elevate the limb (worsens perfusion). Imaging: CT angiography or Duplex USS to identify the level of occlusion. Treatment: embolectomy (Fogarty catheter) for embolic occlusion; bypass or thrombolysis for thrombotic occlusion. Paraesthesia and paralysis are signs of impending irreversibility — escalate immediately.

What is the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist and why does it matter for foundation doctors?

The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist is completed in three stages at Sign In, Time Out, and Sign Out in every operating theatre. Foundation doctors are often present in theatre and may be asked to participate in, or lead, part of the checklist. The purpose is to prevent wrong-site surgery, ensure allergy checks, confirm antibiotic prophylaxis, ensure all team members know the procedure, and confirm correct specimen labelling. UKMLA questions may ask which stage a particular check occurs at, or test knowledge of when antibiotic prophylaxis should be given (at induction, before skin incision — confirmed at Time Out). The checklist is a statutory requirement in NHS England under patient safety initiatives.

How do I manage a patient with a postoperative fever on day 5?

Day 5 postoperative fever raises the probability of wound infection (SSI) or DVT/PE — both peak at this time. Systematic approach: take history (wound symptoms, calf pain, breathlessness), examine wound (erythema, swelling, discharge), check calves and respiratory system, review fluid balance and urine output. Investigations: wound swab, FBC (WCC), CRP, blood cultures if systemically unwell, urine dipstick and MC&S, Doppler USS of legs if DVT suspected, D-dimer only if low-moderate clinical probability of PE. If wound infection: open wound, irrigate, send swab, prescribe antibiotics (flucloxacillin for superficial SSI; co-amoxiclav if broader cover needed). If DVT confirmed: therapeutic LMWH or DOAC. Do not diagnose wound infection without examining the wound — this is a common CPSA station pitfall.

What are the signs of bowel obstruction on plain AXR?

Small bowel obstruction: dilated small bowel loops (greater than 3 cm) in the central abdomen; the valvulae conniventes (circular folds, plicae circulares) cross the full width of the bowel lumen — distinguishing feature from colonic haustral folds which are incomplete; absence of gas in the large bowel. Large bowel obstruction: dilated colon (>6 cm, caecum >9 cm — higher risk of perforation) around the periphery of the abdomen; haustral folds are incomplete and do not cross the full width. Sigmoid volvulus: "coffee bean" or "omega loop" sign — massively dilated sigmoid loop in the right upper quadrant. Caecal volvulus: single dilated loop in left upper quadrant. Perforation: free air under the diaphragm on erect CXR (Rigler's sign on AXR — air on both sides of bowel wall).

What is the difference between strangulated, incarcerated, and reducible hernias?

A reducible hernia can be manually pushed back (reduced) into the abdominal cavity. An irreducible (incarcerated) hernia cannot be reduced — the contents are stuck but the blood supply is not yet compromised. A strangulated hernia has a compromised blood supply to the herniated contents — this is a surgical emergency because the bowel can become gangrenous within hours. Features of strangulation: severe localised pain over the hernia, tender irreducible lump, erythema and warmth of overlying skin, systemic signs of sepsis (fever, tachycardia), signs of bowel obstruction. All strangulated hernias require emergency surgical repair. Femoral hernias have the highest risk of strangulation due to the narrow femoral canal — hence all femoral hernias, even when asymptomatic, should be referred for elective repair without delay.

Conclusion

Surgical presentations UKMLA questions reward candidates who can apply acute management protocols under time pressure, know their perioperative drug management rules, interpret postoperative complication timelines using the 5 Ws, and understand hernia types, vascular emergencies, and urological presentations. The triple assessment pathway for breast disease, the WHO checklist, and consent under the Mental Capacity Act are also consistently tested.
