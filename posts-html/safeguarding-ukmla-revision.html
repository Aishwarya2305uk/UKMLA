Safeguarding in the UKMLA: A Comprehensive Guide to Children and Adults Safeguarding

Safeguarding is one of the highest-weighted topic areas in the UKMLA, and a robust understanding of both children's and adults' safeguarding frameworks is essential for passing the Applied Knowledge Test and for performing competently in Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment stations. In this comprehensive guide to safeguarding for the UKMLA, we cover every major piece of legislation, every category of abuse, the clinical indicators you need to recognise, and the referral processes you must be able to navigate. Whether you are approaching safeguarding from scratch or refining your exam technique, this guide will give you the depth of knowledge the UKMLA expects.

Children Safeguarding: The Legal and Policy Framework

Children Act 1989 and Children Act 2004

The Children Act 1989 remains the cornerstone of children's law in England and Wales. It introduced the paramountcy principle — that the welfare of the child is the court's paramount consideration — and created the framework of parental responsibility, care orders, and child protection processes. Key provisions include:

  • Section 17: Local authorities have a duty to safeguard and promote the welfare of children in need
  • Section 20: Local authorities can provide accommodation to children whose parents consent or where there is no person with parental responsibility
  • Section 31: Courts can make a Care Order (placing a child in local authority care) or a Supervision Order if the child is suffering or is likely to suffer significant harm attributable to inadequate parental care or the child being beyond parental control
  • Section 47: Local authorities have a duty to investigate when they have reasonable cause to suspect that a child in their area is suffering or is likely to suffer significant harm

The Children Act 2004 strengthened multi-agency working following the public inquiry into the death of Victoria Climbié. It created Local Safeguarding Children Boards (subsequently replaced by Safeguarding Children Partnerships under Working Together 2018/2023), and introduced the duty on local authorities, NHS bodies, police, and other agencies to cooperate to safeguard and promote the welfare of children.

Working Together to Safeguard Children 2023

Working Together to Safeguard Children is the statutory guidance that sets out how organisations and individuals should work together to safeguard children. The 2023 edition (updated from 2018) emphasises earlier intervention, strengthened multi-agency safeguarding arrangements, and clearer thresholds for action. For UKMLA candidates, the most important elements are:

  • The information sharing framework — when and how to share information without consent in safeguarding contexts
  • The threshold for referral to children's social care
  • The child protection process: referral → strategy discussion → Section 47 enquiry → child protection conference → child protection plan
  • The role of health professionals in contributing to multi-agency assessments

NICE Guideline NG76: Child Abuse and Neglect

NICE guideline NG76 (updated 2022) provides evidence-based guidance on recognising and responding to child abuse and neglect. It is directly relevant to UKMLA clinical stations. Key points include: recognising that most injuries in non-mobile infants are non-accidental; the importance of a full body examination; documentation of injuries using body maps; the threshold for skeletal surveys; and the importance of taking a full developmental and social history.

Categories of Child Abuse and Clinical Indicators

Physical Abuse (Non-Accidental Injury, NAI)

Physical abuse involves inflicting physical harm on a child, including hitting, shaking, throwing, poisoning, burning, drowning, or suffocating. Fabricated or induced illness (FII, formerly Munchausen syndrome by proxy) is also classified as physical abuse.

Key clinical indicators that should raise concern include:

  • Bruising in unusual sites — bruising on the trunk, back, buttocks, face, ears, or neck in a pre-mobile infant or toddler; bruising in a child who does not yet walk; patterned bruising suggesting an implement
  • Retinal haemorrhages — bilateral retinal haemorrhages extending to the periphery in an infant are highly associated with abusive head trauma (shaken baby syndrome) and require urgent ophthalmological assessment and investigation. They are rarely caused by accidental mechanisms
  • Posterior rib fractures in infants — particularly at the costo-vertebral junctions, caused by compressive squeezing forces during shaking; these are not caused by normal handling or CPR in infants
  • Classic metaphyseal lesions (CML / bucket-handle fractures) — small corner fractures at the metaphyses of long bones in infants, caused by sudden traction or twisting forces; highly specific for non-accidental injury
  • Multiple fractures of different ages — on skeletal survey, fractures at different stages of healing strongly suggest repeated trauma
  • Delay in presentation — presenting days after an injury occurs, without a plausible explanation
  • Inconsistent history — explanations that change, are inconsistent between caregivers, or are incompatible with the child's developmental stage (e.g. a 3-month-old described as having rolled off a sofa)

Differential diagnoses that must be considered and excluded include: osteogenesis imperfecta (brittle bone disease), bleeding disorders (always check a coagulation screen before attributing bruising to NAI), Mongolian blue spots (common in darker-skinned infants, often mistaken for bruising — do not fade on pressure), and metabolic bone disease of prematurity.

Emotional Abuse

Emotional abuse is the persistent emotional maltreatment of a child in a way that severely and persistently affects their emotional development. It may include conveying to a child that they are worthless or unloved, developmentally inappropriate expectations, overprotection, and exposure to domestic abuse. Emotional abuse is present in all other forms of abuse. Clinical indicators include: developmental regression, low self-esteem, anxiety, withdrawal, extreme behaviour, and poor peer relationships. It is often the most difficult category to identify and evidence.

Sexual Abuse

Child sexual abuse involves forcing or enticing a child to take part in sexual activities, whether or not the child is aware of what is happening. It includes contact abuse (touching) and non-contact abuse (exposure to sexual material, grooming online). Indicators include: sexually explicit or precocious knowledge or behaviour; sexualised drawings or play; unexplained genital injury; recurrent urinary tract infections in young girls without anatomical cause; STI in a prepubertal child; and behavioural changes including withdrawal, aggression, or regression.

Child Sexual Exploitation (CSE) is a specific form of sexual abuse where the child is coerced into sexual activity in exchange for something they need or want (e.g. affection, food, accommodation, drugs). Warning signs include: being found in cars with older males; having multiple mobile phones; going missing repeatedly; unexplained gifts or money; withdrawn or secretive behaviour; self-harm or substance misuse; and being known to frequent areas associated with exploitation.

Neglect

Neglect is the persistent failure to meet a child's basic physical and/or psychological needs. It is the most commonly recorded form of abuse in England. Categories include: physical neglect (failure to provide food, clothing, shelter, warmth, or medical care), emotional neglect (failure to provide love and affection), educational neglect (failure to ensure school attendance), and supervisory neglect (leaving young children unsupervised). Clinical indicators include: poor growth and weight gain not explained by medical causes; dental caries; repeated missed medical appointments; dirty, inappropriate, or ill-fitting clothing; and children who present frequently with minor infections or injuries that have not been attended to.

Female Genital Mutilation (FGM)

FGM is a mandatory reporting duty for healthcare professionals in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. Under the Female Genital Mutilation Act 2003 (as amended by the Serious Crime Act 2015), if a healthcare professional, in the course of their work, identifies that FGM appears to have been carried out on a girl under 18, they must report this to the police. This is a mandatory personal duty — it cannot be delegated. There is no public interest balancing test. The duty also requires asking about FGM in appropriate antenatal consultations. UKMLA candidates must know that the mandatory reporting duty applies specifically to under-18s; for adult women, the standard safeguarding and information sharing principles apply.

Radicalisation and the Prevent Duty

The Prevent duty (under the Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015) requires specified authorities, including NHS bodies, to have due regard to the need to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism. In practice, healthcare professionals who are concerned that a patient (including a child) may be vulnerable to radicalisation should refer to the Channel programme through their local Prevent lead. Indicators of radicalisation concern include: expressions of extremist ideology; sudden change in behaviour or social circle; increased secretiveness; expressions of grievance that justify violence; and accessing extremist material online. Channel is a multi-agency early intervention programme — a referral is not a police report and does not automatically trigger criminal investigation.

Trafficking and Modern Slavery

The Modern Slavery Act 2015 criminalises human trafficking and slavery. Children who have been trafficked may present with: unexplained injuries; signs of physical or sexual abuse; malnourishment; signs of excessive work; accompanied by controlling adults who are not family; reluctance to discuss home life; and not knowing their address or seeming confused about their location. Healthcare staff should refer concerns about potential victims to the National Referral Mechanism (NRM) and, for children, to children's social care.

Domestic Abuse and Its Impact on Children

Exposure to domestic abuse is a recognised form of child abuse in its own right. Under the Domestic Abuse Act 2021, a child who sees, hears, or experiences the effects of domestic abuse, and is related to the abuser or victim, is recognised as a victim of domestic abuse. Healthcare professionals should ask about domestic abuse routinely using validated tools, and should be aware that children living with domestic abuse are at significantly increased risk of physical, emotional, and developmental harm.

The Child Safeguarding Process: What Healthcare Professionals Must Do

Information Sharing: When Consent Is Not Required

In safeguarding situations, information can and must be shared without consent if: sharing is necessary to protect a child from harm; the risk would be increased by seeking consent; or seeking consent is not reasonably practicable. The GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 do not prevent information sharing in safeguarding contexts — they require that sharing is lawful, necessary, and proportionate. Working Together 2023 states that the fear of sharing information must not be allowed to stand in the way of the need to promote the welfare and protect the safety of children.

Referral to Children's Social Care

If a healthcare professional has concerns about a child's safety or welfare, they should:

  1. Discuss the concern with their named/designated safeguarding professional (doctor, nurse, or midwife) in the first instance, unless doing so would cause delay that would place the child at risk
  2. Make a referral to children's social care — this can be by phone (followed up in writing within 48 hours) if there is urgent concern, or in writing if the concern is non-urgent
  3. Document all concerns, observations, and discussions clearly and contemporaneously
  4. If a referral is made and social care decides not to investigate, the referring professional can challenge this decision

Child Protection Medical Assessment

A child protection medical assessment is carried out by a designated or named doctor for child protection, or by a paediatrician with appropriate training. It involves: a detailed history (including full developmental, social, and medical history); full physical examination including a body map; appropriate investigations (skeletal survey, blood tests, ophthalmology review); photography of injuries with consent; and production of a written medical report for the child protection conference. The assessment should always be child-centred and trauma-informed.

Child Protection Plan and Looked After Children

Following a child protection conference, if a child is found to be at continuing risk of significant harm, they are placed on a child protection plan (CPP). The plan sets out the actions required to reduce risk and promote the child's welfare, and is reviewed regularly at review conferences. If a child becomes Looked After (in local authority care), healthcare professionals have specific duties to ensure the child's health needs are assessed at the time of entry into care (initial health assessment) and reviewed annually (review health assessment). These assessments cover physical, emotional, and developmental health needs.

Child Death Review

All child deaths (under 18) in England should be reviewed by a Child Death Review (CDR) process. This involves two components: the Rapid Response (an immediate multi-agency review for unexpected child deaths) and the Child Death Overview Panel (CDOP), which reviews all child deaths in a local area to identify patterns and learning. Healthcare professionals may be required to contribute information to CDR processes.

Adults Safeguarding: The Legal and Policy Framework

The Care Act 2014

The Care Act 2014 placed adult safeguarding on a statutory footing for the first time in England. Section 42 of the Care Act requires local authorities to make enquiries (or cause enquiries to be made) when they have reasonable cause to suspect that an adult in their area:

  • Has needs for care and support (whether or not the authority is meeting those needs)
  • Is experiencing, or is at risk of, abuse or neglect
  • As a result of those care and support needs, is unable to protect themselves from abuse or neglect or the risk of it

This is the Section 42 enquiry — the adult equivalent of the children's Section 47. The Care Act also established Safeguarding Adults Boards (SABs), multi-agency bodies responsible for overseeing and coordinating adult safeguarding in each local area.

The Six Principles of Adult Safeguarding

The Care Act and associated statutory guidance set out six principles that should underpin all adult safeguarding activity:

  1. Empowerment — People being supported and encouraged to make their own decisions and informed consent
  2. Prevention — It is better to take action before harm occurs
  3. Proportionality — The least intrusive response appropriate to the risk presented
  4. Protection — Support and representation for those in greatest need
  5. Partnership — Local solutions through services working with their communities
  6. Accountability — Accountability and transparency in delivering safeguarding

These principles appear in UKMLA scenarios, particularly in questions about balancing a vulnerable adult's right to autonomy against the duty to protect them from harm. The Care Act's emphasis on empowerment means that adult safeguarding is not about removing autonomy — it is about ensuring that adults with care needs can exercise genuine choice and control.

Categories of Adult Abuse

The Care Act 2014 and associated statutory guidance define ten categories of adult abuse:

Coercive Control and Domestic Abuse

The Domestic Abuse Act 2021 extended the legal framework significantly, creating a statutory definition of domestic abuse that includes physical, sexual, financial, emotional, and psychological abuse. The Act recognised children as victims in their own right (as noted above) and strengthened protection for victims. From a criminal law perspective, coercive control (a pattern of controlling or coercive behaviour in an intimate or family relationship) has been an offence under the Serious Crime Act 2015 since 2015. Healthcare professionals should be familiar with the signs of coercive control, which may be subtler than physical violence: partners who attend all appointments and answer on the patient's behalf; patients who appear to require permission to speak or to make decisions; unexplained financial difficulties; and social isolation.

Domestic Abuse Risk Assessment Tools

The DASH (Domestic Abuse, Stalking, Honour-Based Violence and Forced Marriage Risk Identification Checklist) is the most widely used risk assessment tool for domestic abuse in the UK. It helps practitioners identify whether a victim is at standard, medium, high, or very high risk of further harm. High-risk cases are referred to a MARAC (Multi-Agency Risk Assessment Conference), a regular meeting of statutory and voluntary agencies that share information to manage the highest-risk cases of domestic abuse. An Independent Domestic Violence Adviser (IDVA) provides specialist support to high-risk victims and coordinates the response to their needs.

Mental Capacity Act and Adult Safeguarding

MCA and Safeguarding Interface

One of the most complex areas in adult safeguarding is the interface between the Mental Capacity Act 2005 and safeguarding decisions. The key principle is that adult safeguarding must respect the autonomy of adults who have capacity to make their own decisions, even if those decisions appear unwise or place them at risk. An adult with capacity who chooses to remain in an abusive relationship, or who chooses to live in unsanitary conditions (self-neglect), has the right to make that choice. The role of safeguarding professionals is to ensure the choice is genuinely informed and uncoerced, to offer support and information, and to document that the risks have been discussed.

When an adult may lack capacity, a formal MCA capacity assessment must be carried out. If they are found to lack capacity for the relevant decision, a best interests process involving all relevant parties must be undertaken. Deprivation of liberty (e.g. restricting a person's movement to protect them from harm) requires authorisation under either the Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS) or the new Liberty Protection Safeguards (LPS).

Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS) and Liberty Protection Safeguards (LPS)

DoLS (under the Mental Capacity Act 2005, schedule A1, inserted by the Mental Health Act 2007) currently apply to care homes and hospitals. If a person who lacks capacity is being cared for in circumstances that amount to a deprivation of their liberty (as defined by the Cheshire West Supreme Court ruling 2014 — the acid test: is the person under continuous supervision and control, and not free to leave?), a DoLS authorisation must be obtained from the supervisory body (local authority for care homes, hospital managers for NHS settings). The application is made by the care home or hospital (the managing authority).

The Liberty Protection Safeguards (LPS), introduced by the Mental Capacity (Amendment) Act 2019, are intended to replace DoLS. At the time of writing, LPS have not yet been implemented. UKMLA candidates should be familiar with both systems and be aware that DoLS is the current legal framework, while LPS represents the intended future system.

Referral Thresholds: Knowing When to Act

One of the most practically important skills in safeguarding is knowing the appropriate threshold for action. The following framework applies in both children's and adults' safeguarding:

Level of Concern Examples Action
Universal (no additional needs) Healthy child attending routine checks; adult with no care needs No specific safeguarding action; provide universal services
Additional needs (early help) Child with developmental delay; family under stress; adult with emerging care needs Early help assessment; signpost to support services; common assessment framework
Child in need (S17) Child whose health or development is impaired or likely to be impaired without services Referral to children's social care for assessment; family support services
Significant harm / abuse (S47 / Care Act S42) Suspected physical abuse; domestic abuse with child in home; adult with care needs being financially exploited Urgent referral to children's or adults' social care; strategy discussion / S42 enquiry; consider immediate protective action (police, emergency court order)
Immediate danger Child in immediate physical danger; adult at imminent risk of serious harm Call 999 / police; Emergency Protection Order (children); if no one else to act — clinician may need to take immediate protective steps

Safeguarding in the UKMLA: Common Exam Scenarios

Scenario Type 1: The Infant with Unexplained Injuries

A 4-month-old is brought to A&E by their mother with a swollen thigh. X-ray shows a mid-shaft femoral fracture. The mother says the child "must have got stuck in the cot." What do you do? In this scenario: femoral fractures in pre-mobile infants are highly suspicious of non-accidental injury; the explanation is implausible for the injury; the correct actions include admitting the child, informing the on-call paediatric consultant, informing your safeguarding lead, requesting a skeletal survey (to look for other injuries), and making a referral to children's social care — not discharging the child with the parents until a full assessment has been completed. Do not confront the parents aggressively, but explain that you need to investigate the cause of the fracture fully.

Scenario Type 2: The Adult with Capacity Refusing Help

An 80-year-old woman with moderate dementia (documented to have fluctuating capacity) is referred by her GP. Her family believe her husband of 60 years is financially abusing her — withdrawing large sums from her account. She says she is happy and does not want anyone to "interfere." What do you do? Key considerations: first, carry out a formal capacity assessment for the specific decision (whether to engage with safeguarding support). If she lacks capacity for this decision, a best interests decision must be made. If she has capacity, respect her autonomy but ensure she has had the opportunity to receive information about the support available, document the conversation, and consider a S42 enquiry by the local authority regardless (the Care Act duty to enquire does not depend on victim consent). Referral to the local authority should be made because she has care and support needs and appears to be at risk of financial abuse, even if she is reluctant to engage.

Scenario Type 3: Disclosure of Domestic Abuse

A 32-year-old woman attends her GP for the third time in six months with minor injuries — this time a bruised forearm. Her male partner is in the waiting room. She discloses that her partner hits her when he has been drinking, but begs you not to tell anyone because she is afraid of what he will do. What do you do? Correct approach: thank her for telling you; validate her experience; ensure her immediate safety (is she safe to go home today?); provide information about support services (IDVA, local domestic abuse organisation, the 24-hour National Domestic Abuse Helpline); ask about any children in the household (their safety may require a separate safeguarding referral); complete a DASH risk assessment; document carefully; do not breach confidentiality without her consent unless there is an immediate risk to life or children are at risk. Arrange follow-up. Offering to contact the police without her consent, unless there is immediate risk to life, is not the correct response.

Frequently Asked Questions About Safeguarding and the UKMLA

Do I need consent to make a safeguarding referral?

For children's safeguarding, you should seek consent from parents/carers and (where appropriate) the child before making a referral to children's social care, unless doing so would place the child at increased risk of harm. You do not need parental consent if you believe obtaining it would compromise the child's safety. For adult safeguarding, you should ordinarily seek consent from the adult at risk, respecting their autonomy. However, a referral can be made without consent if the person lacks capacity to consent, if seeking consent would increase the risk of harm, or if there is a serious public interest in disclosure. The safeguarding concern, risk, and consent discussion should all be documented clearly.

What is the difference between a Section 47 enquiry and a Section 17 assessment?

A Section 47 enquiry is triggered when there is reasonable cause to suspect that a child is suffering, or is likely to suffer, significant harm. It is led by children's social care and involves police and health professionals. Its purpose is to determine whether action needs to be taken to safeguard the child. A Section 17 assessment relates to children in need — those whose health or development is impaired, or is likely to be impaired, without provision of services. The threshold for Section 17 is lower than for Section 47; it does not require suspected harm, only unmet need. S47 investigations can run concurrently with S17 assessments if both apply.

What should I do if a colleague is suspected of abusing a patient?

Allegations against professionals who work with children must be reported to the Local Authority Designated Officer (LADO) within one working day. Failure to report in a timely way is itself a safeguarding concern. The LADO coordinates the response to allegations against professionals and ensures that the investigation is conducted appropriately, separating any criminal investigation from any disciplinary or child protection enquiry. For allegations involving adults, the equivalent process involves notifying the Safeguarding Adults Board and the appropriate regulatory bodies. You should not attempt to investigate the allegation yourself.

How does the Mental Capacity Act interact with safeguarding adults?

The MCA and adult safeguarding run in parallel but serve different purposes. The MCA governs decision-making for people who lack capacity; adult safeguarding applies to people with care and support needs who are at risk of abuse or neglect, regardless of their capacity. A person with capacity can still be subject to a Section 42 enquiry if they have care and support needs and are at risk. However, if a person with capacity has made an informed, uncoerced decision to remain in a situation that others consider dangerous, safeguarding professionals must respect that decision and focus on empowering the person and reducing risk rather than overriding their choice.

Is it ever appropriate to break confidentiality in safeguarding without the patient's consent?

Yes, in specific circumstances. Confidentiality can be broken without consent in safeguarding when: a child is at risk of significant harm; an adult lacks capacity and the disclosure is in their best interests; the risk of harm to others (including children in the household) is serious, real, and imminent; there is a mandatory reporting duty (FGM in under-18s); or there is a statutory obligation to share information (e.g. during a Section 47 enquiry). The decision to break confidentiality must be documented carefully, including the reason for the decision and (where appropriate) whether an attempt was made to obtain consent. Even in safeguarding contexts, only the minimum necessary information should be shared.

Conclusion: Building Safeguarding Confidence for the UKMLA

Safeguarding in the UKMLA requires you to move beyond knowing the legislative framework to demonstrating the kind of professional judgement that keeps vulnerable people safe. The exam scenarios will test your ability to recognise abuse indicators, apply the correct threshold for referral, navigate consent and capacity in safeguarding contexts, and understand the roles of the different professionals and organisations involved. Practise with scenario-based questions, revisit the key indicators for each category of abuse, and make sure you can distinguish between the children's and adults' safeguarding frameworks without confusing them.
