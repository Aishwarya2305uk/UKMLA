OET vs IELTS for GMC registration is the first genuinely consequential decision most international medical graduates (IMGs) make on the road to practising in the UK, and yet it is often treated as an afterthought squeezed in between exam preparation and paperwork. Both tests are fully accepted by the General Medical Council (GMC) as proof of English language proficiency, and neither is officially preferred over the other — but they are built differently, scored differently, and reward genuinely different kinds of candidates. Choosing badly does not just cost a test fee; it can cost weeks of delay at exactly the point in the pathway when a candidate is trying to finish registration, not restart a step.

This guide is a neutral, head-to-head comparison of the two tests themselves — their format, content, scoring thresholds, cost, availability, and the kind of candidate each tends to suit — rather than a country-specific eligibility walkthrough. Quick Answer: OET or IELTS for GMC Registration?

Both OET and IELTS Academic are accepted by the GMC for registration purposes, and neither is treated as superior to the other — the right choice depends on the individual candidate's strengths, not on any GMC preference. For IELTS, the GMC requires an overall score of 7.5, with a minimum of 7 in each of the four sub-tests (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking). Crucially, the GMC accepts only IELTS Academic — the General Training version of IELTS, which is commonly used for immigration and non-academic purposes, is not accepted for GMC registration. For OET, the GMC requires a minimum of Grade B (350 or above on OET's numerical scale) in all four components, achieved in a single sitting.

Both certificates share the same validity rule: results from either test remain valid for two years from the date the test was sat, and that validity must still be current at the moment the full GMC registration application is actually submitted — not merely at the moment the candidate first decides which test to take. A certificate that has lapsed by the time an application reaches the GMC is treated as though no evidence had been provided at all, regardless of how strong the original scores were. The GMC sets out the full, current version of these requirements on its own evidence of your knowledge of English page, and since these thresholds are reviewed periodically, that page should always take precedence over any summary, including this one.

In broad terms, IELTS Academic tends to suit candidates who are comfortable with general academic English, formal essay writing, and topics that range well beyond medicine, while OET tends to suit candidates who think and communicate fluently in a clinical register — writing referral letters, taking histories, and speaking with patients — since that is precisely what OET's Medicine-specific tasks simulate. The sections that follow work through the format, scoring, cost, and practical differences between the two tests in detail, before turning to a 2026-specific visa wrinkle that makes the choice of test matter for reasons beyond GMC registration alone.

How OET and IELTS Differ in Format and Content

The most fundamental difference between the two tests is not the scoring scale but the subject matter each one tests. Understanding this distinction properly is usually more useful for choosing between them than comparing raw pass rates or anecdotal difficulty claims found online.

OET Medicine: A Healthcare-Themed Test Throughout

OET (Occupational English Test) was designed specifically for healthcare professionals, and its Medicine-specific stream builds every task around genuine clinical scenarios rather than generic topics. The four components are structured as follows:

  • Listening (approximately 45 minutes): recordings of consultations, ward-round discussions, and healthcare-related presentations, testing the ability to follow clinically relevant detail in real time.
  • Reading (60 minutes): healthcare-related texts, including a timed expeditious-reading task and detailed comprehension passages drawn from genuine medical and healthcare-policy material.
  • Writing (45 minutes, profession-specific): for the Medicine sub-test, candidates write a referral letter based on a set of case notes — closely mirroring the kind of clinical correspondence a junior doctor routinely produces in NHS practice.
  • Speaking (approximately 20 minutes, profession-specific): two role-played consultations with a trained interlocutor playing a patient or relative, assessed on clinical communication, empathy, and structure — a close simulation of a real consultation.

Because every task is grounded in clinical context, many practising doctors find OET considerably more intuitive to prepare for than IELTS Academic. Writing a referral letter or conducting a role-played consultation draws directly on skills a working doctor already uses daily, whereas an unfamiliar general-interest reading passage or discursive essay topic requires a different, less familiar kind of preparation entirely.

IELTS Academic: General-Purpose English, Not Medically Themed

IELTS Academic, by contrast, is a general-purpose test used across many fields — not just medicine — and its content reflects that breadth. The four components are:

  • Listening (approximately 30 minutes): four recorded sections of increasing difficulty, covering everyday conversations, monologues, and academic discussions on non-medical subjects.
  • Reading (60 minutes): three long academic passages drawn from journals, books, and periodicals on topics that can range across science, history, urban planning, or the arts — content with no guarantee of any clinical relevance.
  • Writing (60 minutes): two tasks — a shorter report describing a graph, chart, or diagram (Task 1), and a longer discursive essay responding to a general point of view or argument (Task 2).
  • Speaking (11 to 14 minutes): a face-to-face or video-call interview with a certified examiner, covering general questions about familiar topics, a two-minute individual long-turn on a given cue card, and a follow-up discussion exploring more abstract aspects of the same topic.

Consequently, IELTS Academic tests general academic and communicative English rather than healthcare-specific English. A candidate's command of spoken and written English can be excellent in a clinical setting while still requiring dedicated practice for IELTS's non-medical Reading passages, essay-writing conventions, and broader Speaking topics — this is precisely why preparation time invested in one test does not transfer especially well to the other.

Which Format Suits Which Kind of Candidate

As a general pattern — not a fixed rule, since individual variation is significant — candidates who have been working clinically, writing discharge summaries, taking histories, and communicating with patients day to day, tend to find OET's healthcare framing plays directly to existing strengths. Meanwhile, candidates who are more comfortable with formal academic writing, structured essay argumentation, or who are already sitting IELTS for an unrelated purpose such as a university application or a visa, may find it more efficient to sit a single IELTS test that serves multiple goals at once. Recent medical graduates who have not yet built up extensive clinical correspondence experience sometimes find IELTS's more generic format slightly less intimidating for that reason, while doctors several years into practice often gravitate toward OET precisely because its tasks feel like an extension of their daily work rather than an unfamiliar exam genre.

Exact Score Thresholds the GMC Requires

Whichever test a candidate ultimately sits, the GMC applies a strict, non-negotiable scoring standard, and understanding this standard in precise detail before booking a test avoids one of the most common and entirely avoidable causes of delay in the registration pathway: narrowly missing the required score in a single component and having to re-sit.

IELTS Academic Score Requirement

For GMC registration, the required IELTS Academic score is an overall band score of 7.5, and — this is the detail that trips up otherwise strong candidates — no individual component may fall below 7. In other words, a candidate who scores 9.0 in Reading, 8.5 in Listening, and 8.0 in Writing, but only 6.5 in Speaking, does not meet the GMC's requirement, even though their overall average comfortably clears 7.5. Each of the four components — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking — must independently clear the 7 threshold, and the overall band score across all four must reach 7.5. This all-components-plus-overall rule is the single most common reason candidates need to re-sit the entire test, and it is worth building a preparation plan around whichever specific skill is weakest, rather than around the test as a whole.

It is also worth restating a detail that is easy to overlook when researching IELTS informally: the GMC accepts IELTS Academic specifically. IELTS General Training — the version more commonly associated with immigration applications to countries such as Australia or Canada — is not accepted as evidence for GMC registration, however strong the resulting score. Candidates should always confirm they are booking the Academic version at the point of registration, since the two versions are booked through the same providers and the distinction is occasionally missed by candidates more familiar with General Training from a previous, unrelated application.

OET Score Requirement

For GMC registration, OET requires a minimum of Grade B (350 or above on OET's numerical 0–500 scale) in all four components — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking — achieved in a single sitting. As with IELTS, this is an all-components requirement: a candidate scoring Grade A in three sub-tests but Grade C in the fourth does not meet the GMC's standard for that sitting. OET does, however, offer a practical advantage here that IELTS generally does not: in certain circumstances, candidates who fall short in only one or two components may be able to re-sit those specific sub-tests rather than the entire test, which can meaningfully reduce both the cost and the time lost to a narrow miss. The current rules on partial re-sits should always be confirmed directly with OET at the time of booking, since eligibility can depend on how recently the original sitting took place.

Why the "All Components" Rule Catches Strong Candidates Out

Both scoring systems apply the same underlying philosophy: an excellent average is not sufficient if any single skill falls below the floor. This reflects the GMC's reasoning that safe clinical communication requires competence across listening, reading, writing, and speaking simultaneously — a doctor who reads and writes exceptionally well but cannot reliably follow a rapid, accented conversation, or vice versa, still poses a genuine patient-safety risk in day-to-day practice. Candidates who treat English testing as a single combined "score to beat" rather than four independent hurdles are the ones most likely to be caught out by this rule, typically in the Speaking component, which both test providers' own data consistently shows to be the most common component to fall marginally short among IMG candidates generally.

Certificate Validity and Application Timing

Both IELTS and OET results share an identical validity rule for GMC purposes: certificates are valid for two years from the date of the test, and — this is the detail that matters most for sequencing — that validity must still hold at the point the full GMC registration application is actually submitted, not merely at the point the candidate decides which test to sit or even at the point the certificate first arrives. A certificate that expires between passing the test and submitting the registration application is treated by the GMC exactly as though no valid evidence had been provided, forcing an entirely avoidable re-sit.

This matters in practice because GMC registration depends on several parallel workstreams — primary source verification of the medical qualification, sitting and passing the UKMLA's Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), assembling supporting documents, and only then submitting the full application. These processes do not always move at a predictable pace, and a certificate sat too early in the sequence — for example, immediately after graduation, well before qualification verification is even underway — risks quietly expiring before the rest of the pathway catches up. The safer practical approach is to treat English testing as a mid-pathway milestone rather than a first-day task: begin it once qualification verification is underway and the AKT is being actively prepared for, so the certificate's two-year validity window comfortably covers the expected date of the full registration application, with a reasonable buffer for unexpected delays elsewhere in the process.

Candidates who have already worked through eligibility questions such as which qualifications the GMC recognises should consult the dedicated guide to which primary medical qualifications the GMC recognises before finalising an English test date, since confirming basic eligibility first avoids sitting an English test for a registration pathway that turns out not to apply. Similarly, doctors preparing to move into UK practice more broadly should read the guide on the GMC's welcome to UK practice induction programme, since English testing is only one stage among several that need to be sequenced correctly relative to each other.

Cost and Availability Considerations

Cost and test-centre availability are two of the more practical factors in deciding OET vs IELTS for GMC registration, alongside format and content. Both test providers revise their fees periodically, so any specific figure quoted here should be treated as indicative and cross-checked directly with the relevant provider before booking. That said, some general patterns hold consistently across most countries where IMGs sit these tests.

Cost

IELTS Academic and OET are typically priced within a broadly similar range of each other in most countries, though OET has, in many markets, historically carried a marginally higher fee than IELTS Academic, reflecting its more specialised, profession-specific examiner and marking requirements. Beyond the headline test fee, candidates should budget realistically for the possibility of a re-sit, since the all-components scoring rule described above means a strong overall performance with one narrowly missed component still requires paying again — for OET, potentially only for the specific sub-test that fell short, where that flexibility is available; for IELTS, generally for the whole test. A realistic total budget should assume at least one possible re-sit is a normal part of the process, not an exceptional failure, particularly for candidates whose weakest skill is Speaking.

Availability

IELTS Academic benefits from an exceptionally wide global test-centre network, operated jointly by the British Council, IDP Education, and Cambridge Assessment English, with sittings available in the large majority of countries multiple times per month and, in many locations, multiple times per week via the computer-delivered format. OET's global network has expanded substantially in recent years as the test has grown in popularity among healthcare professionals applying not only to the UK but also to Australia, New Zealand, and Ireland, though its centre density in some smaller or more remote locations can still lag behind IELTS's longer-established network. Candidates outside major cities should check current centre locations and sitting dates directly through each provider's official booking system rather than assuming availability based on a neighbouring country's test calendar.

Results turnaround also differs slightly: computer-delivered IELTS results are typically available within a matter of days, while OET results generally take a little longer to process, though both providers have continued to speed up turnaround for on-screen sittings over recent years. Neither turnaround difference is usually significant enough on its own to decide between the two tests, but it is worth factoring into a tightly sequenced application timeline.

Which Test Tends to Suit Which Kind of Candidate

Bringing the format, scoring, and practical factors together, a few broad patterns are worth stating plainly, while acknowledging that individual variation always matters more than any general rule.

  • Practising clinicians with several years of NHS-style documentation experience — writing discharge summaries, referral letters, and clinical notes regularly — often find OET's Writing and Speaking tasks feel like a natural extension of daily work, reducing the amount of genuinely new preparation required.
  • Recent graduates with strong general academic English — particularly those who have already prepared for IELTS for a university application, scholarship, or general immigration purpose — may find it more time-efficient to sit a single IELTS Academic test that serves both goals, rather than learning an entirely new test format from scratch.
  • Candidates whose weakest skill is Speaking under exam conditions — a very common pattern among IMGs generally — should weigh OET's role-played consultation format against IELTS's open-topic interview and choose whichever style plays better to their existing comfort zone, since Speaking is the component most likely to determine whether a re-sit is needed either way.
  • Candidates planning to apply to multiple English-speaking healthcare systems (for example, the UK alongside Australia, New Zealand, or Ireland) may find OET's broader acceptance across those specific healthcare regulators makes a single OET sitting more efficient than sitting different tests for different countries.
  • Candidates who also need an English test result for an unrelated visa or immigration purpose should check whether that specific purpose requires IELTS for UKVI or another Home Office-approved Secure English Language Test (SELT), since standard IELTS Academic and OET results sat for GMC purposes are not automatically the same product as a SELT booked for a visa application — this distinction is explored further in the section below.

No test is objectively "easier" than the other in any measurable sense — both are calibrated by their respective providers to a comparable standard of rigour. The better choice is consistently the one that matches an individual candidate's existing communication style and professional background, not a generic reputation either test may have acquired online.

The 2026 Visa Language Requirement: A New Wrinkle Worth Understanding

Alongside the GMC's own English language requirement for registration, a separate development in UK immigration rules has become relevant to IMGs from 2026 onward, and it is worth understanding clearly since it can affect which test a candidate chooses to sit and when.

What Changed on 8 January 2026

From 8 January 2026, in addition to meeting the GMC's own English language requirement for registration, doctors applying for a UK work visa must also meet a minimum English language level of CEFR B2 (upper-intermediate) under UK immigration rules, applicable to new Skilled Worker visa applications (including the Health and Care Worker visa route most doctors use) and settlement applications from that date. This raised the previous standard immigration-rules threshold, which had generally sat at CEFR B1, to B2 for applications made on or after the effective date.

This is a genuinely separate requirement from the GMC's own English language rule for registration — the GMC assesses whether a doctor can communicate safely in a clinical setting, while the Home Office assesses whether a visa applicant meets a general immigration-rules threshold for English proficiency. Historically, these two requirements have not always been identical in scope, source, or evidentiary format, which is precisely why this 2026 change is worth understanding on its own terms rather than assuming that clearing the GMC's bar automatically and unconditionally satisfies the separate visa bar.

How an Existing OET or IELTS Certificate May Help

Because CEFR B2 sits at or below the practical standard already required for GMC registration — the GMC's IELTS threshold of overall 7.5 and OET's Grade B both correspond to English proficiency comfortably at or above B2 on the CEFR scale — a doctor who has already used OET or IELTS to satisfy the GMC's registration requirement may not need to provide separate, additional English language evidence for a Skilled Worker visa application or a subsequent application for settlement (Indefinite Leave to Remain). UK immigration guidance has generally recognised that a registered health professional's English assessment, when accepted by their regulatory body, can be treated as satisfying the parallel immigration-rules requirement, sparing the applicant from sitting a separate Secure English Language Test purely for visa purposes.

This is genuinely useful guidance in principle, but it is exactly the kind of cross-departmental rule that benefits from direct, current verification before anyone relies on it for a specific application. Home Office immigration rules are reviewed and amended relatively frequently, sometimes with limited advance notice, and the precise mechanics of how a professional body's English assessment is recognised for immigration purposes can change independently of the GMC's own registration rules. Doctors relying on this route should double-check the current position directly on gov.uk's Skilled Worker visa English language guidance or with a qualified immigration adviser before assuming an existing OET or IELTS certificate removes the need for any further evidence — this article is not immigration advice, and the specific evidentiary requirements for a given visa application should always be confirmed at the time that application is actually prepared.

Why This Matters When Choosing Between OET and IELTS

This 2026 change adds a small but genuine consideration to the OET-versus-IELTS decision that did not exist in quite the same form previously: a candidate who is confident they will also need to satisfy a UK visa's English requirement, not just the GMC's registration requirement, has an added incentive to ensure whichever test they sit is very clearly and comfortably above both bars, since a certificate that only just scrapes the GMC's minimum leaves less margin if a visa caseworker requests additional clarification. It also reinforces the practical value of understanding the other 2026 changes affecting doctors on UK work visas, since English language requirements are only one part of a wider set of immigration-rules updates that took effect around the same period and are worth reading together rather than in isolation.

Exemptions From the English Language Test

The GMC does allow limited exemptions from its standard English language testing requirement in specific, narrowly defined circumstances — most notably for doctors who are nationals of a majority-English-speaking country and were also awarded their primary medical qualification there, and for doctors who have recently and substantially practised medicine within another majority-English-speaking healthcare system. However, these exemption criteria are assessed case by case, can change over time as GMC policy is reviewed, and should never be assumed to apply without direct confirmation. The site's dedicated guide to GMC English language exemptions for IMGs sets out exactly who qualifies and how to check, and should be consulted before any candidate decides to skip test preparation on the assumption that an exemption will apply to their own circumstances.

For the great majority of IMGs applying for GMC registration without a period of substantial recent practice in a majority-English-speaking health system, the realistic expectation should be that either IELTS Academic or OET Medicine will need to be sat as a standard, planned part of the pathway, rather than treated as an optional step that might be waived. As with every other detail in this comparison, the current exemption criteria are set out directly on the GMC's own evidence of your knowledge of English page, and should be checked there before any candidate assumes an exemption applies to their own circumstances.

Practical Steps: Choosing and Preparing for the Right Test

Having compared the two tests in detail, a structured decision process is more reliable than choosing based on general reputation or anecdote alone.

Step 1: Assess Genuine Strengths Honestly

Before booking either test, it is worth sitting a full official practice test for each — under strict, timed conditions — to establish a genuine baseline across all four components for both formats. This diagnostic step reveals whether a candidate's gap to the required score is a single-component problem (most commonly Speaking) or a broader issue affecting the whole test, and whether that gap looks smaller under OET's clinical framing or IELTS's general-academic framing.

Step 2: Factor In Practical Constraints

Test-centre availability, results turnaround time, and cost in the candidate's own country should all factor into the final decision alongside raw suitability. A candidate who is otherwise evenly matched between the two tests but has significantly easier access to one format locally, or a tighter results-turnaround requirement because of an approaching application deadline, may reasonably let that practical factor tip the decision.

Candidates who are still finalising their overall study plan may find it useful to read the general guide to preparing for the UKMLA alongside this comparison, since English test preparation and AKT/CPSA preparation draw on overlapping but distinct skills and are best planned as parallel workstreams rather than sequential ones.

Step 3: Prepare With Official Material First

Both British Council/IDP/Cambridge Assessment English (for IELTS) and Cambridge Boxhill Language Assessment (for OET) publish official practice tests and sample answers, and these should form the backbone of preparation before turning to third-party resources, since official material most accurately reflects each test's actual scoring criteria and task types.

Step 4: Sequence the Test Correctly Within the Wider Pathway

As set out above, English testing should generally be sequenced as a mid-pathway milestone — begun once qualification verification is underway — rather than as the very first task after graduation or left until immediately before the registration application is due. Getting this sequencing right is what protects the certificate's two-year validity window and avoids an unplanned, costly re-sit driven purely by poor timing rather than a genuine language shortfall.

Step 5: Budget for a Possible Re-Sit

Given how common it is for otherwise strong candidates to fall marginally short in a single component — usually Speaking — a realistic budget and timeline should assume at least the possibility of one re-sit, rather than assuming a first-attempt clean pass across all four components in every case. Building this contingency into an overall plan is more realistic than budgeting only for the best-case outcome.

Frequently Asked Questions

The questions below address the most common, practical points candidates raise when researching OET vs IELTS for GMC registration, each answered as a self-contained response.

Is OET or IELTS better for GMC registration?

Neither test is officially preferred by the GMC — both are fully accepted, provided the required scores are met. IELTS Academic requires an overall 7.5 with no component below 7, while OET requires Grade B (350+) in all four components in a single sitting. The better choice depends on whether a candidate is more comfortable with general academic English (IELTS) or healthcare-themed content such as referral letters and clinical consultations (OET).

What IELTS score is needed for GMC registration?

The GMC requires an overall IELTS Academic band score of 7.5, with no individual component — Listening, Reading, Writing, or Speaking — scoring below 7. This is an all-components-plus-overall requirement, so a strong overall average does not compensate for a single component falling short of 7. The GMC also requires the Academic version of IELTS specifically; IELTS General Training is not accepted.

What OET score is needed for GMC registration?

The GMC requires a minimum of Grade B, equivalent to a score of 350 or above on OET's 0–500 numerical scale, in all four components — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking — achieved in a single sitting. A candidate who scores below Grade B in even one component does not meet the GMC's requirement for that sitting, though single-sub-test re-sits may be available in some circumstances.

How long are OET and IELTS results valid for GMC registration?

Both OET and IELTS results are valid for two years from the date the test was sat, and this validity must still hold at the point the full GMC registration application is submitted, not merely when the test is taken or the certificate first issued. Candidates should sequence their test so its two-year window comfortably covers their expected application date, since an expired certificate is treated as though no evidence had been provided at all.

Does OET satisfy the new 2026 UK visa English requirement?

From 8 January 2026, UK Skilled Worker and settlement applications generally require a minimum of CEFR B2 under immigration rules, separate from the GMC's own registration requirement. Because the GMC's OET and IELTS thresholds already sit at or above B2, a doctor who has used OET or IELTS to meet the GMC's registration requirement may not need additional evidence for a visa or settlement application — but this should always be verified directly against current Home Office rules before an application is submitted, since immigration requirements are reviewed and can change.

Can I use IELTS General Training instead of IELTS Academic for GMC registration?

No. The GMC accepts only IELTS Academic for registration purposes; IELTS General Training, which is more commonly used for immigration or non-academic purposes, is not accepted regardless of the score achieved. Candidates should confirm they are booking the Academic version specifically when scheduling a test for GMC purposes.

Is OET easier than IELTS for doctors?

Neither test is objectively easier; both are calibrated to a comparable standard of rigour by their respective providers. Many practising doctors find OET more intuitive because its tasks are built around genuine clinical scenarios they already encounter daily, while candidates more comfortable with formal academic writing and general-knowledge topics sometimes find IELTS Academic a better fit. The right test is the one that matches an individual candidate's existing communication style.

Can I re-sit only one component if I fail OET or IELTS?

OET allows single-sub-test re-sits in some circumstances, meaning a candidate who falls short in only one or two components may not need to re-sit the entire test — current eligibility for this should be confirmed directly with OET at the time of booking. IELTS, by contrast, generally requires the entire test to be re-sat if any single component falls below the required threshold, even if the other three components comfortably passed.

Conclusion

There is no universally "correct" answer to OET vs IELTS for GMC registration — both tests are fully accepted, both apply a demanding all-components scoring standard, and both carry the same two-year validity window. The decision comes down to matching the test's format to the candidate's own strengths: OET's healthcare-themed tasks tend to suit practising clinicians comfortable with clinical documentation and consultation-style speaking, while IELTS Academic tends to suit candidates confident with general academic writing and broader topic areas. Whichever test is chosen, hitting the exact thresholds the GMC requires — 7.5 overall with no component below 7 for IELTS, or Grade B across all four components for OET — and sequencing the test correctly against the two-year validity window are what actually determine whether English testing becomes a smooth, on-schedule stage of registration or an unplanned source of delay. Always confirm current thresholds, accepted tests, and the CEFR B2 visa wrinkle directly with the GMC and the Home Office before finalising a test booking, since both sets of rules are reviewed periodically and can change.
