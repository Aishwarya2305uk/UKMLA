Introduction: Psychiatry in the UKMLA

Psychiatry is one of the highest-yield domains in the UKMLA. The Global Burden of Disease study consistently ranks mental illness as the leading cause of years lived with disability worldwide, and the UK prevalence of conditions such as depression, anxiety disorders, psychosis, and dementia means that every doctor — regardless of eventual specialty — will encounter patients with mental health problems regularly. The GMC's Outcomes for Graduates explicitly requires that medical graduates can assess mental state, recognise psychiatric emergencies, apply the Mental Health Act and Mental Capacity Act appropriately, and communicate sensitively with patients experiencing mental illness.

In the AKT, psychiatry questions test diagnostic criteria, risk assessment, pharmacological treatment, legislation, and safeguarding. In the CPSA, psychiatry stations are among the most complex — they require not only clinical knowledge but the full range of professional and communication skills assessed in the UKMLA. The mental state examination is the fundamental clinical tool that underpins all CPSA psychiatry stations, and mastering its structure and documentation is essential for any student aiming to perform well in the clinical assessment.

This guide covers everything you need to know: what the AKT tests in psychiatry, the full structure of the Mental State Examination for CPSA stations, common CPSA psychiatry station types with detailed approach guidance, and key legislation you must understand. What the AKT Tests in Psychiatry

Diagnostic Criteria and Classification

The AKT uses DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria as the basis for psychiatric diagnosis questions, though questions are written to test clinical application rather than verbatim memorisation of criteria. You should know the core diagnostic features of: depressive episode (low mood, anhedonia, fatigue, sleep disturbance, appetite change, poor concentration, guilt, suicidality; classified by severity — mild requires two core symptoms plus two others, moderate three core plus three, severe all three core plus four or more, psychotic depression adds hallucinations or delusions); generalised anxiety disorder (excessive worry about multiple domains, physical anxiety symptoms, at least six months duration); panic disorder (recurrent unexpected panic attacks plus anticipatory anxiety); obsessive-compulsive disorder (obsessions and/or compulsions, time-consuming, causing significant distress); post-traumatic stress disorder (traumatic event, intrusions, avoidance, negative cognitions and mood, hyperarousal, more than one month); schizophrenia spectrum disorders (positive symptoms — hallucinations, delusions, disorganised speech/behaviour; negative symptoms — alogia, avolition, flat affect, anhedonia, asociality; at least one month duration, significant functional impairment); bipolar disorder type 1 (manic episodes with or without depressive episodes) and type 2 (hypomanic and depressive episodes); and dementia syndromes (Alzheimer's — gradual onset memory-first; Lewy body — visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, fluctuating cognition, REM sleep behaviour disorder; frontotemporal — personality and language first, relative memory sparing; vascular — stepwise decline, cardiovascular risk factors).

Risk Assessment

Risk assessment is both an AKT knowledge topic and a CPSA skill. AKT questions test your knowledge of risk factors for suicide (male sex, older age, previous attempt, hopelessness, social isolation, chronic pain, substance misuse, recent life events, access to means, mental illness especially depression, borderline personality disorder, schizophrenia), protective factors (reasons for living, future orientation, social support, religious beliefs, engagement with treatment, children at home), and the evidence-based assessment tools (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale is the most widely validated in clinical settings).

Psychiatry Pharmacology

Pharmacology questions in psychiatry cover: antidepressants (SSRIs as first-line for depression and anxiety disorders; common side effects including GI upset, initial anxiety, sexual dysfunction, hyponatraemia, SIADH; serotonin syndrome risk with combination treatments; SNRIs for generalised anxiety disorder and pain conditions; tricyclics — amitriptyline/nortriptyline — cardiotoxic in overdose, monitor ECG, useful for neuropathic pain and migraine prophylaxis; MAOIs — now rarely used, cheese reaction with tyramine); antipsychotics (typical antipsychotics — haloperidol/chlorpromazine — D2 blockade, extrapyramidal side effects including acute dystonia/akathisia/parkinsonism/tardive dyskinesia, high prolactin; atypical antipsychotics — olanzapine/quetiapine/risperidone/aripiprazole/clozapine — better tolerated, metabolic side effects including weight gain, dyslipidaemia, diabetes; clozapine — reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, requires FBC monitoring for agranulocytosis, also causes seizures, myocarditis); mood stabilisers (lithium — narrow therapeutic index 0.4-1.0 mmol/L, toxicity risk above 1.5 mmol/L, signs including tremor/ataxia/confusion/arrhythmia, monitor renal and thyroid function, teratogenic — Ebstein's anomaly; sodium valproate — effective for bipolar mania, teratogenic causing neural tube defects and developmental delay, avoid in women of childbearing age or use with highly effective contraception and full counselling; lamotrigine — preferred for bipolar depression in women, Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk especially with rapid titration); anxiolytics (benzodiazepines — lorazepam/diazepam — acute anxiety and alcohol withdrawal, dependence risk, not for long-term use; buspirone — non-addictive for generalised anxiety disorder).

Mental Health Act and Mental Capacity Act

Mental health legislation is a high-frequency AKT topic. Key Mental Health Act 1983 (amended 2007) sections: Section 2 — assessment, up to 28 days, requires two doctors and approved mental health professional (AMHP), one doctor must be Section 12 approved; Section 3 — treatment, up to 6 months (renewable), same requirements as Section 2; Section 4 — emergency admission, up to 72 hours, one doctor only, used when Section 2 delay would involve undesirable delay; Section 5(2) — detention of informal patient by responsible clinician, up to 72 hours; Section 5(4) — detention by registered mental health or learning disability nurse, up to 6 hours; Section 136 — police power to remove person from public place to place of safety, up to 24 hours (extendable to 36). Mental Capacity Act 2005: the four components of capacity — understand information relevant to the decision, retain it long enough to make the decision, weigh or use the information, communicate the decision; capacity is decision-specific and time-specific; best interests decision-making process for those who lack capacity; lasting power of attorney — property and financial affairs, health and welfare (must be activated); Independent Mental Capacity Advocate required for unbefriended patients facing serious medical treatment or accommodation decisions.

The Mental State Examination for CPSA Stations

The Mental State Examination is the psychiatric equivalent of a physical examination — a structured, systematic assessment of a patient's current mental functioning documented in a standardised format. In CPSA psychiatry stations, you are expected to conduct components of the MSE as part of your clinical assessment, and to document your findings accurately. Examiners specifically look for systematic coverage of all MSE domains, accurate use of psychiatric terminology, and integration of MSE findings into your clinical formulation and management planning.

Appearance

Observe and document: age appearance relative to stated age (older appearing suggests chronic illness, self-neglect, or substance misuse); grooming and personal hygiene (poor grooming in depression, mania with flamboyant dress, self-neglect in psychosis or dementia); dress (appropriate to weather and context, unusual or flamboyant in mania); eye contact (reduced in depression, avoidant, or culturally normal — note context); abnormal movements (tremor suggesting medication side effects or Parkinson's; tardive dyskinesia from antipsychotics; psychomotor retardation in severe depression; restlessness/akathisia from antipsychotics; tics).

Behaviour

Document: level of cooperation with assessment; rapport (easily established, guarded, hostile, suspicious); psychomotor activity — agitation (increased purposeless movement in anxiety, mania, akathisia) or retardation (slowed movement and speech in severe depression, catatonia); eye contact; any unusual behaviours observed during interview (responding to hallucinations, unprovoked laughter, stereotyped movements).

Speech

Assess and document: rate (fast in mania — pressure of speech; slow in depression or sedation); volume (loud in mania; quiet and monotonous in depression); tone and prosody (flat in depression or negative schizophrenia); fluency (dysphasia — expressive or receptive — suggests organic cause); coherence (ability to follow a logical thread); thought-language relationship (whether language accurately conveys thought).

Mood and Affect

Mood is subjective — what the patient reports about their emotional state ("How would you describe your mood?" — document their exact words using quotation marks). Affect is objective — what the examiner observes about the patient's emotional expression during the interview. Document affect as: normal range; restricted (narrowed range, less expressive than normal); blunted (markedly reduced range and intensity, emotionally flat); flat (almost complete absence of emotional expression, monotonous voice, blank face); labile (rapidly shifting between emotional states without clear external cause); elated (elevated, euphoric, infectious quality in mania); dysphoric (irritable, anxious, or unpleasant). Document congruence between mood and affect (congruent = affect matches reported mood; incongruent = dissociation, e.g., patient laughing while describing sad events, seen in psychosis).

Thought Form

Thought form refers to the way thoughts are connected and expressed — the process rather than the content. Document: flight of ideas (rapid acceleration of thought with understandable connections between ideas, typical of mania); thought block (sudden complete arrest of a train of thought, experienced as mind going blank, associated with schizophrenia); loosening of associations (thought connections become difficult to follow, tangential or absent — knight's move thinking); perseveration (repetition of a word, phrase, or theme despite changed questions, seen in organic states and schizophrenia); circumstantiality (over-inclusive thinking, patient eventually reaches the point but via many diversions); tangentiality (patient moves away from the point and never returns); thought broadcast (patient believes thoughts are being broadcast so others can hear them — Schneiderian first-rank symptom); thought insertion (patient believes thoughts are being inserted into their mind from outside — first-rank symptom); thought withdrawal (patient believes thoughts are being removed from their mind — first-rank symptom).

Thought Content

Thought content refers to what the patient is thinking about — the themes and preoccupations. Document: delusions (fixed, false beliefs held with conviction not amenable to reason, not consistent with cultural or religious background); classify delusions by type: persecutory (being followed, watched, or harmed), grandiose (special powers, identity, or mission — prominent in mania), reference (neutral events refer specifically to the patient — TV news directed at them), nihilistic (belief that the self, others, or the world does not exist or has been destroyed — Cotard syndrome in severe depression), somatic (false belief about the body — infestation with parasites, internal organ disease), jealousy (erotomania or morbid jealousy), and religious delusions; also note overvalued ideas (intensely held beliefs not quite of delusional intensity, with some emotional investment) and obsessions (intrusive, recurrent, egodystonic thoughts recognised by the patient as their own but unwanted).

Perceptions

Perceptions refer to the patient's sensory experiences. Document: hallucinations (perceptions occurring in the absence of an external stimulus; classified by modality — auditory hallucinations are most common in functional psychosis, command hallucinations carry high risk, third-person voices discussing the patient in the third person and running commentary are Schneiderian first-rank symptoms; visual hallucinations more common in organic and substance-related states; tactile hallucinations in alcohol withdrawal and cocaine use; olfactory and gustatory hallucinations in temporal lobe epilepsy); illusions (misperceptions of real stimuli, more common in delirium); depersonalisation (feeling detached from oneself, as if watching from outside — common in anxiety, depression, and dissociative disorders); derealisation (the external world feels unreal, dreamlike — similarly common in anxiety and dissociation).

Cognition

Cognitive assessment is essential in all psychiatric presentations, not only where dementia or delirium is suspected, because cognitive deficits affect capacity assessment, medication management, and prognosis. In CPSA stations, brief cognitive testing is appropriate: the AMT-4 (Abbreviated Mental Test Score four questions: age, date of birth, place, year — scores 4 out of 4 expected in normal cognition) or AMT-10 (full ten-question version) for bedside screening; the MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination) scores from 0 to 30, with below 24 suggesting cognitive impairment, 21-23 mild, 10-20 moderate, below 10 severe impairment; the MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) is more sensitive than MMSE for mild cognitive impairment, scores from 0 to 30, below 26 abnormal. Document findings in all cognitive domains: orientation (time, place, person), attention and concentration (serial 7s, months of year backwards), memory (registration, short-term recall after three minutes, long-term), language (naming, repetition, reading, writing), praxis (copying a figure, drawing a clock), and executive function (verbal fluency, abstraction, trail making).

Insight

Insight is the patient's understanding of their own mental illness. Document: whether the patient acknowledges that something is wrong, whether they recognise it as a mental illness rather than a physical problem, whether they accept that they need treatment, and whether they comply with treatment. Insight exists on a spectrum: full insight (patient understands their diagnosis, recognises their experiences as symptoms of illness, accepts need for treatment), partial insight (some understanding but incomplete acceptance — e.g., acknowledges distress but attributes it to external circumstances rather than illness), and no insight (complete lack of recognition of illness — common in acute mania and paranoid psychosis).

Risk Assessment within the MSE

Risk assessment is integrated throughout the MSE and should be documented explicitly in a structured format. Document: risk to self (suicidal ideation — passive ideation "life not worth living" versus active ideation with plan and intent; current ideation, previous attempts with details of method, medical severity and concealment of previous attempts, protective factors, access to means, current level of intent and plan, command hallucinations directing self-harm); risk to others (homicidal ideation, specific threats or targets, command hallucinations directing violence, historical violence, access to weapons, substance misuse disinhibiting violence); risk from others (safeguarding concerns, exploitation, abuse, neglect — particularly in patients with cognitive impairment, learning disabilities, or severe mental illness).

Common CPSA Psychiatry Station Types

Risk Assessment: Suicide

Suicide risk assessment stations test your ability to explore suicidal ideation systematically, assess risk factors and protective factors, formulate risk, and communicate your management plan. Begin with a warm, direct approach — research shows that asking directly about suicide does not increase risk and is essential for accurate assessment. Use open questions initially ("I'd like to ask you some questions about how things are for you in terms of thoughts about harming yourself — is that alright?") before moving to structured enquiry.

The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale provides a validated framework: ideation type (wish to be dead; active suicidal ideation without plan; active ideation with some plan; active ideation with specific plan and intent), behaviour (preparatory behaviour, interrupted attempt, aborted attempt, actual attempt), and intensity modifiers (controllability, deterrents, reasons for ideation, duration and frequency). For CPSA stations, you do not need to administer the full CSS-RS, but demonstrating knowledge of its dimensions — specifically distinguishing passive ideation from active ideation with or without intent and plan — shows systematic risk thinking.

After exploring ideation, explore risk factors (previous attempts — single strongest predictor of future attempt; hopelessness — stronger predictor than depression severity alone; social isolation; substance misuse; chronic illness or pain; recent loss or life event; access to means) and protective factors (reasons for living, children or dependants, religious beliefs, future orientation, therapeutic relationship, family support). Conclude with explicit risk formulation ("Based on what you've told me, I am concerned about your safety because...") and management plan (if immediate risk, urgent psychiatric referral and cannot leave alone; if high but not immediate, same-day assessment and safety plan; if moderate, community follow-up and safety planning; discuss information sharing if patient does not consent but risk to life is serious — threshold for breaching confidentiality).

Mental Capacity Assessment

Capacity assessment stations require you to apply the four-part Mental Capacity Act test to a specific decision. The four components must be assessed for the specific decision in question — capacity is decision-specific and time-specific, not global. A patient may have capacity for some decisions (which visitors to see) and lack capacity for others (consent to major surgery) simultaneously.

Understand: Can the patient understand the information relevant to the decision when it is explained in accessible terms? Can they retain it long enough to make the decision (even short-term retention of a few minutes may suffice)? Can they weigh or use the information — consider the pros, cons, risks, and alternatives? Can they communicate their decision by any means — speech, writing, AAC device, gestures? Any doctor can assess capacity for the decision within their clinical remit — it does not require a psychiatrist. Document each of the four components explicitly, with evidence from the assessment. If capacity is lacking, proceed to best interests decision-making: consider the patient's past wishes, values, and beliefs; consult relevant people (family, carers) unless patient has expressed a wish for privacy; consider least restrictive option; document the process and rationale.

Depressive Illness Assessment

Depression assessment stations require systematic history taking covering all diagnostic features, severity assessment, and treatment discussion. Structure your approach: establish the presenting complaint in the patient's own words; explore core symptoms (low mood, anhedonia, fatigue) and associated symptoms (sleep disturbance — typically early morning awakening in biological depression; appetite change with weight loss or gain; poor concentration; guilt and worthlessness; psychomotor retardation or agitation; suicidal ideation); assess severity using PHQ-9 as a structured tool (scores 0-27; 0-4 minimal, 5-9 mild, 10-14 moderate, 15-19 moderately severe, 20-27 severe); identify biological features (early morning awakening, diurnal variation with mood worse in morning, weight loss, psychomotor changes, loss of libido); screen for psychotic features (mood-congruent delusions of guilt, poverty, nihilism; hallucinations); and exclude organic causes (hypothyroidism, vitamin B12 deficiency, anaemia, sleep apnoea, Addison's disease, occult malignancy).

Treatment discussion should follow NICE guidance (CG90/CG91): mild depression — watchful waiting, sleep hygiene, exercise, self-help, structured psychological intervention (CBT, behavioural activation); moderate to severe depression — antidepressant (SSRI first-line) plus high-intensity psychological therapy (CBT); severe or psychotic depression — may require inpatient treatment, antidepressant plus antipsychotic, and ultimately ECT if treatment-resistant; review within two weeks of starting treatment, continue for six months after remission for first episode, two years after second episode.

Psychosis Assessment

Psychosis assessment stations may involve a patient presenting with a first episode or a relapse. The approach integrates a full MSE with history taking focused on: duration of symptoms (at least one month for schizophrenia diagnosis; less than one month — schizophreniform or drug-induced psychosis); pervasiveness and impact on functioning; positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganised speech and behaviour — screen specifically for Schneiderian first-rank symptoms as they have high diagnostic specificity for schizophrenia); negative symptoms (alogia, avolition, anhedonia, flat affect, asociality — often more disabling than positive symptoms but less dramatic); mood symptoms (distinguish schizoaffective disorder — concurrent mood episode of sufficient severity and duration); organic exclusion (urine drug screen for cannabis, amphetamines, cocaine, novel psychoactive substances; FBC, LFTs, TFTs, B12, folate, calcium, glucose, inflammatory markers; EEG if temporal lobe epilepsy suspected; MRI brain if organic pathology suspected; lumbar puncture for anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis if subacute onset with psychiatric and neurological features); and At-Risk Mental State (ARMS) criteria if prodromal presentation (attenuated psychotic symptoms not meeting full criteria, transient self-limiting psychotic experiences, family history of psychosis with functional decline).

Breaking Bad News in a Psychiatric Context

Breaking bad news stations in psychiatry might involve communicating a new diagnosis of schizophrenia or dementia to a patient or relative. Use the SPIKES protocol: Setting (private, quiet, appropriate people present); Perception (assess what the patient already understands about their condition); Invitation (ask how much the patient wants to know); Knowledge (deliver the information clearly, in small chunks, avoiding jargon — "schizophrenia" is a term patients often find distressing, so contextualise it: "a condition that affects how the brain processes information and can cause experiences like hearing voices"); Empathy (acknowledge emotional response with silence and empathic response before proceeding); Summary and Strategy (summarise, check understanding, outline next steps, provide written information, arrange follow-up).

Mental Health Act Explanation Station

MHA stations require you to explain detention to a patient or, more commonly, explain the process to a relative. Key points for patient explanation: they are being assessed or treated under the Mental Health Act because doctors are concerned about their mental health and safety; the specific section being used (Section 2 is for assessment up to 28 days; Section 3 is for treatment up to six months); they retain rights — to be informed of the reason for detention, to appeal to the Mental Health Tribunal, to have an Independent Mental Health Advocate (IMHA), to have access to their solicitor; informal admission (voluntary) is always preferred if the patient is willing and safe to be treated voluntarily; Section 4 emergency detention (one doctor only, 72 hours) is used when urgent admission is needed and two-doctor Section 2 cannot be organised without undesirable delay. Approach MHA explanation with empathy, acknowledging that it may feel frightening and that the aim is to help, not punish.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of the CPSA is psychiatry?

Psychiatry stations are a significant component of the CPSA, reflecting the high prevalence of mental health problems in clinical practice. You can expect at least one to two dedicated psychiatry stations, and elements of psychiatric assessment — particularly capacity assessment, safeguarding, and risk — may appear in stations nominally set in other specialties. Treat psychiatric communication skills as core competencies, not niche preparation.

Do I need to know the full MMSE by heart for the CPSA?

You should know how to conduct a brief cognitive assessment including the AMT-4, and be able to describe what a full MMSE or MoCA assesses. In a CPSA station you would use the abbreviated version appropriate to the time available, and document your findings. You should know the normal score ranges and what scores in each range suggest about severity of cognitive impairment.

When can I break confidentiality for a patient at risk of suicide?

Confidentiality can be broken without consent when there is serious risk to life — whether the patient's own life or the life of others. The risk must be serious, real, and immediate. You should always attempt to obtain consent first, explain why you need to share information, and share only the minimum necessary. Document your decision-making clearly. A threat of self-harm without clear immediate intent to act does not automatically justify confidentiality breach — the assessment of seriousness, imminence, and likelihood is a clinical judgment.

What is the difference between Section 2 and Section 3 of the Mental Health Act?

Section 2 is used for assessment — it allows detention for up to 28 days when a patient needs to be assessed for mental disorder and where voluntary admission is not appropriate. Section 3 is used for treatment — it allows detention for up to six months (renewable) when the diagnosis is known and treatment is needed. Both require applications from an AMHP and recommendations from two doctors (one of whom must be Section 12 approved). Section 2 is used when diagnosis is uncertain or a new assessment is needed; Section 3 when the patient has a known diagnosis and treatment is clearly indicated.

How do I assess capacity in a CPSA station?

Apply the four-part MCA test to the specific decision in question. Assess each component explicitly: provide information in clear, accessible terms and check understanding; assess ability to retain the information during the consultation; explore how the patient weighs the pros and cons; establish how they communicate their decision. Document each component with specific evidence from the assessment. If capacity is lacking, explain this respectfully to the patient and explain the best interests process. In a station, walk the examiner through your assessment verbally as you conduct it — they need to see your reasoning, not just your conclusion.

Are Schneiderian first-rank symptoms still clinically relevant?

Schneiderian first-rank symptoms — thought insertion, thought withdrawal, thought broadcast, passivity experiences, made actions/feelings/impulses, running commentary, and third-person voices discussing the patient — have high diagnostic specificity for schizophrenia (they rarely occur in other conditions) but are not pathognomonic and are not required for a schizophrenia diagnosis under DSM-5 or ICD-11. They remain clinically relevant because they directly indicate psychosis and their presence significantly affects risk assessment, management, and insight assessment. In the AKT, questions about first-rank symptoms specifically ask about their diagnostic significance in schizophrenia.

Conclusion

Psychiatry is a high-stakes domain in both the AKT and CPSA. In the AKT, you need solid knowledge of diagnostic criteria, pharmacology, the Mental Health Act, the Mental Capacity Act, and risk assessment principles. In the CPSA, you need the ability to conduct and document a systematic mental state examination, approach risk assessment sensitively and comprehensively, explain psychiatric diagnoses and legislation clearly, and demonstrate professional capabilities including maintaining a therapeutic relationship in challenging clinical situations.

The mental state examination is the clinical tool that ties all psychiatry CPSA stations together — it is the framework within which you gather and organise your clinical findings, whatever the specific station type. Master it systematically, practice documenting it, and approach every psychiatry station with the same structured calm you would bring to a physical examination.
