GMC Fitness to Practise Explained: What New Doctors Need to Know

GMC fitness to practise is the framework the General Medical Council uses to look into concerns about a doctor's practice, conduct or health, with the single aim of protecting patients and maintaining public confidence in the medical profession. If you have just registered with the GMC — whether you qualified in the UK or arrived as an international medical graduate (IMG) after clearing the Applied Knowledge Test and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment — you will have heard the phrase "fitness to practise" mentioned during induction, in your employment contract, or in Good Medical Practice itself, and it can sound intimidating if nobody has explained what it actually means in practice. This guide sets out, calmly and factually, what the fitness to practise framework is for, how a concern is typically handled from the moment it is raised, the range of possible outcomes (and how rarely the most serious ones occur), and the sensible day-one professional habits that meaningfully reduce risk for any newly registered doctor.

Nothing in this article is legal or procedural advice for an active case. If you are currently facing a concern, complaint, or GMC enquiry, the right first step is to contact your medical defence organisation or the British Medical Association (BMA) — not to rely on a general explainer. This piece exists purely so that a doctor new to UK practice understands the shape of the system they now sit within, in the same way they would want to understand any other part of their professional environment: calmly, in advance, and before it is ever personally relevant.

Quick Answer: What Is GMC Fitness to Practise?

Fitness to practise (FtP) is the GMC's statutory process for assessing whether a doctor's conduct, performance or health means they should face some restriction on their registration in order to protect patients and the public. The GMC publishes its own official explanation of how concerns about doctors are assessed, which is a useful primary source to bookmark alongside this guide. Concerns can come from patients, colleagues, employers, NHS trusts, coroners, the police, or the doctor's own disclosure of a health condition or criminal matter. The great majority of concerns raised with the GMC are resolved without any serious sanction — many are closed at an early stage with no further action, or resolved with advice or a warning — and only a small minority progress all the way to a hearing before the Medical Practitioners Tribunal Service (MPTS), which is the independent body that decides on sanctions in the most serious cases.

The reference point against which every fitness to practise concern is ultimately measured is Good Medical Practice, the GMC's core statement of the standards expected of every licensed doctor. If you have internalised Good Medical Practice, kept careful clinical records, and formed the habit of asking for help early when something goes wrong, you have already done the overwhelming majority of what "reduces FtP risk" actually means in real life. The rest of this guide fills in the detail behind that summary.

What Fitness to Practise Is For

It helps to start with the underlying purpose, because the framework makes far more sense once you understand what it is trying to achieve — and, just as importantly, what it is not trying to achieve.

Protecting Patients and Public Confidence, Not Punishing Doctors

The GMC's fitness to practise process exists to fulfil its statutory duty under the Medical Act 1983 to protect, promote and maintain the health, safety and wellbeing of the public, and to promote and maintain public confidence in the medical profession and proper professional standards and conduct for members of that profession. This is a deliberately forward-looking test. The GMC is not principally asking "should this doctor be punished for something that happened?" — it is asking "does this doctor's current fitness to practise pose a risk to patients or to public confidence in the profession, and if so, what is needed to address that risk?" That distinction matters enormously in practice. A single, well-handled clinical error, openly disclosed and learned from, is treated very differently from a pattern of concealment or repeated disregard for patient safety, even if the underlying clinical outcome was similar.

This is also why the process is not, and is not designed to be, a disciplinary tool wielded against doctors for minor or one-off lapses. The GMC's own guidance to case examiners repeatedly emphasises proportionality: the response to a concern should be proportionate to the risk it represents, not to how uncomfortable or high-profile the underlying incident felt at the time. For a newly registered doctor, understanding this distinction is often the single most reassuring piece of context available — the system is built around risk and pattern, not around treating every reported incident as presumptively serious.

Concerns Can Come From Many Different Sources

New doctors sometimes assume fitness to practise concerns arise only from a formal patient complaint. In reality, the GMC receives concerns from a wide range of sources, and understanding this list helps put any single concern in proportion:

  • Patients and their families, usually about communication, a missed diagnosis, or a perceived lapse in care
  • Colleagues and employers, including through NHS trust "duty to refer" obligations when a trust dismisses a doctor or accepts their resignation while an investigation into a serious concern is ongoing
  • Criminal justice sources, such as police forces or the courts, particularly following a caution or conviction
  • Coroners, following an inquest that raises questions about a doctor's care of a patient who died
  • Other regulators or public bodies, including the Care Quality Commission or equivalent bodies in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland
  • Self-referral, where a doctor discloses a health condition, a criminal caution, or a significant event affecting their own practice — something Good Medical Practice explicitly expects

Self-referral is worth pausing on, because it is often the source new doctors are least prepared for emotionally. Good Medical Practice asks doctors to tell the GMC promptly if they are subject to a criminal caution or conviction, or if a health condition might affect their ability to practise safely. This can feel counterintuitive — why would you disclose something yourself? — but in practice, doctors who disclose promptly and engage constructively are treated very differently from doctors whose non-disclosure is later discovered. Early, honest self-referral, ideally with support from a medical defence organisation from the outset, is consistently one of the factors that leads to a swifter and less severe outcome.

Fitness to Practise Sits Alongside Revalidation, Not Instead of It

It is worth briefly distinguishing fitness to practise from revalidation, since new doctors sometimes conflate the two. Revalidation is the routine, five-yearly process by which every licensed doctor confirms they remain up to date and fit to practise, based on ongoing appraisal — it is a proactive, continuous quality-assurance mechanism that applies to every doctor, regardless of whether any concern has ever been raised about them. Fitness to practise, by contrast, is reactive: it is triggered by a specific concern. The two systems do interact — a serious fitness to practise finding can affect revalidation, and, more rarely, appraisal information can itself surface a concern that is referred into the fitness to practise process — but for the vast majority of doctors, revalidation is simply routine professional housekeeping, entirely separate from any fitness to practise involvement. If you want the detail on how revalidation actually works day to day, a companion piece on the annual appraisal and five-year licence renewal cycle covers that process end to end.

Why This Matters Differently for UK Graduates and IMGs

The GMC fitness to practise framework applies identically to every doctor holding full registration and a licence to practise, regardless of where they trained — but the two audiences typically encounter it from a different starting point, and it is worth addressing each explicitly rather than assuming one experience fits everyone.

For UK Medical Graduates

If you trained in a UK medical school, you will already have encountered a simplified version of the fitness to practise framework as a student — most UK medical schools operate their own "student fitness to practise" processes, dealing with concerns about a student's conduct or health during training. This means the underlying logic (concern raised, investigated proportionately, resolved at the lowest level consistent with public protection) is not entirely new territory. What changes on full registration is the scale and formality of the adult, professional version of the same idea: the GMC itself, rather than your medical school, is now the relevant body, and the stakes (registration and licence to practise, rather than a place on a course) are naturally higher. The habits that served you well as a student — honesty, timely disclosure, engaging constructively with any concern raised — remain exactly the right habits now.

For International Medical Graduates

If you registered with the GMC as an IMG, having reached this point via the AKT and CPSA and the wider GMC registration process, fitness to practise is more likely to be an entirely new concept, since most countries' medical regulators operate under different statutory frameworks, different terminology, and sometimes a different overall philosophy about the balance between investigation and support. It is worth being explicit that the UK's approach — proportionate, risk-based, weighted heavily towards early resolution and support rather than punishment — is not universal, and doctors who trained under a stricter or more punitive home regulator sometimes over-estimate how quickly a minor concern in the UK system would escalate. Equally, doctors from systems with very little regulatory oversight sometimes under-estimate how seriously the UK system treats concealment or dishonesty specifically, even where the underlying clinical error was minor. Neither instinct is quite right; the accurate picture is the one set out in this guide — proportionate to risk, not to the doctor's country of training.

How a Concern Is Typically Handled

Understanding the stages a concern moves through demystifies the process considerably. Very few concerns move through every stage described below — most are resolved much earlier — but knowing the full pathway helps put any single stage in context.

Stage One: Initial Assessment

When the GMC receives information suggesting a concern about a doctor, the first step is an initial assessment against a threshold test: does the information, if true, raise a question about the doctor's fitness to practise that is serious enough to require investigation? A large proportion of concerns are closed at this very first stage, because they do not meet that threshold — for example, a complaint that reflects a genuine service or communication issue but does not raise any question about the individual doctor's fitness to practise, or a concern that is more appropriately dealt with by the doctor's employer through local processes, clinical governance, or a routine complaints procedure. This initial filtering stage is one of the main reasons the overall proportion of concerns that ever reach a hearing is small: many perfectly legitimate complaints are the right kind of concern for a local resolution process, not a national regulatory one.

Stage Two: Provisional Enquiries and Gathering Information

If a concern passes the initial threshold, the GMC may make provisional enquiries — for example, requesting the doctor's clinical records, a statement from the doctor, or further detail from the person who raised the concern — before deciding whether a full investigation is warranted. This stage exists precisely so that concerns which turn out, on closer look, not to raise a genuine fitness to practise question can be closed without the doctor ever entering a formal, lengthy investigation. A doctor contacted at this stage is not yet "under investigation" in the fullest sense, but it is still sensible to notify a medical defence organisation at this point rather than waiting, since early, informed engagement tends to produce both a swifter resolution and a better outcome.

Stage Three: Formal Investigation

Where a concern is serious enough to warrant it, the GMC opens a formal investigation. This typically involves gathering further evidence — clinical records, statements from colleagues, expert opinion on clinical standards where relevant, and a formal invitation for the doctor to respond in writing to the concern raised. The doctor is told, in reasonably plain terms, what the concern is and is given a genuine opportunity to respond before any decision is made. This is also the stage at which doctors most benefit from involving their medical defence organisation or the BMA, since a well-structured, factual, well-supported response at this stage often materially affects the eventual outcome.

Stage Four: Case Examiner Decision

Once an investigation concludes, two case examiners — one medical, one non-medical — jointly review the evidence and decide the outcome. Case examiners work through a structured framework built around three core questions: how serious is the concern, what relevant context (such as system pressures, personal circumstances, or health factors) surrounds it, and how has the doctor responded — including whether they have shown insight, remediation, and genuine reflection. This is where the great majority of concerns that do proceed past initial assessment are ultimately resolved, without ever reaching a public tribunal hearing.

Possible Outcomes of a Fitness to Practise Concern

One of the most reassuring facts about the fitness to practise system, for a newly registered doctor trying to understand the landscape they now sit within, is how heavily weighted the range of outcomes is towards resolution well short of a full tribunal hearing. The table below sets out the main possible outcomes, roughly in ascending order of severity.

The overwhelming majority of doctors who are ever the subject of a concern — including many entirely legitimate, correctly-raised concerns — never see anything beyond an early closure, advice, or, at most, a warning. This is not a reason to be complacent about professional standards, but it is an accurate, verifiable fact that should replace any imagined worst-case scenario with a realistic one.

What a Tribunal Hearing Actually Involves

Where a case is serious enough to be referred, the Medical Practitioners Tribunal Service — a service operationally separate from the GMC, specifically to ensure independence between investigation and adjudication — convenes a panel, typically comprising a legally qualified chair, a medically qualified panel member, and a lay panel member. The tribunal considers the evidence, hears from the doctor (who is entitled to legal representation, most commonly arranged through a medical defence organisation), and reaches a decision on both facts and, where relevant, sanction. Sanctions available to a tribunal include no action, undertakings, conditions, suspension for a defined period, or, in the most serious cases, erasure from the medical register. Doctors also retain rights of appeal against tribunal decisions through the courts. It is also worth knowing that the wider legislative framework behind this whole system is itself under active review — a separate piece on this site covers the proposed reform of the GMC's governing legislation and protected medical titles, which touches on how the regulator's powers and structures may evolve, though the core fitness to practise principles described here are expected to remain stable.

Why Health Concerns Are Treated Differently in Practice

Where a concern relates purely to a doctor's health — rather than conduct or performance — the GMC's approach is explicitly geared towards support and safe return to practice wherever possible, rather than sanction. Confidential health assessments, agreed monitoring, and time-limited conditions are the typical tools used, reflecting the reality that a doctor who is unwell but engaging constructively with treatment and oversight is very rarely the kind of ongoing patient safety risk the fitness to practise system exists to address. New doctors experiencing a health difficulty that may affect safe practice — including significant stress or mental health strain, which is common in the demanding early years of clinical practice — are strongly encouraged to seek support early, both from occupational health and from a wellbeing support resource, rather than concealing a difficulty until it affects patient care.

Roughly How Long Does the Process Take?

Timescales vary considerably depending on complexity, but it is worth setting realistic expectations rather than leaving this vague. A concern closed at initial assessment is typically resolved within a matter of weeks. A concern that proceeds to a full investigation — particularly one requiring expert clinical opinion, multiple witness statements, or coordination with an ongoing employer or police investigation — can reasonably take many months, and in more complex cases, over a year, before reaching a case examiner decision. This is one of the most frequently raised frustrations among doctors who have been through the process, and it is a genuine, acknowledged pressure point in the system rather than something to be minimised. It is precisely because of these realistic timescales that early engagement with a medical defence organisation matters so much — a well-organised, well-supported response from the outset tends to prevent unnecessary delay later, even though it cannot always shorten a process that depends on external evidence-gathering.

What "Impairment" Means in GMC Decision-Making

A term that recurs throughout fitness to practise decision-making is "impairment" — shorthand for whether a doctor's fitness to practise is currently impaired, which is a distinct question from whether the doctor did something wrong in the past. A doctor can be found to have fallen below the expected standard on a specific occasion without their fitness to practise being currently impaired, particularly where they have shown full insight, taken remedial action, and demonstrated the issue will not recur. This is why genuine reflection, evidenced learning, and constructive engagement with any concern matter so much to the eventual outcome — the GMC and, where relevant, the tribunal are assessing risk going forward, not simply totting up past mistakes.

Common Misconceptions New Doctors Have About Fitness to Practise

A number of misunderstandings recur so often among newly registered doctors that they are worth addressing directly, since each one tends to generate disproportionate anxiety relative to the actual risk involved.

"Any Complaint Means I Am Under GMC Investigation"

This is not correct. The great majority of patient complaints are handled entirely locally, through an employer's complaints process, and never reach the GMC at all. Even where a complaint is referred to the GMC, it must first clear the initial threshold test described earlier in this guide, and a substantial proportion do not. Being named in a complaint is not the same as being under GMC investigation, and conflating the two is one of the most common sources of unnecessary distress among junior doctors.

"One Mistake Will End My Career"

Clinical practice inherently involves risk, and every doctor — including the most experienced consultants — will be involved in an adverse incident of some kind during their career. The fitness to practise system is explicitly designed to distinguish between an isolated, honestly disclosed error handled with insight, and a pattern of concealment or repeated disregard for patient safety. A single well-managed mistake, particularly one openly disclosed under the duty of candour, overwhelmingly does not result in erasure, and in the great majority of cases does not result in any formal sanction at all.

"Contacting a Medical Defence Organisation Means Something Is Seriously Wrong"

In reality, contacting a medical defence organisation is a routine, sensible step recommended at the very first sign of any concern, however minor — precisely so that reassurance and context can be obtained early, rather than waiting until a situation has genuinely escalated. Most calls to a medical defence organisation's advice line concern matters that are resolved quickly with simple guidance, not full-blown fitness to practise cases.

"IMGs Are Investigated More Often or More Harshly Than UK Graduates"

The fitness to practise framework, its thresholds, and its range of outcomes apply identically regardless of where a doctor trained. Any perception otherwise generally reflects the genuine, practical adjustment period IMGs go through when adapting to unfamiliar systems, escalation pathways, and documentation conventions in their first months of UK practice — a challenge addressed directly by good induction support — rather than any difference in how the GMC itself applies its framework.

Sensible Day-One Habits That Reduce Risk

The single most useful thing a newly registered doctor can do about fitness to practise is not to worry about it in the abstract, but to build a small number of concrete professional habits from the very first week of practice. None of these are complicated, and all of them are well within reach of any doctor, regardless of how unfamiliar the UK system initially feels.

Know Good Medical Practice, Not Just of It

Good Medical Practice is the single reference document against which fitness to practise concerns are ultimately assessed, and it is genuinely readable — it is written in plain, practical language, not dense legal drafting. New doctors who have only skimmed it during induction should set aside a genuine hour to read it properly, ideally alongside the detailed breakdown in this site's guide to Good Medical Practice and its four domains. Almost every scenario that ends up as a fitness to practise concern maps onto one of a small number of recurring themes in that document: honesty and candour, working within the limits of your competence, maintaining clear professional boundaries, and raising concerns about patient safety rather than staying silent.

Keep Clear, Contemporaneous Clinical Records

Clinical records are, in practice, the single most decisive piece of evidence in almost any fitness to practise investigation — far more so than a doctor's own after-the-fact recollection of events. A record made at the time, clearly dated and timed, describing what was found, what was decided, and why, protects a doctor far more effectively than the same information reconstructed weeks later from memory. Good habits here are simple: document promptly rather than at the end of a shift, record your clinical reasoning (not just your conclusion), note what you told the patient and what they told you, and never alter a record retrospectively without clearly marking it as a later addition, dated and signed. Doctors who form this habit early rarely have to think about it again — it becomes automatic — and it consistently proves to be the difference between a well-supported explanation and an unsupported one, should any concern ever arise.

Understand and Apply the Duty of Candour From Day One

The duty of candour — being open and honest with patients when something has gone wrong, offering an appropriate apology, and explaining what happened and what will be done about it — is both a professional duty under Good Medical Practice and, at an organisational level, a statutory duty on NHS trusts. For an individual doctor, the practical habit is straightforward: when something goes wrong, tell the patient (or those close to them) promptly, apologise sincerely (an apology is not, in itself, an admission of legal liability), and report the incident through your organisation's usual incident-reporting channels. Doctors sometimes delay disclosure out of an instinct to wait until "all the facts are known" — but GMC guidance is consistently clear that openness should not wait for a completed investigation. This single habit, more than almost any other, is associated with concerns being resolved constructively rather than escalating.

Seek Help Early When Something Goes Wrong

Every doctor, no matter how experienced, will at some point be involved in a clinical incident, receive a complaint, or make a mistake. What distinguishes a well-handled situation from a poorly handled one is rarely the underlying error itself — it is almost always how quickly and openly the doctor sought support afterwards. Newly registered doctors should know, before they ever need it, that support is genuinely available and worth engaging early:

  • Medical defence organisations (the Medical Defence Union, the Medical Protection Society, and the Medical and Dental Defence Union of Scotland are the three long-established UK bodies) provide confidential advice, and, where needed, formal representation, from the earliest point a doctor becomes aware of a concern — not only once a case has become serious
  • The British Medical Association (BMA) offers confidential advice and pastoral support to members facing a workplace or regulatory difficulty, alongside its wider industrial and employment role
  • Your named educational or clinical supervisor is a legitimate, appropriate first point of contact for anything from a clinical uncertainty to a difficult interaction with a patient or colleague
  • Occupational health services exist specifically to support doctors experiencing health difficulties that may affect safe practice, in confidence and without automatic escalation

Joining a medical defence organisation is genuinely worth doing in your first week of practice, not after a concern arises — cover typically cannot be backdated to before a concern was raised, and the earliest possible engagement with defence support consistently produces the best outcomes. This is a practical administrative task, not a sign that anything is wrong; think of it the same way you would think of any other form of professional insurance.

Escalate, Don't Absorb, Patient Safety Concerns

Good Medical Practice expects doctors to raise concerns about patient safety, not to manage them silently or informally. If something about the environment you are working in — short-staffing, unsafe workloads, a colleague's practice, or a systemic gap — genuinely worries you, the correct response is to raise it through the appropriate channel (your line manager or clinical lead, your trust's Freedom to Speak Up Guardian, or, where local processes have failed, the GMC itself) and, if the first escalation does not resolve the risk, to escalate again. This is directly connected to patient safety practice around prescribing and clinical error more broadly — a doctor who raises a genuine safety concern through the right channel is acting entirely in line with professional expectations, not creating a fitness to practise risk for themselves.

Understand Your Scope and Ask Before You Act Beyond It

A significant proportion of avoidable fitness to practise concerns arise not from malice or incompetence, but from a doctor practising beyond their current competence without adequate supervision — often because they felt unable to say so. Good Medical Practice explicitly protects and expects doctors to recognise the limits of their competence and to seek help, supervision, or escalation rather than proceeding alone. For an IMG in particular, adjusting to unfamiliar systems, protocols, and escalation pathways in the first months of UK practice is entirely normal, and asking questions during this adjustment period is a sign of good professional judgement, not weakness. The induction period covered in this site's guide to settling into UK clinical practice is specifically designed to shorten this adjustment curve.

Keep Your Registration and Personal Details Current

A surprising number of avoidable administrative concerns arise simply from a doctor failing to keep the GMC informed of a change of address, employer, or contact details, which can delay important correspondence — including, in rare cases, correspondence relating to a concern itself. Keeping your GMC online account up to date is a two-minute task worth doing whenever your circumstances change, and it sits alongside the other practical registration housekeeping covered in the full GMC registration guide.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does GMC fitness to practise actually mean?

GMC fitness to practise refers to the GMC's process for assessing and, where necessary, acting on concerns about a doctor's conduct, performance, or health, in order to protect patients and maintain public confidence in the medical profession. It is a forward-looking assessment of current risk, not a punitive response to every incident, and the large majority of concerns raised are resolved without any serious sanction.

What happens if a complaint is made against a newly registered doctor?

Most complaints against doctors are resolved locally, through an employer's own complaints process, without ever becoming a GMC fitness to practise matter. Where a complaint is referred to the GMC, it is first assessed against a threshold test, and a large proportion are closed at this initial stage because they do not raise a genuine fitness to practise question. A doctor who receives any complaint, however minor it seems, should feel able to discuss it with a senior colleague or their medical defence organisation for reassurance and context.

Do most GMC fitness to practise concerns lead to a doctor losing their licence?

No. Losing a licence — erasure from the register — is reserved for the most serious cases and represents only a small fraction of the small minority of concerns that reach a full MPTS tribunal hearing in the first place. The overwhelming majority of concerns are closed with no further action, resolved with advice, or, in a smaller number of cases, result in a warning or agreed undertakings well short of any restriction on practice.

Should I contact a medical defence organisation even if I have never had a concern raised against me?

Yes. Joining a medical defence organisation such as the MDU, MPS, or MDDUS — or ensuring you have appropriate BMA membership and support — is standard, sensible practice for every doctor from the very start of their career, not something to arrange only once a concern arises. Cover and support typically cannot be arranged retrospectively once a concern has already been raised, so early membership is itself a risk-reducing habit.

How is fitness to practise different from revalidation?

Revalidation is the routine, five-yearly confirmation that every licensed doctor remains up to date and fit to practise, based on ongoing annual appraisal, and it applies to every doctor regardless of whether any concern has ever been raised. Fitness to practise, by contrast, is a reactive process triggered specifically by a concern about an individual doctor's conduct, performance, or health. The two interact only in limited circumstances, such as where a serious fitness to practise finding affects the timing or terms of revalidation.

What should I do if I am worried about something I have done at work?

If you are worried about a specific clinical incident, error, or interaction, the sensible first step is to be open about it — with your supervisor, and where relevant with the patient, in line with the duty of candour — and to document what happened accurately and promptly. If you are unsure whether the matter could become a formal concern, contact your medical defence organisation or the BMA for confidential advice; this guide is not a substitute for that kind of case-specific support, and reaching out early is always a reasonable and professionally appropriate step to take.

Does the GMC fitness to practise process apply equally to UK graduates and IMGs?

Yes. Once a doctor holds full GMC registration and a licence to practise, the fitness to practise framework applies identically regardless of where they qualified. The process, thresholds, and range of outcomes are the same for a UK medical graduate and an international medical graduate; the only meaningful difference for IMGs is the practical adjustment period to unfamiliar systems and escalation pathways during their first months of UK practice, which is a normal part of induction rather than a regulatory distinction.

How long does a GMC fitness to practise investigation usually take?

Timescales depend heavily on complexity. Concerns closed at initial assessment are typically resolved within weeks, while a full investigation requiring expert opinion or witness evidence can reasonably take several months to over a year before a case examiner decision is reached. This is a genuine, acknowledged pressure point in the system, which is exactly why early, organised engagement with a medical defence organisation is worth doing as soon as any concern is raised, rather than waiting.

Can a doctor keep working while a GMC fitness to practise concern is being looked into?

In the great majority of cases, yes — a doctor continues practising as normal while a concern is assessed or investigated, since only the small minority of cases involving an immediate, serious risk to patient safety result in interim measures such as suspension pending investigation. Interim orders of this kind are relatively rare and are themselves subject to their own review process, separate from the final outcome of the underlying concern.

Conclusion

The GMC fitness to practise framework exists to protect patients and uphold trust in the medical profession — not to treat every newly registered doctor as a risk waiting to materialise. Understood correctly, it is a system heavily weighted towards early resolution, proportionate response, and support rather than sanction, with only a small minority of concerns ever reaching a tribunal hearing. The practical takeaway for any doctor in their first months of UK practice is straightforward: know Good Medical Practice properly, keep clear and honest clinical records, be open when something goes wrong, and join a medical defence organisation before you ever need one. Doing these things consistently is, in almost every real case, the entire difference between a concern that resolves quietly and one that does not.
