NHS pay scales and banding are the two mechanisms that together determine what any doctor working in the NHS actually earns, and confusing the two is one of the most common sources of pay surprise for both new UK graduates and internationally trained doctors starting their first NHS post. NHS Pay Scales and Banding: The Quick Answer

NHS pay scales and banding work together as a two-part system: the pay scale sets a doctor's basic salary according to grade and incremental pay point, while banding adds a percentage supplement on top of that basic salary to reflect the intensity of the specific rota a doctor actually works. Basic pay increases with seniority — moving from Foundation Year 1 through core or specialty training grades and eventually to consultant level — and, within most grades, also increases modestly with each additional year of service at that grade through incremental pay points. Banding, in contrast, has nothing to do with seniority; it is calculated separately for each individual rota based on measurable factors such as the total hours worked, how many nights and weekends are included, and how frequently a doctor is on call.

The practical result is that a doctor's payslip typically shows two distinct components: a basic salary figure tied to grade, and a banding supplement expressed as a percentage of that basic salary, tied to the specific rota the doctor is currently working. A doctor on a demanding rota with frequent night shifts and weekend cover receives a considerably higher percentage supplement than a colleague at the identical basic grade working a rota concentrated in standard daytime hours. Consequently, two doctors with the same job title, the same basic salary band, and the same number of years' experience can have noticeably different gross pay, and the difference is explained entirely by banding rather than by any difference in underlying seniority or performance.

How Basic Pay Is Set: NHS Pay Scales by Grade

The NHS pay scale for doctors and dentists is negotiated nationally rather than by individual employer, which means basic salary for a given grade is broadly consistent across NHS trusts in a given UK nation, subject to a small number of standard additions such as a London-area supplement. Basic pay is structured around grade first and incremental pay point second, and understanding both layers matters for anyone trying to interpret where they sit on the scale.

Foundation Doctors (FY1 and FY2)

Foundation doctors are paid on the first rung of the medical pay scale, split into two years, FY1 and FY2, each carrying its own basic salary point. Foundation Year 1 sits at the base of the scale, since this is a doctor's first year of paid clinical practice following medical school, and Foundation Year 2 sits one increment above it, reflecting a year of additional clinical responsibility and experience. Both Foundation years are training grades, meaning the basic salary is fixed nationally for all Foundation doctors at that year, regardless of specialty or trust, aside from the London-area supplement mentioned below.

Core and Specialty Registrar Grades

After the Foundation Programme, doctors who enter formal postgraduate training move onto core training grades — commonly labelled CT1 through CT3 or their run-through specialty training equivalents — and later progress into higher specialty training, often labelled ST4 and above depending on the specialty's overall training length. Each of these grades carries its own basic salary point, generally increasing as a doctor moves from core training into higher specialty training, reflecting increasing clinical responsibility and decision-making autonomy. Doctors who have not entered a formal training number, working instead in non-training posts such as trust grade, clinical fellow, or specialty doctor roles, are paid on a related but administratively distinct scale, since these posts sit outside the formal national training programme even though the clinical work performed can be very similar in practice.

Specialty Doctors and SAS Grades

Specialty and Associate Specialist, or SAS, doctors occupy an important and growing part of the medical workforce, sitting outside the formal training numbered grades but carrying substantial clinical experience and, in many cases, considerable seniority. The SAS pay scale has its own set of incremental pay points, generally reflecting years of relevant experience rather than progress through a structured training curriculum, and a doctor can remain on this pathway for an entire career without ever holding a training number, provided they meet the relevant experience and competency thresholds for that grade.

Consultant Grade

Consultant is the most senior substantive grade a doctor can hold after completing specialty training, evidenced by a Certificate of Completion of Training, or an equivalent CESR or CEGPR route for doctors who reach consultant-level competence by an alternative pathway. Consultant basic pay also progresses through incremental pay points tied to years of service at consultant level, meaning a newly appointed consultant and a consultant with many years' post-CCT experience are not paid identically even though both hold the same job title. Consultants may also take on additional programmed activities, clinical excellence awards, or job-planned responsibilities that add further to total pay, though these sit outside the core basic pay scale discussed here.

Because every one of these basic salary figures is subject to annual pay awards — and because pay awards in recent years have varied considerably in size and have sometimes been implemented in stages rather than as a single uplift — this guide will not state specific current pound figures for any grade as fixed facts. The single most reliable way to check a current, accurate figure for a specific grade is to consult the BMA's published pay scales for doctors, which is updated each time a new pay award takes effect and is the single most current, doctor-facing reference available.

What a Banding Supplement Actually Is

A banding supplement is an additional percentage payment added on top of basic salary to compensate a doctor for working a rota that is more intensive, more unsociable, or more disruptive to normal sleep and rest patterns than a standard working week. Banding exists because two doctors at an identical basic grade can, in practice, be asked to work very different patterns of hours — one might work almost entirely daytime, weekday shifts, while another on the same grade might work frequent nights, regular weekends, and be on call overnight multiple times a month. Basic pay alone does not distinguish between these two situations, so the banding supplement was introduced specifically to reflect that difference in workload intensity and unsociability.

In practical terms, a banding supplement is expressed as a percentage uplift applied to basic salary, and different rota patterns attract different percentage bands depending on a small number of measurable factors set out below. A doctor's total gross pay is therefore basic salary plus the banding percentage applied to that basic salary, meaning the banding supplement can represent a very substantial proportion of total pay for doctors on the most intensive rotas, and a comparatively small proportion for doctors on lighter, more standard rotas.

What Determines a Rota's Banding Percentage

A rota's banding percentage is calculated from a defined set of factors relating to how the hours are actually structured across a working pattern, rather than from the doctor's grade, specialty, or personal preference. The main factors generally considered include:

  • Total hours worked across the rota cycle, including whether the pattern exceeds standard full-time hours.
  • The proportion of hours falling at night, since night work is considered more disruptive to normal rest and is weighted accordingly.
  • The frequency and pattern of weekend working, since regular weekend shifts reduce a doctor's ability to maintain a stable non-working routine.
  • On-call frequency and intensity, including how often a doctor is required to be contactable or physically present out of hours, and how frequently that on-call time actually results in being called in to work.
  • Rest and break compliance, since a rota that does not reliably provide adequate rest between shifts or sufficient continuous days off is treated as more intensive than one that does.

These same factors are also the ones assessed under the working-time protections covered in the site's separate guide to EWTD working hours and rest rules for doctors, since banding and working-time compliance are assessed from largely the same underlying rota data, even though they serve two different purposes — one setting pay, the other setting maximum safe working limits. A rota that would raise working-time compliance concerns is very often also a rota that attracts a higher banding percentage, because both assessments are responding to the same underlying pattern of unsociable and intensive hours.

Why Two Doctors on the Same Basic Grade Can Take Home Different Amounts

Because banding is calculated per rota rather than per grade, it is entirely normal for two doctors holding an identical job title, identical basic salary point, and identical years of experience to receive different total gross pay, purely because they are rostered onto different patterns of work. A doctor in a department with a demanding on-call rota — frequent nights, regular weekend cover, a high proportion of unsociable hours — will typically attract a meaningfully higher banding percentage than a colleague at the same basic grade working in a department with a lighter, more daytime-concentrated rota. This is one of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of NHS pay scales and banding for doctors moving between rotations or comparing notes with colleagues in different departments or specialties, since a like-for-like comparison of basic salary alone will systematically understate the real pay difference between two apparently similar posts.

This also means that changing rotation — a routine, expected part of postgraduate training, where a doctor moves department every few months — can noticeably change a trainee's gross monthly pay even though their basic grade and pay point remain completely unchanged. A trainee moving from a lighter-banded rotation into a more intensive one, for example an acute or emergency specialty with frequent night and weekend cover, should expect gross pay to rise accordingly for the duration of that rotation, and to fall back again once they move to a lighter-banded placement. Payroll departments generally handle this automatically as rotations change, but it is worth a doctor proactively checking their payslip against their actual rota each time a rotation changes, since banding misapplication is one of the more common payroll errors affecting trainees.

Rota Monitoring: How Banding Is Assessed and Verified

Banding is not simply assigned once at the start of a post and left unchanged; it is formally assessed and periodically re-verified through a process generally referred to as rota monitoring. Rota monitoring exists because a rota as designed on paper does not always reflect the hours a doctor actually ends up working in practice — cover for sickness, unfilled posts, or informally extended shifts can all mean the real pattern of hours worked is more intensive than the theoretical rota suggests. Rota monitoring exercises collect real data on the hours doctors are actually working over a defined monitoring period and compare that data against the banding percentage currently being paid, adjusting it if the two no longer match.

Who Carries Out Rota Monitoring

Rota monitoring is typically organised at trust or department level, usually coordinated between the employing trust's medical staffing or workforce team and the doctors actually working the rota being assessed, often with input from a doctor acting as an educational or rota representative. Because the exercise depends on accurate, honestly reported data about actual hours worked, doctors are generally encouraged, and in many trusts formally expected, to complete diary cards or electronic time-tracking records honestly and consistently during a monitoring period, since understated hours can result in a rota being under-banded relative to the work actually being done.

What Happens When a Rota Fails Monitoring

If a rota monitoring exercise finds that the actual hours worked exceed what the current banding percentage was designed to compensate, or that rest and break requirements are not being reliably met in practice, the rota is generally considered to have failed monitoring. This typically triggers one of two responses: either the rota is redesigned to bring the actual working pattern back within safe and compliant limits, or the banding percentage applied to that rota is increased to reflect the more intensive pattern actually being worked, or in many cases both responses happen together. This is precisely why rota monitoring sits so closely alongside the working-time protections discussed in the companion guide to EWTD working hours and rest rules for doctors — the same monitoring data is frequently used both to check working-time compliance and to verify that a rota's banding percentage still accurately reflects the hours genuinely being worked.

For doctors newly arrived in the NHS, whether from a UK medical school or from overseas, the practical takeaway is that banding is not a fixed, one-off label attached permanently to a job title. It is an ongoing, evidence-based assessment of a specific rota's real intensity, and doctors who believe their rota has changed in ways that are not reflected in their current banding percentage generally have a route — usually through their trust's guardian of safe working hours or equivalent post, medical staffing team, or the BMA — to request a fresh monitoring exercise.

Pay Progression: How Salary Grows With Experience and Seniority

Basic pay under NHS pay scales and banding rules is not static within a grade; it progresses in two distinct ways as a doctor gains experience, and understanding both is useful for anyone trying to project their earnings over the coming years rather than just the current year.

Incremental Progression Within a Grade

Most grades on the medical pay scale include multiple incremental pay points, meaning a doctor's basic salary rises automatically after a defined period of service at that grade, even without a change in job title or responsibility. This is distinct from, and in addition to, any annual pay award applied to the whole scale — incremental progression reflects a single doctor's accumulating experience at a given grade, while an annual pay award adjusts the whole scale for every doctor at every grade simultaneously. A doctor who remains at the same grade for several years, for instance in a longer specialty training programme or as a specialty doctor who has not sought promotion, should therefore still expect gradual basic pay increases at each incremental step, separate from any wider pay award.

Progression Between Grades

The larger and more visible increases in basic pay happen when a doctor moves between grades entirely — from Foundation Year 1 to Foundation Year 2, from Foundation into core or specialty training, from core into higher specialty training, and eventually from specialty training into a substantive consultant post. Each of these transitions carries its own step up in basic salary, reflecting the increased clinical responsibility and decision-making autonomy that comes with the new grade. For doctors who do not follow the formal run-through training pathway, similar step increases occur when moving up through SAS pay points as relevant experience accumulates, or when transitioning from a SAS role into a consultant post via CESR or CEGPR.

How Rotation Changes Interact With Progression

It is worth explicitly separating two things that often get conflated: progression through the pay scale, which is about grade and seniority, and changes in banding, which are about the specific rota currently being worked. A doctor can progress up the pay scale — for example moving from core training into higher specialty training — while simultaneously moving onto a less intensively banded rota, meaning their basic pay rises but their banding percentage falls, producing a smaller overall pay increase than the headline grade change alone would suggest. Equally, a doctor whose grade and basic pay remain unchanged for a period can still see gross pay rise or fall as they rotate between differently banded departments. Doctors projecting their own future earnings should therefore track both variables separately rather than assuming pay always rises smoothly and predictably alongside seniority alone.

Where to Find Current, Authoritative Pay Scale Figures

Because NHS pay scales and banding percentages are reviewed and adjusted through annual pay awards, and because contract terms are periodically renegotiated between the government, NHS Employers, and the BMA, this guide has deliberately avoided quoting specific current figures as permanent facts. Doctors who need an exact, current, and reliable figure for their own grade and circumstances should consult one of two authoritative sources.

The BMA's published pay scales for doctors set out current basic salary figures by grade and pay point, updated whenever a new pay award or contract change takes effect, and are written specifically for a doctor-facing audience rather than an HR or payroll specialist audience. The BMA also publishes detailed guidance on how banding percentages are calculated and what to do if a doctor believes their banding no longer reflects their actual rota. Separately, NHS Employers' pay circulars for doctors and dentists provide the official employer-facing detail behind each pay award, including the precise mechanics of how figures are implemented across trusts, and are the definitive reference that individual trust payroll and workforce teams themselves use.

Before accepting a post, changing rotation, or querying a payslip, cross-checking the specific basic salary and banding percentage against these two sources — rather than against a job advert, a colleague's recollection, or any article, including this one — is the single most reliable way to confirm what a specific post actually pays. Doctors who have questions specific to their own rota and believe their banding may be miscalculated should also consider contacting the BMA directly or their trust's guardian of safe working hours, both of whom deal with exactly this kind of query routinely.

NHS Pay Scales and Banding in the Wider Context of an NHS Career

Pay is only one part of the overall financial picture a doctor should build when starting NHS employment, and NHS pay scales and banding sit alongside several other structural elements worth understanding early rather than discovering piecemeal. Pension contributions, for instance, are deducted from gross pay — including the banding supplement — before a doctor's actual take-home figure is calculated, and the NHS Pension Scheme for doctors is itself a valuable, defined-benefit-style long-term entitlement that should be weighed alongside monthly take-home pay rather than viewed purely as a deduction. Common Misunderstandings About NHS Pay Scales and Banding

Several recurring misunderstandings catch both new UK graduates and newly arrived international doctors off guard, and being aware of them in advance avoids unnecessary confusion once a first payslip arrives.

  • Assuming basic salary is the whole story. A job advert or contract that states only a basic salary figure is not stating total gross pay; the banding supplement, where applicable, is added on top and can represent a very substantial proportion of total earnings for doctors on intensive rotas.
  • Assuming banding is fixed for the duration of a training programme. Banding is tied to the specific rota currently being worked, not to a training programme as a whole, so gross pay can rise or fall at each rotation change even though basic grade and pay point remain unchanged.
  • Assuming two doctors at the same grade are paid the same total amount. As set out earlier in this guide, banding is calculated per rota, so identical basic grade does not guarantee identical total pay — the actual rota pattern matters just as much as seniority.
  • Not querying a payslip after a rotation change. Because banding should update automatically when a doctor moves to a differently banded rota, but payroll errors do occur, checking a payslip against the actual new rota after each rotation change is a sensible habit rather than an unnecessary one.
  • Treating a single article or forum post as a current source of exact figures. Because pay awards happen annually and are sometimes staged, any specific pound figure or banding percentage quoted anywhere other than the BMA's or NHS Employers' own current publications should be treated as potentially out of date.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between NHS pay scales and banding?

NHS pay scales set a doctor's basic salary according to grade — Foundation, core or specialty trainee, specialty doctor, or consultant — and years of experience within that grade. Banding is a separate additional percentage supplement added on top of basic salary to reflect how intensive and unsociable a doctor's specific rota is, meaning the two mechanisms answer different questions: the pay scale answers "what does this grade pay," and banding answers "how demanding is this particular rota."

Why do two doctors at the same grade get paid different amounts?

Two doctors holding an identical basic grade and pay point can receive different total gross pay because banding is calculated per rota rather than per grade. A doctor working a more intensive rota — more nights, more weekends, more frequent on-call — receives a higher banding percentage than a colleague at the same basic grade working a lighter, more daytime-concentrated rota, and this difference can be substantial.

Who decides a doctor's banding percentage?

Banding percentages are assessed through rota monitoring exercises, typically organised between a trust's medical staffing or workforce team and the doctors working the rota, based on real data about hours actually worked rather than the rota's theoretical design alone. If monitoring shows a rota is more intensive than its current banding reflects, the rota is either redesigned or its banding percentage is increased, and this same monitoring data is closely linked to working-time compliance checks.

Does banding change when a doctor moves to a new rotation?

Yes. Because banding reflects the specific rota currently being worked rather than a doctor's grade or training programme as a whole, gross pay typically rises or falls at each rotation change according to how intensively banded the new rota is, even though basic salary and pay point stay the same. Doctors are generally advised to check their payslip against their actual new rota shortly after each rotation change to confirm the update has been applied correctly.

Where can I find the current, accurate NHS pay scale figures for my grade?

The two authoritative sources are the BMA's published pay scales for doctors and NHS Employers' pay circulars for doctors and dentists, both of which are updated whenever a new pay award or contract change takes effect. Any specific pound figure quoted elsewhere, including in general articles, should be checked against these two current sources before being relied upon.

Do international medical graduates receive the same NHS pay scales and banding as UK graduates?

Yes. Basic pay scales and banding rules are set nationally and apply to any doctor holding the relevant grade and rota, regardless of where they trained or their nationality, since pay is determined by grade, experience, and rota intensity rather than by a doctor's country of medical qualification. The practical entry grade and route into the NHS may differ between UK graduates and IMGs, but once employed at a given grade, the same pay scale and banding mechanics apply to both.

Does the banding supplement count towards pension contributions?

Generally, yes — gross pay including the banding supplement is what pension contributions and tax are calculated against, meaning a higher banding percentage increases both gross pay and the pensionable earnings figure used for long-term pension calculations. Doctors wanting the precise current treatment of banding within pension calculations should check the specifics through the NHS Pension Scheme guide or NHS Pensions directly, since exact rules are periodically reviewed.

Can a doctor challenge or request a review of their banding?

Yes. If a doctor believes their rota has become more intensive than their current banding percentage reflects, they can generally raise this through their trust's guardian of safe working hours or equivalent post, their medical staffing team, or the BMA, which can trigger a fresh rota monitoring exercise. Because banding is meant to reflect actual hours worked rather than the rota's theoretical design, a mismatch between the two is a legitimate basis for requesting review rather than something a doctor is expected to simply accept.

Conclusion

NHS pay scales and banding together determine a doctor's real total pay far more precisely than basic salary alone ever can, and understanding both — rather than assuming a single headline figure applies uniformly to every doctor at a given grade — is essential for any doctor, whether newly graduated in the UK or newly registered as an international medical graduate, starting NHS employment for the first time. Basic pay rises with grade and incremental service, banding rises and falls with the intensity of a doctor's specific rota, and both are formally monitored and reviewed rather than fixed indefinitely. Always confirm current figures directly with the BMA or NHS Employers before making a financial decision based on any single number, including those in this guide.
