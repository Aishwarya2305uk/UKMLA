UKMLA for Zimbabwean doctors means passing the two GMC-recognised assessments that sit under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, commonly called the AKT and the CPSA, then completing GMC registration through EPIC/MyIntealth verification, an accepted internship and an approved English language score. For most Zimbabwean MBChB graduates, from the University of Zimbabwe, the National University of Science and Technology (NUST) or Midlands State University, the whole journey from booking the first test to holding full GMC registration typically takes somewhere between twelve and eighteen months, and the core GMC-facing costs alone run to roughly £1,345–£1,800 before English tests, verification, travel and visa costs are added. This guide walks through every stage of that route using the General Medical Council's own Medical Licensing Assessment guidance as the primary source, alongside the Medical and Dental Practitioners Council of Zimbabwe's (MDPCZ) own published registration pathway, so a final-year student in Harare and a working doctor in Bulawayo can both use it as a single reference.

Key takeaways

  • Zimbabwean doctors follow the same UKMLA-aligned route as every other international medical graduate (IMG): a knowledge test (AKT) and a clinical and professional skills assessment (CPSA), both set to Medical Licensing Assessment standards.
  • From 1 April 2026, the AKT costs £283 and the CPSA costs £1,036, according to the GMC's official fee schedule; registration adds a further £26 (provisional) or £481 (full).
  • An MBChB from the University of Zimbabwe, NUST or Midlands State University is generally accepted as a valid primary medical qualification, since all three are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools that the GMC checks during registration.
  • The MDPCZ is Zimbabwe's own domestic regulator and is separate from the GMC application, but MDPCZ registration and internship completion evidence typically feature in the documents a Zimbabwean doctor assembles for the UK route.
  • EPIC verification, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform, must confirm your Zimbabwean medical degree before the GMC will process your UK registration application.
  • Zimbabwe has one of the longer-established histories of doctor migration to the UK and NHS among Commonwealth countries; GMC data show the number of Zimbabwean-trained doctors on the UK register has risen steadily over the past decade, though exact figures should always be checked directly against GMC workforce publications.

Who this route is for: Zimbabwean doctors and the UKMLA

The Medical Licensing Assessment, or MLA, is the GMC's overarching quality bar for anyone joining the UK medical register, whether trained at a UK medical school or overseas. UK final-year students sit the MLA directly as part of their degree. International medical graduates, including doctors who completed their MBChB in Zimbabwe, sit what the GMC's own paperwork still sometimes labels "PLAB," restructured so that its two parts are aligned with, and equivalent to, the MLA's AKT and CPSA components. In practice, when this site and most independent guides refer to "UKMLA for Zimbabwean doctors," they mean the AKT-and-CPSA route that Zimbabwean IMGs use to reach the same licensing standard as a UK graduate.

This naming overlap trips up a fair number of Zimbabwean applicants: your GMC correspondence may still reference "PLAB 1" and "PLAB 2," while revision providers, question banks and this site describe the same two assessments as "UKMLA AKT" and "UKMLA CPSA." They are the same tests under two conventions. If you want the deepest breakdown of the exam format itself, our companion pillar pages on the Eligibility requirements and the Fees for every stage of the process apply equally to Zimbabwean candidates, and both are worth bookmarking before you start.

Consequently, a Zimbabwean MBChB graduate's path into UK practice runs through exactly the same eligibility gateway as every other IMG: an accepted primary medical qualification, a completed internship, an approved English language score, EPIC/MyIntealth verification and a pass in both the AKT and the CPSA. What differs for Zimbabwe specifically is the role played by the MDPCZ as the domestic regulator, the historically strong pipeline of Zimbabwean doctors already working in the NHS, the practicalities of finding an AKT test centre, and the currency backdrop against which every fee has to be planned. This guide is closely related to our existing regional coverage, including UKMLA for South African Doctors, since Zimbabwe and South Africa share a border, a Southern African medical training culture, and a similarly well-established history of doctors moving into the NHS, as well as our wider UKMLA for Kenyan and East African Doctors guide and the cross-country UKMLA for International Medical Graduates: Country Routes hub, which situates Zimbabwe's route alongside every other major source country.

Step 1: Confirm your eligibility as a Zimbabwean IMG

Before spending money on tests, a Zimbabwean doctor should confirm four eligibility pillars, because failing any one of them stalls the whole application regardless of exam results.

1. Your primary medical qualification (PMQ)

Zimbabwe's medical schools, the University of Zimbabwe College of Health Sciences, the National University of Science and Technology (NUST), Midlands State University, and more recently Great Zimbabwe University's Simon Mazorodze Medical School, award the MBChB (or, at NUST, sometimes referred to as MBBS) after a five- to six-year undergraduate programme. Provided your medical school is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC checks as part of primary source verification, your MBChB is generally accepted as a valid PMQ. Acceptance of the qualification itself is a separate question from whether you have passed the UKMLA route or completed an internship, so do not assume a listed degree alone makes you eligible to register.

2. Your internship

The GMC expects an acceptable period of supervised clinical practice completed immediately before or after your degree, generally at least twelve months split across medicine and surgery placements with defined minimum periods in each. In Zimbabwe, locally qualified MBChB graduates complete a prescribed internship at approved teaching or central hospitals before being placed on the MDPCZ's main register, a structure the MDPCZ describes on its own registration FAQ page. Because internship structures, rotation lengths and hospital accreditation can change over time, always confirm your specific internship still meets current GMC criteria via our GMC registration requirements for international doctors guide before assuming it counts automatically.

3. English language evidence

Almost every Zimbabwean doctor still needs to provide English language evidence for the GMC file, even though English is Zimbabwe's principal language of secondary and medical education. In practice, this means most Zimbabwean candidates sit IELTS Academic or OET Medicine regardless of how confident they already are in spoken and written English, simply because the GMC needs a standardised, dated score on record. A small number of candidates may qualify for an exemption where their medical education was conducted and assessed substantially in English and meets the GMC's specific exemption criteria, but this should always be checked directly rather than assumed.

4. EPIC / MyIntealth primary source verification

Historically, this verification step ran through a service called EPIC (the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials). ECFMG, now operating as Intealth, has since moved candidates onto a newer platform called MyIntealth, although many guides, forums and even some older paperwork still refer informally to "EPIC verification." Whichever name you see, the underlying requirement is unchanged: your Zimbabwean medical school must confirm directly to Intealth that your degree is genuine before the GMC will accept your UK registration application. It is worth being clear that this GMC-facing EPIC/MyIntealth process is distinct from the separate primary-source-verification arrangement the MDPCZ itself operates through ECFMG for doctors trained abroad who want to register to practise inside Zimbabwe; a Zimbabwean doctor heading to the UK needs the GMC-facing verification, not the MDPCZ-facing one. Because this step depends on your university responding to a verification request, it is often the single biggest source of delay in the whole process, so start it as early as possible, ideally before booking the AKT. Our dedicated GMC EPIC verification process explained guide walks through account setup and document requirements step by step, and it applies in full to Zimbabwean graduates.

Where the MDPCZ fits in

The Medical and Dental Practitioners Council of Zimbabwe (MDPCZ) is the statutory body that regulates and registers doctors and dentists to practise inside Zimbabwe, operating out of offices in Harare and Bulawayo. It is not part of the GMC's UK application process, and passing MDPCZ registration does not itself satisfy any GMC requirement. That said, most Zimbabwean doctors keep their MDPCZ registration current and in good standing, because MDPCZ documentation, including proof of internship completion and, where relevant, a Certificate of Good Standing, commonly appears among the supporting papers a Zimbabwean doctor assembles for GMC registration and for EPIC/MyIntealth verification. Doctors who intend to work abroad while keeping their Zimbabwean registration active should also check the MDPCZ's own retention-fee rules, since the Council explicitly allows registrants to remain on its register while working outside the country, subject to paying an annual retention fee.

Building a documents checklist early

Because so many of the delays Zimbabwean doctors experience come from missing or slow-to-obtain paperwork rather than exam performance, it helps to assemble a documents folder before booking anything. At minimum, most Zimbabwean applicants need: their MBChB degree certificate and final transcript, internship completion certificate, MDPCZ registration certificate or Certificate of Good Standing, passport, English test certificate (IELTS or OET), and their EPIC/MyIntealth verification report once it is issued. Keeping certified, dated copies of each of these ready, rather than requesting them piecemeal as the GMC or Intealth asks for them, is one of the simplest ways a Zimbabwean doctor can shorten the overall timeline. Step 2: Understand the AKT and CPSA as a Zimbabwean candidate

Once eligibility is confirmed, the practical work begins with the two assessments. The AKT is a computer-based knowledge test covering the breadth of the MLA content map, delivered as a single sitting of best-answer-style questions at a Pearson VUE test centre. Test centre availability inside Zimbabwe can vary and should always be checked directly with Pearson VUE and the GMC rather than assumed; many Zimbabwean candidates have historically sat the AKT in a neighbouring country such as South Africa where centre availability and capacity are more established, so it is worth planning this logistics question early rather than at the point of booking.

The CPSA is different in kind. It tests clinical examination skills, practical procedures, data interpretation and communication across a circuit of stations, and it is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester. This means every Zimbabwean candidate, however strong their AKT result, must travel to the UK at least once to complete the CPSA. Because of that, most experienced advisers recommend treating the CPSA trip as a single, well-organised visit, booking flights and any CPSA-specific short courses together rather than travelling more than once if it can be avoided.

How Zimbabwean doctors typically prepare for the AKT

Because the AKT draws on the same content map as UK final-year students use, most Zimbabwean doctors combine a recognised question bank with the official MLA content map as a syllabus checklist, rather than relying on general MBChB revision alone, since UK prescribing conventions, NICE-aligned guidelines and NHS-specific processes are tested in ways that differ from Zimbabwean undergraduate assessments. A structured three-to-six-month preparation window is typical, with many candidates layering in timed mock papers during the final six to eight weeks. Our Preparation pillar page gives a realistic week-by-week structure that Zimbabwean candidates can adapt around hospital shifts or MDPCZ internship commitments.

Preparing for the CPSA as a Zimbabwean doctor

The CPSA also tests communication style and professionalism in ways that catch some Zimbabwean candidates off guard, not because clinical knowledge is lacking, but because UK patient-communication norms, such as explicit consent-seeking language and structured breaking-bad-news frameworks, are assessed quite specifically. Reviewing the GMC's own Good Medical Practice standards alongside CPSA-specific practice stations, rather than assuming general clinical competence will carry the station, tends to make a measurable difference to performance on the day.

Step 3: Budget for UKMLA fees as a Zimbabwean doctor

Fee planning deserves particular care for Zimbabwean candidates, because Zimbabwe currently operates a dual-currency system in which both the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) and the US dollar are recognised as legal tender, with the US dollar still dominant for larger, cross-border transactions such as international exam and visa fees. Given how much the ZiG/USD exchange rate has moved in recent years, this guide gives GMC fees in GBP first, with a rough US dollar reference for budgeting purposes only; always confirm the live exchange rate and the current GMC figures on gmc-uk.org before committing funds, since neither currency conversion nor GMC fees are fixed for long periods.

Because many Zimbabwean doctors are not yet earning UK-level income at the point of applying, this discount is genuinely worth checking on the GMC's fees page before assuming you must pay the full rate.

Step 4: The application and registration process, step by step

Although every candidate's exact order varies slightly, most Zimbabwean doctors follow a recognisable sequence. Working through it as a checklist helps avoid the common trap of booking an exam before a prerequisite is actually in place.

  1. Confirm eligibility. Check your PMQ, internship and English language position against current GMC criteria before spending money on anything else.
  2. Start EPIC/MyIntealth verification early. Because your Zimbabwean medical school must respond to a verification request, this step has the least predictable timeline and should be initiated months, not weeks, before you plan to register.
  3. Keep your MDPCZ registration current where relevant, since MDPCZ documents commonly support your GMC application and EPIC/MyIntealth file.
  4. Sit IELTS or OET and receive your certified score, allowing time for a resit if your first attempt falls short of the required bands.
  5. Book and sit the AKT at a Pearson VUE centre, confirming current availability in or near Zimbabwe well in advance, typically after several months of structured revision.
  6. Book and travel for the CPSA in Manchester, usually scheduling a short UK visit that combines final preparation with the assessment itself.
  7. Submit your GMC registration application once your AKT and CPSA results, EPIC/MyIntealth report, English language evidence and internship confirmation are all in hand.
  8. Pay the relevant registration fee (provisional or full, per the table above) and await GMC processing.
  9. Receive GMC registration with a licence to practise, at which point you can legally begin applying for NHS posts.
  10. Arrange your visa once you have a confirmed job offer, most commonly the Health and Care Worker visa (see below).

Because several of these steps run in parallel rather than strictly one after another, experienced candidates recommend starting EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English testing as early as possible, so that by the time your AKT and CPSA results arrive, the rest of the paperwork is not the bottleneck. Our GMC registration requirements for international doctors guide sets out this sequencing in more depth and applies directly to Zimbabwean applicants.

Provisional or full registration: which should a Zimbabwean doctor apply for?

Many Zimbabwean doctors are unsure whether to apply for provisional or full registration first, and the answer depends mainly on whether your internship is already complete and confirmed at the point you apply. Provisional registration, at the lower £26 application fee, is generally for doctors who still need to complete part of their internship requirement under supervision, while full registration, at £481, is for doctors who can already evidence a complete, GMC-acceptable internship alongside their other documents. Applying for the wrong category can cost a Zimbabwean doctor weeks of otherwise avoidable delay, so it is worth checking your MDPCZ internship completion paperwork carefully before submitting anything, particularly if your internship finished some years ago and you are unsure how the GMC will treat the gap.

Most Zimbabwean doctors report a total timeline of roughly twelve to eighteen months from the point they begin serious AKT preparation to the point they hold full GMC registration, though this varies considerably depending on how quickly EPIC/MyIntealth verification completes and whether a resit is needed at either stage. Sitting the CPSA in the UK: visas and travel

Because the CPSA takes place in Manchester, Zimbabwean candidates need a UK visa purely to attend the assessment, even though this is not yet an employment visa. Most Zimbabwean doctors travelling for the CPSA use a Standard Visitor visa, since the trip is for an examination rather than paid work. This is a different visa entirely from the one needed later, once you have an NHS job offer: at that stage, most Zimbabwean doctors move onto the Health and Care Worker visa, a reduced-fee, sponsored route with no Immigration Health Surcharge, specifically designed for NHS-facing roles. Our full UK visa guide for international doctors explains both visa types and when each one applies, so you are not caught applying for the wrong category at the wrong stage.

Because CPSA travel adds a real cost layer beyond the exam fee itself, most advisers suggest budgeting separately for flights, UK accommodation for several days, and, ideally, a short CPSA-focused preparation course taken either online beforehand or in person just before your sitting. Treat this as part of your overall UKMLA budget rather than an afterthought, and see our broader practical guide to relocating to the UK for IMG doctors for the wider move beyond just the CPSA trip, including housing, banking and registering with a GP on arrival.

Zimbabwe's long history of doctor migration to the UK and NHS

Zimbabwe has one of the longer-established histories of doctor migration to the UK among Commonwealth countries, a pattern rooted in shared English-medium medical training, historic professional and educational ties, and, more recently, sustained domestic pay and resourcing pressures inside Zimbabwe's health system. Academic and NHS workforce research has tracked this trend for some years: at the end of December 2023, there were 563 Zimbabwean-trained medical doctors on the GMC register, up from 322 in 2010, and Zimbabwe has featured among the leading source countries for health and care workers using UK Certificates of Sponsorship in recent years. These figures move over time and are best treated as directional rather than fixed, so always check the GMC's own published workforce data and research for the current picture rather than relying on any single year's snapshot.

This context matters for two practical reasons. First, it means a Zimbabwean doctor entering the UKMLA route today is joining an already well-established diaspora community, with peer networks, WhatsApp groups and mentoring relationships that can meaningfully shorten the learning curve around test centres, CPSA preparation and NHS job-hunting. Second, it means recruiters, hospital trusts and question-bank providers are generally very familiar with Zimbabwean qualifications and the MDPCZ registration structure, which tends to reduce friction compared with routes from countries with a smaller or newer UK pipeline. It is also worth reading this guide alongside UKMLA for South African Doctors, since South Africa is Zimbabwe's immediate Southern African neighbour, shares several structural similarities in medical training and registration, and has an equally long-standing NHS migration history, meaning many of the logistical lessons (test centre planning, CPSA travel, visa sequencing) genuinely transfer between the two guides.

UKMLA route vs staying within Zimbabwe or moving regionally

A meaningful share of Zimbabwean medical graduates weigh the UKMLA route against staying within Zimbabwe's own public or private health system, or against regional options such as South Africa, because all represent genuine next steps after MBChB and internship. Remaining in Zimbabwe under MDPCZ registration keeps a doctor close to family and community and avoids the cost and disruption of relocation, but it also means continuing to work within a health system that many diaspora doctors cite as under-resourced relative to demand. Moving into the UK's NHS through the UKMLA route, by contrast, generally offers a more predictable salary structure, clearer specialty training pathways, and access to the wider Commonwealth medical diaspora already established in the UK, at the cost of a demanding exam-and-registration process and a significant life relocation.

Neither option is inherently "better," and the right choice depends on career goals, family circumstances and risk tolerance. Is an MBChB from Zimbabwe actually recognised for UK practice?

This is one of the most common anxieties among Zimbabwean doctors considering the UKMLA route, and the honest answer has two parts. First, an MBChB from a Zimbabwean medical school listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools is generally accepted by the GMC as a valid primary medical qualification, meaning the degree itself is not the obstacle. Second, holding an accepted degree does not by itself entitle you to practise in the UK: you must still pass the AKT and CPSA (or hold a recognised exemption), complete an accepted internship, provide English language evidence, and pass EPIC/MyIntealth verification. In other words, the question "is my Zimbabwean MBChB valid in the UK" and the question "can I practise in the UK yet" have different answers, and conflating the two is a common source of confusion among candidates researching this route for the first time.

What happens after you register: NHS jobs, salary and career progression

Once you hold GMC registration with a licence to practise, you are legally able to apply for NHS posts, but registration itself is not a job offer, and Zimbabwean doctors sometimes underestimate how much separate work the job search stage requires. Realistically, most Zimbabwean IMGs move into their first NHS role either through direct trust-level applications, through NHS-facing recruitment campaigns aimed specifically at overseas doctors, or, in some cases, via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive post over the following years.

Salary expectations matter here too, since many international doctors significantly overestimate or underestimate likely UK earnings before they arrive. Foundation-level and equivalent junior doctor posts sponsored under the Health and Care Worker visa typically start in a defined NHS junior doctor pay band, rising considerably as a doctor moves into specialty training and, eventually, consultant or GP-partner level roles, though exact figures depend on grade, specialty, location and whether the post is a training or non-training contract. Our UK doctor salary guide, though originally written with Indian IMGs in mind, sets out the same generic NHS pay bands and take-home pay considerations that apply to any international doctor, including Zimbabwean IMGs, once tax, National Insurance and GMC annual fees are deducted.

Foundation Programme, direct NHS posts, or specialty training first?

Not every Zimbabwean IMG follows the same entry point into NHS work, and this is a genuine decision rather than a formality. Some Zimbabwean doctors, particularly those who register relatively early after completing their internship, pursue routes connected to the Foundation Programme or apply for Foundation-equivalent posts; others move straight into a suitable non-training NHS role, often described as a "trust grade" or "clinical fellow" post, and gain UK clinical experience before applying into formal specialty training later. Because entry requirements, competition ratios and the Multi-Specialty Recruitment Assessment (MSRA) differ across these routes, Zimbabwean doctors should decide deliberately rather than defaulting to whichever job appears first. Common mistakes Zimbabwean applicants make on this route

Several avoidable mistakes recur often enough among Zimbabwean applicants that they are worth naming directly.

  • Leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification until after the AKT. Because verification depends on your university's response time, starting late is the single most common cause of an otherwise avoidable multi-month delay.
  • Confusing MDPCZ registration with GMC registration. Being registered and in good standing with the MDPCZ does not itself satisfy any GMC requirement; the two are separate regulators with separate applications, even though MDPCZ paperwork often supports the GMC file.
  • Assuming a local AKT test centre slot is guaranteed. Pearson VUE centre availability in and around Zimbabwe can change, so candidates should confirm current options, including neighbouring countries, well before their planned exam date.
  • Underestimating CPSA travel costs. Flights, UK accommodation and a short preparation course add a meaningful sum on top of the £1,036 CPSA fee itself, and candidates who budget only for the exam fee are often caught short.
  • Applying for the wrong registration category. Confusing provisional and full registration, particularly around an older or ambiguous internship record, can add weeks of otherwise avoidable delay.
  • Treating currency conversions as fixed. Because Zimbabwe's currency situation involves both the ZiG and the US dollar, and exchange rates move, candidates who lock in a budget based on an old conversion rate risk a shortfall; always re-check GBP figures directly against the current rate near the time of payment.
  • Not reading the GMC's own guidance directly. Independent blogs and forums are useful for context, but fee amounts, eligibility criteria and processing times should always be confirmed on gmc-uk.org before any major financial or travel decision is made.

Frequently asked questions

Do Zimbabwean medical graduates have to sit the UKMLA?

Yes. Doctors who qualified outside the UK, including holders of an MBChB from a Zimbabwean medical school, must pass the two GMC-recognised assessments under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, generally called the AKT and the CPSA, before they can register with the GMC, unless they already hold a recognised exemption such as specialist or GP registration through a portfolio route.

What is the UKMLA fee for Zimbabwean doctors in 2026?

As of 1 April 2026, the GMC charges £283 for the AKT and £1,036 for the CPSA, plus a registration application fee of £26 for provisional registration or £481 for full registration. Combined exam and registration costs typically fall between roughly £1,345 and £1,800, before English test, EPIC/MyIntealth, travel and visa costs, so always confirm the current figures on gmc-uk.org.

Is an MBChB from Zimbabwe recognised by the GMC?

An MBChB from the University of Zimbabwe, the National University of Science and Technology (NUST) or Midlands State University is generally accepted as a primary medical qualification because these schools are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC checks during registration. Recognition of the degree itself is separate from passing the UKMLA route and satisfying every other registration requirement.

What is the role of the MDPCZ in a Zimbabwean doctor's UK application?

The Medical and Dental Practitioners Council of Zimbabwe (MDPCZ) is Zimbabwe's own regulator; it registers doctors to practise inside Zimbabwe and is not part of the GMC application itself. However, most Zimbabwean doctors keep their MDPCZ registration current, since it evidences internship completion and good standing, both of which the GMC and EPIC/MyIntealth verification process may reference.

What is EPIC/MyIntealth verification and do Zimbabwean doctors need it?

EPIC, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform operated by ECFMG/Intealth, is the primary source verification service that confirms a medical degree is genuine by checking directly with the awarding university. GMC registration cannot proceed without a valid EPIC/MyIntealth verification report, so Zimbabwean doctors should start this process as early as possible, ideally before booking the AKT.

Can Zimbabwean doctors sit the AKT in Zimbabwe?

Availability of Pearson VUE test centres inside Zimbabwe can change, so candidates should always check current seat availability directly with Pearson VUE and the GMC before assuming a local sitting is guaranteed. Many Zimbabwean candidates end up sitting the AKT in a neighbouring country such as South Africa if no local centre slot is available.

What English language test do Zimbabwean doctors need for the UKMLA route?

Most Zimbabwean doctors sit IELTS Academic, needing an overall score of 7.5 with at least 7.0 in each of the four components, or OET Medicine with at least grade B in every section. Some candidates may qualify for an exemption if their medical education was conducted and assessed substantially in English, which is worth checking given that English is Zimbabwe's primary language of medical instruction.

How long does the whole UKMLA route take for a Zimbabwean doctor?

Most Zimbabwean candidates take roughly twelve to eighteen months from starting AKT preparation to receiving full GMC registration, once English test scores, EPIC/MyIntealth verification, AKT, CPSA, visa arrangements and internship confirmation are all accounted for. Candidates who plan documents and verification early tend to move faster than those who leave paperwork until after passing exams.

Do Zimbabwean doctors need a UK visa to sit the CPSA?

Zimbabwean nationals normally require a Standard Visitor visa to travel to the UK for the CPSA sitting in Manchester, since it is a short assessment visit rather than employment. The Health and Care Worker visa becomes relevant later, once a Zimbabwean doctor has a confirmed NHS job offer.

Why have so many Zimbabwean doctors historically moved to the UK?

Zimbabwe has one of the longer-established histories of doctor migration to the UK and NHS among Commonwealth countries, driven by shared English-medium medical training, historic ties, and, more recently, domestic pay and resourcing pressures. GMC registration data show the number of Zimbabwean-trained doctors on the UK register rising over the past decade, though exact figures should always be checked against the GMC's own published workforce statistics.

Can Zimbabwean doctors get a discount on GMC fees?

The GMC offers a 50% discount on the annual retention fee and, in some cases, the full registration fee for doctors whose worldwide income falls below the GMC's income discount threshold, which rises to £38,500 from April 2026. Zimbabwean doctors who are not yet earning UK-level income at the time of applying may be eligible, so it is worth checking current criteria on gmc-uk.org.

What happens after a Zimbabwean doctor passes the UKMLA route and registers with the GMC?

Once registered with a licence to practise, most Zimbabwean IMGs move into NHS employment, either directly into a suitable post or via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive NHS role and, eventually, applying for specialist or GP registration if they wish to practise independently in the UK.
