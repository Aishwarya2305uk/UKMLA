The Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) is the objective structured clinical examination (OSCE) component of the UKMLA. It is sat in the final year of medical school and tests the physical examination, clinical reasoning, communication, and professional skills that every foundation doctor must demonstrate before entering independent practice. This guide covers everything you need to know about CPSA clinical examinations: the format, the mark scheme, how to approach every major system examination, common examiner expectations, and a structured preparation strategy.

CPSA Format and Structure

The CPSA is an OSCE — a series of timed clinical stations, each testing a defined set of skills. Candidates rotate through stations in sequence, with rest stations interspersed. Examiners mark each station using a standardised mark scheme while you complete the examination or communication task.

Key structural features:

  • Multiple stations (typically 16–20 in full OSCE format), each lasting five to ten minutes
  • Mix of clinical examination stations, communication/history stations, procedural skills stations, and clinical reasoning/data interpretation stations
  • Standardised patients or clinical mannequins, depending on station type
  • Each station marked by one or two examiners using a structured mark scheme
  • A global rating component in addition to task-specific checklist marks
  • Compensatory passing: strong performance in some stations can compensate for weaker performance in others, provided no single station falls below the minimum threshold

The CPSA mark scheme typically allocates marks across three to four domains within each station: clinical technique (performing the examination correctly); clinical interpretation (correctly identifying findings and reasoning about them); communication (explaining findings to the patient, discussing a plan); and professionalism (introduction, consent, patient comfort, hand hygiene).

Core Principles for Every CPSA Station

Before examining individual system examinations, the following principles apply to every station and are marked in every station:

WIPING — the acronym used by many medical schools for the opening sequence:

  • Wash hands (gel or soap — gel is acceptable and typically available at the station)
  • Introduce yourself (full name, role: "I'm [name], a final-year medical student")
  • Patient's name and date of birth (confirm you have the correct patient)
  • Informed consent ("Would it be alright if I examined your chest today?")
  • Not in pain (check the patient is comfortable and not in acute pain before examining)
  • General exposure (position and expose appropriately for the examination you are performing)

Positioning and exposure: Each examination has a specific starting position. Getting this right before you begin earns marks and demonstrates clinical knowledge. Cardiovascular: 45 degrees, chest and abdomen exposed. Respiratory: 45 degrees, chest exposed. Abdominal: lying flat at 45 degrees. Neurological (upper limb): sitting. Neurological (lower limb): lying flat or sitting for different components.

Verbalize your findings as you go: Many CPSA mark schemes award marks for verbalising findings, not just performing the action. If you find a normal apex beat, say so: "The apex beat is in the fifth intercostal space, mid-clavicular line — this is normal." If you find dullness to percussion, say "I can hear dullness to percussion here — this is abnormal." Examiners can only mark what they hear you say.

End with a presentation: Most stations require a verbal presentation of your findings at the end. Structure this as: "On examination, this patient's cardiovascular system appears to be within normal limits. I found no peripheral stigmata of heart disease, the JVP was not elevated, the pulse was regular at 70 beats per minute, heart sounds one and two were present with no additional sounds, and there was no peripheral oedema."

Cardiovascular Examination

This is the most commonly tested system in CPSA OSCE stations and is reliably tested in almost every sitting.

Peripheral examination (hands and arms): Look for clubbing (loss of angle between nail and nail bed; subungual fluctuation — the "floating nail" sign); cyanosis (peripheral — blue digits from poor perfusion; central cyanosis is checked at the lips and tongue); splinter haemorrhages (linear brown lines beneath the nail — associated with infective endocarditis but also found with nail trauma); koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails — associated with iron deficiency); leukonychia (white nails — hypoalbuminaemia); capillary refill (press for 5 seconds, refill should be less than 2 seconds).

Pulse assessment: Radial pulse — rate (count for 15 seconds × 4 if regular; count for 60 seconds if irregular), rhythm (regular, irregularly irregular = AF, regularly irregular = ectopics or second-degree heart block), character (describe as normal in most cases; collapsing/bounding pulse of aortic regurgitation confirmed by lifting the arm). Simultaneously assess for radio-radial delay (coarctation of aorta, cervical rib) and radio-femoral delay (coarctation — feel femoral pulse with other hand).

Blood pressure measurement: The examiner may ask you to describe how you would measure blood pressure rather than perform it in the station. Know the correct technique: seat the patient, cuff at heart level, appropriate cuff size (bladder 80% of arm circumference), inflate to 30 mmHg above obliteration of radial pulse, deflate at 2 mmHg/second, record Korotkoff 1 (systolic) and Korotkoff 5 (diastolic). Explain that you would measure postural BP (lying and standing) if clinically indicated.

Face and eyes: Corneal arcus (white/grey ring around the iris — associated with hyperlipidaemia in patients under 50; non-specific in older patients); xanthelasma (yellow cholesterol deposits around the eyes — hyperlipidaemia); pallor (conjunctival pallor for anaemia — pull down lower lid); malar flush (mitral stenosis facies — now rare).

JVP assessment: Position patient at 45 degrees. Look for the JVP at the right internal jugular vein, lateral to the sternocleidomastoid, medial to the external jugular. Normal: <3 cm above the sternal angle. Raised JVP: right heart failure, cardiac tamponade, SVC obstruction, fluid overload. Characteristics: pulsatile (two waves per cardiac cycle), descends on inspiration, obliterated by pressure (unlike a carotid pulse which is palpated, not obliterated). Hepatojugular reflux: apply firm pressure to the right upper quadrant — a sustained rise in JVP suggests right heart failure.

Precordium inspection and palpation: Look for scars (midline sternotomy — previous CABG or valve surgery; lateral thoracotomy — previous lung surgery or mitral valvotomy; pacemaker or ICD scar below left clavicle). Feel the apex beat: locate position (normally 5th ICS, MCL), assess character (tapping — mitral stenosis; heaving/pressure-loaded — aortic stenosis; thrusting/volume-loaded — aortic or mitral regurgitation; diffuse/displaced — dilated cardiomyopathy). Parasternal heave: right ventricular hypertrophy. Thrills (palpable murmurs).

Auscultation: Mitral area (apex, 5th ICS MCL with diaphragm); tricuspid area (lower left sternal edge, 4th ICS); pulmonary area (upper left sternal edge, 2nd ICS); aortic area (upper right sternal edge, 2nd ICS). Additionally auscultate in the axilla (radiation of mitral regurgitation) and carotids (radiation of aortic stenosis or carotid bruit). Comment on S1 (mitral and tricuspid valve closure), S2 (aortic and pulmonary valve closure), additional sounds (S3 = ventricular gallop — volume overload/heart failure; S4 = atrial gallop — stiff ventricle in hypertension or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy), and murmurs (timing, location, character, radiation, response to manoeuvres if asked).

Completing the cardiovascular examination: Check for peripheral oedema (sacral and pedal — press for 30 seconds). Auscultate the lung bases bilaterally (bibasal crackles in pulmonary oedema from left heart failure). Palpate the abdomen for hepatomegaly (hepatic congestion in right heart failure). State you would review observations, perform an ECG and request a chest X-ray and echocardiogram.

Respiratory Examination

General inspection: Cyanosis; respiratory rate and work of breathing; accessory muscle use; position (patients with severe COPD may sit forward, using the tripod position); oxygen delivery devices; sputum pot; inhaler devices.

Hands and arms: Clubbing (bronchogenic carcinoma, bronchiectasis, pulmonary fibrosis, cystic fibrosis — note that COPD does NOT cause clubbing); peripheral cyanosis; carbon dioxide retention flap (asterixis — coarse tremor when wrists extended — in severe hypercapnia); tar staining (cigarette smoking — clinical sign, not a finding to emphasise positively).

Face and neck: Pursed-lip breathing (COPD); central cyanosis (tongue and lips); trachea position (midline, deviated towards collapse, deviated away from tension pneumothorax/massive effusion); Horner's syndrome (miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis — Pancoast tumour).

Expansion: Place hands on the chest wall symmetrically, thumbs meeting in the midline. Ask patient to take a deep breath. Asymmetric expansion: reduced on the affected side in effusion, collapse, consolidation, pneumothorax. Reduced bilaterally: COPD, pulmonary fibrosis.

Percussion: Compare left and right at each level — anterior, lateral, posterior. Dull: consolidation, effusion, collapse. Stony dull: effusion. Resonant: normal. Hyper-resonant: pneumothorax, emphysema.

Auscultation: Vesicular (normal); bronchial breathing (consolidation); absent breath sounds (effusion, pneumothorax); added sounds — crackles (fine crackles = pulmonary fibrosis; coarse crackles = pulmonary oedema, bronchiectasis; crackles that clear on coughing = secretions); wheeze (polyphonic — asthma/COPD; monophonic — local obstruction); pleural rub (pleurisy — creaking/rubbing quality).

Vocal resonance: Ask patient to say "99". Normal: muffled. Increased: consolidation (bronchophony). Absent: effusion. Whispering pectoriloquy (consolidation — whispered words transmitted clearly) and egophony ("e" sounds like "a" — consolidation above effusion).

Completing the respiratory examination: Review oxygen saturations, check peak flow if appropriate, review observations chart and any recent sputum cultures or spirometry. State: "I would like to complete my examination by reviewing the chest X-ray, spirometry, and arterial blood gas results."

Abdominal Examination

General inspection: Jaundice; pallor; cachexia; abdominal distension (fluid, fat, flatus, faeces, fetus, fibroids — the "6 Fs"); visible peristalsis; herniae; prominent venous pattern (caput medusae in portal hypertension — veins radiate from umbilicus).

Hands and periphery: Clubbing (Crohn's disease, liver disease); leukonychia (hypoalbuminaemia); koilonychia (iron deficiency from GI blood loss); palmar erythema (chronic liver disease, pregnancy); spider naevi (chronic liver disease — more than five over the SVC territory); Dupuytren's contracture (alcohol-related liver disease); asterixis (hepatic encephalopathy); gynaecomastia (chronic liver disease); testicular atrophy (chronic liver disease).

Face: Jaundice (scleral icterus — check conjunctiva; yellow skin); anaemia (conjunctival pallor); parotid enlargement (alcohol-related liver disease); Kayser-Fleischer rings (green-brown rings at corneal periphery — Wilson's disease — rare but tested).

Palpation: Light palpation first (9 regions, start away from pain); then deep palpation. Liver (start from right iliac fossa, move upwards — percuss first to identify lower border); spleen (start from right iliac fossa, move towards left hypochondrium — turn patient right side up to bring spleen forward; confirm with splenic notch); kidneys (bimanual palpation — ballotable, moves on inspiration, not crossing midline — distinguishes from spleen); aorta (pulsatile midline mass above umbilicus).

Percussion: Liver span (upper border = hepatic dullness on percussion from resonance — typically 5th ICS MCL; lower border = first resonance below lower edge). Splenic dullness. Shifting dullness and fluid thrill for ascites (test if you suspect ascites from inspection).

Auscultation: Bowel sounds (active, absent, tinkling — small bowel obstruction); renal bruits (renal artery stenosis); hepatic bruit (hepatoma, acute alcoholic hepatitis); splenic rub (rare — splenic infarct, polycythaemia).

Completing the abdominal examination: State you would examine the hernia orifices (inguinal canals, umbilicus, scars); perform a rectal examination if indicated; examine external genitalia in male patients if indicated; request LFTs, FBC, U&Es, abdominal ultrasound.

Neurological Examination: Upper Limb

The standard approach: inspection, tone, power (MRC grade), reflexes, coordination, sensation, functional assessment.

Inspection: Wasting (loss of muscle bulk — suggests lower motor neuron lesion or primary muscle disease); fasciculations (involuntary muscle twitching — motor neuron disease, radiculopathy); deformity; abnormal posture (flexion at elbow and wrist — upper motor neuron lesion affecting upper limb).

Tone: Passively flex and extend each joint, asking the patient to relax. Spasticity (velocity-dependent increased tone — upper motor neuron lesion; "clasp-knife" quality); rigidity (lead-pipe — basal ganglia disease; cogwheel — Parkinson's plus tremor).

Power: MRC grading: 0 = no movement, 1 = flicker only, 2 = movement with gravity eliminated, 3 = movement against gravity but not resistance, 4 = movement against some resistance, 5 = normal. Test shoulder abduction (C5), elbow flexion (C5,6), elbow extension (C7), wrist extension (C7), finger extension (C7), finger abduction (T1), grip strength.

Reflexes: Biceps (C5,6), brachioradialis (C5,6), triceps (C7). Grade: 0 = absent, + = present only with reinforcement, ++ = normal, +++ = brisk (UMN sign), ++++ = clonus. Hyperreflexia (UMN); hyporeflexia/areflexia (LMN, peripheral neuropathy, cerebellar).

Coordination: Finger-nose test (past-pointing = cerebellar); dysdiadochokinesis (rapid alternating movements — cerebellar). Intention tremor (worsens on approaching target — cerebellar). Resting tremor (present at rest, reduced by movement — Parkinson's).

Sensation: Light touch (cotton wool — spinothalamic and dorsal column); pinprick (neurological pin — spinothalamic); vibration sense (128 Hz tuning fork at bony prominences — dorsal column); proprioception (joint position sense — dorsal column). Test from periphery inward to identify the level of any deficit.

Neurological Examination: Lower Limb

Follows the same structure as upper limb but adapted for lower limb anatomy. Additional assessments: gait, Romberg's test, plantar reflexes.

Power: Hip flexion (L1,2), hip extension (L5,S1), knee flexion (L5,S1), knee extension (L3,4), ankle dorsiflexion (L4,5), plantar flexion (S1,2), great toe extension (L5).

Reflexes: Knee jerk (L3,4), ankle jerk (S1,2), plantar response (Babinski — normal = downgoing; UMN = upgoing/extensor, with fanning of toes).

Gait: Observe normal walking: step length, arm swing, heel strike. Then ask the patient to walk heel-to-toe (tandem walking — tests cerebellar). Note: broad-based gait (cerebellar); high-stepping gait (foot drop — common peroneal nerve palsy, L4/5 radiculopathy); scissor gait (UMN — spastic); antalgic gait (pain); Parkinsonian gait (shuffling, reduced arm swing, festination, difficulty initiating).

Romberg's test: Stand with feet together, eyes open (uses vision, vestibular, and proprioception); then close eyes (removes visual input — now relies on proprioception and vestibular). Positive Romberg = sways or falls with eyes closed but not open = proprioceptive deficit (dorsal column lesion, peripheral neuropathy). Note: cerebellar ataxia causes swaying with eyes open — Romberg's test is positive only for proprioceptive deficits.

Thyroid and Endocrine Examination

Thyroid examination is a reliable CPSA station in many institutions. It combines peripheral assessment, neck examination, and specific thyroid signs.

Peripheral signs of thyroid disease: Hands — fine tremor (thyrotoxicosis); onycholysis (Plummer's nails — separation of distal nail from bed, thyrotoxicosis); dry, coarse skin and hair (hypothyroidism); pretibial myxoedema (bilateral non-pitting oedema below the knees — Graves' disease specifically, not all hyperthyroid states); acropachy (periosteal new bone formation — Graves').

Face: Exophthalmos/proptosis (Graves' disease); lid lag and lid retraction (thyrotoxicosis); periorbital oedema (hypothyroidism); loss of outer third of eyebrows (hypothyroidism — classical sign).

Neck inspection: A visible goitre (diffuse, nodular, or focal); ask patient to swallow a sip of water — thyroid tissue rises on swallowing (distinguishing it from lymph nodes and lipomas).

Palpation: Stand behind the patient; use both hands to palpate the gland; feel both lobes and the isthmus; identify whether diffuse (Graves', Hashimoto's, simple goitre) or nodular (multinodular goitre, adenoma, carcinoma); note tenderness (thyroiditis); ask patient to swallow again — confirm movement on swallowing.

Percussion: Percuss over sternum for retrosternal goitre (dullness rather than expected resonance).

Auscultation: Listen over the goitre with the bell — a thyroid bruit indicates increased vascularity (Graves' disease; large goitre).

Peripheral Vascular Examination

Inspection: Skin changes (shiny, hairless, pale skin — arterial insufficiency); colour changes (pallor on elevation, rubor on dependency — Buerger's test); ulceration (arterial ulcers: punched-out, painful, necrotic, on pressure points and distally; venous ulcers: shallow, wet, sloughy, gaiter distribution above medial malleolus); gangrene; varicose veins; lipodermatosclerosis (brown pigmentation from haemosiderin deposition in chronic venous disease).

Palpation: Temperature (cooler peripherally — arterial insufficiency); capillary refill. Pulses: femoral (midpoint of inguinal ligament), popliteal (knee slightly flexed, two-handed technique, feel behind knee), posterior tibial (behind medial malleolus), dorsalis pedis (dorsum of foot, lateral to extensor hallucis longus). An absent pulse is a significant finding — document and report.

ABPI (Ankle Brachial Pressure Index): State you would measure the ABPI with a handheld Doppler — systolic ankle pressure divided by systolic brachial pressure. Normal: 0.9–1.3. >1.3 suggests calcified vessels (diabetes, renal failure). 0.5–0.9: mild-moderate peripheral arterial disease. <0.5: severe ischaemia. <0.3: critical ischaemia (rest pain, ulceration, gangrene).

Buerger's test: Elevate legs to 45 degrees — pallor of the foot suggests arterial insufficiency. Then lower to dependent position — reactive hyperaemia (bright red/purple foot) = Buerger's sign = critical ischaemia.

How to Present Your CPSA Findings

A structured verbal presentation at the end of each CPSA station is a key mark-scoring opportunity. Use this structure:

  1. State what you have done: "I have performed a cardiovascular examination on [patient's name]."
  2. Present positive findings in a logical order: "On examination, I found that the pulse was irregularly irregular at a rate of 80 beats per minute. The blood pressure was not measured but I noted no peripheral stigmata of heart disease. The JVP was elevated at 4 cm above the sternal angle. On precordial auscultation I heard an early diastolic murmur loudest at the left sternal edge, consistent with aortic regurgitation."
  3. Summarise your impression: "In summary, the findings are consistent with aortic regurgitation with evidence of volume overload."
  4. State investigations: "I would like to confirm this with an ECG, chest X-ray, and echocardiogram, and I would discuss these findings with my senior colleague."

CPSA Preparation Strategy

Six to eight months before the CPSA, establish a weekly practice routine. The minimum effective practice frequency is two to three full system examinations per week on real patients. OSCE preparation without real patient contact is substantially less effective — mark schemes test clinical technique that can only be refined with practice on actual human anatomy.

Frequently Asked Questions: CPSA Clinical Examinations

How long do I have for each CPSA station?
Station length varies by institution, but typically five to eight minutes for a single-system examination, with a brief reading time (one to two minutes) before entry to read the station stem.

Are all findings in the CPSA real clinical findings or simulated?
Standardised patients in CPSA stations may have real clinical signs, simulated clinical signs (using make-up, props, or trained patients who can mimic examination findings), or no findings at all (a "normal" examination is equally valid and must be correctly identified as normal). Mannequins are used for procedural stations.

What if I cannot find a clinical sign that I believe should be present?
If you have performed a thorough, systematic examination and have not elicited a finding, present your negative finding confidently: "On examination of the respiratory system, I found no clinical signs — specifically, the chest expansion was equal bilaterally, percussion was resonant throughout, and breath sounds were vesicular with no added sounds." A false finding is worse than a correct negative finding.

Should I ask the patient about symptoms during a CPSA examination station?
Usually not — examination stations test examination technique. The station stem will specify whether you should take a history as well. Asking the patient "are you having any pain?" before you start examining is appropriate (part of the WIPING sequence); but launching into a full history in an examination station will cost you time and marks.

What is the global rating and how much does it affect the mark?
The global rating is a holistic assessment by the examiner of whether your overall performance was appropriate for a borderline foundation doctor at this station. In most CPSA marking schemes, the global rating contributes 20–40% of the total station mark. Candidates who perform well on the checklist but receive a low global rating have typically been technically correct but clinically robotic, unsafe, or poorly patient-centred.

Can I go back to a previous step if I forget it?
Yes — within reason. If you have omitted the JVP assessment and remember before you have completed the precordial examination, go back and check it. Most examiners will award the mark even if the sequence was not perfectly ordered. What you cannot do is go back to a step after you have already completed the station and moved to your presentation.

Conclusion: Clinical Examination in the CPSA Rewards Systematic Practice

The CPSA rewards candidates who have practised clinical examination systematically, deliberately, and repeatedly in real clinical environments. There is no substitute for examining actual patients. The mark schemes are learnable, the expected sequence is teachable, and the clinical signs are finite — but only real clinical experience builds the pattern recognition, clinical confidence, and patient-centred fluency that produces a high global rating from the examiner.

Start early, practise often, seek senior feedback, and approach every ward patient as a potential CPSA station. For more information on the UKMLA overall, see our What Is the UKMLA page, and for the AKT clinical knowledge component, see our UKMLA Preparation Guide.

Handling Unexpected Findings in CPSA Examination Stations

One of the most anxiety-provoking moments in a CPSA station is finding something unexpected — a murmur you did not expect, a cranial nerve deficit you are unsure how to characterise, or apparent clinical signs you have never seen before. The correct approach is systematic and calm.

Complete the examination before interpreting: Do not stop mid-examination to announce your interpretation. Complete the full sequence, then present. This is how real clinical examinations work, and CPSA mark schemes reward completion of the full examination even when you have identified a positive finding early.

Describe what you find, even if you cannot name it: If you find an irregular cardiac rhythm and cannot confidently name the arrhythmia, say "I noted an irregularly irregular pulse that I would investigate with an ECG." If you find an abdominal mass you cannot characterise fully, describe its location, size, surface (smooth or irregular), consistency, borders, and whether it moves on respiration. Accurate description is rewarded; an incorrect diagnosis is not penalised the same way as missing the finding entirely.

Never fabricate findings: If you genuinely cannot find a clinical sign that you believe is present, present a thorough negative finding. Inventing auscultatory findings or claiming to feel an organomegaly you cannot find will undermine your credibility and lose you marks. Examiners are looking for clinical honesty.

Physical Examination in the Context of UKMLA AKT Questions

Physical examination knowledge is not confined to the CPSA — it also appears in AKT questions. These questions typically take one of three forms:

Sign interpretation: "A 67-year-old man is found to have a pansystolic murmur radiating to the axilla. What is the most likely diagnosis?" The answer is mitral regurgitation. The AKT expects you to know the characteristic features of common heart murmurs, chest signs, abdominal findings, and neurological signs — not just how to examine for them, but what the findings mean clinically.

Examination technique decision: "Which physical examination finding would best differentiate cardiac failure from a pleural effusion in a patient with acute dyspnoea?" The answer requires knowledge of the distinctive clinical signs of each condition — the AKT is not testing your examination sequence but your understanding of which findings discriminate between diagnoses.

Investigation choice based on examination finding: "A 55-year-old woman is found to have a raised JVP, bibasal crackles, and pedal oedema. What is the most appropriate initial investigation?" The physical examination findings lead directly to the clinical reasoning and investigation choice. This integrates examination knowledge with clinical management decision-making — a common AKT structure.

The Role of Clinical Placements in CPSA Preparation

There is no substitute for clinical placement experience as preparation for the CPSA. Every patient you examine on a ward, in an outpatient clinic, or in a primary care setting is CPSA practice — provided you approach the examination systematically, verbalise your findings, and seek supervisor feedback.

Make specific requests of your clinical supervisors and registrars during placements:

  • "Please can you watch me examine this patient and give me feedback on my technique?"
  • "I found X on examination — is that accurate and what does it indicate?"
  • "I struggled with the cardiovascular auscultation on this patient — could we go through the murmur together?"
  • "Would you be willing to assess me on a structured history-taking station with feedback?"

Consultants and registrars who have been through UKMLA-era medical training will often be willing to provide structured CPSA-oriented feedback — particularly if you frame the request as: "I'm working towards my CPSA and would find it really helpful to get some clinical examination feedback." This framing is more likely to produce useful, specific feedback than a generic "can I get some feedback?"

Simulation as a CPSA Preparation Tool

Clinical simulation centres — available at most medical schools — offer opportunities to practise procedural skills (venepuncture, cannulation, catheterisation, basic life support) and clinical examination on standardised mannequins before the CPSA. For CPSA procedural stations, simulation is the primary preparation pathway: you cannot practise IV cannulation on a real patient for the first time in a licensing examination.

Use simulation sessions to practise not just the technical procedure but also the communication that accompanies it: introducing yourself, obtaining consent, explaining the procedure to the simulated patient (or talking aloud through the steps as if addressing a patient), and checking the patient's comfort throughout. CPSA procedural stations often include a communication component alongside the technical skill, and candidates who only practise the technical procedure in silence lose easy communication marks.

Lymph Node Examination

Lymphadenopathy is a common presentation in the AKT (differential diagnosis of cervical lymphadenopathy, axillary lymphadenopathy in suspected breast disease, inguinal lymphadenopathy in lower limb and genital conditions) and is tested as a clinical examination skill in the CPSA.

The systematic approach to lymph node examination follows anatomical groups. For cervical lymphadenopathy — the most commonly tested region:

Inspect first: visible swellings, overlying skin changes (redness suggests infection; adherence to skin suggests malignancy). Palpate from behind the seated patient, using the flat of the fingers. The systematic cervical sequence: submental (under the chin), submandibular (under the mandible), pre-auricular (in front of the ear), post-auricular (behind the ear), occipital (posterior skull), anterior cervical chain (along the anterior border of sternocleidomastoid), posterior cervical chain (along the posterior border of sternocleidomastoid), supraclavicular (in the supraclavicular fossa — supraclavicular lymphadenopathy is particularly significant as it suggests intrathoracic or intra-abdominal malignancy; a hard, fixed left supraclavicular node is Virchow's node — associated with gastric carcinoma, also called Troisier's sign).

When palpating lymph nodes, characterise each by: size (in centimetres); consistency (soft = reactive lymphadenitis or viral infection; rubbery = lymphoma; hard/stony = malignancy); surface (smooth or irregular); tenderness (tender = infective; non-tender = malignancy or lymphoma); fixity (mobile = reactive; fixed/matted = malignancy or TB); overlying skin (warm and erythematous = infection; fluctuant = abscess; peau d'orange = malignancy with dermal lymphatic involvement).

Complete a lymph node examination by examining all lymph node groups relevant to the clinical presentation — not just the group the patient presents with. A patient with cervical lymphadenopathy requires examination of axillary nodes, epitrochlear nodes, and inguinal nodes to determine whether the lymphadenopathy is localised (suggests a local cause) or generalised (suggests a systemic cause such as lymphoma, leukaemia, viral illness such as EBV, or HIV).

Breast Examination

Breast examination is a CPSA station at many institutions and tests both examination technique and communication skills (obtaining consent for an intimate examination; maintaining patient dignity throughout).

Inspection: Patient seated, arms by sides — inspect for asymmetry, skin changes (dimpling, peau d'orange, erythema), nipple changes (inversion, discharge, eczematous change, retraction). Then ask patient to raise arms above head — accentuates tethering from underlying tumour. Then hands on hips pressing inward — contracts pectoralis major, accentuating medial tethering.

Palpation: Patient supine, arm raised on side being examined. Use the flat of the hand in a systematic pattern (radial or quadrant). Feel each quadrant and the axillary tail. If a lump is found, characterise it: site, size, shape, consistency, surface, edge, tenderness, tethering to skin or deep fascia, nipple involvement. Palpate the axillary, supraclavicular, and infraclavicular nodes for lymphadenopathy. Examine the contralateral breast equally.
