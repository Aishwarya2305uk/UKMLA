Medically unexplained symptoms UKMLA revision is one of the most commonly under-prepared areas of the exam, despite functional presentations accounting for a significant proportion of everyday clinical work in both primary and secondary care. Candidates spend weeks memorising the pharmacology of rare organic diseases while giving almost no structured thought to the patient sitting in front of them with chronic pain, fatigue, or neurological symptoms that no scan or blood test can explain. This is a mistake the UKMLA blueprint does not make. The Applied Knowledge Test rewards candidates who understand the appropriate, proportionate investigation of these presentations, while the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) directly tests the communication skill of explaining a functional diagnosis without dismissing the patient's distress. This guide covers the concept of somatisation and functional disorders, the exam's central investigation principle, the specific communication techniques examiners look for, chronic fatigue syndrome/ME as a worked example, and the multidisciplinary management model that underpins good practice in this area.

Quick Answer: What Are Medically Unexplained Symptoms?

Medically unexplained symptoms (MUS) are physical symptoms — pain, fatigue, weakness, dizziness, gastrointestinal upset, or neurological disturbance — for which no adequate structural or biochemical explanation is found after appropriate clinical assessment. They are sometimes called functional symptoms, functional disorders, or persistent physical symptoms, and the umbrella term somatisation describes the general process by which psychological distress is expressed through physical complaints. For the UKMLA, the essential exam principle is twofold: recognise that a thorough but proportionate work-up has already reasonably excluded serious organic pathology, and then manage the patient without either over-investigating (repeated, low-yield tests that reinforce anxiety) or under-investigating (dismissing new or changing symptoms as "just functional" and missing an evolving organic diagnosis). Both directions are genuine exam traps, and safe answers sit deliberately in the middle. Because this pattern of presentation recurs across almost every specialty, revising medically unexplained symptoms UKMLA-style — as a distinct, structured topic rather than a psychiatry footnote — pays off across a disproportionate number of AKT items and at least one CPSA station type.

Understanding Somatisation and Functional Disorders

Somatisation is the unconscious process by which psychological or emotional distress manifests as physical symptoms. It is not the same as malingering (deliberate fabrication for secondary gain) or factitious disorder (deliberate production of symptoms for the internal psychological gratification of the sick role) — both of these involve conscious intent, whereas somatisation and functional disorders do not. This distinction is tested repeatedly in the AKT, and confusing the categories is one of the most common single-best-answer errors candidates make when a medically unexplained symptoms question is disguised as a straightforward psychiatry stem.

Terminology the Exam Expects You to Know

The language in this area has shifted over the past decade, and the UKMLA reflects current usage rather than older, more stigmatising terms. "Hysteria" and "conversion disorder" have largely been replaced in clinical practice by functional neurological disorder (FND) when symptoms affect the nervous system (weakness, seizures, sensory disturbance, gait abnormality) and by the broader term persistent physical symptoms or bodily distress disorder (the ICD-11 term) when symptoms are more generalised. Somatic symptom disorder (the DSM-5 term) requires the physical symptom plus excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviours related to the symptom — persistent high anxiety about the symptom's seriousness, disproportionate time and energy devoted to it, or repeated help-seeking. Illness anxiety disorder (previously hypochondriasis) is distinct: it describes a preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness in the relative absence of significant somatic symptoms. Candidates should be comfortable distinguishing these categories, since AKT questions frequently present a vignette and ask which label best fits.

Why These Presentations Are So Common

Functional and medically unexplained presentations are not a niche curiosity — they represent a very large share of outpatient medicine, and a candidate who has never deliberately revised this territory is likely underprepared relative to how often it appears. Studies cited by the Royal College of Psychiatrists estimate that around one in four patients seen in general practice, and as many as one in three patients seen in neurology outpatient clinics, present with symptoms that cannot be fully explained by identifiable disease. In gastroenterology, functional presentations such as irritable bowel syndrome are the single most frequent diagnosis made. In cardiology, non-cardiac chest pain accounts for a substantial proportion of emergency department presentations once acute coronary syndrome has been excluded. The scale of this problem is precisely why the UKMLA tests it so directly: a newly registered doctor who cannot manage functional presentations safely and compassionately will struggle across almost every specialty, not just psychiatry.

There is also a workforce and resource dimension the exam expects candidates to appreciate. Patients with unexplained symptoms who are repeatedly investigated without a clear framework often undergo excessive imaging, invasive procedures, and specialist referrals, consuming disproportionate NHS resources while frequently feeling more, not less, reassured. Breaking this cycle safely — not by refusing further tests outright, but by applying a structured, proportionate approach — is a genuine patient safety and health economics issue, not merely a communication nicety.

The Biopsychosocial Model

The biopsychosocial model underlies almost all UKMLA content on this topic. It holds that biological, psychological, and social factors all interact to produce and maintain physical symptoms, regardless of whether a structural cause is ultimately found. Biological factors might include a genuine history of previous illness or altered pain processing; psychological factors include anxiety, depression, health-related beliefs, and past trauma; social factors include stressful life circumstances, secondary gain (financial or social benefits associated with the sick role, which need not be consciously sought), and family or cultural attitudes to illness. The model is deliberately non-dualistic — it does not frame symptoms as "either physical or psychological" but as the product of an interacting system. Exam answers that treat MUS as "all in the patient's head" or that ignore psychological and social contributors entirely are both incorrect framings.

Appropriate Investigation: Avoiding the Two Opposite Traps

The single most heavily tested exam principle in this topic area is proportionate investigation — doing enough to rule out organic disease, and no more. AKT questions on functional presentations are frequently designed with two wrong answers representing the two opposite failure modes, and only one answer representing the safe, guideline-concordant middle path.

The Trap of Over-Investigation

Over-investigation is the pattern of ordering repeated, escalating, or low-yield tests in response to a patient's ongoing anxiety or repeated presentations, rather than because new clinical findings warrant it. This is harmful for several distinct reasons the exam expects candidates to articulate. First, every investigation carries a false-positive risk, and incidental findings on imaging (an indeterminate nodule, a minor structural variant) can trigger a cascade of further invasive tests and patient anxiety disproportionate to any genuine risk. Second, repeated normal results can paradoxically increase health anxiety rather than reduce it, since brief reassurance is followed by renewed worry when a new symptom appears — patients can come to feel that "something must have been missed" precisely because so much has been tested. Third, over-investigation reinforces the belief that the symptoms must have an undiscovered organic cause, undermining any subsequent attempt to introduce a functional or psychological explanation. Fourth, repeated invasive procedures carry direct iatrogenic risk (bleeding, infection, radiation exposure from repeated CT imaging) that is not justified once a reasonable initial work-up has been completed.

A classic AKT stem describes a patient with a several-year history of unexplained abdominal pain, multiple normal colonoscopies and CT scans, and a general practitioner considering "one more scan just to be safe." The correct answer is rarely "arrange the scan" — it is usually to consolidate the existing negative work-up, avoid further imaging in the absence of new red-flag features, and move toward explanation and management. Candidates who have separately revised how to interpret laboratory results and imaging reports often find this the easiest style of question to master quickly, since the skill being tested is restraint and pattern recognition rather than any additional factual recall.

The Trap of Under-Investigation

The opposite error is equally tested and, if anything, more dangerous in real practice: dismissing new, changed, or red-flag symptoms as "just the patient's usual functional symptoms" without proper reassessment. This is sometimes called diagnostic overshadowing when it occurs in patients with an existing psychiatric or functional diagnosis, and it is a recognised cause of serious harm and missed diagnoses. A patient with a long-established diagnosis of functional abdominal pain who develops new weight loss, rectal bleeding, or a palpable mass needs the same objective work-up as anyone else — the pre-existing functional label must never be allowed to anchor the clinician away from red-flag features. Similarly, a patient with an established diagnosis of chronic fatigue syndrome who develops new focal neurological signs, a new murmur, or unexplained weight loss requires fresh assessment on the merits of the new symptom, not reflexive reassurance.

The exam's safe answer, in essence, always asks: has an adequate initial assessment already excluded serious pathology, and if so, are there any genuinely new red-flag features now present? If the answer to the first is yes and the second is no, further routine escalation of investigation is not indicated. If any new red flag appears, it is reassessed on its own merits regardless of the patient's existing functional diagnosis.

Red Flags That Always Warrant Further Assessment

Certain features should never be attributed to a functional cause without fresh objective assessment, irrespective of how long a patient has carried a functional diagnosis.

  • Unintentional, significant weight loss
  • New focal neurological signs (as opposed to the non-anatomical, inconsistent pattern typical of functional neurological disorder — see below)
  • Rectal bleeding, haematemesis, or other overt blood loss
  • A new palpable mass or organomegaly
  • Fever, night sweats, or other systemic features suggesting infection or malignancy
  • Progressive symptoms that deviate from the patient's established, stable pattern
  • Objective abnormalities on basic bedside observations (abnormal vital signs, new murmur, abnormal reflexes)

A useful exam heuristic: functional symptoms are typically variable, inconsistent with recognised anatomical or physiological patterns, and influenced by attention or distraction, whereas organic disease tends to follow a more fixed, anatomically or physiologically coherent pattern that does not fluctuate with distraction. This is not absolute — functional disorders can and do coexist with organic disease in the same patient — but it is a genuinely useful bedside discriminator that the CPSA and AKT both test.

Functional Neurological Disorder as the Prototype Case

Functional neurological disorder (FND) deserves specific attention because it is now understood as a genuine, positively diagnosable neurological condition rather than simply "a diagnosis of exclusion." Modern neurology increasingly diagnoses FND on the basis of positive clinical signs demonstrating internal inconsistency, rather than purely by ruling everything else out. Recognised positive signs taught for the UKMLA include Hoover's sign (weakness of hip extension that resolves when the contralateral leg is tested for hip flexion against resistance, demonstrating involuntary preserved strength), tremor entrainment (a functional tremor changes frequency or temporarily stops when the patient is asked to copy a different rhythmic movement with the unaffected limb), and give-way weakness (strength that suddenly gives way rather than showing a smooth, graded reduction). Functional seizures (previously called pseudoseizures or non-epileptic attacks) can be distinguished from epileptic seizures by features such as duration (often longer), pelvic thrusting, side-to-side head movement, closed eyes with resistance to opening, and preserved awareness during apparent bilateral limb movement — though video-EEG telemetry remains the definitive investigation when the diagnosis is genuinely uncertain.

The exam-relevant point is that a positive FND diagnosis, once made confidently by a specialist using recognised clinical signs, does not require an ever-expanding volume of normal MRI and EEG results to be "confirmed." Explaining FND as a real, diagnosable disorder of nervous system functioning (the "software" rather than the "hardware," in the analogy widely used in patient-facing FND resources) rather than an absence of any diagnosis at all is central to good communication in this area, covered further below. FND is arguably the clearest single example of why medically unexplained symptoms deserve dedicated revision time rather than being folded into general psychiatry preparation.

Communicating a Functional Diagnosis Without Dismissing the Patient

This is the domain the CPSA tests most directly, and it is a genuine, learnable communication skill rather than an innate trait. The single biggest pitfall candidates fall into under exam pressure is unintentionally implying that the symptoms are not real, imagined, or "nothing to worry about" — language that patients with lived experience of medically unexplained symptoms consistently describe as invalidating and that damages the therapeutic relationship, sometimes permanently. Communicating medically unexplained symptoms well, in other words, is as much a scoring criterion as any single piece of factual knowledge in this station type.

Why This Conversation Is Difficult

Patients presenting with functional symptoms have frequently already had the experience of feeling disbelieved, either by previous clinicians or by family and friends. Many will have actively feared, before the consultation even begins, that a doctor is about to tell them "there's nothing wrong with you" or "it's all in your head." A poorly delivered explanation confirms this fear and can entrench the very anxiety and symptom-focused attention that maintains the condition. A well-delivered explanation, by contrast, can be genuinely therapeutic in itself — a clear, validating, and mechanistically coherent explanation is one of the few interventions shown to improve outcomes in functional disorders, independent of any other treatment that follows.

A Structured Approach for the CPSA

Examiners are typically looking for a recognisable structure rather than any single "correct" script. The following sequence reflects widely taught good practice and maps closely onto CPSA mark schemes for these stations.

Step 1: Validate the Symptom and the Distress

Begin by explicitly acknowledging that the symptom is real and that the patient's distress is understandable. Avoid opening with "the good news is we haven't found anything" — from the patient's perspective, an unexplained symptom that persists is not obviously good news, and this phrasing can sound dismissive. A stronger opening validates first: "I can see this pain has been really difficult for you, and I want to talk through what we've found and what I think is going on."

Step 2: Summarise the Negative Work-Up Honestly and Positively

Explain, in plain language, what has been tested and what those tests show — framed as good news about the absence of serious disease, not as a failure to find anything useful. "The blood tests, the scan, and the examination haven't shown any sign of a serious underlying disease affecting your organs" is more reassuring, and more accurate, than "we can't find anything wrong."

Step 3: Give a Positive, Mechanistic Explanation

This is the step candidates most often skip, moving straight from "tests are normal" to "so let's manage your anxiety" — a jump patients experience as a non-sequitur that implies the symptom is psychological in a dismissive sense. Instead, offer a genuine, positive mechanism. For functional neurological disorder, an example explanation used widely in UK neurology clinics describes the nervous system's "software" sending or receiving abnormal signals despite the "hardware" being structurally normal — the limb weakness, tremor, or seizure-like episode is a real disturbance of nervous system function, not a sign of an underlying degenerative or structural disease, and not something the patient is consciously producing or able to simply will away. For chronic pain or fatigue syndromes, an explanation grounded in altered central pain or fatigue processing (the nervous system becoming over-sensitised to normal signals) is both accurate and validating.

Step 4: Explicitly Name and Defuse the Fear of Not Being Believed

Directly addressing the unspoken fear — "I want to be clear that I do believe your symptoms are real, and this isn't a case of there being nothing wrong" — is a specific, learnable phrase that CPSA mark schemes reward highly, because it closes the exact gap that damages trust if left unaddressed.

Step 5: Offer a Constructive, Forward-Looking Plan

Close by moving to what can be done, not just what has been ruled out. This naturally leads into the multidisciplinary management approach discussed below, and gives the patient a concrete next step rather than leaving the consultation feeling like a dead end.

Language to Avoid in the CPSA

Certain phrases are recognised in CPSA mark schemes as risking marks even if the underlying medical content is otherwise correct, because of how they land with a patient in genuine distress.

  • "There's nothing wrong with you" — implies the symptom is not real
  • "It's all in your head" — implies the symptom is imagined rather than a genuine disturbance of function
  • "It's just stress" — minimises a complex, multifactorial presentation into a single dismissed cause
  • "The good news is the tests are normal" as an opening line, without first validating distress — sequencing matters as much as content
  • Any phrasing that suggests the patient is exaggerating, malingering, or seeking attention

The same communication skill set that underpins these stations is developed more broadly in the site's dedicated guide to breaking difficult news and managing emotionally charged consultations, and candidates preparing for this station type should treat that material and this one as complementary rather than duplicative. Further patient-facing background on how this condition is explained and supported is available from the Royal College of Psychiatrists' patient information page, which is a useful source for candidates wanting to see the explanation style recommended to patients directly.

Chronic Fatigue Syndrome/ME as a Worked Example

Chronic fatigue syndrome, also known as myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME/CFS), is the single most useful worked example of a functional, medically unexplained symptoms presentation for UKMLA revision, because it combines a genuine, biologically grounded illness with almost every principle covered above: proportionate investigation, careful communication, and multidisciplinary management. It is essential to state clearly, and the exam expects candidates to understand, that ME/CFS is a real, disabling, biologically recognised condition — it is not synonymous with "unexplained tiredness" or a psychological diagnosis of convenience, even though no single diagnostic biomarker currently exists.

Diagnostic Criteria

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline NG206, published in 2021, sets out the current UK diagnostic and management framework and represents a significant shift from earlier guidance. Diagnosis should be considered when a person has debilitating fatigue that is not caused by ongoing exertion, not substantially relieved by rest, and worsened by activity, present for at least six weeks in adults (or four weeks in children and young people, with paediatric assessment), alongside at least one of: post-exertional malaise (a delayed, disproportionate worsening of symptoms after physical, cognitive, or emotional exertion, often 24 to 72 hours later), cognitive difficulties ("brain fog," problems with word-finding, memory, or concentration), unrefreshing sleep, or orthostatic intolerance or other symptoms worsened on standing. NICE NG206 specifically emphasises that post-exertional malaise is the defining, cardinal feature that must be actively asked about — its presence is central to the diagnosis, and its absence should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis. A formal diagnosis can be made after three months of these symptoms in adults, once alternative explanations have been reasonably excluded.

Appropriate Investigation in Suspected ME/CFS

Investigation should be proportionate and aimed at excluding recognised alternative or comorbid explanations, not exhaustive. Recommended baseline tests typically include full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, thyroid function tests, coeliac screening, creatine kinase, C-reactive protein, and urinalysis — a focused panel aimed at ruling out anaemia, thyroid disease, coeliac disease, inflammatory or infective processes, and renal or hepatic dysfunction as alternative causes of fatigue. Any abnormal renal or liver result uncovered at this stage should prompt the same careful, dose-adjusted approach to any subsequent medication choices covered in the site's guide to safe prescribing when organ function is impaired, since patients with a functional diagnosis are not exempt from needing dose adjustment if genuine organ impairment is also found. Beyond this proportionate initial panel, repeated or escalating imaging and specialist referral in the absence of new red-flag features is not indicated and reflects the over-investigation trap discussed earlier in this guide.

Management: What Changed in NICE NG206

The 2021 update to NICE guidance marked a genuinely significant, and heavily examinable, change in management philosophy. Previous guidance had recommended graded exercise therapy (GET) — a structured, progressively increasing exercise programme — as a first-line treatment. NICE NG206 explicitly states that graded exercise therapy involving fixed incremental increases in physical activity should not be offered as a treatment for ME/CFS, in direct recognition of the risk that fixed increases in activity can trigger post-exertional malaise and worsen the underlying condition. This is one of the most frequently tested single facts in this topic area precisely because it represents a guideline reversal candidates may not encounter unless they specifically revise the current version.

In place of GET, NICE NG206 recommends an individualised, symptom-led approach to activity management, sometimes referred to as "energy management" or "pacing" — helping the patient establish a sustainable baseline level of activity that does not trigger post-exertional malaise, and only very cautiously increasing activity if and when the patient's own tolerance allows, guided by the patient rather than imposed as a fixed protocol. Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) may still be offered, but NG206 is explicit that it should be offered as a supportive tool for symptom management and coping, not as a treatment presented as curative of the underlying condition — a subtle but exam-relevant distinction from how CBT was sometimes framed in earlier guidance. Medication is used symptomatically (analgesia for pain, individualised approaches to sleep disturbance) rather than as disease-modifying treatment, since no such treatment currently exists. NICE also emphasises structured care planning, energy management support from an appropriately trained physiotherapist or occupational therapist, and recognition of the significant impact on education, employment, and family life, particularly in children and young people.

Multidisciplinary Management of Functional Disorders

Beyond the specific example of ME/CFS, the UKMLA expects candidates to understand that medically unexplained symptoms and functional disorders are best managed through coordinated, multidisciplinary care rather than by any single specialty acting in isolation. This multidisciplinary lens is precisely what separates a strong answer on medically unexplained symptoms from a merely adequate one when a management question offers several plausible next steps.

The Role of the Generalist

The GP or general physician typically remains the central coordinating clinician, responsible for the initial proportionate work-up, the explanation of the diagnosis, and ongoing continuity of care. Continuity is itself therapeutic in this patient group — repeated presentations to different, unfamiliar clinicians tend to trigger repeat investigation and re-explanation from scratch, undermining the consistent message the patient needs to hear. A single, trusted clinician who can confidently avoid both over- and under-investigating over time produces measurably better outcomes than fragmented care across multiple providers.

The Role of Psychology and Psychiatry

Referral to psychology or, where appropriate, liaison psychiatry does not imply that the underlying problem is "actually psychiatric" in the dismissive sense — it reflects the biopsychosocial model's recognition that psychological therapies are genuinely effective treatments for the physical symptoms themselves, in the same way that physiotherapy is a genuine treatment for a musculoskeletal complaint. Cognitive behavioural therapy helps patients identify and modify unhelpful illness beliefs and behaviours (excessive checking, avoidance, catastrophising) that can perpetuate symptoms. Liaison psychiatry services, based within general hospitals, specifically bridge physical and mental health care for patients whose presentations cross both domains, and are a recognised, examinable part of the UK secondary care landscape.

The Role of Physiotherapy and Occupational Therapy

Physiotherapy plays a central role in functional neurological disorder specifically, using specialist retraining techniques that help patients "relearn" normal movement patterns by redirecting attention away from the affected limb — a specific, evidence-based physiotherapy approach distinct from generic strengthening exercise. In ME/CFS and chronic pain syndromes, physiotherapists and occupational therapists support energy management, pacing, and graded functional rehabilitation tailored to the individual's own tolerance, as described above, rather than a fixed incremental protocol. Occupational therapy additionally addresses practical adaptations to daily living, education, and work that allow patients to maintain function and independence within their symptom limits.

Building an effective care plan also means being alert to overlapping presentations more broadly — many patients with functional symptoms have coexisting anxiety or depressive disorders, and safe practice requires screening for and, where present, treating these comorbidities using the same structured approach covered in the site's broader psychiatric revision content, since diagnosable mental illness and functional somatic presentations frequently coexist without either explaining away the other.

Safeguarding and Vulnerable Groups

A final multidisciplinary consideration the exam expects is recognising when a patient's presentation intersects with safeguarding risk — for example, an adult whose repeated unexplained symptoms coincide with signs of coercive control, neglect, or abuse, where the physical presentation may in part reflect an unsafe home environment rather than, or in addition to, a primary functional disorder. Clinicians should never let a functional label foreclose a broader safeguarding assessment where the clinical picture warrants one, a principle explored in more depth in the site's dedicated coverage of recognising and responding to abuse in adult patients.

Putting It All Together: A Worked Exam Approach

When an AKT stem or CPSA station presents a patient with a chronic, previously investigated, unexplained symptom — the classic setup for a medically unexplained symptoms question — work through the same sequence every time. First, check whether the history describes an adequate initial work-up appropriate to the presenting symptom — if so, do not select an answer that orders further routine testing without a new red-flag feature. Second, actively scan the stem for any new or changed feature (weight loss, focal neurology, bleeding, systemic symptoms) that would justify fresh assessment regardless of the existing functional diagnosis. Third, in communication stations, structure the explanation as validate, summarise negative findings positively, give a genuine mechanistic explanation, explicitly address the fear of disbelief, and close with a forward-looking plan. Fourth, think multidisciplinary rather than single-specialty when selecting a next step for ongoing management, matching the professional to the specific therapeutic task rather than defaulting to "refer to psychiatry" for every functional presentation. This structured approach transfers across abdominal pain, chronic fatigue, functional neurological disorder, and non-cardiac chest pain alike, since the underlying exam logic in each case is identical even though the clinical details differ. It is also worth deliberately practising this sequence against past station feedback and mock CPSA scenarios, since fluency under time pressure is what separates a merely correct answer from a confident, well-communicated one on exam day. Building this fluency well in advance of the exam, rather than a few days before, consistently produces stronger performance in both the knowledge-based AKT questions and the applied CPSA stations covering this topic — an argument for weaving this subject into a broader, structured revision timetable rather than leaving it as an afterthought late in the run-up to test day.

Consider also how this approach differs subtly by specialty context, since the exam draws vignettes from general practice, emergency medicine, and hospital outpatient clinics alike. In general practice, the emphasis is usually on longitudinal continuity and avoiding unnecessary onward referral. In the emergency department, the priority shifts toward efficiently excluding acute, dangerous causes in a single attendance while still avoiding reflexive full-body imaging for a patient with a well-documented chronic functional presentation. In outpatient neurology or gastroenterology clinics, the emphasis often falls on making a confident positive diagnosis (using signs such as Hoover's sign, or a normal-calibre colonoscopy alongside a Rome IV-consistent history for irritable bowel syndrome) rather than treating the diagnosis as one of pure exclusion. Recognising which of these contexts a given stem is set in helps narrow down the single best answer far more efficiently than trying to recall an isolated fact in the abstract.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between medically unexplained symptoms and malingering?

Medically unexplained symptoms and somatisation are unconscious processes — the patient genuinely experiences the symptom and is not deliberately producing or exaggerating it for gain. Malingering, by contrast, is the conscious, deliberate fabrication or exaggeration of symptoms, typically for an identifiable external incentive such as financial compensation or avoiding a legal or work obligation. Factitious disorder sits between the two conceptually: symptoms are deliberately produced, but the motivation is an internal psychological need to occupy the sick role rather than an external material gain.

Is chronic fatigue syndrome a real illness or a psychiatric diagnosis?

ME/CFS is recognised by NICE and the wider medical community as a real, biologically grounded, and often severely disabling long-term condition, not a psychiatric diagnosis or a label for unexplained tiredness. It has a defined set of diagnostic features, most importantly post-exertional malaise, and current NICE guidance (NG206) explicitly moved away from framing it primarily through a psychological treatment lens, replacing graded exercise therapy with individualised, patient-led energy management.

Should I keep investigating a patient who keeps returning with the same unexplained symptom?

Only if there is a genuinely new or changed clinical feature, particularly a recognised red flag such as unintentional weight loss, new focal neurological signs, bleeding, or systemic symptoms such as fever. If the initial work-up was proportionate and thorough for the presenting complaint and nothing new has emerged, repeating the same investigations is not indicated and can reinforce the patient's anxiety rather than reassure them.

How do I explain a functional neurological disorder diagnosis without sounding dismissive?

Validate the reality of the symptom first, summarise the negative work-up as reassuring rather than as a failure to find anything, and then give a genuine mechanistic explanation — for example, describing a disturbance in how the nervous system is sending or processing signals rather than structural damage, using the commonly taught "software, not hardware" framing. Explicitly stating that you believe the symptoms are real, and are not implying the patient is imagining them, closes the most common gap that damages trust in this conversation.

What did the 2021 NICE guideline change about chronic fatigue syndrome management?

NICE NG206, published in 2021, stated that graded exercise therapy involving fixed incremental increases in activity should no longer be offered, because it risks triggering post-exertional malaise and worsening symptoms. In its place, NICE recommends individualised, patient-led energy management and pacing, with cognitive behavioural therapy offered as a supportive coping tool rather than a curative treatment.

Which healthcare professionals are typically involved in managing a functional disorder?

Care is usually coordinated by a GP or general physician, with input from clinical psychology for illness beliefs and coping strategies, liaison psychiatry for complex physical-mental health overlap, and physiotherapy or occupational therapy for movement retraining in functional neurological disorder or pacing and energy management in chronic fatigue and pain syndromes. Specialist referral (for example, to neurology) may still be needed to make a confident positive diagnosis using recognised clinical signs.

Do UK medical students and IMGs need to prepare for this topic differently?

The underlying clinical knowledge is identical for both groups, but IMGs should pay particular attention to UK-specific communication norms and NICE-guideline terminology, since some training systems abroad still use older, more dismissive framings of these conditions that would lose marks in a CPSA station. UK medical students, conversely, sometimes underestimate how heavily this topic is weighted in the AKT because it receives comparatively little dedicated teaching time in traditional undergraduate curricula relative to its representation in the exam blueprint.

Conclusion

Medically unexplained symptoms and functional disorders sit at the intersection of investigation strategy, communication skill, and coordinated multidisciplinary care — precisely the combination the UKMLA is designed to test across both the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment and the AKT. Candidates who can reliably avoid both over-investigating and under-investigating, who can deliver a validating and mechanistically honest explanation of a functional diagnosis, and who understand chronic fatigue syndrome and functional neurological disorder as genuine, structured clinical entities rather than diagnoses of exclusion, will consistently outperform peers who treat this as a minor psychiatric footnote. Revise this topic with the same rigour applied to any major organ system, since its representation across both exam components reflects just how frequently it appears in everyday clinical practice.
