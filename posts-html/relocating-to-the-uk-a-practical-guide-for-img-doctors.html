Relocating to the UK for IMG doctors is a genuinely different challenge from winning the job offer or passing the exam that made the move possible in the first place. Once a Certificate of Sponsorship has been issued, a visa has been granted, and a start date has been agreed with an NHS trust, a completely different set of problems takes over — finding somewhere to live without a UK credit history, opening a bank account before you have a fixed address, registering with a GP as a patient rather than as a clinician, applying for a National Insurance number, and working out whether your overseas driving licence lets you get behind the wheel on day one. None of this is covered by the visa paperwork itself, and very little of it is explained clearly in one place, which is precisely the gap this guide is written to close.

This article is deliberately narrow in scope. It assumes you already have a visa, or are far enough into the process that a decision letter is close, and that your focus has shifted from "how do I qualify to work in the UK" to "how do I actually get there and function as a resident from week one." It does not repeat the visa-type and eligibility detail covered in the site's Health and Care Worker visa guide for international doctors, nor does it revisit the process covered in the site's GMC registration requirements guide. Instead, it walks through the practical logistics of relocating: accommodation, banking, GP registration, National Insurance, driving, and a realistic first-month checklist, in the order most newly arrived doctors actually encounter them.

A caveat that applies throughout: exact fees, processing times, and administrative rules referenced below are correct as a general guide but are reviewed periodically by the relevant UK bodies, so always verify current detail on the official gov.uk pages linked throughout this article, or with your sponsoring trust's international recruitment or relocation team, before making firm plans or spending money.

Quick Answer: What Does Relocating to the UK Actually Involve for an IMG Doctor?

Relocating to the UK for IMG doctors typically involves five practical strands that run in parallel rather than in strict sequence: securing accommodation (often starting with short-term lets before a longer tenancy), opening a UK bank account, registering with a local GP practice as a resident patient, applying for a National Insurance number, and establishing whether an existing overseas driving licence remains valid to drive in the UK or needs to be exchanged. None of these five things depend on each other in a fixed order, but several create awkward circular dependencies — a bank often wants proof of a UK address before opening an account, while some landlords want proof of UK income or a bank account before signing a tenancy. Understanding these interdependencies in advance, rather than discovering them one by one after arrival, is what separates a smooth first month from a stressful one.

Many of these hurdles exist because the underlying UK systems — credit referencing, banking risk assessment, residency registration — were designed around people who already have a UK footprint. A newly arrived doctor, however well qualified and however secure their NHS job, simply does not yet have a UK credit history, a UK address history, or a UK utility bill, and every one of these systems asks for exactly that. The sections below explain, strand by strand, both the standard process and the practical workarounds that genuinely help when you are, administratively speaking, starting from zero.

Finding Accommodation as a Newly Arrived IMG Doctor

Finding somewhere to live is usually the first problem to solve when relocating to the UK for IMG doctors, often before they have even landed in the country, and it is also the one where the "guarantor and credit history" problem bites hardest. Renting in the UK operates on a system that assumes a prospective tenant already has a UK financial footprint: a credit score built from UK bank accounts, UK utility bills, and a UK payslip history. A doctor arriving from overseas has none of these things on day one, no matter how strong their income or professional standing is in reality, which is why so many newly arrived IMGs describe the private rental market as the single most frustrating part of relocating to the UK.

Understanding the UK Rental Market Basics

Most private rentals in the UK are arranged through letting agents rather than directly with landlords, and the standard process involves viewing a property, submitting an application, passing referencing checks (income, credit history, and often a guarantor), paying a deposit — legally capped and protected in a government-backed deposit scheme — and signing an Assured Shorthold Tenancy agreement, typically for an initial fixed term of six or twelve months. Rent is usually quoted and paid monthly, and it is standard practice for a letting agent to ask for the first month's rent plus a deposit (commonly equivalent to five weeks' rent) before the keys are handed over. Council tax, a local authority charge based on the property's value band, is usually the tenant's responsibility on top of rent, and utility bills (gas, electricity, water, broadband) are typically arranged separately by the tenant rather than included in the rent, unless a listing explicitly states otherwise.

Properties advertised as "all bills included" exist, particularly for house-shares and some purpose-built rental developments, and can meaningfully simplify the first few months by removing several separate account-opening steps at once. For a first UK tenancy, particularly one arranged before or immediately after arrival, this kind of simplified arrangement is often worth the modest rent premium it typically carries.

The Guarantor and Credit History Challenge

The single most common obstacle newly arrived IMG doctors report is the referencing process itself. UK letting agents routinely run a credit check and an income affordability check on every applicant, and both checks are built around UK data — a credit file built from UK bank accounts, loans, or mobile phone contracts, and payslips or an employment contract from a UK employer. A doctor who has just arrived, or who is applying before arrival, typically has no UK credit file at all, which referencing software often treats as a red flag rather than a neutral absence of information, even though the underlying reason (having lived abroad until now) has nothing to do with financial reliability.

The standard solution letting agents ask for in this situation is a UK-based guarantor: someone with an established UK credit history and sufficient income who agrees to be legally liable for the rent if the tenant cannot pay it. This is workable for a doctor who already has UK-based family or friends willing to act as guarantor, but it is a genuine dead end for someone with no personal UK contacts at all, which describes a large proportion of first-time IMG arrivals.

Fortunately, several practical workarounds exist and are increasingly well understood by letting agents who deal regularly with international NHS staff:

  • Guarantor insurance schemes — commercial services that act as a paid guarantor on the tenant's behalf, for a one-off fee, when no personal guarantor is available. These are widely accepted by mainstream letting agents and are often the single most practical fix for a doctor with no UK-based contact willing or able to act as guarantor.
  • Paying several months' rent upfront — some landlords and agents will waive the guarantor requirement entirely if a tenant can pay three, six, or even twelve months' rent in advance, since this removes the ongoing payment risk the guarantor was meant to cover. This requires having accessible savings before arrival, so it is worth planning for specifically if a guarantor is unlikely to be available.
  • A signed employment contract or offer letter from an NHS trust — many agents treat a confirmed NHS contract as strong evidence of stable income even without a UK payslip history yet, and NHS employment specifically tends to be viewed favourably by agents and landlords because of its perceived job security.
  • Trust-arranged relocation support — a genuinely underused resource. A meaningful number of NHS trusts, particularly those actively recruiting internationally, offer some form of relocation support to newly sponsored overseas doctors: temporary accommodation for the first weeks after arrival, a list of landlords or agents the trust already has a working relationship with, or occasionally a formal rent guarantee scheme run through the trust's own HR or international recruitment office. This support varies considerably from trust to trust and is not always advertised proactively, so it is worth asking your sponsoring trust's international recruitment team directly, well before your start date, exactly what relocation or accommodation support is available rather than assuming none exists.

Because these options vary by agent, landlord, and trust, doctors should treat "no UK credit history" as a solvable administrative problem rather than a genuine barrier to renting — it very rarely is, in practice, once one of the workarounds above is applied.

Short-Term Accommodation Before a Longer Tenancy

Many relocating doctors deliberately avoid signing a twelve-month tenancy sight-unseen from overseas, and instead book short-term accommodation — a serviced apartment, an extended-stay hotel, or a short-let arranged through a specialist relocation platform — for the first two to six weeks after arrival. This buys time to view properties in person, understand which neighbourhoods suit a commute to the assigned hospital site, and avoid committing to a full tenancy under time pressure from overseas. Some trusts specifically arrange or subsidise this kind of short-term "landing" accommodation as part of their relocation support, which is another reason to ask the question directly rather than assume you are on your own.

When choosing where to live longer-term, commute time and reliability matter more than raw distance, since UK public transport quality and driving times vary considerably even across relatively short distances in and around major cities. Speaking to current staff at the assigned hospital or department — often arranged informally through the trust's international recruitment contact — about where colleagues in similar roles actually live is one of the most useful, low-effort research steps available before committing to a tenancy. Understanding the general shape of NHS pay for a Trust Grade or Foundation-equivalent post, covered in the site's guide to NHS doctor salaries, is also worth doing before signing a long tenancy, since it affects how much rent is realistically affordable in the first year.

Opening a UK Bank Account as a New Arrival

Banking is another area where relocating to the UK for IMG doctors involves genuinely UK-specific quirks that are rarely explained clearly in advance. A UK bank account is close to essential almost immediately after arrival: NHS salaries are paid into a UK account, most landlords expect rent paid from one, and many everyday services simply assume you have one. Yet opening a bank account as a brand-new arrival with no UK address history and no UK credit file is its own small obstacle course, closely related to the accommodation problem above.

Why It Can Be Tricky Without a UK Address or Credit History

Mainstream UK banks are required, under money-laundering and identity-verification regulations, to confirm a new customer's identity and address before opening a current account. The standard way banks confirm address is a recent utility bill, council tax statement, or similar document in the applicant's name at a UK address — precisely the kind of document a newly arrived doctor does not yet have, since it typically takes weeks of UK residence to generate one. This creates the same kind of circular problem seen with renting: banks want proof of a UK address, but obtaining many of the documents that prove a UK address is easier once you already have a bank account and a fixed home.

Because banks are well aware that this affects a predictable population of new arrivals — international students, skilled workers, and NHS staff among them — most have built specific processes to work around it, rather than simply refusing accounts to anyone without a long UK paper trail.

Practical Tips for Opening an Account Before or Shortly After Arrival

  • Use your passport and visa/immigration status document as primary ID — banks generally accept a valid passport together with a Biometric Residence Permit, eVisa share code, or equivalent immigration status evidence as strong identity verification, even without a UK address document yet.
  • A confirmed job offer or employment contract from an NHS trust is frequently accepted by banks as supporting evidence, since NHS employment is widely recognised and considered low-risk, and some banks have simplified onboarding specifically for NHS staff relocating from overseas.
  • Some high-street banks allow the account-opening process to begin before arrival, using a UK branch appointment booked for shortly after landing, or an app-based onboarding process that verifies identity remotely and only requires an in-person visit once a UK address is available. Researching which banks currently offer this before travelling can save several weeks of delay after arrival.
  • Digital-only "challenger" banks often have noticeably lighter address-verification requirements than traditional high-street banks, and many can be opened entirely via a smartphone app within days, sometimes accepting a temporary or short-let address as sufficient at the account-opening stage. A growing number of relocating doctors open one of these accounts in the first week purely to receive their first salary payment and pay a deposit, then add or switch to a traditional high-street account once a longer-term address and address-proof documents are in place.
  • Ask your sponsoring trust's HR or international recruitment team whether they have an existing relationship with a particular bank for newly arrived international staff — a genuinely common arrangement where a trust can vouch for an employee's status directly, smoothing over the address-verification gap considerably.
  • A letter confirming your address from a hotel, serviced apartment provider, or the trust itself is sometimes accepted as interim address evidence where a formal utility bill is not yet available — it is always worth asking a bank directly what alternative evidence they will accept rather than assuming a utility bill is the only option.

Once a full tenancy and the first utility bills are in place, switching to whichever account offers the best everyday banking terms is straightforward — the priority in the first few weeks is simply having any working UK account to receive a salary and pay a deposit, not necessarily the ideal long-term one.

Registering With a GP as a Resident

One of the most frequently overlooked steps in relocating to the UK for IMG doctors is registering with a GP surgery as a patient — not in any clinical or professional capacity, but as an ordinary NHS-registered resident entitled to primary care like anyone else living in the UK. It is a genuinely common misconception among newly arrived IMGs that being a registered medical practitioner somehow substitutes for this, or that GP registration is something to worry about later. It does not, and it is not.

Why Every Resident Needs Their Own GP, Doctors Included

GP registration is how the NHS primary care system identifies who is entitled to receive routine care, repeat prescriptions, referrals to specialists, and — importantly — how a new resident accesses non-emergency healthcare at all in the first place. A doctor working within a hospital trust has no automatic route to NHS primary care simply by virtue of being employed as a clinician; registering as a patient with a local GP practice is a completely separate, everyday administrative step that applies equally to every UK resident, doctors and non-doctors alike. Doctors who delay this, assuming they can "sort it out later" or rely on informal advice from hospital colleagues if something comes up, often find themselves without a straightforward route to routine care exactly when they need it, whether that is a repeat prescription, a referral, or simply a health concern that surfaces in the first few months of a demanding new job.

How GP Registration Actually Works

Registering with a GP in the UK is normally free and does not require British citizenship, permanent residency, or any specific immigration status beyond simply living in the practice's catchment area — a visa holder on a Health and Care Worker visa is fully entitled to register. The standard process involves choosing a practice near your home address (rather than your hospital), completing a registration form either in person, online, or via an NHS app, and providing basic identification and address evidence, though most practices will register a patient even without perfect documentation rather than turn someone away from primary care entirely. Some practices offer online registration through the NHS website or app, which can be completed before an in-person visit is even needed.

Newly arrived doctors should register with a GP as soon as a fixed address is confirmed, even if there is no immediate health concern, because registration itself can take a short period to process, and having an already-registered GP is what allows fast access to an appointment, a prescription, or a referral if something does come up unexpectedly. Registering reactively — only once you are already unwell — routinely adds days of avoidable delay at exactly the wrong moment.

It is also worth registering with a dentist separately, since NHS dental care operates as a distinct registration system from GP care, and access to NHS dentists can be more limited in some areas than GP registration, making it sensible to look into local availability early rather than waiting until a dental issue arises.

Applying for a National Insurance Number

A National Insurance number is a unique personal reference number used by HM Revenue and Customs and the Department for Work and Pensions to record an individual's tax and National Insurance contributions throughout their working life in the UK. It is not the same thing as a visa, a right-to-work document, or a GMC registration number, and it is entirely possible — indeed, normal — to start an NHS job and receive a first salary payment before a National Insurance number has actually arrived.

Who Needs One and When to Apply

Anyone living and working in the UK needs a National Insurance number, and it is used by an employer to correctly record tax and National Insurance contributions against the right person over time. Applicants must generally live in the UK and have the right to work here — both of which a doctor on a valid Health and Care Worker visa satisfies — and the process can typically be started as soon as you arrive, or in some cases before, depending on current guidance. According to the official guidance, processing an application can take up to four weeks, so applying promptly after arrival, rather than waiting until it becomes urgent, is the sensible approach.

Some doctors already have a National Insurance number without realising it, particularly those who previously studied or worked in the UK, or who received one automatically around age sixteen while resident here as a dependant or student. It is worth checking existing paperwork, including any previous visa or Biometric Residence Permit documentation, before assuming a fresh application is needed, since duplicate numbers are not issued and an existing one remains valid for life.

Practical Notes on the Application Process

This is one of the few steps in relocating to the UK for IMG doctors that has a fixed, predictable timeline rather than an open-ended wait, which makes it worth prioritising early. A National Insurance number is not required to legally start work — an employer can process payroll using other right-to-work evidence in the interim, such as a Biometric Residence Permit or eVisa share code, and update payroll records once the number arrives. Doctors should nevertheless apply as early as possible after confirming their UK address, since the number is also used for a range of other everyday purposes beyond payroll, including some benefit applications and building a formal record with HMRC. For the current eligibility rules, application routes, and processing expectations, the definitive source is gov.uk's official page on applying for a National Insurance number, which should be checked directly rather than relying on secondhand summaries, since application routes have been updated in recent years and can vary depending on immigration status and visa type.

Once the number arrives, it is worth recording it somewhere secure and permanent rather than only on the original letter, since it will be needed repeatedly — by payroll, by HMRC for tax queries, and potentially by pension providers — for the entire duration of UK employment.

UK Driving Licence Rules for Overseas Licence Holders

Whether an existing overseas driving licence remains valid in the UK, and for how long, is one of the more commonly misunderstood parts of relocating to the UK for IMG doctors, partly because the rules genuinely differ depending on where the original licence was issued.

When an Existing Overseas Licence Is Valid Temporarily

Full, valid driving licences issued in most non-UK countries can generally be used to drive in the UK for a limited period after becoming a UK resident, without needing to exchange or retest immediately — this window is commonly around twelve months from the date a driver becomes resident, though the exact rule depends on where the licence was issued and whether it is from a country covered by a formal licence-exchange agreement with the UK, the EU/EEA, or neither. During this initial window, a valid overseas licence, together with an International Driving Permit in some cases, is generally sufficient to drive a car in the UK, which is often enough to cover the first weeks or months of settling in without needing to resolve the licence question immediately.

However, this temporary validity period is not indefinite, and it is a genuinely common and entirely avoidable mistake for a newly arrived doctor to keep driving on an overseas licence well past the point it remains valid, simply because no one explicitly warned them the window had a fixed end date.

When a UK Exchange or a UK Driving Test Is Needed

What happens once the temporary validity period ends depends on which country originally issued the licence. Some countries have a formal reciprocal exchange arrangement with the UK, meaning a licence from that country can be exchanged directly for a UK driving licence without needing to sit a UK driving test at all — a straightforward administrative swap once the paperwork is submitted. Licences from countries without such an arrangement, however, generally cannot simply be exchanged, and the driver instead needs to apply for a provisional UK licence and pass both the UK theory and practical driving tests in the normal way, exactly as any first-time UK learner driver would, in order to hold a full UK licence beyond the temporary window.

Because the list of countries eligible for direct exchange is periodically reviewed and can change, and because the exact evidence and process required also depends on individual circumstances, doctors should check their own licence's status directly using the DVLA's official guidance on exchanging a foreign driving licence as soon as possible after arrival, rather than assuming their home country's licence will automatically qualify for exchange. Confirming this early matters practically, since a doctor who discovers late that their licence requires a full UK test, rather than a simple exchange, needs considerably more lead time to book a test, which can itself have a waiting list of several weeks or months in busy areas.

The table below summarises the general shape of the rules, without attaching them to specific named countries, since exchange eligibility is reviewed periodically and the definitive, current, country-by-country answer sits only on the DVLA's own tool.

Doctors who anticipate needing a car for their commute, particularly those taking up posts outside major cities where public transport is less frequent, should treat the driving licence question as a priority item to resolve in the first few weeks rather than an afterthought, precisely because the UK theory and practical test route, if required, can take considerably longer to complete than any other item on this list. A Realistic First-Month Checklist for Relocating IMG Doctors

The list below sequences the practical steps covered in this guide into a realistic order, acknowledging that several strands run in parallel rather than one strictly after another. It is not a rigid script — individual circumstances, trust support, and personal preference will shift the exact order — but it reflects the sequence that tends to cause the least friction in practice.

  • Before departure: confirm exactly what relocation support your sponsoring trust offers (temporary accommodation, guarantor schemes, orientation sessions), and ask specifically rather than assuming none exists.
  • Before departure: research which UK banks allow account opening to begin remotely or via app before arrival, and gather digital copies of your passport, visa/immigration documents, and employment contract.
  • Before departure: check your driving licence's country of issue against the DVLA's exchange guidance so you know in advance whether you are looking at a simple exchange or a full UK test.
  • First few days: move into short-term or trust-arranged accommodation if a longer tenancy is not yet in place, rather than committing to a full tenancy under time pressure from overseas.
  • First week: open a UK bank account, using an NHS employment contract and passport/visa documents as supporting evidence, or a digital-first bank if a UK address document is not yet available.
  • First week: apply for a National Insurance number as early as possible, since processing can take up to four weeks and does not need to wait for your first payday.
  • First two weeks: begin viewing longer-term rental properties in person, and ask your sponsoring trust whether a guarantor scheme, rent guarantee, or landlord contact list is available before approaching the open market alone.
  • Once a fixed address is confirmed: register with a GP surgery near your home as a resident patient, and separately register with an NHS dentist, even if you have no immediate health concern.
  • Once a fixed address is confirmed: update your bank, employer, and any relevant government accounts with your permanent UK address.
  • Within the first month: confirm your overseas driving licence's remaining valid period, and if a UK test is required rather than a direct exchange, book it as early as possible given typical waiting lists.
  • Within the first month: set up council tax and utility accounts for your permanent address, and confirm whether your tenancy is on an "all bills included" basis before opening separate accounts unnecessarily.
  • Ongoing: keep copies of every tenancy agreement, payslip, and official letter from this period — the same records useful for a future visa extension or settlement application are also useful for everyday administrative queries.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a UK guarantor to rent a flat as a newly arrived IMG doctor?

Not necessarily. While many letting agents ask for a UK-based guarantor when a tenant has no UK credit history, workable alternatives exist, including guarantor insurance schemes, paying several months' rent upfront, using a confirmed NHS employment contract as supporting evidence, or asking your sponsoring trust whether it offers relocation or rent-guarantee support.

Can I open a UK bank account before I have a permanent address?

In many cases, yes. Some banks accept a passport, visa or immigration status document, and an NHS employment contract as sufficient evidence to open an account, and several digital-only banks have simplified onboarding that does not strictly require a traditional utility bill. A temporary or short-let address is sometimes accepted as interim evidence, with a permanent address updated once available.

Do doctors need to register with a GP separately from their hospital role?

Yes. Registering with a GP is a completely separate step from being employed as a hospital doctor, and every UK resident, including doctors, needs their own registered GP to access routine NHS primary care, prescriptions, and referrals. Being a registered medical practitioner does not substitute for this in any way.

How long does it take to get a National Insurance number after moving to the UK?

According to official guidance, applying for a National Insurance number can take up to four weeks to process. It is not required to legally start work, since an employer can use other right-to-work evidence in the meantime, but applying as soon as possible after confirming a UK address is the sensible approach.

Can I drive in the UK on my overseas driving licence?

Generally yes, for a temporary period after becoming a UK resident, often around twelve months, depending on where the licence was issued. After that window, whether you can simply exchange it for a UK licence or need to pass a UK theory and practical test depends on your licence's country of issue, which should be checked directly on the DVLA's official guidance.

What should I do first when relocating to the UK for an NHS job?

There is no single mandatory first step, since accommodation, banking, and National Insurance can often be progressed in parallel, but confirming what relocation support your sponsoring trust offers before departure, and opening a bank account and applying for a National Insurance number within the first week of arrival, tends to reduce the most friction early on.

Does my sponsoring NHS trust help with accommodation when relocating?

Some do, though support varies considerably by trust and is not always advertised proactively. It can include temporary accommodation on arrival, a list of landlords or agents familiar with sponsoring international staff, or occasionally a formal rent guarantee arrangement. It is worth asking your trust's international recruitment or HR team directly, well before your start date, rather than assuming no support exists.

Conclusion

Relocating to the UK for IMG doctors is, in practice, a series of solvable administrative problems rather than one single overwhelming task, and nearly every obstacle described in this guide — from the guarantor gap in renting to the address-verification gap in banking — has a well-understood, practical workaround once you know it exists. Doctors who ask their sponsoring trust directly about relocation support, apply for a National Insurance number early, register with a GP as soon as a fixed address is confirmed, and check their driving licence status against current DVLA guidance tend to move through the first month with considerably less friction than those who discover each obstacle only as it arrives.
