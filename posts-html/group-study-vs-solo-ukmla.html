One of the most practical questions UKMLA candidates face is whether to study alone or in a group — and the honest answer is that it depends on which phase of preparation you are in, what type of content you are working on, and what your own cognitive style looks like. This guide examines the evidence for group study versus solo study in medical education, how to structure both approaches effectively for the AKT and CPSA, how digital tools have created hybrid options, and how to build a revision timetable that uses the right approach at the right time.

The Core Tension: Comprehensiveness vs. Efficiency

Group study and solo study each have a fundamental structural advantage and a fundamental weakness. Understanding this trade-off is the foundation of a sound revision strategy.

Solo study is efficient. You move at exactly the pace that your own comprehension and retention require. You spend more time on material you find difficult and skip material you already know. There is no social overhead — you do not need to coordinate schedules, wait for peers to catch up, or divert from your own agenda when group discussion wanders. When you are in a productive flow state of focused study, solo work preserves it.

Group study is comprehensive in a different sense: it surfaces blind spots you cannot find alone. When you explain a concept to another person, you are forced to identify exactly where your understanding stops being solid and starts being approximate. When a peer asks a question you cannot answer, it reveals a gap your own self-assessment missed. When a colleague approaches a problem differently, you see an alternative reasoning pathway that may be more robust than your own.

Neither approach is sufficient alone. A candidate who only studies solo may revise confidently within a narrow framework, passing over gaps they cannot see because they never had to explain their reasoning. A candidate who only studies in groups may cover ground quickly without the sustained, focused consolidation that individual retention requires.

What the Evidence Says

Educational research on medical student learning supports several specific conclusions relevant to UKMLA preparation:

Active recall outperforms passive review in both settings. The "testing effect" (also called retrieval practice) is one of the most robust findings in cognitive science: recalling information from memory — rather than rereading it — produces substantially stronger long-term retention. This applies equally in group and solo settings. A group session built around quizzing each other is educationally superior to a group session where members take turns presenting slides. Solo use of a question bank is educationally superior to rereading notes.

Spaced repetition is more effective than massed practice. A solo study advantage: spaced repetition algorithms (as implemented in Anki) require studying alone, at specific intervals, and work best when each individual's forgetting curve is calibrated to their personal performance. Group spaced repetition is logistically very difficult to implement effectively. If spaced repetition is a core component of your revision strategy — and there is strong evidence it should be — much of this work must be done solo.

Collaborative elaboration improves conceptual understanding. When two or more students work through a clinical scenario together, generating explanations and testing competing interpretations, they show better performance on transfer tasks (applying knowledge to novel scenarios) than students who studied the same material alone. This is the group study advantage — it builds the reasoning flexibility that the AKT's applied SBA format demands.

Peer teaching produces gains for both teacher and learner. Explaining a concept to a peer forces deeper processing than reviewing it alone. Studies in peer instruction consistently show that the student doing the teaching gains as much or more from the interaction as the student receiving the explanation. Structured peer teaching — where each group member prepares and delivers a focused explanation — is one of the highest-yield group study formats.

When to Use Solo Study

Solo study is the right choice when:

  • You are in the initial learning phase of a new topic — building basic familiarity with a presentation, a pharmacological class, or a set of guidelines. You need to acquire knowledge before you can discuss it productively.
  • You are using spaced repetition — Anki, Quesmed, or any algorithm-driven review system. These systems are inherently individualistic; trying to use them in a group session wastes time and defeats the algorithm.
  • You are doing question bank sessions — solo question practice with immediate review of explanations is the highest-yield AKT preparation method. This requires uninterrupted concentration; group question sessions frequently deteriorate into discussion that slows the pace without proportionately improving retention.
  • You are in a productive study state and group logistics are costly — if coordinating with peers requires significant travel, scheduling overhead, or social energy you don't have, the efficiency loss may outweigh the group benefit.
  • The content is factual and discrete — drug doses, reference ranges, guideline cutoffs, Never Events lists. These are best memorised alone using targeted practice; group discussion does not add meaningful value.

When to Use Group Study

Group study is the right choice when:

  • You are applying knowledge to complex clinical scenarios — the AKT's extended matching questions and multi-step clinical reasoning questions are ideal for group discussion. "What would you do next for this patient?" is better explored with two or three people generating competing answers than alone.
  • You want to identify knowledge gaps you cannot see in yourself — a group quiz format where each person answers independently, then compares answers, reveals discrepancies between your self-assessment and your actual performance.
  • You are preparing for CPSA clinical stations — physical examination and communication skills require a second person. Examining a colleague, receiving feedback from a peer or senior, and role-playing communication scenarios are all inherently group activities.
  • You are peer-teaching a topic you need to consolidate — if you have individually revised a topic and now need to deepen your understanding, preparing and delivering a brief explanation to peers is extremely effective.
  • You are demotivated or isolated and need social accountability — motivation and consistency in a long revision period are not purely cognitive challenges. Group study provides accountability, social connection, and shared purpose that can sustain revision when purely solo studying would falter.

Structuring an Effective Group Study Session

Poor group study is very common and very costly: a two-hour session can feel productive because it was social and engaged, while delivering less actual learning than one hour of focused solo question practice. The difference between high-yield and low-yield group study lies almost entirely in structure.

Define the session objective in advance: "We will work through 30 AKT-style questions on cardiology this session." Not "we'll cover cardiology." A specific, measurable objective keeps the session on track and gives you a clear end point.

Divide responsibilities for peer-teaching sessions: Each member prepares a focused 10-minute explanation of a specific topic before the session — not slides, just a verbal explanation plus questions to test the group's understanding. This preparation is solo; the delivery and discussion are group.

Use the "struggle before explain" model: When discussing a clinical scenario, each member should attempt an answer independently before group discussion begins. If the first person states the answer immediately, the group never engages in the reasoning process that produces learning. A brief independent attempt (even 60 seconds) before opening discussion substantially improves the learning value of the subsequent conversation.

Apply a time cap: Discussions without time caps drift. Each scenario or topic in a group session should have a maximum discussion time — two to three minutes per question in a question review session; ten minutes maximum for a peer teaching segment. Enforce these limits.

End with individual consolidation: After a group session, each member should spend 15–20 minutes alone reviewing what they learned, adding new Anki cards for gaps identified, and updating their revision notes. This solo consolidation step is where the group insights are converted into durable individual memory.

Structuring an Effective Solo Study Session

Solo study fails most commonly through distraction, passive review, and failure to test retention. The Pomodoro technique, deliberate practice principles, and active recall protocols all address these failure modes.

Pomodoro technique: 25 minutes focused study, 5 minutes complete rest, repeat four times, then 20–30 minute break. The forced break prevents cognitive fatigue accumulation, and the time constraint creates urgency within each block. For question bank sessions, this is particularly effective.

Active recall over passive review: At any point in a solo session, if you have been reading or watching for more than 15 minutes without actively testing yourself, you are in passive review mode. Switch: close your notes and write down everything you remember about the topic; use flashcard apps to test yourself; answer questions before reading the answer. Every passive review minute is less valuable than an active recall minute.

Environment control: The research on cognitive distraction is unambiguous: notifications, background noise with semantic content (conversations, TV, radio with speech), and access to social media during study sessions impair consolidation. Noise-cancelling headphones; phone in a different room; website blockers during study periods; a dedicated study space — these are performance enhancers, not preferences.

End-of-session review: Spend the final 5–10 minutes of a solo session reviewing the key points of what you studied — without referring to notes if possible. This brief retrieval practice at the end of a session substantially improves retention compared to simply stopping when the time runs out.

Digital and Hybrid Study Tools for UKMLA

The traditional binary between solo desk study and in-person group study has been substantially blurred by digital tools. Several tools support hybrid approaches that combine individual efficiency with group accountability and social learning:

Anki and shared decks: Anki is primarily a solo tool, but medical school communities (r/medicalschool, UK-specific Discord servers, Anki forums) share high-quality pre-built decks that encode the collective work of hundreds of students. Using a shared deck means benefiting from group effort while retaining the individualised learning algorithm of spaced repetition.

Question bank group modes: Passmedicine and some other platforms offer group or shared question session modes where a team can work through the same question set synchronously online, see each other's answers before discussion, and review explanations together. This retains the structured question practice of solo use while adding peer comparison and discussion.

Group note-sharing via Notion or similar: Many UKMLA cohorts build collaborative Notion databases, OneNote notebooks, or Google Docs where each member contributes notes on specific topics. Each member benefits from the group's collective writing effort (breadth) while refining their own notes (depth). The caution: reading other people's notes is passive; using your own actively-created notes is active. Ensure you are using the shared resource as a supplement to individual learning, not a replacement for it.

Virtual OSCE sessions via video call: Online examination practice via Zoom or Teams — one person examines a standardised patient scenario described by the facilitator, peers observe and provide feedback — is a highly effective adaptation of in-person OSCE practice for students who cannot meet in person regularly. The examiner plays a role, the observer(s) mark on a shared mark scheme, and feedback is given immediately. This format scales well to groups of two to four people.

Podcast and audio resources for commutes: One-time, high-density exposure to content via audio (medical education podcasts, recorded lectures) during commute time is a genuine efficiency gain — provided the audio is used for initial exposure and later reviewed through active recall, not treated as a replacement for deliberate practice.

Self-Assessment Strategies: Testing What You Know Before the Exam Tests It

Whether you are studying alone or in a group, the quality of your self-assessment determines whether you are using your revision time on the right material. Common self-assessment mistakes:

Familiarity bias: Material that feels familiar after rereading is not the same as material you have retained. Exposure to information creates a feeling of recognition without necessarily producing recall. Test yourself: can you retrieve this without prompting? If not, you haven't learned it yet — you've recognised it.

Performance vs. learning confusion: Doing well in a mock paper immediately after revising a topic is less predictive of exam performance than performance on the same material tested two weeks later. If you only ever test yourself immediately after revision, you are measuring short-term performance, not durable learning.

Coverage bias: It is tempting to track how many topics you have covered rather than how well you have retained them. A covered-topic list that is 100% complete is less useful than a 70% list where each covered topic has been tested and consolidated to a high standard. Track retention, not coverage.

Practical self-assessment tools for UKMLA: Monthly diagnostic question bank sessions of 50–100 unseen questions give objective data on your overall performance trajectory. Compare your percentage correct month-over-month to identify whether you are improving. Break down performance by domain to identify which areas require more attention. Use this data to adjust your timetable, not just your confidence.

When Solo Study Is Not Working

Signs that your solo study approach is not working and adjustments are needed:

  • Your question bank performance is not improving after two or more weeks of consistent practice in the same domain
  • You feel you are revising efficiently but your mock exam scores do not reflect this
  • You are consistently procrastinating or finding it very difficult to start sessions
  • You feel isolated, demotivated, or anxious about the examination in a way that is affecting your revision consistency
  • You notice you are spending revision time on material you already know well rather than engaging with difficult material

If several of these apply, consider: changing the location of your study; joining or forming a study group for one or two sessions per week; using a productivity accountability partner (someone who is also studying, whom you briefly check in with at the start and end of each session); adjusting the content area you are revising; accessing your medical school's student wellbeing support if motivation loss is connected to broader wellbeing challenges.

Managing Group Study Dynamics

Group study has social dynamics that can either enhance or derail the session. Common challenges and solutions:

Dominance of one voice: One person speaks most of the time; others disengage. Solution: use structured roles (facilitator, timekeeper, question-asker) and rotate them each session. Require each person to answer independently before group discussion.

Off-topic conversation: Session drifts into social conversation. Solution: set a specific objective with a time limit at the start of each session; designate a timekeeper whose role is explicitly to bring the group back to the agenda.

Uneven preparation: Some members have prepared thoroughly; others have not. Solution: establish an explicit expectation that group sessions presuppose individual preparation on the designated topic. Unprepared members should complete the preparation before contributing to peer teaching.

Different revision paces and priorities: Members are at different stages of preparation. Solution: be explicit about the purpose of each session — it should serve everyone's current needs. If preparation levels are too different, consider two separate groups.

Frequently Asked Questions: Group vs. Solo Study for the UKMLA

How many hours per week should I be studying for the UKMLA in final year?
This varies by starting level of knowledge, but most candidates in the final year of a five-year programme are studying 30–50 hours per week in the months immediately before the AKT and CPSA. The quality of those hours matters more than the quantity — 30 hours of active recall is more effective than 50 hours of passive review.

Is studying with friends from my medical school better than studying with students from other schools?
Peers from the same school share the same curriculum, placement experiences, and formative assessment patterns — which can be helpful for shared contextualisation. However, the UKMLA is a common national standard, and studying with students from different schools exposes you to different approaches and educational cultures, which can broaden your reasoning. Both work; cohort familiarity is a minor factor compared to the quality of the study session itself.

How do I know if a study group is helping or hindering my preparation?
Track your question bank performance before and after joining the group. If your domain scores are improving at the same or better rate than they were in solo study, the group is contributing. If your improvement rate has slowed and you are spending more time in group sessions, restructure the group or reduce its frequency.

Should the CPSA be prepared for entirely in groups?
No — but it needs at least some group preparation that solo study cannot replicate. Physical examination requires a second person. Communication stations require a role-play partner. These elements cannot be practised solo. However, reviewing mark schemes, memorising examination sequences, studying clinical signs, and researching what specific findings mean (e.g., "what does a collapsing pulse indicate?") can all be done solo.

Is video-based OSCE practice as effective as in-person practice?
In-person practice with real patients and real anatomy is irreplaceable. But video-call OSCE practice with peers for communication stations and examination demonstrations is genuinely effective — particularly for students with limited access to in-person group sessions during clinical placements. It is substantially better than no CPSA practice at all.

Conclusion: Design Your Revision Around the Task, Not the Format

The group versus solo debate is resolved by asking a simpler question: what does this specific revision task require? Memorising drug doses requires solo focused retrieval practice. Discussing a complex clinical scenario requires two or more minds. Practising an OSCE station requires a partner. Building spaced repetition habits requires solo algorithm-driven review. Consolidating a topic through peer teaching requires a group.

Build your timetable by identifying what each task requires, then deploy the appropriate format. The candidates who perform best in the UKMLA are almost always those who use both approaches deliberately — not those who committed ideologically to one or the other.

Dealing With Information Overload in UKMLA Revision

One of the most common experiences in UKMLA preparation is feeling overwhelmed by the volume of material. The MLA Content Map lists 130 presentations and 430 conditions — and each condition links to pharmacological management, clinical signs, investigations, and professional considerations. The breadth is genuinely large, and candidates who do not manage information architecture deliberately find themselves unable to distinguish what they know from what they have been exposed to.

Whether you study alone or in a group, the following information management principles reduce overload:

Curate ruthlessly: You cannot use every available resource. Choose one question bank and use it consistently; choose one clinical reference (BNF + NICE CKS) and use it as your authoritative source; choose one spaced repetition system (Anki or a built-in question bank spacing algorithm) and use it. Every additional resource you add competes for time and attention with the resources you have already invested in. More resources is not better revision; better use of fewer resources is better revision.

Build a "done" system: For each of the 130 MLA presentations, you should have a clear binary: I have covered this to a standard I am confident in, or I have not. A simple spreadsheet, Notion database, or even a paper checklist serves this purpose. Crossing items off a list is motivationally important in a long revision period — it provides visible evidence of progress and prevents the cognitive load of repeatedly re-deciding where to start.

Distinguish between exposure and retention: "I covered cardiac failure" means you read about it or watched a video. "I know cardiac failure" means you can answer 20 AKT-style questions about it correctly, unprompted, after a week's gap. Track retention, not exposure. The simplest test: close your notes and write down everything you know about a presentation from memory. What you cannot retrieve without notes is not solidly learned.

Don't mistake recognition for recall: Reading a topic in a resource and feeling like you understand it is recognition. Being tested on it and getting it right from memory is recall. For AKT performance, recall is what matters. Build revision sessions that test recall, not recognition.

Managing Clinical Placement Demands Alongside AKT and CPSA Revision

Final year clinical placements and UKMLA revision are simultaneous demands competing for the same time and cognitive energy. The tension between clinical learning and examination revision is real and does not resolve by ignoring one in favour of the other.

Treat placements as both clinical learning and CPSA preparation: Every patient you examine is CPSA practice. Every drug chart you review is AKT reinforcement. Every handover you give is SBAR practice. This reframing is not a cognitive trick — it is genuinely true. Clinical placements and UKMLA revision are not in competition when you approach placements actively rather than passively. Passive placement is standing in the clinic without engaging; active placement is examining every patient, asking about the diagnosis and management, and connecting the clinical experience to the AKT question bank material you reviewed that morning.

Protect revision time with calendar blocking: Final year placements often have less prescribed learning time than earlier years, which can create an illusion of flexibility that makes it easy to defer revision. Block specific revision times in your calendar — treat them as fixed appointments, not optional sessions. Three to four hours of focused revision on non-placement days, plus one to two hours of integration (linking clinical experience to question bank material) on placement days, is a sustainable and effective pattern for the twelve months before the UKMLA.

Use placement travel time: Commutes to and from placements are an underused revision resource. Anki reviews, medical education podcasts, and audio-based content can convert commute time into low-intensity revision without adding to your total study load.

Managing Exam Anxiety Through Study Approaches

Examination anxiety is universal — but its impact on performance varies significantly. Candidates who have robust, systematically tested knowledge experience exam anxiety very differently from candidates who know they have large content gaps: the first group's anxiety is productive (it generates focus and attention); the second group's anxiety is corrosive (it confirms a genuine deficiency). The best treatment for exam anxiety is comprehensive preparation — not relaxation techniques alone.

The role of consistent practice in anxiety reduction: Candidates who have done daily question bank practice for several months before the AKT have sat through the experience of being tested on clinical content thousands of times before the examination. This volume of practice produces a familiarity with the examination format and with clinical reasoning under time pressure that makes the actual sitting less threatening. Question bank practice is anxiety prophylaxis.

Deliberate exposure to difficult material: Many candidates unconsciously avoid the topics they find hardest — spending revision time on material they know rather than material that challenges them. This avoidance reduces short-term anxiety (the discomfort of struggling through unfamiliar content) but increases long-term risk (arriving at the exam with large unseen gaps). Build deliberate exposure to difficult topics into your revision schedule: spend proportionately more time on your weaker domains, not less.

Physical preparation matters: Sleep quality, exercise, and nutrition in the weeks before the UKMLA affect cognitive performance demonstrably. An AKT sitting requires sustained cognitive performance for three hours — the same length as many demanding physical endeavours. Preparation for cognitive performance should include the physical foundations: consistent sleep (seven to nine hours), regular moderate exercise, and regular meals. Candidates who attempt to revise while sleep-deprived are less effective than they would be with more rest and less revision time.

On the day of the AKT: Arrive early; have food and water available; read every question stem fully before selecting your answer (rushing in the first section is a common source of avoidable errors); flag and return to uncertain questions rather than spending too long on any single item. The format (SBA, 180 questions, 3 hours) gives approximately one minute per question on average — a fast but manageable pace for candidates who have practised under similar time pressure.

Screen Fatigue and Digital Study Habits

Medical students in the current era spend a high proportion of total study time on screens — question banks, spaced repetition apps, video resources, lecture recordings, and communication platforms. This is both an opportunity (unprecedented access to high-quality, interactive educational content) and a challenge (digital fatigue, difficulty sustaining focus in the presence of constant notification stimuli, and the blurring of revision time and social media time).

Evidence on cognitive fatigue from sustained screen exposure is less conclusive than popular discussion suggests — the research does not support a simple "screens are bad for learning" claim. What is clear is that switching between cognitively demanding educational content and social media content within the same session substantially reduces learning efficiency. The mechanism is attentional fragmentation: each switch requires re-establishing the cognitive context of the educational task, and the cumulative cost of repeated switches is significant over a two to four hour study session.

Practical digital study habits that reduce this effect:

  • Use separate physical or virtual workspaces for study (browser profiles, app-blocking apps like Cold Turkey or Freedom) and leisure — the environmental context helps prevent drift between modes
  • Keep your phone in another room or in Do Not Disturb mode during timed study sessions — notification checking is the single most common cause of attentional fragmentation in student study sessions
  • Use the Pomodoro technique with explicit rest periods where screen-based leisure is permitted — this provides designated "release valve" time that reduces the impulse to interrupt study sessions
  • Alternate screen-based work with paper-based work — writing by hand (creating summary notes, drawing concept maps, working through calculations) provides a natural screen break while maintaining cognitive engagement
  • At the end of each screen-based study session, close all study tabs before logging off — starting the next session with a clear workspace reduces the cognitive overhead of re-establishing context

Building Accountability Structures That Work

One of the underappreciated benefits of group study is external accountability — having committed to a session with a peer creates a social obligation that makes showing up more likely than if the commitment is only to yourself. Research on commitment devices in behaviour change consistently shows that social accountability (committing to another person) is more effective than private intention in sustaining behaviour over time.

You can build accountability structures without formal group study sessions:

Study partners: A single study partner — not necessarily someone you study with in the same room — can provide accountability through brief daily check-ins: "What are you planning to study today?" and "What did you cover?" These conversations take two minutes and provide the social commitment mechanism without requiring coordination of schedules for joint study sessions.

Public commitment: Telling a friend, family member, or flatmate your revision goal for the day creates a mild social commitment that increases follow-through. "I'm revising pharmacology this afternoon and doing 40 questions tonight" is more likely to be completed than an identical private intention.

Study tracking apps: Apps like Toggl (time tracking), Habitica (habit gamification), or simple spreadsheet logs that track daily question counts and revision sessions create accountability to your own historical record — a form of self-competition that many students find motivating. Seeing a consistent streak of daily question practice is itself a powerful incentive to maintain the streak.

Progressive milestones: Rather than tracking only toward the final UKMLA date, set progressive milestones — for example, "by end of November I will have covered all cardiovascular and respiratory MLA presentations"; "by end of January my Passmedicine score will be above 65% consistently." Each milestone provides an intermediate accountability point and prevents the "I'll start seriously revising soon" procrastination that defers effective preparation until it is too late to be maximally effective.
