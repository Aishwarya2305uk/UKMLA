The history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is really the story of two separate problems the General Medical Council (GMC) decided, after years of review, to solve with one framework. For decades, UK medical students qualified through their own university's final examinations, with no single national exam standing between graduation and provisional registration, while international medical graduates (IMGs) coming from outside the UK sat an entirely separate, IMG-only test called PLAB. Those two systems were built independently, assessed different things in different ways, and were never formally required to test candidates against the same standard — until the GMC introduced the Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA), branded the "UKMLA" for UK graduates, to close that gap for good.

This article is not a comparison of what PLAB and the UKMLA look like today, and it is not a general introduction to what the UKMLA is — both of those already exist as dedicated guides on this site. What follows here is the history itself: why the old two-track system existed in the first place, why the GMC eventually judged it unsustainable, how the transition was actually phased in across 2022 to 2026, and what genuinely changed — and did not change — for UK medical students and IMGs at each stage. Understanding this history matters for a practical reason as much as an academic one: candidates on both sides of the system, and doctors already registered, regularly encounter outdated forum threads, old coaching-provider marketing, and secondhand explanations that predate the transition and are now simply wrong. Knowing the real sequence of events is the fastest way to tell an accurate resource from a stale one.

Where this history is heading next is also worth knowing before diving in. The convergence that began with the GMC's original MLA proposal is not yet finished — the next scheduled milestone is a fully updated content map applying to both UKMLA and PLAB sittings from September 2026, which this article places in its proper place on the timeline without repeating the full syllabus-change detail already covered elsewhere on this site.

Quick Answer: What Actually Happened, and When

In short, the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition runs like this: before the mid-2020s, UK medical students qualified via their own medical school's final assessments, with no shared national exam, while IMGs sat PLAB — the Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board test — as a wholly separate, independently maintained assessment route. The GMC judged this arrangement inconsistent and, after a multi-year design and consultation process, introduced the Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA) as a single content standard applied to both groups. From August 2024, PLAB was formally rebuilt to be MLA-compliant for IMGs. From the 2024–25 academic year, UK medical schools were required to embed the MLA's two components — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — into their own final assessments, delivered under the "UKMLA" name.

The result is not one universal exam that every doctor sits in the same room. It is a single shared content standard delivered through two different routes: UK graduates meet it through their university's UKMLA sitting, and IMGs meet it through PLAB. Both routes converge fully from September 2026, when an updated MLA content map takes effect identically for both. That is the shape of the transition in outline — the rest of this guide unpacks why the GMC built it this way, what came before it, and what changed for each audience at each stage.

Life Before the UKMLA: Two Separate Worlds

To understand why the GMC introduced a single national standard, it helps to see clearly what came before it — because the pre-UKMLA system was not one system at all, but two, running in parallel with almost no formal connection between them.

UK Medical Students: No Single National Exam

For most of the history of UK medical education, there was no single national licensing exam that every UK medical student sat before joining the register. Instead, each of the UK's GMC-approved medical schools designed, delivered, and marked its own final examinations — typically a mix of written papers and an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) — according to that institution's own curriculum, question style, and internal pass standard. A student's route to provisional GMC registration ran entirely through their own university's assessment framework, with the GMC's role largely limited to approving that each school's overall programme met broad quality benchmarks, not to setting or marking a shared exam itself.

In practice, this meant two students graduating from two different UK medical schools in the same year could have sat meaningfully different final assessments — different numbers of questions, different topic emphasis, different question styles, and, critically, no external guarantee that both papers were pitched at an equivalent level of difficulty or covered an equivalent breadth of the clinical curriculum. Each school's own examiners set that school's own pass mark, using that school's own judgement of what a safe, newly qualified doctor from their programme should know.

International Medical Graduates: The PLAB-Only Route

IMGs, meanwhile, followed a completely separate pathway. Doctors who trained outside the UK and wanted to join the UK medical register sat the Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board test — PLAB — a GMC-administered examination with roots going back to 1975, when its precursor, the Temporary Registration Assessments Board (TRAB), was established to assess the professional knowledge and language proficiency of overseas-qualified doctors. The board was renamed PLAB in 1978 and, in the decades that followed, became the standard, dedicated licensing route for IMGs: PLAB 1, a written knowledge test, and PLAB 2, a station-based clinical skills assessment held at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester.

Crucially, PLAB was built and maintained as its own, independently governed assessment system — its content, question style, and syllabus were not formally mapped against what any individual UK medical school was testing its own students on. An IMG preparing for PLAB was, in effect, preparing for a different kind of exam from the one a UK final-year student was sitting, even though both were nominally being tested toward the same underlying goal: demonstrating readiness for safe, independent practice as a newly registered UK doctor.

Why PLAB Existed in the First Place

PLAB's origins are worth understanding on their own terms, because they explain why an IMG-only route existed at all rather than IMGs simply being asked to sit whatever UK medical students sat. The National Health Service, from its expansion through the 1960s and 1970s, relied heavily on doctors recruited from overseas, particularly from Commonwealth countries, to staff a rapidly growing service. At the time, there was no standardised, GMC-administered mechanism for verifying that an overseas medical qualification represented an equivalent standard of training to a UK degree — qualifications varied enormously in rigour and structure across different countries and institutions. The Temporary Registration Assessments Board was established specifically to provide that missing verification step: a dedicated, GMC-run check of professional knowledge and English language proficiency for doctors whose primary qualification the GMC could not otherwise directly benchmark.

This is an important piece of context for the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition, because it shows that PLAB was never designed as a lesser or supplementary version of UK finals — it was designed to solve a different problem entirely: verifying an overseas qualification the GMC had no other mechanism to assess, rather than re-testing content a UK graduate had already demonstrably covered through a GMC-approved degree programme. That distinct original purpose is part of why PLAB persisted as its own separate system for so long, largely unexamined, even as concerns about consistency between it and UK finals slowly grew.

The Gap Between the Two Systems

This is the structural gap that sits at the heart of the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition: two different populations of doctors, both seeking entry to the same UK medical register, assessed through two entirely separate systems with no shared content standard, no shared pass-mark methodology, and no external mechanism confirming that a pass in one system represented the same demonstrated competence as a pass in the other. For decades, this arrangement was simply how UK medical licensing worked, and it attracted relatively little formal scrutiny. That began to change as the GMC's own research, and wider concerns from medical education bodies, started asking a pointed question: how confident could anyone actually be that "safe to practise" meant the same thing across every route into the register?

Why the GMC Decided a Single National Standard Was Needed

The GMC's own explanation for introducing the MLA framework centres on two closely related goals: patient safety and public confidence. Put simply, a regulator that licenses doctors through multiple different assessment pathways has a duty to be able to demonstrate — not just assert — that every pathway leads to the same demonstrable floor of competence. Before the MLA, the GMC could not fully make that demonstration, because no common content map or shared standard-setting process linked UK medical school finals to PLAB.

The Patient Safety Rationale

The GMC's own account of the reasoning, published on its Medical Licensing Assessment pages, is direct: the MLA exists to give patients and employers greater confidence in doctors joining the UK register, wherever they trained. This is not framed as a statement about any individual medical school or about PLAB being inadequate on its own terms — both systems had their own internal quality assurance — but about the absence of an externally verified, shared guarantee that "passed their finals" and "passed PLAB" meant the same demonstrated level of readiness for Foundation Year 1 practice. A single content map, applied identically regardless of route, closes that gap directly: it lets the GMC say, with evidence rather than assumption, that every doctor newly added to the register has cleared the same bar.

Fairness and Consistency for Both Groups

The GMC has also framed the change explicitly in terms of fairness — not only for patients, but for the two groups of doctors being assessed. Under the old system, a UK student's chances of being judged "ready" depended partly on which medical school they happened to attend, since standards, question difficulty, and pass marks varied between institutions with no shared external check. IMGs, meanwhile, were being tested through PLAB's own separate content framework, which had not been formally benchmarked against what UK finals were actually testing. Neither group had a guarantee that the assessment they sat was pitched at a genuinely equivalent standard to the other's. A single, shared content map — the MLA content map — directly answers that fairness concern by removing the ambiguity: everyone entering the register, regardless of route, is tested against the same defined scope of knowledge, skills, and professional behaviours.

A Long-Considered, Not Sudden, Decision

It is worth stressing that this was not a rushed reform. The GMC's move toward a national licensing assessment was signalled years before it was implemented, developed in consultation with the Medical Schools Council, individual UK medical schools, and IMG stakeholder groups, precisely because a change of this scale — touching every UK medical graduate and every IMG applying to the register — required careful design, piloting, and phased rollout rather than an abrupt switch. The GMC has been explicit that the MLA's purpose is to introduce "a threshold for safe practice, while improving fairness and consistency for international and UK graduates" — language that captures both halves of the rationale: patient safety on one side, and equitable treatment of the two candidate populations on the other.

Why Not Simply Require IMGs to Retrain in the UK?

It is worth briefly addressing an alternative the GMC could, in theory, have pursued: requiring every IMG to complete additional UK-based training or re-sit a full UK medical degree before registration, rather than building a converged assessment standard. Historically, this was never a realistic or proportionate option — many IMGs arrive in the UK as fully qualified, often experienced doctors from robust medical education systems overseas, and re-training them from scratch would have been both wasteful of genuine competence and impractical given the NHS's long-standing reliance on internationally trained doctors to meet workforce needs. The GMC's actual solution — a shared content and standard-setting framework applied to an existing, purpose-built assessment route (PLAB) — achieved the same patient-safety assurance without discarding the practical, decades-proven infrastructure PLAB already represented. This is part of why the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition unfolded as a convergence of two existing systems rather than the wholesale replacement of one by the other.

The Phased Rollout: How the Transition Actually Happened

The shift from the old two-track system to the unified MLA standard did not happen on a single date. It unfolded in distinct, verifiable stages across several years, and understanding each stage separately is the best way to make sense of why so much online discussion of "when did PLAB become the UKMLA" gives inconsistent answers — different sources are often describing different stages of the same multi-year rollout.

Design and Piloting (Early 2020s to 2023–24)

Before any candidate sat a live MLA-aligned assessment, the GMC spent several years designing the content map and standard-setting methodology in consultation with medical schools and professional bodies. A pilot phase followed, in which a limited number of UK medical schools trialled elements of the new assessment structure ahead of full national rollout, allowing the GMC and participating institutions to test logistics, question quality, and standard-setting before every UK medical school was required to participate. This pilot period ran through the 2022–23 and 2023–24 academic years, with the last pilot cohorts sitting shortly before mandatory national rollout began.

PLAB's Alignment to the MLA Standard (From August 2024)

For IMGs, the practical turning point arrived in 2024, when PLAB was formally rebuilt so that PLAB 1 met the requirements of the MLA's Applied Knowledge Test and PLAB 2 met the requirements of the MLA's Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment. From August 2024 sittings onward, an IMG passing PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 was, for the first time, being assessed against the same content map that UK medical schools were simultaneously rolling out to their own final-year students — not a separately maintained PLAB syllabus. This is the single most important date in the IMG side of this history: it marks the point at which "pass PLAB" and "meet the MLA standard" became the same thing in substance, not just in stated intention.

Results IMGs had already obtained on PLAB sittings before this alignment were not invalidated or required to be re-sat — they retained their standard five-year validity under the new framework, a continuity point the GMC was explicit about to avoid penalising candidates who had already progressed partway through the old system when the change took effect.

Mandatory Rollout for UK Medical Students (2024–25 Academic Year)

For UK medical students, the MLA became a mandatory graduation requirement from the 2024–25 academic year onward, delivered under the "UKMLA" name at every GMC-approved UK medical school. From this point, every UK medical school was required to embed the AKT and CPSA into its own final-year assessment process, in partnership with the GMC and the Medical Schools Council, replacing the old model in which each institution set its final exams entirely independently. Individual schools' exact scheduling varied — some had already piloted early cohorts through the AKT ahead of the mandatory date — but the 2024–25 academic year is the point at which sitting the UKMLA became a universal requirement across the whole UK medical school sector, rather than a pilot exercise at a handful of institutions.

Full Content Map Convergence (From September 2026)

The most recent and most complete stage of this history is scheduled for September 2026. In October 2025, the GMC published a substantially updated MLA content map — expanding the list of named examinable conditions, removing a grid that had previously restricted which conditions could be tested within which specialty context, and formally integrating new topic areas. From September 2026, every MLA-linked assessment — the UKMLA sat by UK medical students and PLAB sat by IMGs — is assessed against this identical, updated content map. The dedicated guide to the 2026 syllabus changes covers exactly what has been added and restructured in that update; this article is focused on the history and sequencing of the transition itself, not the syllabus content, so it deliberately does not repeat that detail here.

Seen as a whole, the phased rollout tells a coherent story: years of design and piloting, followed by two parallel implementation dates in 2024 — one for PLAB, one for UK medical schools — that brought both groups onto the same standard for the first time, followed by a scheduled full convergence of the content itself in 2026. Each stage closed a specific remaining gap rather than attempting the entire change at once.

How the Standard-Setting Methodology Changed

Beyond the headline dates, one of the least-discussed but most consequential parts of the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is the change in how the pass mark itself is set. Before the MLA framework, each UK medical school's examiners set their own pass mark using their own internal judgement, and PLAB's pass mark was set independently by GMC examiners using PLAB's own separate methodology. Neither process was formally required to align with the other.

A Shared, Criterion-Referenced Approach

Under the MLA framework, both the UKMLA and PLAB now use the same criterion-referenced standard-setting methodology — most notably the Angoff method, in which a panel of experienced clinicians independently judges, question by question, the proportion of borderline-competent candidates who should be expected to answer correctly. This produces a pass mark that reflects the actual demonstrated difficulty of that specific sitting's paper, applied identically regardless of whether the candidate is a UK final-year student or an IMG sitting PLAB 1. This shared methodology is a direct, practical expression of the fairness rationale discussed above: it is not simply that the content is the same, but that the process for deciding what counts as a pass is now the same too.

Why This Detail Matters to the History

This shift is easy to overlook because it is procedural rather than visible in the way a new exam name or a new date is, but it closes a gap that the content map alone would not fully close on its own. Two assessments could theoretically share identical content and still apply meaningfully different pass standards if the process for deciding "good enough" differed between them. By adopting a single, shared standard-setting methodology alongside the shared content map, the GMC ensured that the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition delivered genuine equivalence in outcome, not just equivalence in what was asked.

What Changed for UK Medical Students

Set against the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition, it is worth being precise about exactly what changed for UK medical students, since the shift was structural rather than a change in the underlying difficulty of qualifying.

A Shared National Bar, Not a School-Specific One

The single biggest change for UK graduates is that the pass standard for entering the register is no longer set independently by each medical school. Before the UKMLA, a student's route into provisional registration ran entirely through their own institution's internally designed and marked finals. Now, every UK medical school delivers the same AKT and CPSA content, marked against the same nationally set standard, as a graduation requirement. Medical schools retain full control over how they teach — the UKMLA is an end-point assessment, not a prescribed curriculum — but the bar students must ultimately clear before joining the register is now uniform across every institution rather than locally determined.

What Stayed the Same

Despite this structural change, a great deal remained familiar for UK students. The assessment is still delivered by, and coordinated through, their own medical school rather than requiring an independent booking process. There is still no separate per-attempt exam fee paid directly by the student in the way an IMG pays for PLAB — the assessment remains embedded in the existing institutional and tuition arrangements. Progression into provisional GMC registration and the Foundation Programme on successful completion also continues largely as before, just now gated by a nationally consistent assessment rather than a school-specific one.

What Changed for IMGs

For IMGs, the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition changed something more fundamental than logistics — it changed what PLAB actually tests, without changing how PLAB is booked or delivered.

Same Exam Name, Rebuilt Content

The most important thing for an IMG to understand about the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is what did not change: the exam is still called PLAB, still consists of PLAB 1 and PLAB 2, and is still booked, sat, and paid for by the candidate directly through the GMC's own systems, with no new parallel "UKMLA exam" that IMGs are separately required to sit. This companion explainer sets out in full why PLAB has not been abolished or scheduled for replacement, addressing that specific, commonly searched misconception directly — this article's focus is the history behind why the two systems converged, not a restatement of that separate question.

What genuinely changed is the content standard behind the PLAB name. Before August 2024, PLAB was assessed against its own, independently maintained syllabus. From August 2024 onward, a pass on PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 represents having met the identical MLA content map and standard that UK graduates meet through their university's UKMLA sitting — new substance delivered through an unchanged, familiar route.

A Reassurance, Not an Added Hurdle

It is worth stating plainly, given how easy it is to read any regulatory change as a new obstacle: this transition was not designed to make PLAB harder for IMGs, and there is no evidence in the GMC's published approach that it has done so. Its effect is to give an IMG's PLAB pass exactly the same demonstrated value, in the eyes of employers and patients, as a UK graduate's UKMLA pass — closing a credibility gap that, before the transition, could leave IMGs' qualifications viewed with more scepticism than the underlying competence actually warranted.

Eligibility and Booking Logistics Sit Outside This History

One further point of clarity matters here: the content and standard-setting convergence covered in this article is separate from the question of who is eligible to sit PLAB in the first place, and separate from the mechanics of actually booking a sitting. The GMC's core eligibility requirements — an acceptable primary medical qualification, English language proficiency, and a completed internship period — existed before this transition and continue to apply exactly as before; none of them were introduced or altered by the MLA content alignment. Similarly, how a candidate actually prepares for the current, MLA-aligned version of PLAB is a distinct, practical question from the historical one this article addresses, and is covered in dedicated preparation guides elsewhere on this site.

How This Compares with Licensing Reform in Other Countries

The history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is easier to place in context alongside similar reforms other national medical regulators have made. The underlying problem the GMC was solving — reconciling a domestic medical school assessment system with a separate international-graduate licensing exam — is not unique to the UK. Several comparable health systems have, at different points, moved toward some form of unified national licensing standard for exactly the same patient-safety and consistency reasons.

A Recognisable Pattern, Not a UK-Only Experiment

Other jurisdictions with large numbers of internationally trained doctors have, over time, tightened the relationship between domestic medical school assessment and international-graduate licensing exams, generally moving in the same direction as the UK: toward a single, shared content and competency standard rather than two independently maintained systems. This broader pattern reinforces that the GMC's reform was a considered response to a well-recognised regulatory challenge, not an untested, UK-specific experiment. How This Sets Up the Next Phase of the Same Story

The history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is not a closed chapter — it is an ongoing convergence, and September 2026 is best understood as its next scheduled milestone rather than its endpoint. The pattern established since 2024 — identify a remaining gap between the two routes, then close it through a defined, publicly announced update — is precisely what is happening again with the 2026 content map revision. Candidates preparing for an upcoming sitting under either route can review what the exam day itself now looks like as a practical next step once the historical context in this guide is clear, since the format and delivery mechanics remain distinct from the content-standard history covered here.

Whether the GMC continues this same pattern of periodic, scheduled convergence beyond 2026 — or eventually moves toward some further structural change — is not yet publicly confirmed. What the history to date shows clearly is a consistent direction of travel: from two independently built systems with no formal connection, toward one shared, regularly updated standard delivered through two distinct, purpose-built routes. Candidates and doctors researching either PLAB or the UKMLA today are, in effect, reading the latest chapter of a multi-year regulatory project, not a finished, static system.

Common Misunderstandings About This History, Addressed Directly

  • "The UKMLA is a brand-new exam invented from nothing." Not quite. The UKMLA is the delivery name for UK graduates meeting the MLA standard — a standard built specifically to replace the old system of independently set medical school finals, not an exam conjured without relation to what came before.
  • "PLAB was replaced by the UKMLA." Inaccurate. PLAB continues to exist under its own name, booking process, and fee structure. What changed is the content it tests, which is now the same MLA content map used for UK graduates.
  • "Before the UKMLA, IMGs and UK graduates were tested on completely unrelated subjects." Not accurate either. Both were always being tested toward the same broad goal — demonstrating readiness for safe Foundation-level practice — but through separately designed assessment systems with no formal, verified guarantee that they tested that readiness to an equivalent standard.
  • "The transition happened overnight in 2024." Inaccurate. 2024 marked the point at which both PLAB and UK medical school finals became mandatorily aligned to the MLA standard, but the design, consultation, and piloting process behind that alignment ran for several years beforehand, and the convergence itself continues with the September 2026 content map update.
  • "Doctors who qualified under the old system are somehow at a different standard now." Inaccurate. The transition is prospective — it governs assessments sat from the relevant implementation dates onward. Doctors who qualified under the pre-MLA system are not required to retrospectively demonstrate compliance with the new standard; their ongoing registration continues through the GMC's standard revalidation and retention requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

When did the UKMLA and PLAB transition actually begin?

The formal implementation began in 2024, though the GMC had been designing and piloting the framework for several years before that. PLAB was aligned to the MLA content standard from August 2024, and UK medical schools were required to embed the same standard into their own assessments, delivered as the UKMLA, from the 2024–25 academic year.

Why did the GMC decide UK graduates needed a national exam when they didn't have one before?

Before the UKMLA, each UK medical school set and marked its own final examinations independently, with no external, shared mechanism confirming that every school's pass standard represented an equivalent level of readiness for safe practice. The GMC introduced a shared content map and standard so that a pass at any UK medical school carries the same demonstrated guarantee, rather than depending on which institution a student happened to attend.

Did PLAB exist before the UKMLA, and has it changed name?

Yes, PLAB has existed since 1978 (evolving from the 1975 Temporary Registration Assessments Board) and has not been renamed. What changed from August 2024 is the content standard behind it — PLAB now tests the same MLA content map used for UK graduates' UKMLA assessments, rather than a separately maintained PLAB syllabus.

Were UK medical students tested to a lower standard before the UKMLA existed?

Not necessarily lower, but inconsistent between institutions. Each medical school independently set its own final exam standard, and there was no external, shared verification that every school's threshold for graduation represented an equivalent level of competence. The UKMLA's purpose is to make that standard consistent and independently verifiable, not to correct a confirmed deficiency at any specific school.

Does the history of the UKMLA and PLAB transition affect doctors who already passed their exams years ago?

No. The transition applies prospectively to assessments sat from each stage's implementation date onward. Doctors who qualified through pre-transition UK medical school finals or pre-August 2024 PLAB sittings are not required to retrospectively re-sit or demonstrate compliance with the newer standard; their continued registration is governed by the GMC's ordinary revalidation and retention requirements.

Is the transition from the old system to the UKMLA and PLAB standard finished now?

Not entirely. While PLAB and the UKMLA have shared the same core MLA standard since 2024, a further, fully updated content map takes effect for both routes from September 2026, representing the next scheduled stage in the same ongoing convergence process rather than a separate, unrelated change.

Where can I read the GMC's own account of why it introduced the MLA?

The GMC publishes its own explanation directly on its Medical Licensing Assessment pages at gmc-uk.org, covering the purpose of the assessment and the fairness and patient-safety rationale behind introducing a shared national standard. Checking the GMC's own published position directly is always the most reliable way to confirm the current, authoritative account, since third-party summaries — including this one — can only ever reflect a snapshot in time.

Why is PLAB still called PLAB if it now tests the MLA content map?

The GMC has kept the PLAB name because the booking route, test centre network, and administrative infrastructure behind it are unchanged and still serve IMGs specifically — only the content standard behind the name was rebuilt. There has been no announced plan to rename PLAB, and candidates should treat "PLAB" and "the IMG route into the MLA standard" as describing the same current thing rather than two different systems.

Did the history of UKMLA and PLAB transition change how the syllabus itself is structured, or just who sits what?

Both, at different points. The initial 2024 alignment unified which content standard applied to each route without a major restructuring of the syllabus itself; the more substantial restructuring — including an expanded conditions list and the removal of a specialty-context grid — arrived later, as a separate, subsequent stage of the same broader convergence, detailed fully in the site's dedicated syllabus guide.

Conclusion

The history of UKMLA and PLAB transition is the story of the GMC replacing two separately built, never-formally-aligned assessment systems with a single shared content standard, delivered through two distinct, purpose-built routes — PLAB for IMGs, the university-embedded UKMLA for UK graduates. It began with decades of two-track licensing, moved through a carefully piloted, multi-year design process, reached its first major implementation milestone in 2024 for both groups, and continues toward a further scheduled convergence in September 2026. Understanding this sequence is not just a historical curiosity — it is the fastest way to recognise which explanations of the current system are accurate and current, and which are relics of a two-track system that no longer exists in the way it once did.
