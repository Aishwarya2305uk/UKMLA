Endocrinology and Diabetes for the UKMLA AKT: A Complete Revision Guide

Endocrinology and diabetes represent one of the highest-yield areas of the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test. This specialty cuts across almost every system — cardiovascular, renal, neurological, and reproductive — making it essential to master not just individual conditions but the clinical reasoning that connects them. This guide covers the full UKMLA endocrinology syllabus with the most up-to-date guidelines, including the landmark February 2026 NICE NG28 update on type 2 diabetes management.

If you are new to the UKMLA, begin with our introduction to the UKMLA and our full syllabus breakdown before diving into individual topics.

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus — NICE NG28 (February 2026 Update)

The February 2026 update to NICE NG28 represents the most significant change to type 2 diabetes management guidance in a decade. The key shift is the move away from metformin monotherapy as universal first-line treatment toward a stratified, dual-therapy approach from diagnosis in most patients.

Initial Pharmacological Management

For most people with type 2 diabetes at diagnosis, NICE 2026 now recommends metformin plus an SGLT-2 inhibitor as initial dual therapy. This applies particularly to patients who have established cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, or are at high cardiovascular risk. The rationale is the robust evidence base showing that SGLT-2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin) reduce major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) and slow progression of diabetic nephropathy independently of their glucose-lowering effect.

SGLT-2 inhibitors work by blocking sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 in the proximal renal tubule, causing glycosuria. Key benefits include:

  • Reduction in HbA1c of approximately 7–10 mmol/mol
  • Weight loss of 2–3 kg
  • Modest blood pressure reduction
  • Proven reduction in hospitalisation for heart failure (EMPA-REG, CANVAS, DECLARE-TIMI)
  • Slowing of eGFR decline in CKD (CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD)

Important contraindications and cautions for SGLT-2 inhibitors include eGFR below 30 ml/min/1.73m² (not licensed for glucose lowering at this level, though dapagliflozin retains heart failure and CKD indication), recurrent urinary tract infections, genital mycotic infections, and patients with type 1 diabetes (risk of euglycaemic DKA). Patients should be counselled to withhold SGLT-2 inhibitors during acute illness or surgical starvation — the so-called sick-day rules.

Individualising HbA1c Targets

NICE 2026 maintains the principle of individualised HbA1c targets. The general target is 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) for most people newly diagnosed on lifestyle or single non-hypoglycaemia-risk drugs. For those on drugs that carry hypoglycaemia risk (sulfonylureas, insulin), the target is relaxed to 53 mmol/mol (7.0%). Targets should be further relaxed to 58–64 mmol/mol for frail elderly patients, those with limited life expectancy, or where tight control poses more risk than benefit.

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide) have a specific role in NICE guidance for patients with:

  • BMI ≥35 kg/m² with significant psychological or other comorbidity
  • Established cardiovascular disease where weight management is a priority
  • Inadequate control on dual or triple oral therapy

Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) has attracted enormous public attention as a weight-loss agent. In the UKMLA context, the key facts are: GLP-1 RAs stimulate glucose-dependent insulin secretion, suppress glucagon, slow gastric emptying, and reduce appetite. They carry low intrinsic hypoglycaemia risk. Common side effects are nausea and vomiting (usually transient). They are not used in patients with personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MEN2).

Insulin Initiation in Type 2 Diabetes

When oral/injectable therapies have failed to achieve targets, insulin is initiated. NICE recommends starting with once-daily basal insulin (isophane/NPH or insulin glargine/detemir). Key principles for AKT questions: start at 10 units at bedtime, titrate according to fasting glucose, combine with metformin to reduce weight gain and total dose requirements, and educate on hypoglycaemia recognition and DVLA reporting requirements.

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus — NICE NG17

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition caused by T-cell mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Insulin deficiency is absolute, making insulin therapy non-negotiable from diagnosis.

Structured Education

NICE NG17 places enormous emphasis on structured self-management education. The DAFNE (Dose Adjustment For Normal Eating) programme is specifically recommended — a 5-day group course teaching carbohydrate counting and dose adjustment. Evidence shows DAFNE improves HbA1c, reduces hypoglycaemia, and improves quality of life. All patients with type 1 diabetes should be offered structured education within 6–12 months of diagnosis.

Technology in Type 1 Diabetes

The UKMLA AKT increasingly tests knowledge of diabetes technology:

  • Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM): Flash glucose monitoring (FreeStyle Libre) measures interstitial glucose every minute. NICE now recommends offering CGM to all people with type 1 diabetes as standard. Key metrics: time-in-range (TIR) target >70% between 3.9–10.0 mmol/L; time below range <4%.
  • Sensor-augmented pump (SAP) therapy: Insulin pump (CSII) combined with CGM. NICE recommends for those with frequent severe hypoglycaemia, hypoglycaemia unawareness, or HbA1c ≥69 mmol/mol despite optimal MDI.
  • Closed-loop systems: Automated insulin delivery systems (hybrid closed-loop or artificial pancreas) — insulin delivery adjusted automatically based on CGM readings.

Sick-Day Rules in Type 1 Diabetes — SADMANS

Patients should know to withhold certain drugs during illness. For type 1 diabetes, the SADMANS mnemonic identifies drugs that worsen DKA or AKI during illness:

  • Sulfonylureas
  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs
  • Diuretics
  • Metformin
  • Agents for NSAIDs (NSAIDs themselves)
  • NSGLT-2 inhibitors
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors (repeated for emphasis)

Insulin must never be stopped in type 1 diabetes even if not eating — dose may need adjustment but omitting insulin risks DKA.

Hypoglycaemia

Hypoglycaemia is defined biochemically as plasma glucose <4 mmol/L, though symptoms vary. Whipple's triad remains the diagnostic cornerstone: (1) symptoms consistent with hypoglycaemia, (2) low plasma glucose at the time of symptoms, (3) relief of symptoms with glucose correction.

Symptoms

Autonomic (adrenergic) symptoms occur first: sweating, tremor, palpitations, anxiety, hunger. Neuroglycopaenic symptoms occur at lower glucose levels: confusion, aggression, seizures, loss of consciousness. Hypoglycaemia unawareness — absent autonomic warning — is a particular danger and develops after recurrent hypoglycaemia or long-standing diabetes.

Management

  • Conscious patient: 15–20g fast-acting carbohydrate (150–200ml fruit juice, Lucozade, or glucose tablets), recheck after 15 minutes, repeat if still <4 mmol/L. Follow with long-acting carbohydrate to prevent recurrence.
  • Impaired consciousness: IM glucagon 1mg (caution: ineffective in alcohol-related hypoglycaemia and after prolonged fasting as glycogen stores are depleted) OR IV 75–100ml 20% dextrose or 150–200ml 10% dextrose.
  • Identify and address precipitant: missed meal, excess insulin/sulfonylurea dose, increased exercise, alcohol.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) — JBDS 2023 Protocol

DKA is a life-threatening emergency occurring almost exclusively in type 1 diabetes (rarely type 2, and increasingly described with SGLT-2 inhibitors even at normal glucose — euglycaemic DKA).

Diagnostic Criteria

The JBDS 2023 protocol defines DKA by ALL THREE of:

  • Blood glucose >11 mmol/L (or known diabetes)
  • Blood ketones >3 mmol/L (or urine ketones ≥2+)
  • Bicarbonate <15 mmol/L or venous pH <7.3

Severity classification: mild (pH 7.25–7.30), moderate (pH 7.10–7.24), severe (pH <7.10).

Management Protocol

Fluid resuscitation: 1L 0.9% sodium chloride over first hour, then subsequent litres over 2 hours, 4 hours, 4 hours, 6 hours based on assessment. Avoid hypotonic fluids. Switch to 10% glucose when blood glucose <14 mmol/L to allow continued fixed-rate insulin infusion.

Fixed-rate insulin infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hour using a separate syringe pump. Do NOT bolus. Continue until ketones <0.6 mmol/L, pH >7.35, and patient eating/drinking — then convert to subcutaneous insulin and discontinue infusion 30–60 minutes after first subcutaneous dose.

Potassium management: Insulin drives potassium intracellularly — hypokalaemia is a major risk. Check potassium before starting insulin. If K <3.5 mmol/L, correct before or simultaneously with starting insulin. Replace potassium in IV fluids (add 40 mmol/L if K 3.5–5.5 mmol/L). Stop potassium replacement if K >5.5 mmol/L.

Cerebral oedema: A rare but devastating complication, particularly in children and young adults. Risk factors include rapid rehydration, excessive insulin, high initial urea. Management: IV mannitol 0.5–1g/kg or hypertonic saline 2.7% 5ml/kg. Have neurosurgical input.

Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)

HHS is the equivalent emergency in type 2 diabetes. Key differences from DKA: glucose is typically very high (>30 mmol/L), osmolality >320 mOsm/kg, minimal ketosis (residual insulin prevents lipolysis), and develops gradually over days to weeks. Patients are profoundly dehydrated.

Management: Gradual rehydration over 48 hours with 0.9% sodium chloride. Target reduction of osmolality by 3–8 mOsm/kg/hour. Low-dose insulin (0.05 units/kg/hour) is introduced only after fluid resuscitation has begun and glucose is not falling with fluids alone. Thromboprophylaxis with LMWH is essential — HHS carries very high VTE risk. Monitor for cerebral oedema with rapid correction.

Thyroid Disorders

Hypothyroidism

Primary hypothyroidism (elevated TSH, low free T4) most commonly results from Hashimoto thyroiditis (autoimmune) in iodine-replete countries, or post-thyroidectomy/radioiodine. Symptoms: weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, hair loss, bradycardia, depression, menorrhagia, myalgia, slowed reflexes, carpal tunnel syndrome, macrocytic anaemia.

Treatment: Levothyroxine, starting dose usually 50 micrograms daily. In elderly patients and those with known or suspected ischaemic heart disease, start at 25 micrograms to avoid precipitating angina or arrhythmia from sudden increase in metabolic demand. Titrate TSH to 0.4–4.0 mU/L (target lower end in symptomatic patients, allow slightly higher in elderly). Taken on empty stomach, 30 minutes before food. Interactions: calcium, iron, PPIs reduce absorption.

Myxoedema coma: Extreme untreated hypothyroidism — hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, altered consciousness. Medical emergency: IV T3 (liothyronine) or IV T4, IV hydrocortisone (adrenal insufficiency commonly coexists), gentle rewarming, supportive care in ICU.

Hyperthyroidism and Graves Disease

Graves disease accounts for ~75% of hyperthyroidism. Autoimmune — TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) stimulate the thyroid continuously. Symptoms: weight loss despite increased appetite, heat intolerance, sweating, palpitations, tremor, diarrhoea, anxiety, oligomenorrhoea, lid lag, proptosis (exophthalmos — specific to Graves).

Investigation: Suppressed TSH with elevated free T4 and/or T3. TRAb elevated in Graves. Thyroid uptake scan differentiates causes (diffuse uptake in Graves, single hot nodule in toxic adenoma, low/absent uptake in thyroiditis).

Management options:

  • Carbimazole: First-line antithyroid drug. Blocks thyroid peroxidase. Risk of agranulocytosis (<1%) — warn patients to report sore throat/fever urgently and check FBC. Block-and-replace or titration regimen.
  • Propylthiouracil (PTU): Preferred in first trimester of pregnancy (lower teratogenic risk than carbimazole) and thyroid crisis. Risk of serious hepatotoxicity — switch to carbimazole after first trimester.
  • Radioiodine (I-131): Definitive treatment; not in pregnancy, active Graves ophthalmopathy (may worsen), or breastfeeding. Hypothyroidism almost inevitable — patients need lifelong levothyroxine.
  • Surgery (thyroidectomy): For large goitres, suspected malignancy, patient preference, or where drugs/radioiodine contraindicated.

Thyroid crisis (thyroid storm): Life-threatening exacerbation — tachycardia, hyperpyrexia, confusion, vomiting, multi-organ failure. Precipitated by surgery, infection, or radioiodine. Management: PTU (blocks hormone synthesis and peripheral conversion), potassium iodide (blocks release), dexamethasone, propranolol, supportive care in ICU.

Thyroid Nodules and Cancer

Thyroid nodules are common (prevalence ~50% in adults on ultrasound). Majority are benign. Malignant features on ultrasound (microcalcification, irregular margins, taller-than-wide shape, solid hypoechoic nodule) prompt fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC).

Thyroid cancer prognosis (best to worst): Papillary (most common, excellent prognosis, lymph node spread but indolent) > Follicular (blood-borne spread, good prognosis) > Medullary (calcitonin-secreting, C-cell origin, associated with MEN2, intermediate prognosis) > Anaplastic (undifferentiated, rapidly fatal, median survival weeks).

Adrenal Disorders

Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

Autoimmune destruction of adrenal cortex is the most common cause in the UK. Results in deficiency of cortisol (glucocorticoid) and aldosterone (mineralocorticoid).

Symptoms and signs: Fatigue, anorexia, weight loss, nausea, abdominal pain, postural hypotension (aldosterone deficiency), hyperpigmentation (elevated ACTH drives MSH production — bronze skin especially in skin creases, scars, buccal mucosa), hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, hypoglycaemia, eosinophilia.

Investigation: Short synacthen test — measure cortisol at 0 and 30 minutes after IM/IV tetracosactide 250 mcg. Normal: cortisol rises to >450–500 nmol/L. Addison's: subnormal response. Elevated ACTH confirms primary (vs secondary where ACTH is low/normal).

Treatment: Hydrocortisone (replacement dose 15–20mg daily in divided doses, higher morning dose to mimic diurnal rhythm) + fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid replacement, typically 100 micrograms daily). Medical alert bracelet essential.

Sick-day rules (steroid dose doubling): During illness, fever, or surgery, patients must double or triple hydrocortisone dose. For vomiting, IM hydrocortisone 100mg is given.

Addisonian crisis: Acute adrenal insufficiency — circulatory shock, severe hypoglycaemia. Management: IV hydrocortisone 100mg STAT, IV 0.9% saline 1L rapidly, 10% glucose if hypoglycaemic, treat precipitant.

Cushing's Syndrome

Excess cortisol. Causes: exogenous steroids (most common overall), ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease, ~70% of endogenous), ectopic ACTH (small cell lung cancer, carcinoid), adrenal adenoma/carcinoma.

Clinical features: Central obesity, buffalo hump, moon face, purple striae (wide), proximal myopathy, bruising, poor wound healing, hypertension, hyperglycaemia, osteoporosis, hirsutism, mood disturbance.

Investigation: Screening tests — late-night salivary cortisol, 24-hour urinary free cortisol, overnight low-dose dexamethasone suppression test (1mg at midnight, measure cortisol at 9am — failure to suppress below 50 nmol/L = positive). Then localise: ACTH level (high in pituitary/ectopic, low in adrenal), MRI pituitary, CRH stimulation test, high-dose dexamethasone suppression, inferior petrosal sinus sampling if doubt.

Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)

Primary hyperaldosteronism from an aldosterone-secreting adrenal adenoma (Conn syndrome) or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. Key biochemical triad: hypertension + hypokalaemia + suppressed renin. Aldosterone:renin ratio elevated. Adrenal CT to locate adenoma. Unilateral adenoma: adrenalectomy. Bilateral hyperplasia: spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist).

Phaeochromocytoma

Catecholamine-secreting tumour of adrenal medulla (80%) or extra-adrenal paraganglioma. Associated with MEN2, NF1, VHL disease. Presenting triad: episodic hypertension, severe headache, sweating, and palpitations (the "hypertensive paroxysms"). Hypertensive crisis can be precipitated by abdominal palpation, contrast media, certain drugs (TCAs, metoclopramide).

Investigation: 24-hour urinary metanephrines (normetanephrine + metanephrine) or plasma metanephrines. CT/MRI adrenals. MIBG scan for metastatic disease.

Pre-operative management: Alpha-blockade first (phenoxybenzamine or doxazosin) for 10–14 days before surgery, then add beta-blocker. Never give beta-blocker first — unopposed alpha stimulation causes severe hypertensive crisis.

Pituitary Disorders: Acromegaly

GH-secreting pituitary adenoma causes acromegaly in adults (gigantism if before epiphyseal fusion). Features: coarsening of facial features, macroglossia, prognathism, enlarging hands/feet (ring/shoe size change), hyperhidrosis, headache, bitemporal hemianopia (chiasm compression), carpal tunnel, arthropathy, hypertension, diabetes mellitus.

Investigation: IGF-1 elevated (best screening test). Glucose suppression test: oral glucose 75g → normally GH suppresses to <0.4 µg/L; in acromegaly, GH fails to suppress or paradoxically rises. MRI pituitary to visualise adenoma.

Treatment: Transsphenoidal surgery first-line. If residual/recurrent: somatostatin analogues (octreotide, lanreotide) reduce GH secretion; pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist) for resistant cases; radiotherapy.

Hypercalcaemia

The CHIMPANZEES mnemonic: Calcium supplements, Hyperparathyroidism (primary), Iatrogenic/Immobility, Metastases/Myeloma, Paget disease, Addison's disease, Neoplasia (malignancy — PTHrP), Zollinger-Ellison, Excess vitamin D, Excess thyroxine, Sarcoidosis (and other granulomatous disease).

Symptoms: "Bones, groans, moans, and stones" — bone pain, abdominal pain/constipation (groans), psychosis/depression (moans), renal calculi (stones). ECG: shortened QT interval.

Management of severe hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L or symptomatic): IV 0.9% saline rehydration (dilution + promotes urinary calcium excretion) — this is the most important initial step. IV bisphosphonate (pamidronate or zoledronic acid) — onset 24–48 hours, peak effect 4–7 days. Treat underlying cause. Denosumab for malignancy-related hypercalcaemia. Calcitonin for rapid short-term reduction.

SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone)

Diagnostic criteria: Hyponatraemia (Na <135 mmol/L), plasma osmolality <275 mOsm/kg, urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg (inappropriately concentrated), urine sodium >30 mmol/L, no oedema, no hypovolaemia, no adrenal/thyroid/renal insufficiency.

Causes: CNS disease (meningitis, SAH, head injury), pulmonary disease (pneumonia, TB, SCLC), drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide, PPIs, opiates), postoperative, pain.

Management: Fluid restriction (500–1000ml/day) is first-line for chronic. Demeclocycline (tetracycline antibiotic — causes nephrogenic DI) for persistent cases. Hypertonic saline (1.8% or 3%) for severe/symptomatic (Na <120 with seizures/coma) — correct no faster than 6–8 mmol/L in first 24 hours to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome. Tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist) for resistant cases — licensed for SIADH.

FAQ: Endocrinology for the UKMLA

What is the most important change in NICE NG28 (2026) for type 2 diabetes?

The most significant change is the shift to metformin plus an SGLT-2 inhibitor as initial dual therapy for most patients with type 2 diabetes at diagnosis, replacing the previous stepwise approach starting with metformin monotherapy. This change is driven by robust evidence for cardiovascular and renal protection from SGLT-2 inhibitors beyond their glucose-lowering effect.

What is the JBDS DKA diagnostic criteria?

DKA is diagnosed when all three criteria are met: blood glucose >11 mmol/L (or known diabetes), blood ketones >3 mmol/L or urine ketones ≥2+, and bicarbonate <15 mmol/L or venous pH <7.3. The fixed-rate insulin infusion is 0.1 units/kg/hour and must not be started before checking and correcting potassium.

How do you differentiate Cushing's syndrome causes?

After confirming hypercortisolaemia, measure ACTH: elevated ACTH indicates pituitary or ectopic source; suppressed ACTH indicates adrenal adenoma/carcinoma. High-dose dexamethasone (8mg) suppression differentiates pituitary (>50% suppression) from ectopic (no suppression). MRI pituitary and inferior petrosal sinus sampling further localise pituitary sources.

Why must you alpha-block before beta-block in phaeochromocytoma?

Beta-blockade without prior alpha-blockade removes beta-2-mediated vasodilation while leaving alpha-1 vasoconstriction unopposed, causing severe hypertensive crisis. Alpha-blockade first prevents this. The standard regimen is phenoxybenzamine or doxazosin for at least 10–14 days, then add a beta-blocker.

What are the sick-day rules for patients on steroids (Addison's disease)?

Patients with adrenal insufficiency must double or triple their oral hydrocortisone during fever, illness, or physical stress. If vomiting or unable to take oral medication, they must use emergency IM hydrocortisone (100mg) and attend hospital. All patients should carry a steroid emergency card and medical alert bracelet. During surgery, parenteral hydrocortisone cover is mandatory.

What is the test of choice for primary adrenal insufficiency?

The short synacthen test is the investigation of choice. Cortisol is measured at baseline and 30 minutes after 250 micrograms tetracosactide IM/IV. A peak cortisol below 450–500 nmol/L is abnormal. ACTH measurement differentiates primary (elevated ACTH) from secondary/tertiary (low or normal ACTH).

Conclusion: Mastering Endocrinology for the UKMLA

Endocrinology appears throughout the UKMLA AKT in clinical scenarios that require you to interpret blood results, select investigations, manage emergencies, and apply up-to-date guidelines. The February 2026 update to type 2 diabetes management is particularly high-yield given its recency and clinical importance.
