The internship requirement for GMC registration is the piece of the application that catches out more otherwise well-prepared international medical graduates (IMGs) than almost any other single evidence item. Alongside a recognised primary medical qualification (PMQ), the General Medical Council expects proof that a doctor has completed a defined period of supervised clinical practice — commonly called an internship, a housemanship, or a rotating/foundation internship depending on where a candidate trained — after qualifying and before being considered safe to register with a licence to practise. A medical degree certificate on its own, however prestigious the awarding school, is not treated as sufficient evidence that a graduate can function safely and independently in a clinical environment; the internship period is where the GMC expects that gap between "passed finals" and "safe to practise unsupervised" to be closed under proper supervision.

This guide explains, in plain terms, why the internship requirement for GMC registration exists, what generally counts as qualifying supervised practice, exactly what evidence the GMC typically expects to see, the pitfalls that repeatedly trip up applicants from every country, and — critically — how this single requirement sits inside the much bigger registration application alongside your PMQ evidence, your English language certificate, and your UKMLA result. It is not a standalone hurdle bolted onto the end of the process; it is assessed as one interlocking part of a wider evidence package, and treating it in isolation is one of the most common planning mistakes IMGs make. For readers who want the complete end-to-end route from medical degree to full licence to practise, this guide sits alongside the site's broader walkthrough of how to become a doctor in the UK, which covers every stage of the journey rather than this single evidence requirement in isolation.

Because the GMC reviews its evidence requirements periodically, and because what counts as acceptable practical training can genuinely differ depending on your qualifying country's training structure, this article deliberately signposts you to the GMC's own current guidance at several points rather than presenting a single frozen checklist as permanent fact. Wherever a specific document list or duration threshold is quoted below, treat it as the position at the time of writing and always confirm the live requirement directly on the GMC's own page on checking whether your practical training is acceptable before you rely on it for a real application.

Quick Answer: What Is the Internship Requirement for GMC Registration?

The internship requirement for GMC registration is the GMC's rule that, in addition to a recognised primary medical qualification, an international medical graduate must provide evidence of a period of supervised, hands-on clinical practice completed either during the final year of the medical programme or immediately after graduation — evidence the GMC checks separately from, but alongside, the underlying degree itself. In practical terms the GMC currently recognises two broad patterns: a minimum of 12 months of continuous supervised practice (with at least three months in medicine and three months in surgery), or a minimum of 2 years of continuous postgraduate medical practice covering at least one branch of medicine and one branch of surgery. Both patterns must have taken place in a public hospital that meets recognised regulatory, supervision, and governance standards in its own jurisdiction.

The requirement is assessed as part of your wider application — not as an isolated form to fill in — and it is checked using documentary evidence, typically a signed certificate or rotation log from the training hospital or your home country's medical council, rather than the transcript from your medical school alone. Crucially, the GMC does not accept purely observational experience — clinical attachments, shadowing placements, observerships, and clerkships — as satisfying this requirement, because those roles do not involve the same level of direct patient responsibility as a genuine internship rotation.

Why the Internship Requirement Exists

The Patient Safety Rationale

The internship requirement for GMC registration exists because the GMC does not consider a medical degree, by itself, sufficient evidence that a newly qualified doctor can practise safely and independently. Medical school curricula, however rigorous, are built around supervised learning, simulated scenarios, and closely chaperoned clinical placements — a fundamentally different environment from carrying independent (albeit supervised) clinical responsibility for real patients under time pressure, with genuine consequences attached to each decision. The internship period is designed specifically to close that gap: it is the stage at which a graduate is expected to develop the hands-on clinical judgement, prioritisation skill, and comfort with uncertainty that written examinations and supervised placements cannot fully replicate.

This is not a UK-specific quirk. Virtually every mature medical regulatory system in the world — from the UK's own Foundation Programme to equivalent housemanship or rotating-internship years elsewhere — builds in a defined period of supervised, hands-on practice between graduation and full independent licensure, precisely because patient safety regulators worldwide have converged on the same conclusion: theoretical knowledge and supervised observation are necessary but not sufficient, and only a period of genuine, supervised clinical responsibility reliably develops the judgement a licensed doctor needs.

Consequently, when the GMC asks for internship evidence, it is not questioning the quality of a candidate's medical school; it is applying a consistent, patient-safety-driven standard that treats "qualified" and "safe to register with a licence to practise" as two related but distinct thresholds, with the internship period as the bridge between them.

Why a Degree Certificate Alone Is Not Enough

A common source of frustration for IMGs is the perception that, having already had their primary medical qualification verified through primary source verification, an additional layer of internship evidence feels redundant. In practice, the two checks answer different questions. Verification of the PMQ confirms that a specific degree was genuinely awarded by a specific, recognised institution. The internship requirement instead asks a different question entirely: did the graduate, after obtaining that qualification, actually undertake a defined period of supervised, hands-on clinical practice with real patients, and was that practice properly supervised and governed?

These are genuinely separate facts, and a medical school transcript — even a fully verified one — typically does not, by itself, contain enough detail to answer the second question. This is precisely why the GMC asks for separate internship documentation, usually from the training hospital or a national medical council rather than the medical school's academic office, and why applicants should not assume that a strong, well-verified degree automatically carries the internship requirement along with it.

What Generally Counts as Qualifying Supervised Practice

Recognised Duration Patterns

Two broad patterns are generally recognised as satisfying the internship requirement for GMC registration. The first is a minimum of 12 months of continuous practice, completed either in the final year of study or immediately following graduation, which must include at least three months practising medicine and at least three months practising surgery, continuously and without interruption in each. The second is a minimum of two years of continuous postgraduate medical practice, again covering at least one branch of medicine and one branch of surgery, undertaken after the primary qualification was awarded.

Both patterns share several common threads that matter more than the raw duration figure alone:

  • Continuity. The GMC's own guidance describes acceptable practice as continuous, uninterrupted rotations completed as part of a structured training programme — not a patchwork of shorter, disconnected placements stitched together over a longer calendar period.
  • Direct patient interaction. The doctor must have provided genuine medical care with direct patient contact throughout, rather than shadowing a senior colleague or observing from the sidelines.
  • An approved setting. Practice must have taken place in a public hospital that meets the standards for regulation within its own jurisdiction and that has established supervision, safety, and clinical governance systems in place — a private clinic without equivalent oversight, or an informal arrangement outside a recognised institution, will not generally satisfy this element.
  • Limited absence. A defined allowance for sickness or statutory leave (commonly cited in GMC materials as around 20 days in any 12-month period, in addition to annual leave of up to five weeks) is typically permitted without breaking continuity, but absences beyond that threshold can raise a query.
  • Documented supervision. The programme must have involved regular educational and clinical supervision throughout — not just nominal registration on a rota, but active oversight by senior clinicians who can, if needed, attest to the trainee's performance and conduct.

Because different countries structure their internship, housemanship, or rotating-internship years differently — some as a single continuous 12-month block, others as a series of shorter postings across several departments over a longer calendar span — the underlying test the GMC applies is functional rather than purely bureaucratic: did the doctor complete continuous, supervised, hands-on clinical practice covering both medicine and surgery, for a total duration that meets one of the two recognised patterns, regardless of exactly how the home country's own system labels or timetables it.

What Does Not Count

The GMC is explicit that certain categories of experience, however clinically adjacent they may appear on paper, do not satisfy the internship requirement for GMC registration. Clinical attachments, shadowing placements, observerships, and clerkships are not accepted, because trainees in these roles are not carrying the same level of direct patient-care responsibility as a doctor completing a genuine internship rotation — they are, by design, observational rather than practising posts. A candidate who spent time shadowing consultants in a UK hospital, valuable as that experience may be for familiarisation purposes, cannot submit that time as internship evidence.

Similarly, informal arrangements outside a properly regulated institution — for example, practice undertaken in a private setting without the supervision, safety, and governance systems the GMC expects of a public hospital — are unlikely to be accepted even if the doctor genuinely saw and treated patients, because the institutional oversight element is missing. This is one of the areas where applicants most often assume their experience will count, only to find on closer reading of the GMC's guidance that it does not meet the definition being applied.

What Evidence Is Typically Needed

Core Documentation

Evidence for the internship requirement for GMC registration is generally documentary rather than declaratory — a candidate's own statement that they completed an internship is not, by itself, sufficient. The GMC typically expects a signed certificate or document from the training hospital, medical school, or the relevant national medical council, setting out the dates and duration of every individual rotation, the specialty or department covered by each, and confirmation that the practice involved direct patient care under supervision. Where a national medical council issues a formal registration or completion certificate covering the internship year as a whole, that document is often the clearest single piece of evidence, provided it clearly states the dates and nature of the training rather than simply confirming that registration exists.

Because this evidence is assessed alongside primary source verification of the underlying qualification, applicants should expect the GMC (via its verification partner) to check the internship documentation's authenticity in much the same rigorous way it verifies the degree itself — directly with the issuing institution wherever possible, rather than relying solely on the certified copy a candidate submits personally. A detailed, country-specific example of exactly how this verification process runs in practice, including realistic response-time expectations, is set out in the site's guide to GMC registration and EPIC verification for Indian doctors, which walks through the document-request-to-confirmation cycle step by step.

What Good Evidence Looks Like

The strongest internship evidence shares several features. It names the specific hospital or institution where each rotation took place, states the exact start and end date of each posting, identifies the specialty or department (making it straightforward to confirm the required medicine/surgery split), confirms that the role involved direct clinical responsibility rather than observation, and is signed by an appropriately senior authority — a training programme director, medical superintendent, or equivalent — rather than an administrative staff member with no direct oversight of the training itself.

Where a candidate's internship spanned several separate postings across different departments or even different hospitals, it is generally advisable to gather a certificate from each relevant institution rather than assume a single summary letter from the final posting will be treated as covering the whole period. Compiling this evidence early — ideally at the same time as beginning primary source verification of the primary qualification, rather than only once exam preparation is complete — avoids a late, costly scramble to track down programme directors or hospital administrators who may have moved on in the intervening years.

How This Interacts With Country-Specific Documentation

Exactly which document a national system issues to evidence internship completion varies meaningfully by country: some medical councils issue a single formal completion certificate for the whole internship year, others rely on hospital-issued rotation certificates for each individual posting, and some structures require both. Because this variation is genuine and not just a documentation preference, candidates should check the GMC's current specific evidence requirements for their own qualification route directly on the GMC's website rather than assuming the pattern used by doctors from a different country will apply equally to their own. What counts as adequate evidence can differ depending on exactly where and how the internship was completed, which is precisely why the GMC's own guidance, rather than a generic third-party checklist, should always be the final word for an individual applicant's specific circumstances.

How the Internship Requirement Applies Differently to UK Graduates and IMGs

UK Medical Students and the Foundation Programme

For doctors who complete a UK medical degree, the internship requirement for GMC registration is satisfied automatically through the structure of UK postgraduate training itself. UK graduates move directly into the Foundation Programme, and the first year of that programme, Foundation Year 1 (FY1), is specifically designed and regulated to provide the supervised general clinical practice the GMC expects before granting full registration. Because FY1 posts are centrally allocated, closely monitored by deaneries and postgraduate deans, and already built around rotations covering medicine, surgery, and other specialties under defined educational supervision, UK graduates do not need to separately assemble internship evidence in the way an IMG does — the documentation trail already exists within the UK training infrastructure and is confirmed through routine reporting rather than an ad hoc certificate request. Readers who want the full detail of how FY1 and FY2 fit together with UKMLA timing should see the site's dedicated guide to the Foundation Programme after the UKMLA.

This is worth stating explicitly because UK medical students preparing for the UKMLA sometimes worry, unnecessarily, that the internship requirement for GMC registration is a separate box they personally need to tick alongside their degree. In practice, for a UK-trained doctor moving through the standard Foundation Programme route, this requirement is handled structurally by the training system itself, not something to research or document independently.

IMGs: Why the Requirement Demands Active Effort

For IMGs, by contrast, the internship requirement for GMC registration is not handled automatically by any UK institution, since the qualifying practice took place entirely outside the UK's own regulated training infrastructure. This is precisely why IMGs must personally gather, request, and submit internship documentation, rather than relying on an institution to generate and forward it as part of a routine reporting relationship with the GMC. The practical consequence is that IMGs need to treat internship evidence as an active task requiring their own initiative — locating the right contact at each training hospital, requesting a specifically worded certificate, and following up until the document is issued — rather than an administrative formality that happens in the background.

This asymmetry is one of the clearest illustrations of why IMGs' registration timelines are typically longer and more variable than those of UK graduates: not because the underlying training was necessarily different in substance, but because the evidence trail for IMGs has to be actively reconstructed and independently verified, institution by institution, rather than flowing automatically through an established national reporting structure.

How Internship Patterns Vary by Home Country

Because internship, housemanship, and rotating-internship systems are structured differently around the world, IMGs are often unsure whether their own country's pattern maps cleanly onto the GMC's recognised duration thresholds. IMGs whose home country structures its internship in an unusual or non-standard way — for example, where the surgery and medicine postings were of unequal length, or where a portion of the internship was completed at more than one institution — should not assume this automatically disqualifies their evidence; it simply means the documentation needs to demonstrate clearly how the overall pattern still satisfies the underlying duration and specialty-split requirements.

Common Pitfalls With Internship Evidence

Incomplete or Ambiguous Documentation

The single most frequent problem the GMC's verification process encounters is documentation that is technically genuine but insufficiently detailed. A letter that confirms a doctor "completed their internship at [hospital]" without specifying exact dates, individual rotation lengths, or the medicine/surgery split leaves the GMC unable to confirm the requirement is actually met, even though the underlying training may well have satisfied it in full. Applicants are often surprised that a real, honestly obtained document can still trigger a query purely because it was written in general terms rather than itemised detail.

Informal or Undocumented Rotations

Some training systems, particularly where postings were arranged informally between departments rather than through a centrally administered rotation programme, do not always generate the kind of formal paper trail the GMC expects. A doctor who genuinely completed the required clinical practice may still struggle to evidence it if the relevant hospital never issued individual rotation certificates at the time, and reconstructing that record years later — after staff have moved on and paper archives have been reorganised or lost — can become one of the most time-consuming parts of an entire registration application.

Documentation That Does Not Clearly Show Supervised Responsibility

Even where dates and durations are clearly stated, some certificates fail to make explicit that the role involved direct patient care under proper supervision, rather than an observational or shadowing capacity. Because the GMC explicitly excludes clinical attachments, observerships, and clerkships, a certificate that is ambiguous on this point — for example, using a job title that could plausibly describe either a genuine internship post or an observational placement — is a common trigger for a follow-up query, even when the underlying experience was, in fact, a genuine supervised practice rotation.

Gaps and Breaks in Continuity

Because the GMC's guidance emphasises continuous, uninterrupted rotations, gaps in a candidate's internship record — whether from extended illness, family circumstances, administrative delays between postings, or a training programme that was itself disrupted — can raise questions about whether the continuity requirement is satisfied. This does not automatically mean a genuine gap is fatal to an application; it means the gap needs to be clearly explained and, where possible, evidenced (for example, through medical certification for a health-related absence), rather than left for the GMC to interpret from an unexplained hole in the documented timeline.

Assuming a Postgraduate Qualification Substitutes for Internship Evidence

Doctors who went on to complete further postgraduate training or a specialist qualification after their internship sometimes assume that the more advanced qualification automatically demonstrates the earlier internship requirement was met. In practice, the GMC generally still expects to see internship-specific evidence covering the relevant period, since a later postgraduate qualification answers a different question (further specialist competence) rather than retrospectively proving the initial period of supervised general practice took place as required.

Where a doctor's registration route depends specifically on a postgraduate qualification older than a certain threshold, additional evidence expectations can apply — a scenario covered in more depth in the GMC's own guidance for that application category, and worth checking directly if it applies to your route rather than assumed by analogy with the standard internship pathway. Doctors who qualify for a specialist or GP registration route rather than the standard UKMLA pathway should also check the site's guide to UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors, since the internship evidence expectations on that alternative route can differ from the standard pattern described in this guide.

Translated Documents That Lose Critical Detail

Where internship certificates are issued in a language other than English, they require certified translation before submission, and this step introduces its own risk of losing precisely the detail the GMC is checking for. A translator unfamiliar with medical training terminology can inadvertently soften a phrase that originally made clear the doctor carried direct clinical responsibility, rendering it in a more generic way that reads as ambiguous once translated. Using a translation service experienced with medical or professional-registration documents, and reviewing the translated version carefully against the original before submission, meaningfully reduces the risk of an otherwise strong original certificate becoming a weak one purely through translation.

Name and Date-Format Discrepancies Across Documents

Internship certificates issued years apart from a candidate's degree certificate or passport sometimes use slightly different name formats — a maiden name, a transliteration variant, or a different ordering of given and family names. Similarly, date formats vary by country (day-month-year versus month-day-year), and a certificate that is internally consistent but reads ambiguously against a candidate's other documents can trigger an easily avoidable query. Reviewing every submitted document side by side for exactly this kind of inconsistency, and providing a short explanatory note or supporting evidence where a genuine discrepancy exists, is a simple step that prevents a disproportionate amount of delay relative to the effort involved.

How the Internship Requirement Fits Into the Wider Registration Application

Assessed Alongside PMQ Evidence, Not Instead of It

It is easy to think of the internship requirement for GMC registration as a separate, standalone box to tick once the "real" work of verifying a medical degree is done. In practice, the GMC assesses internship evidence as one strand of the same overall evidence package that includes confirmation of the primary medical qualification itself. Both pieces of evidence typically move through primary source verification together, and an application is only considered complete once every strand — qualification, internship, English language proficiency, and the UKMLA result — is in place and internally consistent.

This matters practically because candidates sometimes prioritise gathering degree-related paperwork while leaving internship documentation until later, on the assumption it is a smaller, simpler task. In reality, internship evidence can be just as slow to obtain — sometimes slower, if it depends on a hospital's administrative office rather than a medical school's more established international-affairs function — and should be started at the same time as the wider verification process, not treated as an afterthought.

Assessed Alongside English Language Evidence and the UKMLA

The internship requirement also sits alongside, rather than before or after, English language evidence and success in the UKMLA. None of these four elements individually unlocks registration; the GMC only grants registration with a licence to practise once the qualification, the internship evidence, the English language certificate, and both UKMLA components are all confirmed and consistent with one another. Doctors who pass the UKMLA on their first attempt but have never assembled internship documentation are not, in any meaningful sense, closer to registration than doctors who have gathered every document but not yet sat the exam — both are missing a required piece, and the application only moves forward once all of them are present together.

This is precisely why it is worth reading this requirement in the context of the site's wider overview of GMC registration requirements for international doctors, which sets out how eligibility, verification, English language evidence, and the exam all interact as a single five-part structure rather than a strict sequence of independent hurdles.

Where Internship Evidence Sits Within EPIC Verification

For most IMGs, internship documentation is submitted and checked through the same primary source verification process used for the underlying medical qualification, commonly run through the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC). This means the internship certificate a candidate uploads is not simply filed away; it is typically routed back to the issuing hospital or council for direct confirmation, in much the same way a medical school is asked to confirm a degree. Because this adds an additional institutional response time on top of qualification verification, starting the internship-evidence-gathering process early — ideally in parallel with, not after, degree verification — is one of the most effective ways to avoid the internship requirement becoming the single slowest element of an otherwise complete application.

Why This Is Not a Separate, Standalone Hurdle

Framing the internship requirement as a discrete, isolated obstacle — something to "get out of the way" before moving on to the exam or the English test — misunderstands how the GMC's overall evidence package is actually assessed. In reality, the application is reviewed holistically: a query on internship evidence can hold up an otherwise complete file just as effectively as a query on the qualification itself, and a doctor with impeccable internship documentation but an unresolved English language query is no further forward than one with the reverse problem. Treating every evidence strand — qualification, internship, English, and exam success — as parallel, equally weighted workstreams from the outset, rather than a sequence to be tackled one at a time, is consistently the more efficient approach across every applicant's experience, regardless of nationality or training background.

Realistic Timeline Considerations

Because internship evidence often depends on institutional responsiveness rather than a candidate's own effort — much like primary source verification of a degree — it is sensible to build a realistic buffer into any registration timeline rather than assume documentation will arrive quickly. Hospitals and medical councils vary enormously in how quickly they respond to a verification or documentation request: some issue a clear, itemised certificate within a matter of weeks, while others, particularly where records are still paper-based or where the relevant administrative staff have since moved on, can take considerably longer.

Doctors who begin the process of locating, requesting, and formally certifying their internship documentation as early as possible — ideally at the same time as starting primary source verification of their degree, rather than waiting until exam preparation is finished — consistently report a smoother overall registration experience. Practical Steps: Gathering Your Internship Evidence

The following sequence reflects the order most IMGs find practical when assembling internship evidence for GMC registration, though individual circumstances will vary:

  1. Identify every rotation or posting that formed part of your internship or housemanship year, including exact dates, the hospital or institution, and the specialty or department for each.
  2. Contact each relevant institution — the training hospital, medical school, or national medical council — to request a formal, signed certificate covering the dates and nature of the training, rather than a general confirmation-of-attendance letter.
  3. Check that each document explicitly describes direct patient-care responsibility under supervision, rather than leaving the nature of the role ambiguous or open to being read as observational.
  4. Confirm the medicine/surgery split is clearly evidenced across your documentation, since this specific breakdown is one of the first things checked against the recognised duration patterns.
  5. Address any gaps proactively, gathering supporting evidence (such as medical certification) for any documented absence, rather than leaving an unexplained break in the timeline for the GMC to query later.
  6. Submit the evidence alongside your qualification documents through the same verification channel, ideally at the earliest stage of your overall application rather than as an afterthought once exam preparation is complete.
  7. Check the GMC's current, specific evidence requirements for your exact qualification route directly on the GMC's website before finalising what you submit, since requirements are reviewed periodically and can differ by route.

Frequently Asked Questions

What counts as an acceptable internship for GMC registration?

An acceptable internship generally means a minimum of 12 months of continuous supervised clinical practice (including at least three months in medicine and three months in surgery), or alternatively a minimum of two years of continuous postgraduate practice covering both branches, completed in a public hospital with recognised supervision and governance standards. The practice must involve direct patient care under regular clinical supervision, not observation alone.

Does a clinical attachment or observership count towards the internship requirement?

No. The GMC does not accept clinical attachments, shadowing placements, observerships, or clerkships as evidence of internship completion, because these roles do not involve the same level of direct patient-care responsibility as a genuine internship rotation. Only active, supervised clinical practice with real patient responsibility satisfies this requirement.

What documents do I need to prove I completed my internship?

Typically a signed certificate or document from the training hospital, medical school, or national medical council, stating the exact dates, duration, and specialty of each rotation and confirming that the training involved supervised, direct patient care. Because exact document requirements can vary by qualification route and country, always check the GMC's current specific evidence requirements directly before assuming a particular document will be accepted.

Is the internship requirement checked separately from my medical degree?

The internship requirement is assessed as a distinct piece of evidence from the medical qualification itself, since verifying that a degree was awarded is a different question from confirming that a defined period of supervised practice took place afterwards. However, both are typically submitted and verified through the same overall process, and neither replaces the other.

What happens if there is a gap in my internship record?

A gap does not automatically disqualify an application, but it does need to be explained and, where possible, evidenced — for example, with medical certification for a health-related absence. Leaving an unexplained break in the documented timeline is one of the more common reasons the GMC raises a follow-up query on an otherwise complete application.

Can I use my Foundation Programme or specialist training instead of an internship certificate?

Generally, no — a later postgraduate or specialist qualification answers a different question (further competence gained afterwards) rather than retrospectively proving that the required period of supervised general practice took place immediately after qualifying. The GMC typically still expects internship-specific evidence covering the relevant period, though some specialist and GP-trained doctors qualify for different registration routes entirely, so it is worth checking your own route directly.

How early should I start gathering internship evidence?

As early as possible, ideally at the same time as beginning verification of your primary medical qualification, rather than waiting until exam preparation is finished. Because internship documentation often depends on a hospital's or council's administrative response time rather than your own effort, starting early is the single most effective way to avoid it becoming the slowest part of an otherwise complete application.

Conclusion

The internship requirement for GMC registration is not a bureaucratic afterthought — it reflects a genuine, patient-safety-driven judgement that a medical degree alone does not prove a graduate is ready to practise safely and independently. Understanding what counts as qualifying supervised practice, gathering clear and complete documentary evidence early, and recognising that this requirement is assessed alongside your qualification, English language evidence, and UKMLA result — not as an isolated final hurdle — will save most applicants months of avoidable delay.
