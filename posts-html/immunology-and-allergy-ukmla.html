Immunology UKMLA questions punch above their weight in the Applied Knowledge Test: the topic is a small slice of the GMC content map by condition count, but it recurs constantly because anaphylaxis, drug allergy and hypersensitivity reasoning are woven into scenarios from every other system. A candidate who can recognise a Gell and Coombs pattern, name the correct adrenaline dose, and separate a true penicillin allergy from an intolerance will pick up marks scattered across pharmacology, dermatology, respiratory and paediatric questions, not just the "immunology" ones. This guide pulls that scattered content into one place, with the emergency management sequence, the classification system examiners like to test, the common allergic conditions you will meet in general practice and on the wards, and the red flags that separate a normal childhood cold pattern from a primary immunodeficiency that needs referral.

The guide is written for both audiences sitting the UKMLA: UK final-year medical students revising immunology alongside their other systems, and international medical graduates (IMGs) who may have trained under a different national guideline for anaphylaxis or allergy management and need to align their answers with UK Resuscitation Council practice specifically. Dosing, drug names and management sequences below follow the UK convention tested in the AKT, even where your home country's guidance differs slightly — always flag where that is the case so you are not caught out by a UK-specific distractor option.

Because immunology overlaps so heavily with other systems, this guide also signposts where to go next: for the rheumatological end of autoimmune disease, see the site's dedicated guide to connective tissue and joint disease for the AKT; for the drug-safety angle on anaphylaxis triggers and prescribing errors, see the prescribing safety and pharmacology revision resource; and for the acute resuscitation sequence in context with other emergencies, see the emergency presentations revision guide.

Quick Answer: What Immunology and Allergy Content Does the UKMLA Actually Test?

Immunology UKMLA content centres on five recurring themes: recognising and managing anaphylaxis according to Resuscitation Council UK guidance, classifying hypersensitivity reactions using the Gell and Coombs system, managing common allergic conditions (allergic rhinitis, food allergy and drug allergy), assessing and de-labelling reported penicillin allergy, and recognising red-flag presentations that suggest a primary immunodeficiency. Candidates are rarely asked to recite immunology theory in isolation; instead, questions embed these concepts inside clinical vignettes — a breathless patient after IV antibiotics, a child with recurrent chest infections, a young adult with hay fever unresponsive to over-the-counter treatment — and expect the correct next step or diagnosis to be selected under time pressure.

The single highest-yield fact to fix in your memory before anything else: for adult anaphylaxis, the first-line intramuscular adrenaline dose is 500 micrograms (0.5 mL of 1:1,000), given into the anterolateral thigh, repeated after 5 minutes if there is no improvement. Get this dose and route correct and a large proportion of anaphylaxis questions become straightforward regardless of the surrounding clinical detail.

Anaphylaxis Recognition and Emergency Management

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening, generalised or systemic hypersensitivity reaction characterised by rapidly developing, life-threatening airway, breathing and/or circulatory problems, usually associated with skin and mucosal changes. This definition, and the emergency management sequence below, follows the guidance published by the Resuscitation Council UK, which is the authoritative UK source examined in AKT anaphylaxis questions. Recognising anaphylaxis quickly, rather than mistaking it for a simple allergic rash or an anxiety episode, is the single most time-critical skill this topic tests.

Recognising Anaphylaxis: The Diagnostic Pattern

Anaphylaxis is diagnosed clinically, not by waiting for a laboratory result. The Resuscitation Council UK sets out three criteria that should all be present for a confident diagnosis: sudden onset and rapid progression of symptoms; life-threatening airway, breathing or circulation problems; and, usually, skin and/or mucosal changes such as flushing, urticaria or angioedema. It is worth noting explicitly that skin changes can be subtle or entirely absent in up to 20% of reactions, so their absence must never be used to exclude the diagnosis in a patient who otherwise has airway, breathing or circulatory compromise after a plausible trigger.

Common triggers tested in the AKT include: foods (nuts, shellfish, egg, milk), drugs (penicillins and other beta-lactams, neuromuscular blocking agents, NSAIDs, contrast media), insect venom (wasp and bee stings), and latex. A UKMLA vignette will typically give a temporal clue — symptoms beginning minutes after an IV antibiotic infusion, or shortly after eating a takeaway containing peanuts — and expect the candidate to link the exposure to the presentation rather than treating the trigger identification as an afterthought.

The ABCDE Approach to Anaphylaxis

Once anaphylaxis is suspected, management follows the same structured ABCDE approach used across UKMLA emergency scenarios, but with anaphylaxis-specific actions layered onto each step:

  • Airway: look for and treat swelling, hoarse voice or stridor; call for anaesthetic/airway support early if there is any concern, since laryngeal oedema can progress to complete obstruction rapidly.
  • Breathing: assess for increased respiratory rate, wheeze, fatigue, cyanosis and oxygen saturation below 94%; give high-flow oxygen and treat bronchospasm with nebulised bronchodilators as an adjunct, never as a substitute for adrenaline.
  • Circulation: look for signs of shock — pale, clammy skin, tachycardia, hypotension, decreased consciousness; establish IV access, lay the patient flat with legs elevated (unless breathing is compromised, in which case allow the position of comfort), and give an IV fluid bolus in adults if hypotensive.
  • Disability: assess conscious level, since cerebral hypoperfusion or hypoxia can cause confusion or loss of consciousness as a late feature.
  • Exposure: fully expose the patient to look for urticaria, angioedema or a flare pattern, while preserving dignity and preventing heat loss, and remove any ongoing trigger exposure (e.g. stop the infusion causing the reaction).

Throughout this sequence, the single intervention that must not be delayed for any other step is intramuscular adrenaline. As a result, most correctly structured answers place "give IM adrenaline" either as the very first action or in parallel with calling for help, not after a full top-to-toe ABCDE has been completed at a leisurely pace.

Adrenaline Dosing: The Concept Every Candidate Must Know

Adrenaline is given intramuscularly into the anterolateral aspect of the middle third of the thigh, using a 1:1,000 concentration (1 mg/mL). The Resuscitation Council UK's age-banded dosing concept, which the AKT expects candidates to apply rather than merely memorise as an isolated number, is as follows:

Candidates should be comfortable with the underlying logic even if exact figures are provided in the question stem: the dose scales with age/weight, the route is intramuscular (never subcutaneous or, in the initial community/ward setting, intravenous), and the interval before a repeat dose is short because anaphylaxis can deteriorate within minutes. IV adrenaline is reserved for specialist settings (critical care, anaesthetics, emergency medicine) with cardiac monitoring, titrated in much smaller increments, and should never be selected as a first-line answer for a ward-based or community scenario in the AKT.

After adrenaline, second-line and supportive measures include high-flow oxygen, an IV fluid bolus for hypotension, and — once the patient is stabilising — non-sedating antihistamines and, in some protocols, corticosteroids, though the Resuscitation Council UK's more recent guidance de-emphasises steroids as a routine anaphylaxis treatment and the AKT is increasingly likely to test that antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts, never substitutes for adrenaline.

Biphasic Reactions and Observation Periods

A biphasic reaction is a recurrence of anaphylactic symptoms after apparent resolution of the initial episode, without any further exposure to the trigger, typically occurring within 1 to 72 hours (most commonly within 8 hours). Biphasic reactions are the reason every patient who has had anaphylaxis must be observed in a monitored setting after initial stabilisation rather than discharged as soon as symptoms settle. The observation period recommended depends on the severity and response to treatment of the initial reaction and local protocols, but the underlying principle tested in the AKT is straightforward: resolution of symptoms after adrenaline does not mean the episode is over, and safe discharge planning requires a structured observation period plus provision of an adrenaline auto-injector and a referral to an allergy specialist before the patient leaves.

Every patient who has had a suspected anaphylactic reaction should also have a serum mast cell tryptase level taken (ideally as three timed samples: as soon as possible after resuscitation begins, at 1–2 hours, and at 24 hours or in convalescence) to support the retrospective diagnosis, and should be referred to a specialist allergy clinic for confirmatory testing and long-term management planning, including a personalised emergency action plan.

Hypersensitivity Reaction Types: Practical Gell and Coombs Classification

The Gell and Coombs classification divides hypersensitivity reactions into four types based on their underlying immunological mechanism. The AKT does not usually ask candidates to describe immunology theory from first principles; instead it expects pattern recognition — given a clinical scenario, which type of hypersensitivity reaction best explains it, and by extension, what is the underlying mechanism and expected time course.

Why the Classification Matters Clinically, Not Just Academically

Understanding which Gell and Coombs type underlies a presentation predicts three practically useful things: the expected time course (minutes for Type I versus days for Type IV), the appropriate first-line treatment (adrenaline and antihistamines for Type I versus stopping the causative drug and supportive care for Type IV), and whether the reaction is likely to recur predictably on re-exposure (Type I reactions are highly likely to recur and often escalate; Type IV skin reactions to a drug typically warrant lifelong avoidance of that drug and related compounds).

Type I Reactions in Detail

Type I hypersensitivity is the mechanism behind almost every acute allergic presentation tested in the AKT: anaphylaxis, allergic rhinitis, acute urticaria and angioedema, and the allergic (extrinsic) pattern of asthma. On first exposure to an allergen, the immune system produces allergen-specific IgE, which binds to high-affinity receptors on mast cells and basophils — this is sensitisation, and it is typically asymptomatic. On re-exposure, the allergen cross-links surface-bound IgE, triggering rapid degranulation and release of histamine, tryptase and other mediators, producing the immediate symptoms of itching, urticaria, bronchospasm and, in severe cases, anaphylaxis. The speed of this mechanism — minutes, not hours or days — is the key distinguishing feature examiners use to separate Type I questions from Type III or Type IV distractors in the same question bank.

Type II and Type III Reactions: The Antibody-Mediated Patterns

Type II reactions involve antibodies (usually IgG) directed against antigens that are part of, or attached to, the surface of the patient's own cells, leading to cell destruction via complement activation, antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, or phagocytosis. Classic examples tested include haemolytic transfusion reactions (antibodies against mismatched blood group antigens), autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, Goodpasture syndrome (anti-glomerular basement membrane antibodies causing renal and pulmonary disease), and drug-induced immune thrombocytopenia. Type III reactions differ in that the antibody binds soluble antigen in the circulation rather than a cell surface, forming immune complexes that then deposit in small vessels and tissues (particularly joints, skin and kidneys), where they activate complement and attract neutrophils, causing local tissue damage. Serum sickness — classically following administration of foreign proteins such as antivenom or certain monoclonal antibodies, with fever, rash and joint pain appearing roughly 7–10 days later — is the textbook Type III vignette, and lupus nephritis is the most clinically important chronic example.

Type IV Reactions and the Delayed-Onset Clue

Type IV hypersensitivity is entirely cell-mediated, involving sensitised T-lymphocytes rather than antibodies, and is consequently the slowest to develop — typically 48 hours or more after re-exposure. Contact dermatitis (from nickel, latex or topical medications), the tuberculin skin test reaction used in TB screening, graft-versus-host disease after transplantation, and many delayed maculopapular drug rashes fall into this category. The delayed time course is the single most reliable clue examiners use: if a question describes a rash appearing two or three days after starting a new medication rather than within minutes of the first dose, Type IV delayed hypersensitivity is far more likely than a Type I immediate reaction, and this distinction directly affects whether the correct answer involves adrenaline (Type I) or simply stopping the drug and monitoring (Type IV).

Common Allergic Conditions Tested in the UKMLA

Beyond acute anaphylaxis, the AKT tests management of everyday allergic conditions that GPs and hospital doctors manage constantly: allergic rhinitis, food allergy and drug allergy. These questions tend to focus on stepwise management ladders and on distinguishing genuinely allergic presentations from mimics.

Allergic Rhinitis

Allergic rhinitis is IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal mucosa following exposure to an allergen such as pollen, house dust mite or animal dander, producing sneezing, nasal itching, rhinorrhoea and nasal congestion, often with associated conjunctivitis. Management follows a stepwise ladder that the AKT expects candidates to apply in order rather than jumping straight to the most potent option: allergen avoidance advice first (though often only partially effective), then a non-sedating oral antihistamine (such as cetirizine or loratadine) for mild-to-moderate symptoms, stepping up to an intranasal corticosteroid spray for more persistent or moderate-to-severe symptoms (used correctly and consistently rather than only when symptomatic, since nasal steroids take days to reach full effect), and reserving a short course of intranasal or oral decongestant, or referral for immunotherapy in severe refractory cases, for those who fail the standard ladder. A common trap in AKT questions is prescribing an intranasal steroid as a single "as required" dose for acute symptom relief — steroid sprays only work with regular, sustained use, and expecting an immediate effect (the way a decongestant works) is a frequent stem for testing whether candidates understand the drug's mechanism and appropriate counselling.

Food Allergy

Food allergy is classified as either IgE-mediated (immediate, Type I mechanism, symptoms typically within minutes to two hours of ingestion — urticaria, angioedema, vomiting, or anaphylaxis in severe cases) or non-IgE-mediated (delayed, several hours to days later, typically causing gastrointestinal symptoms such as vomiting, diarrhoea or eczema flares, and mechanistically distinct from the Gell and Coombs Type I pathway). The AKT commonly tests this distinction in the context of infants and children, where cow's milk protein allergy is a frequent vignette: an IgE-mediated pattern presents acutely with urticaria or wheeze shortly after milk exposure, while a non-IgE-mediated pattern presents more insidiously with reflux, colic, blood-streaked stools or poor weight gain over days to weeks, and is managed with an extensively hydrolysed or amino-acid formula rather than emergency treatment.

For children with a confirmed IgE-mediated food allergy who have had a systemic reaction, or who have both asthma and a food allergy (a recognised risk factor for a more severe reaction), an adrenaline auto-injector should be prescribed and both the child's carers and, where age-appropriate, the child themselves should be trained in its use, alongside a written personalised allergy action plan. Current UK paediatric allergy guidance also emphasises early, structured introduction of common allergenic foods (such as peanut and egg) from around 6 months of age in the context of weaning, rather than prolonged avoidance, as this has been shown to reduce the risk of the child developing that food allergy — a shift from older, more cautious advice that AKT questions increasingly reflect.

Drug Allergy and Penicillin Allergy Assessment

Drug allergy questions in the AKT are disproportionately about penicillin, both because true penicillin allergy is clinically important (cross-reactivity considerations, need for alternative antibiotics) and because a reported penicillin allergy is very often not a true allergy at all. Population studies consistently show that around 90% or more of patients labelled as "penicillin allergic" are not truly allergic when formally assessed, yet an inaccurate label leads to broad-spectrum, more expensive and often less effective alternative antibiotics being used for the rest of that patient's life — a genuine patient-safety and antimicrobial-stewardship problem that the AKT expects candidates to recognise and act on.

Structured Penicillin Allergy Assessment

A structured history is the single most useful tool for penicillin allergy assessment, and the AKT rewards candidates who can separate a true allergic reaction from a non-allergic adverse effect or an unrelated childhood illness. Key elements of the history to elicit include:

  • Timing: did the reaction occur within minutes to hours of the dose (suggesting a Type I, IgE-mediated mechanism) or days later (suggesting a Type IV, delayed mechanism, or possibly an unrelated viral exanthem)?
  • Nature of the reaction: was there urticaria, angioedema, wheeze, or anaphylaxis (features suggesting true allergy), or was it isolated nausea, diarrhoea, or a non-itchy rash (features suggesting intolerance or an unrelated cause, not allergy)?
  • Severity: did the reaction require adrenaline, hospital admission, or intensive care (a red flag for a genuine, severe reaction) or was it mild and self-limiting?
  • Context: many "penicillin allergy" labels originate from a childhood rash that occurred during a concurrent viral illness (classically, amoxicillin given during infectious mononucleosis produces a widespread rash that is not a true drug allergy), and clarifying this context can safely remove the label.

Patients with a low-risk history (a distant, mild, non-anaphylactic reaction, or a reaction inconsistent with a true allergic mechanism) can often be considered for de-labelling — a process that may include direct oral challenge with a test dose under observation in appropriate settings, without necessarily needing formal skin-prick or specific IgE testing first. Patients with a high-risk history (recent reaction, anaphylaxis, angioedema, or systemic involvement) require referral to a specialist allergy service for formal skin testing and, where appropriate, a supervised graded drug challenge before the label can be safely removed. The AKT expects candidates to recognise that de-labelling is an active, structured clinical process — not something to attempt casually on the ward without appropriate risk-stratification and, for higher-risk patients, specialist input.

Clinically, this matters because true penicillin allergy has documented cross-reactivity with other beta-lactams: cross-reactivity with cephalosporins is now understood to be low (particularly with later-generation cephalosporins that have dissimilar side chains) and cross-reactivity with carbapenems is very low, but this nuance is frequently oversimplified in older teaching that advised avoiding all beta-lactams outright in any patient with a reported penicillin allergy — the modern, AKT-relevant approach is a risk-stratified one based on the specific reaction history, not a blanket avoidance rule. For a broader view of how this interacts with prescribing decisions and antimicrobial stewardship more generally, the site's pharmacology and prescribing safety guide covers antibiotic choice and adverse drug reaction reporting in more depth.

Primary Immunodeficiency: Red Flags Prompting Investigation

This is an area of immunology UKMLA revision that candidates often under-prepare relative to anaphylaxis and hypersensitivity typing, yet it recurs reliably in paediatric and infection-focused vignettes. Most children and adults who present with recurrent infections do not have a primary immunodeficiency — recurrent minor upper respiratory infections in young children attending nursery are normal. The AKT tests the ability to distinguish this normal pattern from the much rarer, genuinely concerning pattern that should prompt immunological investigation and specialist referral. Primary immunodeficiencies are inherited or congenital disorders of the immune system, distinct from secondary immunodeficiency, which is acquired through causes such as HIV infection, malnutrition, immunosuppressive medication or malignancy — the AKT expects candidates to consider and, where relevant, exclude secondary causes before assuming a primary immunodeficiency.

The "SPUR" Red-Flag Pattern

A widely taught mnemonic for recognising infections that warrant further investigation for an underlying primary immunodeficiency is SPUR: infections that are Severe, Persistent, Unusual (either an unusual organism, such as an opportunistic pathogen, or an unusual site, such as recurrent deep abscesses) or Recurrent. Applying this pattern is more useful for the AKT than memorising the classification of every named immunodeficiency syndrome, because most exam questions test recognition of the red-flag pattern in a vignette rather than naming an obscure genetic diagnosis outright.

  • Severe: infections requiring hospitalisation or IV antibiotics more often than would be expected, or infections that are unusually severe for the causative organism.
  • Persistent: infections that fail to fully resolve with appropriate treatment, or that recur immediately after stopping antibiotics.
  • Unusual: infections with opportunistic organisms (such as Pneumocystis jirovecii, atypical mycobacteria, or recurrent fungal infections) or in unusual locations (recurrent deep-seated abscesses, osteomyelitis at multiple sites).
  • Recurrent: a pattern of two or more serious bacterial infections in a year (such as pneumonia, sepsis or meningitis), rather than frequent but mild, self-limiting viral upper respiratory infections.

Additional Clinical Clues

Beyond the SPUR pattern, other features that should raise suspicion of a primary immunodeficiency in the AKT include: failure to thrive or poor growth in an infant alongside recurrent infection, a family history of early unexplained infant death or known immunodeficiency, recurrent infections at multiple different sites (rather than one recurring focus, which is more suggestive of a structural or anatomical problem), the need for IV antibiotics to clear infections that would normally respond to oral treatment, and a history of unusual complications from live vaccines (which can be catastrophic in T-cell immunodeficiencies and is itself a diagnostic clue worth eliciting in the vaccination history). Recurrent invasive infections with encapsulated organisms (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis) should prompt consideration of an antibody deficiency or a complement deficiency, since these organisms are classically cleared by opsonisation and the complement cascade.

The AKT-appropriate response to a SPUR-positive history is not to attempt to diagnose the exact immunodeficiency syndrome at the bedside, but to recognise that the pattern is abnormal and warrants baseline investigations (typically a full blood count with differential, immunoglobulin levels, and specific antibody responses to previous vaccines) and referral to a specialist immunology service for further work-up. Recognising when a pattern is "normal for age" versus when it crosses into SPUR territory is the practical skill being tested, far more than recalling the genetics of any single named syndrome.

Autoimmune Disease: Where Immunology Meets Other Systems

Immunology UKMLA content does not exist in isolation from the rest of the syllabus — many autoimmune diseases sit at the intersection of immunology and another organ system, and the AKT frequently tests them from whichever system angle the question is framed around. Rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and vasculitides are fundamentally immune-mediated diseases (several with a recognisable Type II or Type III hypersensitivity component, as covered above) but are examined predominantly as rheumatological presentations — joint pain, rash, renal involvement — rather than as pure immunology recall questions. Similarly, autoimmune thyroid disease sits within endocrinology, coeliac disease within gastroenterology, and type 1 diabetes within endocrinology, even though all are, at their core, autoimmune processes with an immunological mechanism worth understanding.

The practical implication for revision is that immunology should be studied as connective tissue linking other systems together, not as an isolated silo. Recognising that an antibody-mediated mechanism underlies a presentation helps predict associated features (such as other autoimmune conditions clustering in the same patient, a recognised feature of autoimmune predisposition) and helps interpret why certain investigations (autoantibody panels, complement levels, inflammatory markers) are requested. For a deeper, joint-and-connective-tissue-focused treatment of this overlap, including the specific autoantibody patterns and disease-modifying treatments tested in that system, see the site's dedicated rheumatology revision guide, which complements the immunological mechanisms covered here with the clinical rheumatology detail the AKT expects.

It is also worth noting, briefly, that immune dysregulation has metabolic and nutritional dimensions that occasionally surface in AKT stems — for example, the nutritional management of a child with multiple food allergies, or metabolic complications of long-term corticosteroid use in autoimmune disease. Readers wanting more detail on that adjacent territory can consult the site's nutrition and metabolic disorders revision guide, and candidates managing chronic autoimmune conditions with a significant pain component (such as vasculitis-related neuropathic pain) may also find the chronic pain management guide useful for the analgesic ladder and prescribing principles that often accompany these cases.

Putting It Together: An AKT Revision Strategy for Immunology and Allergy

Given the relatively small direct footprint of immunology in the GMC content map compared with cardiology, respiratory or gastrointestinal medicine, an efficient revision strategy concentrates effort on the handful of concepts most likely to appear, rather than attempting exhaustive coverage of clinical immunology as a specialty. In practice, that means: over-learning the anaphylaxis ABCDE sequence and adrenaline dosing until it is automatic and cannot be second-guessed under exam pressure; being able to reliably match a clinical vignette to one of the four Gell and Coombs types using the onset-time clue as the primary discriminator; knowing the stepwise management ladder for allergic rhinitis and the IgE- versus non-IgE-mediated distinction in food allergy; being comfortable with the structured, risk-stratified approach to penicillin allergy assessment and de-labelling, since this is tested disproportionately often relative to its apparent simplicity; and recognising the SPUR pattern for when recurrent infection crosses from "normal for age" into "needs specialist referral."

For UK medical students, this content will often appear folded into a broader systems-based question — for example, a respiratory station that turns out to hinge on recognising anaphylaxis rather than asthma, or a paediatric station where the correct answer is investigating for immunodeficiency rather than reassuring the parents. For IMGs, the added task is aligning management sequences (particularly adrenaline dosing, route and repeat interval, and the stepwise drug ladders) specifically with UK Resuscitation Council and NICE-aligned practice, since equivalent national guidelines elsewhere sometimes differ in dose bands, first-line antihistamine choice, or steroid use in anaphylaxis — always default to the UK-specific answer when a UKMLA question does not explicitly ask about international practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis in adults?

The first-line treatment for anaphylaxis in adults is intramuscular adrenaline, 500 micrograms (0.5 mL of a 1:1,000 solution), given into the anterolateral thigh. This dose is repeated after 5 minutes if there is no improvement in the patient's airway, breathing or circulatory status, following Resuscitation Council UK guidance. Antihistamines, corticosteroids and IV fluids are supportive adjuncts, not substitutes for adrenaline, and should never delay its administration.

What are the four types of hypersensitivity reaction in the Gell and Coombs classification?

The Gell and Coombs classification describes four hypersensitivity types: Type I (immediate, IgE-mediated, such as anaphylaxis and allergic rhinitis), Type II (cytotoxic, antibody-mediated against cell-surface antigens, such as haemolytic transfusion reactions), Type III (immune complex-mediated, such as serum sickness and lupus nephritis), and Type IV (delayed, T-cell-mediated, such as contact dermatitis and many delayed drug rashes). Onset time is the most useful practical clue for distinguishing them: minutes for Type I versus 48 hours or more for Type IV.

How do you assess whether a reported penicillin allergy is genuine?

Assessing a reported penicillin allergy starts with a structured history covering the timing of the reaction (minutes to hours suggests true IgE-mediated allergy; days suggests a delayed or unrelated cause), the nature of the reaction (urticaria, angioedema or anaphylaxis suggest true allergy; isolated nausea or a non-itchy rash suggest intolerance or an unrelated illness), and its severity. Patients with a low-risk history can often be safely de-labelled, sometimes via a supervised oral challenge, while those with a high-risk history need referral to a specialist allergy service for formal testing before the label is removed.

What is a biphasic anaphylactic reaction and why does it matter?

A biphasic reaction is a recurrence of anaphylactic symptoms within 1 to 72 hours (most often within 8 hours) of the initial reaction resolving, without any further exposure to the trigger. It matters because it is the reason every patient who has had anaphylaxis must be observed in a monitored clinical setting for a defined period, given an adrenaline auto-injector and a written action plan, and referred to an allergy specialist before being discharged, rather than sent home as soon as the first episode's symptoms settle.

What infection pattern suggests a primary immunodeficiency rather than normal childhood illness?

The SPUR pattern — infections that are Severe, Persistent, Unusual (in organism or site) or Recurrent (two or more serious bacterial infections such as pneumonia or sepsis in a year) — suggests a primary immunodeficiency and warrants baseline investigation and specialist referral. Frequent mild, self-limiting viral upper respiratory infections in a young child, particularly one attending nursery, are a normal pattern and do not, on their own, suggest an underlying immune disorder.

Is food allergy always IgE-mediated?

No. Food allergy can be IgE-mediated (an immediate, Type I hypersensitivity reaction occurring within minutes to two hours, causing urticaria, angioedema or anaphylaxis) or non-IgE-mediated (a delayed reaction occurring hours to days later, typically causing gastrointestinal symptoms or eczema flares, without the classic Type I mechanism). The two patterns are managed differently, and the AKT expects candidates to distinguish acute, IgE-mediated presentations requiring an adrenaline auto-injector from the more insidious non-IgE-mediated pattern managed with dietary exclusion and specialist formula.

Why is immunology examined alongside other systems rather than on its own?

Many diseases with an immunological or autoimmune mechanism — rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, autoimmune thyroid disease, coeliac disease, type 1 diabetes — are classified and examined under their organ-system specialty (rheumatology, endocrinology, gastroenterology) rather than as standalone immunology questions. As a result, the UKMLA tests core immunological reasoning, such as hypersensitivity mechanisms and anaphylaxis management, as a thread running through vignettes from multiple systems rather than as an isolated topic block.

Conclusion

Immunology UKMLA revision rewards focused, high-yield preparation rather than exhaustive specialty-level study rather than exhaustive specialty-level study: master the anaphylaxis ABCDE sequence and adrenaline dosing, be able to match a vignette to its Gell and Coombs hypersensitivity type using the onset-time clue, know the stepwise ladders for allergic rhinitis and food allergy, apply a structured, risk-stratified approach to penicillin allergy assessment, and recognise the SPUR red-flag pattern for primary immunodeficiency. Because these concepts recur inside cardiology, respiratory, paediatric and rheumatology vignettes alike, time spent solidifying them pays dividends across the whole exam, not just in questions explicitly labelled "immunology."
