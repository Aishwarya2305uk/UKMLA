Nephrology and Renal Medicine for the UKMLA: A Complete Revision Guide

Nephrology is an intellectually demanding specialty that underpins clinical practice across multiple domains. Fluid management, electrolyte disorders, kidney injury, and proteinuria assessment appear in almost every clinical setting. The UKMLA AKT tests nephrology extensively, requiring both an understanding of pathophysiology and the ability to apply the latest NICE and KDIGO guidelines in clinical scenarios.

This guide provides a comprehensive review of the nephrology UKMLA syllabus. Before starting, familiarise yourself with the UKMLA overview, the AKT format, and our preparation guide.

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — KDIGO 2024

AKI is a rapid deterioration in kidney function over hours to days. The KDIGO (Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes) 2024 criteria maintain the staging system established in 2012 but include updated guidance on management, biomarkers, and long-term follow-up.

Classification by Cause

Pre-renal AKI: Reduced kidney perfusion. Causes: hypovolaemia (haemorrhage, vomiting, diarrhoea, burns), reduced cardiac output (heart failure, cardiogenic shock), sepsis (vasodilation), renal artery stenosis. Urine sodium <20 mmol/L, FENa <1%, urine osmolality >500 mOsm/kg (kidneys conserving sodium and concentrating urine). Management: fluid resuscitation, treat cause.

Intrinsic (intrarenal) AKI:

  • Acute tubular necrosis (ATN): Most common intrinsic cause. Ischaemic (prolonged pre-renal) or nephrotoxic (aminoglycosides, contrast, myoglobinuria, haemolysis). Urine sodium >40 mmol/L, FENa >2%, muddy brown granular casts on microscopy.
  • Glomerulonephritis: Acute — dysmorphic red cells and red cell casts on urine microscopy.
  • Acute interstitial nephritis (AIN): Drug-induced (NSAIDs, penicillins, PPIs, allopurinol), infection, sarcoidosis. Fever, rash, eosinophilia, eosinophiluria (late sign). Renal biopsy may show eosinophilic infiltrate. Steroids if drug-induced and not resolving after stopping causative drug.
  • Vascular: Thrombotic microangiopathy (TTP/HUS), renal artery occlusion, atherosclerotic emboli (livedo reticularis, eosinophilia, renal failure after angiography or anticoagulation).

Post-renal AKI: Obstruction. Causes: BPH, bladder tumour, cervical cancer, bilateral ureteric obstruction (retroperitoneal fibrosis, malignancy), pelvic haematoma. Ultrasound shows hydronephrosis. Urgent urological input — nephrostomy or catheter decompression. AKI may worsen temporarily with decompression (post-obstructive diuresis).

Contrast-Induced AKI

Risk factors: pre-existing CKD (eGFR <60), diabetes, dehydration, high-volume contrast, repeated exposure. Prevention: use lowest effective contrast dose, IV 0.9% saline hydration pre- and post-procedure (KDIGO recommends if eGFR <30), stop nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB — withhold 48 hours before and after). N-acetylcysteine: not recommended by current KDIGO (no consistent benefit in trials).

Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

Functional renal failure in severe hepatic disease (cirrhosis with ascites, acute liver failure). HRS-AKI (previously HRS-1): rapidly progressive, defined by AKI diagnostic criteria in a patient with cirrhosis, absence of shock, no sustained improvement after 2 days of diuretic withdrawal and IV albumin, no nephrotoxin exposure, no structural kidney disease. Pathophysiology: severe splanchnic vasodilation → reduced effective circulating volume → intense renal vasoconstriction. Treatment: vasoconstrictors (terlipressin IV with albumin — evidence from CONFIRM trial), liver transplantation for HRS-2 (chronic). Very poor prognosis without liver transplant.

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) — NICE NG203 (2021)

Staging

CKD is defined as eGFR <60 ml/min/1.73m² or evidence of kidney damage (proteinuria, haematuria, structural abnormality) persisting >3 months.

GFR categories (G1–G5) are combined with albuminuria categories (A1–A3) to provide a risk matrix:

Albuminuria: A1 <3 mg/mmol (normal); A2 3–30 mg/mmol (moderately increased); A3 >30 mg/mmol (severely increased).

Management of CKD

Blood pressure: Target <140/90 mmHg (<130/80 if diabetes or proteinuria). ACE inhibitor or ARB first-line, particularly with proteinuria — reduces progression (RENAAL, IDNT, REIN trials). Monitor potassium and creatinine after initiation (up to 30% rise in creatinine acceptable).

SGLT-2 inhibitors in CKD: Dapagliflozin (DAPA-CKD trial) and empagliflozin (EMPA-KIDNEY trial) demonstrate significant reductions in eGFR decline, end-stage renal disease, and cardiovascular events in CKD G2–G4 with albuminuria A2–A3, regardless of diabetes status. NICE now recommends dapagliflozin for CKD with eGFR 25–75 and ACR ≥22.6 mg/mmol.

Anaemia of CKD: Caused by reduced erythropoietin production, iron deficiency, and reduced red cell survival. Diagnose when Hb <110 g/L (or <120 g/L in men) with eGFR <60 and no other obvious cause. Investigate and correct iron deficiency first (ferritin <100 µg/L or transferrin saturation <20% — give IV iron if oral poorly tolerated or absorbed). Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESA): EPO/darbepoetin — target Hb 100–120 g/L. Avoid exceeding 120 g/L (increased VTE and stroke risk). Roxadustat (HIF-PHI inhibitor) — oral ESA now available.

CKD-Mineral Bone Disease (CKD-MBD): Rising phosphate (reduced renal excretion), reduced 1-alpha-hydroxylase → low calcitriol → secondary hyperparathyroidism → PTH rises to maintain calcium. Treatment: dietary phosphate restriction, phosphate binders (calcium-based — calcium acetate, non-calcium-based — sevelamer, lanthanum carbonate). Active vitamin D (alfacalcidol or calcitriol) to suppress PTH. Calcimimetics (cinacalcet) for severe secondary hyperparathyroidism.

Referral to nephrology: NICE recommends referral at eGFR <30 (all patients), eGFR <45 with rapid progression (>5 ml/min/1.73m² per year), persistent proteinuria ACR >70 mg/mmol, proteinuria ACR >30 with haematuria, AKI without clear cause, refractory hypertension (4+ agents), rare or inherited CKD.

Nephrotic Syndrome

Defined by the triad: proteinuria >3.5g/24 hours (or ACR >300–350 mg/mmol), hypoalbuminaemia (<25 g/L), and oedema. Hyperlipidaemia (reactive synthesis by liver) and lipiduria are also characteristic.

Complications of Nephrotic Syndrome

  • VTE: Loss of anticoagulant proteins (protein C, protein S, antithrombin III) in urine → hypercoagulable state. Renal vein thrombosis particularly associated with membranous nephropathy. Consider prophylactic anticoagulation if albumin <20 g/L.
  • Infection: Loss of immunoglobulins in urine, oedema as culture medium, immunosuppressive treatment. Pneumococcal vaccine mandatory. Particular risk of encapsulated bacteria (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae).
  • Hyperlipidaemia: Increased hepatic lipoprotein synthesis. Long-term atherosclerosis risk.

Nephritic Syndrome

Nephritic syndrome presents with: haematuria (dysmorphic red cells, red cell casts), hypertension, oliguria, mild-moderate proteinuria (<3.5g/24hr), oedema. The pathophysiology is glomerular inflammation causing haematuria and GFR reduction.

Key Causes

IgA Nephropathy (Berger Disease): Commonest glomerulonephritis worldwide. Mesangial IgA deposits. Presents with macroscopic haematuria coinciding with or 1–3 days after upper respiratory or GI infection ("synpharyngitic haematuria" — unlike post-streptococcal GN which has a 2–3-week latency). Many patients have mild microscopic haematuria with mild proteinuria. ACEi/ARB for proteinuria control. Sparsentan (dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist) now approved for IgA nephropathy. Targeted-release budesonide (Nefecon) — oral intestinal-targeted immunosuppression. Prognosis variable — 20–30% reach ESRD over 20 years.

Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis: 2–3 weeks after Group A streptococcal pharyngitis or 4–6 weeks after skin infection. Children predominantly. Diffuse proliferative GN on biopsy. Low complement C3. Elevated ASOT titre. Usually self-limiting. Penicillin for residual streptococcal infection.

Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN): Aggressive form causing rapid loss of renal function over days to weeks. Crescent formation on biopsy (proliferating parietal epithelial cells ± fibrin). Three types by immunofluorescence: Type I — linear IgG (anti-GBM disease/Goodpasture syndrome); Type II — granular immune complex (post-infectious, SLE, IgA); Type III — pauci-immune (ANCA-associated — GPA, MPA).

Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture Syndrome): Linear IgG deposition against type IV collagen in GBM. Pulmonary-renal syndrome — haemoptysis + rapidly progressive GN. Anti-GBM antibodies specific. Treatment: plasma exchange (removes antibodies) + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids. Poor prognosis if dialysis-dependent at presentation or >50% crescents on biopsy.

Renal Calculi — NICE NG118 (2019)

Renal calculi affect ~10% of the population at some point. More common in men, white ethnicity, hot climates (dehydration), certain metabolic conditions.

Presentation

Sudden onset severe loin-to-groin pain (ureteric colic) — cannot find a comfortable position (unlike peritonitis where still position helps). Associated with haematuria (frank or microscopic), nausea/vomiting, urinary frequency if stone at VUJ.

Investigation

CT KUB (computed tomography of kidney, ureter, bladder) is the gold standard — identifies all stone types (except pure indinavir stones in HIV patients on antiretroviral therapy), stone size, and degree of obstruction. No contrast required. USS is used in pregnancy and follow-up (avoids radiation). Plain X-ray (KUB) detects calcium-containing stones (radiopaque) but misses radiolucent uric acid stones and most cystine stones — not first-line for diagnosis.

Stone Composition and Metabolic Assessment

  • Calcium oxalate (~75%): Most common. Risk factors: low urine volume, hypercalciuria (primary hyperparathyroidism, vitamin D excess, immobility, loop diuretics), hyperoxaluria (inflammatory bowel disease, high oxalate diet), hypocitraturia (RTA, diarrhoea).
  • Calcium phosphate (~10%): Often associated with RTA or primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • Uric acid (~10%): Radiolucent on X-ray. Low urine pH (<5.5). Associations: gout, myeloproliferative disorders, high purine diet. Treatment: alkalinise urine (potassium citrate or sodium bicarbonate), allopurinol, increased fluids.
  • Struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate): Infection stones, urease-producing organisms (Proteus, Klebsiella). Staghorn calculi. Treat infection and stone.
  • Cystine: Rare, autosomal recessive cystinuria. Yellow-brown, soft hexagonal crystals in urine. Radiolucent. Alkalinise urine, penicillamine.

Management by Stone Size

  • <5mm: Likely to pass spontaneously (~90%). Medical expulsive therapy with tamsulosin (alpha-blocker relaxes ureter). Analgesia: NSAIDs first-line (diclofenac, ketorolac) superior to opioids for pain intensity. Adequate hydration.
  • 5–10mm: 50–75% pass spontaneously. Tamsulosin. Active intervention if pain uncontrolled, infection present, or persistent obstruction.
  • >10mm: Unlikely to pass. Active removal: ureteroscopy (URS) with laser lithotripsy for ureteric stones; extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL) for renal stones; percutaneous nephrolithotomy (PCNL) for large renal stones (>2cm). Absolute indication for emergency decompression: infected obstructed system (pyonephrosis) — nephrostomy or retrograde ureteric stent urgently.

Hyperkalaemia

Potentially life-threatening due to risk of cardiac arrhythmia. UKKA (UK Kidney Association) 2023 guidance provides thresholds for treatment and approach to management.

ECG Changes in Hyperkalaemia

Progressive with rising potassium: peaked T waves (K 5.5–6.5) → prolonged PR interval → loss of P waves → widened QRS complex → sine wave pattern → ventricular fibrillation/asystole. Any new ECG change in hyperkalaemia requires immediate cardiac monitoring.

Emergency Management

  1. Cardiac membrane stabilisation: IV calcium gluconate 10ml 10% over 5 minutes (repeat if no ECG improvement). Protects myocardium — does NOT lower potassium.
  2. Shift potassium intracellularly: IV insulin 10 units + IV 50ml 50% dextrose (or 125ml 20%). Onset 15–30 minutes, lasts 4–6 hours. Nebulised salbutamol 10–20mg. IV sodium bicarbonate 8.4% 50ml if metabolic acidosis.
  3. Remove potassium from body: Furosemide IV (if renal function allows, promotes urinary K excretion). Patiromer or sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC, Lokelma) — cation exchange polymers that bind potassium in gut (onset hours to days — maintenance therapy not acute emergency use). Dialysis for refractory severe hyperkalaemia or AKI/CKD precluding other measures.

SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH Secretion)

Diagnostic Criteria

SIADH is euvolaemic hyponatraemia caused by inappropriately elevated ADH. Diagnostic criteria: serum sodium <135 mmol/L, serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg, urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg (key: inappropriately concentrated), urine sodium >30 mmol/L (kidneys excreting sodium despite hyponatraemia), euvolaemia (no clinical signs of dehydration or fluid overload), normal thyroid/adrenal/renal function, no diuretic use.

Causes

CNS: meningitis, encephalitis, SAH, stroke, head injury, brain tumour. Pulmonary: pneumonia, TB, SCLC, positive pressure ventilation. Drugs: SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide, tricyclics, chlorpropamide, desmopressin, opiates, PPIs. Postoperative. Pain and nausea (both stimulate ADH release).

Distinguish SIADH from CSWS (cerebral salt wasting — occurs in CNS disease, particularly SAH): CSWS involves hypovolaemia and natriuresis, while SIADH is euvolaemic. Important distinction because treatment differs: SIADH requires fluid restriction; CSWS requires fluid and sodium replacement.

Management

  • Fluid restriction (500–800ml/day): First-line for chronic SIADH — correction is gradual (goal 1–2 mmol/L/hour, maximum 8–10 mmol/L per 24 hours to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome).
  • Demeclocycline: Tetracycline antibiotic that causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus — used for resistant chronic SIADH.
  • Hypertonic saline (1.8% or 3% NaCl): For severe symptomatic hyponatraemia (Na <120 with seizures, severe confusion, or coma). Give 100–150ml 3% NaCl over 20 minutes, repeat ×2 if needed. Aim to raise sodium 4–6 mmol/L rapidly to control symptoms, then slow correction. Use in ICU setting.
  • Tolvaptan: V2 receptor antagonist — blocks ADH action in collecting duct. First licensed vaptane in UK for SIADH and autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD). Risks: rapid over-correction of sodium, hepatotoxicity.
  • Treat underlying cause.

Diabetes Insipidus and Polyuria

Diabetes insipidus (DI) causes polyuria (usually >3L/day) with dilute urine and polydipsia. Two types: central (ADH deficiency — pituitary/hypothalamic disease, head injury, neurosurgery, craniopharyngioma) and nephrogenic (ADH resistance — renal tubular unresponsiveness: lithium toxicity, hypercalcaemia, hypokalaemia, genetic).

Water deprivation test: Fluid deprivation for up to 8 hours (stop if >3% body weight lost). Measure urine osmolality. Normal response: urine osmolality rises >800 mOsm/kg. Primary polydipsia (psychogenic): urine concentrates to >600 mOsm/kg. DI: urine remains dilute (<300 mOsm/kg). Then give desmopressin (DDAVP): central DI → urine osmolality increases >50%. Nephrogenic DI → minimal or no response.

Treatment: Central DI — desmopressin (intranasal or oral). Nephrogenic DI — stop causative drug; thiazide diuretics (paradoxical reduction in urine output by volume depletion); low-sodium diet; NSAIDs (reduce prostaglandin-mediated inhibition of ADH).

FAQ: Nephrology for the UKMLA

What are the KDIGO 2024 AKI staging criteria?

Stage 1: creatinine ×1.5–1.9 baseline within 7 days, or rise ≥26.5 µmol/L within 48 hours, or urine output <0.5 ml/kg/hr for 6–12 hours. Stage 2: creatinine ×2.0–2.9 or UO <0.5 ml/kg/hr for ≥12 hours. Stage 3: creatinine ×3.0 or ≥354 µmol/L with acute rise, or initiation of renal replacement therapy, or UO <0.3 ml/kg/hr for ≥24 hours or anuria ≥12 hours.

How do nephrotic and nephritic syndromes differ?

Nephrotic syndrome is characterised by massive proteinuria (>3.5g/24hr), hypoalbuminaemia, oedema, and hyperlipidaemia — without haematuria as a primary feature. Nephritic syndrome presents with haematuria (red cell casts), hypertension, oliguria, and mild proteinuria (<3.5g/24hr) — reflecting glomerular inflammation. Both can coexist in mixed syndromes such as membranoproliferative GN or lupus nephritis.

What is the gold-standard investigation for suspected renal calculi?

CT KUB (non-contrast CT of kidneys, ureters and bladder) is the gold standard. It identifies all stone types, determines stone size and location, and assesses degree of obstruction. Ultrasound is used when CT is contraindicated (pregnancy) or for follow-up. Plain KUB X-ray has poor sensitivity and misses radiolucent stones (uric acid, indinavir) — it should not be used as the primary diagnostic tool.

What is the emergency management of hyperkalaemia?

Step 1 — Cardiac protection: IV calcium gluconate 10ml 10% IV over 5 minutes (stabilises cardiac membrane, does not lower potassium). Step 2 — Shift potassium into cells: insulin-dextrose (10 units insulin + 50ml 50% dextrose), nebulised salbutamol 10–20mg, IV sodium bicarbonate if acidotic. Step 3 — Remove potassium: furosemide if kidneys working, patiromer or SZC orally for ongoing management, dialysis if renal replacement needed or refractory.

What distinguishes SIADH from CSWS?

Both cause hyponatraemia with high urine sodium and occur in CNS disease. The key distinguishing feature is volume status: SIADH patients are euvolaemic (no signs of dehydration — JVP normal, no postural hypotension, skin turgor normal). CSWS patients are hypovolaemic (low JVP, postural hypotension, negative fluid balance) because the kidneys inappropriately waste sodium. This distinction is clinically vital — treating SIADH with fluid restriction would dangerously worsen CSWS.

When should CKD patients be referred to nephrology?

NICE NG203 recommends nephrology referral for: eGFR <30 ml/min/1.73m² (all patients for transplant planning and CKD-related complications); progressive CKD with loss >5 ml/min/1.73m² per year; ACR >70 mg/mmol; ACR >30 with haematuria not explained by urological cause; suspected rare or inherited CKD (young age, family history, extrarenal features); refractory hypertension on ≥4 agents; AKI without clear cause or not recovering as expected.

Conclusion

Nephrology in the UKMLA AKT tests clinical reasoning at its most precise — applying staging criteria to diagnose AKI, interpreting urine biochemistry to classify electrolyte disorders, and selecting guideline-concordant management for complex conditions. A solid grasp of fluid and electrolyte physiology is the foundation upon which all nephrology knowledge is built.

The most important recent developments are the incorporation of SGLT-2 inhibitors for CKD beyond diabetes, the KDIGO 2024 AKI criteria updates, and the UKKA 2023 hyperkalaemia management pathway. These are all high-yield for the AKT given their recency and clinical relevance.
