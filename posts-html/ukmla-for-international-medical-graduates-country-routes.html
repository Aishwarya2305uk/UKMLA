Quick Answer: The UKMLA Process Is the Same Wherever You Trained

Every international medical graduate, regardless of home country, follows fundamentally the same route to a GMC licence: confirm your primary medical qualification is listed correctly with the World Directory of Medical Schools, complete Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC) primary source verification, meet the GMC's English language requirement through IELTS or OET, pass the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT), pass the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA), and then apply for full registration with a licence to practise. Consequently, a doctor trained in Accra, Kathmandu, or Muscat follows exactly the same sequence as a doctor trained in Mumbai or Lagos — the destination process does not change, only the starting paperwork and local logistics differ slightly by country.

This post therefore functions as a country-by-country quick reference rather than a full step-by-step manual. For candidates who want the complete, detailed walkthrough of every stage of the pathway, the site's dedicated guide on how to become a doctor in the UK through GMC registration covers the entire journey in depth, and the companion post on GMC registration requirements for international doctors goes further still into the specific documentary and verification requirements every IMG must satisfy. If your home country is India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Egypt, a Gulf state, Sri Lanka, South Africa, or the Philippines, this site already has a dedicated, considerably deeper guide written specifically for your route, and you should read that instead of this reference post.

In addition, it is worth stating plainly why this particular group of countries has been bundled together rather than split into individual posts covering the UKMLA for international medical graduates from each single nation. Ghana, Nepal, Malaysia, Ethiopia, Sudan, Zimbabwe, Oman, the Caribbean, and Southeast Asia each generate real, ongoing search interest from prospective candidates, but none generates the sustained volume that would justify a full 5,500-word dedicated treatment on its own. The Shared GMC/UKMLA Process, Briefly Recapped

Before looking at individual countries, it helps to recap the shared skeleton every IMG candidate moves through, since the country-specific sections below assume this background rather than repeating it. The process begins with confirming that your medical school and qualification appear correctly in the World Directory of Medical Schools, since the GMC uses WDOMS as its reference point for recognising a primary medical qualification from outside the UK. Candidates then register for EPIC, the verification service that confirms directly with your medical school and licensing authority that your degree and good standing are genuine, rather than relying on documents you submit yourself.

Once identity and qualification checks are underway, candidates must also satisfy the GMC's English language requirement, typically through an acceptable IELTS or OET score, unless they qualify for an exemption. From there, the two UKMLA assessments follow: the AKT, a computer-based knowledge test delivered globally through Pearson VUE, and the CPSA, a face-to-face clinical and communication skills assessment held only at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester. Passing both, together with satisfying the "good standing" and identity requirements, allows a candidate to apply for full registration with a licence to practise. For the exact, current requirements around primary source verification, the GMC's own guidance page on primary source verification for international medical graduates remains the single most authoritative reference, and it is worth reading directly rather than relying solely on any third-party summary, including this one.

Because AKT test centre availability varies meaningfully by country and changes between sitting windows, candidates from every country covered in this guide should also consult the dedicated UKMLA AKT test centre guide before assuming a particular city has an open slot. In addition, since the full cost of the pathway adds up across EPIC verification, English testing, the AKT, the CPSA, travel, and registration fees, candidates budgeting in Ghanaian Cedi, Nepalese Rupee, Malaysian Ringgit, or any other currency should read the comprehensive UKMLA fees for IMGs guide, which breaks down every fee across the entire route regardless of home country.

Once both assessments are passed, the remaining steps toward full registration are largely administrative rather than exam-related. The dedicated GMC registration guide pillar page walks through exactly what happens after the CPSA, including document submission and typical processing times, and is worth reading well before you reach that stage so nothing about the final administrative steps comes as a surprise.

Why the Starting Paperwork Differs More Than the Exam Itself

Although the AKT and CPSA are identical for every candidate, the paperwork stage that precedes them is where country-specific differences actually show up, and this is precisely why a country-by-country reference is useful even though the exam itself is uniform. WDOMS listings, the exact documents a national regulator issues for "good standing," and how quickly EPIC can verify a given country's records all vary, sometimes considerably, from one country to the next. A candidate from a country with a long-established, digitised regulatory system may find EPIC verification proceeds relatively smoothly, whereas a candidate from a country whose regulatory infrastructure has faced recent disruption may need to budget significantly more time for the same stage.

Therefore, the single most valuable thing a candidate from any of the countries covered in this guide can do early on is contact their own national regulator directly to confirm exactly what a "certificate of good standing" or equivalent document looks like in their system, how long it typically takes to obtain, and whether it needs to be sent directly to EPIC or can be issued to the candidate first. This single question, asked early, prevents the most common source of delay reported by IMG candidates from smaller-volume countries: discovering, months into the process, that the certificate they assumed would take two weeks actually takes two months.

UKMLA for International Medical Graduates: Country-by-Country Quick Reference

The sections below cover each country or region in turn. Each one names the relevant national medical regulator, mentions well-known accredited medical schools purely as illustrative examples rather than an exhaustive list, and notes the typical local currency referenced in fee planning. However, candidates should treat every specific detail below as a starting point for their own research rather than a final answer, and should always confirm current requirements directly with the GMC, EPIC, and their own national regulator before making any firm plans.

Ghana

Medical education in Ghana is regulated by the Medical and Dental Council of Ghana, which maintains the register of qualified doctors and oversees standards at the country's medical schools. Well-known Ghanaian medical schools that IMG candidates sometimes reference as examples include the University of Ghana Medical School and the Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology School of Medicine and Dentistry, though candidates should always confirm their own specific institution's current WDOMS listing rather than assuming any particular school is included, since accreditation status can change over time.

Ghanaian doctors pursuing the UKMLA pathway should verify their qualification against the World Directory of Medical Schools early, since EPIC verification depends on this listing being accurate and current. Furthermore, when budgeting for the pathway, Ghanaian candidates will typically see UK fees quoted in pounds sterling and will need to convert these into the Ghanaian Cedi for personal financial planning; given that exchange rates fluctuate, it is sensible to check a current conversion shortly before each payment rather than relying on a figure calculated months earlier. Ghana also has an established, if smaller, community of NHS doctors already, and candidates researching this route can reasonably expect to find compatriots further along the same pathway willing to share practical, if informal, experience.

Nepal

The Nepal Medical Council is the regulatory body responsible for registering doctors and overseeing medical education standards in Nepal. Institutions frequently cited as examples of accredited Nepali medical schools include the Institute of Medicine at Tribhuvan University and the B.P. Koirala Institute of Health Sciences, although, as with every country in this guide, candidates must verify their own specific school's current WDOMS status directly rather than assuming inclusion based on general reputation.

Nepali candidates should also confirm their good standing certificate and verification documents meet the Nepal Medical Council's own export requirements before beginning EPIC verification, since processing times for these certificates can vary and may affect the overall UKMLA timeline. In terms of fee planning, Nepali doctors will most commonly reference the Nepalese Rupee when converting UK-denominated costs, and given the meaningful exchange-rate gap between the Nepalese Rupee and pounds sterling, building a realistic sterling-equivalent budget early in the process is particularly important for this route. Because Nepal's total volume of UKMLA candidates is smaller than that of the larger South Asian source countries, Nepali candidates may also find it useful to read the wider South Asian route context covered in the guide to how to become a doctor in the UK from India, purely as a comparison of how a considerably larger neighbouring route operates, even though the specific regulator and documentation differ.

Malaysia

Malaysia's medical profession is regulated by the Malaysian Medical Council, which maintains professional registers and oversees the country's medical schools. Examples of well-known Malaysian institutions include Universiti Malaya's Faculty of Medicine and the International Medical University, though, in line with the same caveat that applies throughout this guide, candidates should verify their exact medical school's current WDOMS listing rather than relying on institutional reputation alone.

Malaysia also functions as a useful reference point for the wider Southeast Asian region covered later in this guide, since it is the Southeast Asian country most commonly represented among UKMLA candidates from the region and has a comparatively well-established path through EPIC verification. Malaysian candidates typically reference the Malaysian Ringgit when budgeting for UK-denominated fees, and given Malaysia's broadly stable exchange rate relative to some other countries covered in this guide, currency planning tends to be somewhat more predictable here than for candidates converting from currencies with greater volatility. Malaysian candidates should nonetheless still confirm current AKT test centre availability directly rather than assuming a centre remains open indefinitely, since Pearson VUE capacity anywhere in the world is subject to change between sitting windows.

Ethiopia

Ethiopian medical regulation sits with the Ethiopian Food, Medicine and Healthcare Administration and Control Authority, alongside the relevant professional medical council responsible for registering practitioners domestically; candidates should confirm precisely which body issues their good standing certificate, since regulatory structures in this area have evolved over recent years. Addis Ababa University's School of Medicine is frequently cited as a well-known example of an Ethiopian medical school, although, as always, the specific WDOMS status of any given institution must be checked directly rather than assumed.

Because Ethiopia's regulatory landscape has seen structural changes, Ethiopian candidates should build extra time into their planning for confirming exactly which authority handles verification requests and good standing certificates before submitting EPIC applications. When it comes to fee planning, Ethiopian doctors will generally convert UK costs into the Ethiopian Birr, and given periodic exchange-rate movements, checking a current conversion close to each payment date is a sensible habit rather than an optional extra. Ethiopian candidates should also treat early, direct contact with EPIC as a priority, since confirming exactly which office to approach for verification documents can otherwise consume weeks of otherwise avoidable delay.

Sudan

The Sudan Medical Council is the national body responsible for registering doctors and overseeing medical education standards in Sudan. Well-known Sudanese medical schools sometimes referenced as examples include the University of Khartoum Faculty of Medicine, though, in keeping with the honesty this guide aims for throughout, candidates should verify their own institution's current WDOMS listing directly, particularly given how significantly ongoing instability in Sudan has affected administrative and educational infrastructure in recent years.

Given the disruption Sudan has experienced, Sudanese candidates pursuing the UKMLA pathway should expect that obtaining good standing certificates, transcripts, or verification documents may take considerably longer or require alternative arrangements compared with candidates from countries with stable regulatory infrastructure, and should contact the GMC and EPIC directly to discuss their specific circumstances rather than assuming a standard timeline applies. For fee planning, the Sudanese Pound is the relevant currency for local budgeting, though candidates should treat any specific exchange-rate figure as provisional given the currency's documented volatility, and should always check a current rate immediately before converting or transferring funds. Sudanese doctors already form part of the NHS workforce, and this existing community is itself evidence that the pathway, while it may take longer to navigate in current circumstances, remains genuinely achievable.

Zimbabwe

Zimbabwean doctors are regulated by the Medical and Dental Practitioners Council of Zimbabwe, which registers practitioners and sets standards for medical education within the country. The University of Zimbabwe College of Health Sciences is commonly cited as a well-known example of an accredited Zimbabwean medical school, although candidates should, as always, verify their own school's current WDOMS status directly rather than relying on general reputation.

Zimbabwe deserves a particularly strong currency caveat in this guide. The country has experienced well-documented and severe currency instability over the past two decades, including periods of hyperinflation and multiple changes to the official currency itself. Consequently, this guide deliberately avoids naming any specific Zimbabwean currency figure or exchange rate as reliable, and Zimbabwean candidates should treat currency conversion as something to check immediately before every single payment, using an official, current source, rather than assuming any rate remains valid even for a few weeks. In addition, given the practical challenges this instability can create around international bank transfers, Zimbabwean candidates should budget extra time and contingency funds for the payment stages of the UKMLA pathway, and should confirm with their bank in advance exactly how an international transfer to a UK-based fee collector will be processed, since this single step has reportedly caused avoidable delay for some candidates from currency-unstable economies.

Oman

Medical regulation in Oman involves both the Oman Medical Specialty Board and the Omani Ministry of Health, depending on the specific stage and type of registration or verification required; candidates should confirm with the relevant body exactly which certificates and verifications they need for GMC purposes, since responsibilities can be split between institutions. Sultan Qaboos University's College of Medicine and Health Sciences is a well-known example of an Omani medical school, though its WDOMS status, like every institution mentioned in this guide, should be verified directly rather than assumed.

Because Oman sits within the wider Gulf region, candidates researching this route may also find it useful to read the site's dedicated guide on the UKMLA pathway for Gulf-region doctors, which covers shared regional considerations relevant to Omani candidates alongside those from neighbouring Gulf states, even though Oman's own regulatory specifics differ in detail. For fee planning, Omani doctors will generally reference the Omani Rial, a currency that trades at a notably different scale to pounds sterling, so candidates should double-check decimal places carefully when converting figures to avoid costly arithmetic errors. Given Oman's geographic and professional links with the wider Gulf healthcare sector, Omani candidates already working alongside doctors from neighbouring Gulf states may also find informal peer guidance more readily available than candidates from some of the other countries in this guide.

The Caribbean

Medical regulation across the Caribbean varies meaningfully from island nation to island nation, and this guide deliberately avoids naming a single regulator, since no single body covers the entire region. Many Caribbean nations operate within the broader context of the Caribbean Community (CARICOM), which supports regional cooperation on professional standards in various fields, but candidates should confirm the specific national medical council or regulatory body relevant to the exact country where they trained, rather than assuming a single pan-Caribbean regulator applies.

Well-known medical schools across the Caribbean include institutions in Jamaica, Trinidad and Tobago, and several other islands, though given the genuine diversity of the region, this guide intentionally avoids naming specific schools as a representative list, since doing so risks implying an endorsement or completeness this section cannot honestly provide. Currency likewise varies considerably by island nation — from the Jamaican Dollar to the Trinidad and Tobago Dollar and several others — so Caribbean candidates should identify and confirm the correct currency for their own specific country before budgeting, rather than assuming any single Caribbean currency applies regionally. Historically, Caribbean-trained doctors have contributed to the NHS for decades, in some cases across multiple generations of the same family, and this long-standing connection is itself a reassuring signal for newer candidates that the pathway, though it requires careful country-specific verification, is well-trodden.

Planning Your Timeline Across Any of These Routes

Regardless of which country listed above applies to you, the practical planning sequence for the UKMLA for international medical graduates is broadly consistent, and it helps to think of it as four overlapping phases rather than a single long queue. The first phase covers confirming WDOMS status and initiating EPIC verification, which should begin as early as possible given that this stage is where country-specific delays are most likely to appear. The second phase covers English language testing, which candidates can often complete in parallel with EPIC verification rather than waiting for it to finish first. The third phase covers booking and sitting the AKT, ideally at a Pearson VUE centre reasonably local to the candidate, and the fourth phase covers the mandatory trip to Manchester for the CPSA, which every candidate worldwide must complete regardless of nationality.

Because the CPSA cannot be booked until the AKT has been passed, a delay at any earlier stage has a compounding effect on the whole timeline, pushing the final registration date back by more than the length of the original delay. Therefore, candidates from any of the countries covered in this guide benefit from starting the EPIC and English-language stages considerably earlier than they might initially expect, rather than treating those steps as quick formalities to be completed shortly before the exams themselves. In addition, candidates should periodically revisit the site's UKMLA AKT test centre guide as their own target sitting period approaches, since test-centre booking windows open and close on a schedule that rewards early, active checking rather than a single check made many months in advance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a Ghanaian medical degree recognised by the GMC?

A Ghanaian medical degree can be recognised for GMC registration purposes provided the specific medical school is correctly listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools and the qualification successfully passes EPIC primary source verification. Candidates should confirm their own institution's current WDOMS status directly rather than assuming recognition based on general reputation, since listings can change over time. Once verification is complete, Ghanaian graduates follow the same AKT and CPSA pathway as any other international medical graduate.

Can Nepali doctors take the UKMLA?

Yes, Nepali doctors are fully eligible to pursue the UKMLA pathway, provided their medical qualification is recognised through the World Directory of Medical Schools and successfully verified through EPIC. Nepali candidates should confirm their good standing certificate meets the Nepal Medical Council's export requirements early, since certificate processing times can vary and may affect the overall timeline. From there, the AKT and CPSA process is identical to that followed by candidates from any other country.

Does the GMC accept medical degrees from Malaysia?

The GMC can accept Malaysian medical degrees for registration purposes provided the relevant medical school appears correctly in the World Directory of Medical Schools and the qualification is verified through EPIC. The remaining steps, including the English language requirement, AKT, and CPSA, apply identically to Malaysian candidates as to any other IMG.

What currency should Zimbabwean candidates use when budgeting for UKMLA fees?
