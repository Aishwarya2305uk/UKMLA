UKMLA for Nepali doctors means passing the two GMC-recognised assessments that sit under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, commonly known as the AKT and the CPSA, then completing GMC registration through EPIC/MyIntealth verification, an accepted internship and an approved English language score. For most Nepali medical graduates the full journey, from booking the first test to holding full GMC registration, takes roughly twelve to eighteen months and costs somewhere between £1,345 and £1,800 in core exam and registration fees, before English tests, travel and visa costs are added. This guide walks through every stage of that route using the General Medical Council's own Medical Licensing Assessment guidance as the primary source, so that a final-year MBBS student at Tribhuvan University and a working doctor in Kathmandu can both use it as a single, reliable reference.

Key takeaways

  • Nepali doctors follow the same UKMLA-aligned route as every other international medical graduate (IMG): a knowledge test (AKT) and a clinical and professional skills assessment (CPSA), both set to Medical Licensing Assessment standards.
  • From 1 April 2026, the AKT costs £283 and the CPSA costs £1,036, according to the GMC's official fee schedule; registration adds a further £26 (provisional) or £481 (full).
  • Nepal's own regulator, the Nepal Medical Council, is a completely different body from India's National Medical Commission, even though both are commonly shortened to "NMC" — do not confuse the two when reading generic South Asia-focused guidance.
  • The CPSA is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester, so every Nepali candidate must travel to the UK at least once; AKT test centre availability in Nepal should always be checked directly with Pearson VUE.
  • EPIC verification, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform, must confirm your Nepali MBBS before the GMC will process your registration application.
  • Most Nepali candidates need IELTS 7.5 overall (7.0 in each band) or OET Medicine grade B in every component, unless they qualify for an exemption.

Who this route is for: Nepali doctors and the UKMLA

The Medical Licensing Assessment, or MLA, is the GMC's overarching quality bar for anyone joining the UK medical register, whether they trained at a UK medical school or overseas. UK final-year students sit the MLA directly as part of their degree. International medical graduates, including doctors who completed their MBBS in Nepal, sit what the GMC's paperwork still often calls PLAB, restructured so its two parts are aligned with, and equivalent to, the MLA's AKT and CPSA components. In practice, when this site and most independent guides refer to "UKMLA for Nepali doctors," they mean the AKT-and-CPSA route that Nepali IMGs use to reach the same licensing standard as a UK graduate.

This naming quirk trips up many Nepali applicants: your GMC paperwork may still reference "PLAB 1" and "PLAB 2" fees, while revision providers, question banks and this site describe the same two assessments as "UKMLA AKT" and "UKMLA CPSA." They are the same tests under two labels. Nepali candidates share almost the entire eligibility gateway with doctors from other South Asian countries, and the closely related guides to UKMLA for Indian doctors, UKMLA for Bangladeshi doctors and UKMLA for Sri Lankan doctors are worth reading alongside this one, since the GMC-facing mechanics, fees and general timeline are almost identical across all four countries even though the domestic medical councils and MBBS structures differ in the details covered below.

A Nepali MBBS graduate's path into UK practice runs through exactly the same eligibility gateway as every other IMG: an accepted primary medical qualification, a completed internship, an approved English language score, EPIC/MyIntealth verification and a pass in both the AKT and the CPSA. What differs for Nepal specifically is the domestic regulatory body involved (the Nepal Medical Council, not to be confused with India's similarly abbreviated regulator), the internship structure attached to the MBBS degree, test centre availability for the AKT, the currency conversion on every fee, and a genuine "stay or go" comparison against Nepal's own licensing exam. The rest of this guide works through each of those in turn, for the broader UKMLA for international medical graduates: country-by-country routes hub, and for the Eligibility and Fees pillar pages that underpin every country-specific post on this site.

Understanding Nepal's medical education and regulatory system

Before addressing the GMC side of the route, it helps to be precise about how Nepal's own system works, because several Nepal-specific details do not map cleanly onto guidance written for other South Asian countries.

The Nepali MBBS and where it is awarded

The primary medical qualification in Nepal is the Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery, or MBBS, typically a programme of around five and a half academic years followed by a compulsory internship. Nepali medical colleges awarding the MBBS include Tribhuvan University Institute of Medicine, the B.P. Koirala Institute of Health Sciences, Kathmandu University School of Medical Sciences and a number of private medical colleges affiliated with these or other recognised universities. For UK registration purposes, what matters is whether your specific medical college and programme is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, the same directory the GMC checks for every other country's graduates, so confirming your college's listing status early is a sensible first step rather than an afterthought.

Nepal Medical Council: Nepal's own regulator, not India's NMC

Every Nepali MBBS graduate must register with the Nepal Medical Council, the statutory body established in 1964 that regulates medical education, accredits medical colleges and registers doctors to practise inside Nepal. This is an important point to state clearly, because Nepal's regulator is also commonly abbreviated "NMC," and India's equivalent regulatory body, the National Medical Commission, is very often shortened to the same three letters in casual conversation and in some articles. These are two entirely separate organisations, each with authority only over its own country's doctors and medical colleges, and a Nepali doctor's registration with Nepal's Nepal Medical Council has no legal standing in India, just as an Indian doctor's National Medical Commission registration has no standing in Nepal. If you are reading generic South Asia-focused guidance, including our own India-facing content, always check which "NMC" a given sentence is actually referring to before assuming it applies to your situation.

Nepal's compulsory internship structure

Nepal's MBBS programme includes a Compulsory Rotating Internship (CRI), typically one calendar year, completed in teaching hospitals affiliated with the graduate's medical college. Reporting from Nepali medical education sources indicates that students who have completed at least six months of this internship can sit Nepal's own licensing examination (covered below), while full registration with the Nepal Medical Council is generally granted only once the complete one-year internship, whether completed in Nepal or, in some cases, recognised equivalently abroad, is finished. Because internship structures and exact timing requirements are periodically reviewed, Nepali doctors planning a UK application should confirm the current position directly with the Nepal Medical Council's official website rather than relying solely on secondary summaries, including this one.

For GMC purposes, the internship question that actually matters is slightly different: whether your internship satisfies the GMC's own requirement for an acceptable period of supervised clinical practice before full registration is granted. This is assessed by the GMC itself, using its own criteria, rather than automatically inherited from Nepal Medical Council recognition, so it is worth reading the GMC's guidance on internship and provisional registration requirements directly, alongside our companion piece on GMC registration requirements for international doctors, before assuming your Nepali internship will be accepted without question.

Step 1: Confirm your eligibility as a Nepali IMG

Before spending money on tests, a Nepali doctor should confirm four eligibility pillars, because failing any one of them stalls the whole application regardless of how well the exams themselves go.

1. Your primary medical qualification (PMQ)

Nepali medical colleges that appear in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC checks as part of primary source verification, generally have their MBBS accepted as a valid PMQ. Acceptance of the qualification itself is a separate question from whether you have passed the UKMLA route or completed an accepted internship, so do not assume a listed college automatically means you are eligible to register today.

2. Nepal Medical Council registration and internship completion

You should hold, or be clearly on track to hold, full registration with Nepal's own Nepal Medical Council, evidencing that your compulsory rotating internship has been completed to the standard Nepal requires. As covered above, this is a separate registration from anything India-related, and it is also a separate question from GMC provisional or full registration, which follows its own criteria once your documents are submitted.

3. English language evidence

Most Nepali doctors need to demonstrate English proficiency through IELTS Academic (overall 7.5, minimum 7.0 in each of the four components) or OET Medicine (minimum grade B in every section), unless a recognised exemption applies. Our companion guide to the English language requirement for the UKMLA route, though titled around one country, describes the same GMC-wide English testing framework that applies to Nepali candidates, and is worth reading in full before booking a test date.

4. EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification

EPIC, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform operated by ECFMG/Intealth, independently verifies that your Nepali MBBS is genuine by contacting your medical college directly. GMC registration cannot proceed without a completed EPIC/MyIntealth verification report, and because your college's response time is outside your control, this step has the least predictable timeline of the whole process. Our dedicated GMC EPIC verification process explained guide covers exactly how the platform works, what documents your college needs from you, and how to track your verification status, and it applies equally whether your degree is from Nepal, India, Bangladesh or any other listed country.

Documents checklist for Nepali applicants

Gathering the following in advance avoids the most common cause of delay:

  • MBBS degree certificate and full academic transcripts from your Nepali medical college
  • Internship completion certificate confirming your one-year Compulsory Rotating Internship
  • Nepal Medical Council registration certificate
  • Valid passport and any prior UK visa history
  • IELTS Academic or OET Medicine certificate meeting the required scores
  • Completed EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification report
  • Curriculum vitae detailing clinical experience since graduation
  • Evidence of any additional postgraduate training, including Nepal's own licensing exam result if applicable

Step 2: Understand the AKT and CPSA as a Nepali candidate

Once eligibility is confirmed, the practical work begins with the two assessments. The AKT is a computer-based knowledge test covering the breadth of the MLA content map, delivered in a single sitting through Pearson VUE test centres. Whether a Pearson VUE centre currently operates inside Nepal changes over time and should always be checked directly on the Pearson VUE website when you are ready to book; many Nepali candidates end up travelling to a neighbouring country's test centre, most often in India, if no Nepal-based sitting is listed at that time. This is one of the few genuinely practical differences between the Nepali route and, for example, the Indian route described in our UKMLA for Indian doctors guide, where AKT test centres inside the country are more consistently available.

The CPSA is different in kind. It tests clinical examination skills, practical procedures, data interpretation and communication across a circuit of stations, and it is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester. Every Nepali candidate, however strong their AKT result, must therefore travel to the UK at least once to complete the CPSA. Because of that, most experienced advisers recommend treating the CPSA trip as a single, well-planned visit, combining travel with final preparation rather than making more than one trip if it can be avoided.

How Nepali doctors typically prepare for the AKT

Because the AKT draws on the GMC's own content map rather than the Nepali undergraduate curriculum specifically, most Nepali doctors combine a recognised international question bank with the official MLA content map as a syllabus checklist, since UK prescribing conventions, NICE-aligned guidelines and NHS-specific clinical processes are tested in ways that differ from Nepali university exams. A structured three-to-six-month preparation window is typical, with mock papers layered in during the final six to eight weeks. The general Preparation pillar gives a realistic week-by-week structure that Nepali candidates can adapt around hospital duty rotas or existing clinical work.

Preparing for the CPSA as a Nepali doctor

The CPSA also tests communication style and professionalism in ways that can catch candidates from any country off guard, not because clinical knowledge is lacking, but because UK patient-communication norms, such as explicit consent-seeking language and structured breaking-bad-news frameworks, are assessed quite specifically. Reviewing the GMC's own Good Medical Practice standards alongside CPSA-specific practice stations, rather than assuming general clinical competence built up in Nepal will automatically transfer, tends to make a measurable difference to Nepali candidates sitting the assessment for the first time.

Because many Nepali doctors are not yet earning a UK-level salary at the point of applying, this discount is genuinely worth checking on the GMC's fees for doctors page before assuming the full rate applies to you.

Step 4: The application and registration process, step by step

Although every candidate's exact order varies slightly, most Nepali doctors follow a recognisable sequence. Working through it as a checklist helps avoid the common trap of booking an exam before a prerequisite is actually in place.

  1. Confirm eligibility. Check your PMQ listing, Nepal Medical Council registration, internship completion and English language position against current GMC criteria before spending money on anything else.
  2. Start EPIC/MyIntealth verification early. Because your Nepali medical college must respond to a verification request, this step has the least predictable timeline and should be initiated months, not weeks, before your planned registration date. See our GMC EPIC verification process explained guide.
  3. Sit IELTS or OET and receive your certified score, allowing time for a resit if your first attempt falls short of the required bands.
  4. Book and sit the AKT at a Pearson VUE centre, in Nepal if a centre is currently listed, or otherwise at the nearest available international centre, typically after several months of structured revision.
  5. Book and travel for the CPSA in Manchester, usually scheduling a short UK visit that combines final preparation with the assessment itself.
  6. Submit your GMC registration application once your AKT and CPSA results, EPIC/MyIntealth report, English language evidence and internship confirmation are all in hand. Our overview of GMC registration requirements for international doctors covers this step in more depth.
  7. Pay the relevant registration fee (provisional or full, per the table above) and await GMC processing.
  8. Receive GMC registration with a licence to practise, at which point you can legally begin applying for NHS posts.
  9. Arrange your visa once you have a confirmed job offer, most commonly the Health and Care Worker visa (see below).

Because several of these steps run in parallel rather than strictly one after another, experienced candidates recommend starting EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English testing as early as possible, so that by the time your AKT and CPSA results arrive, the rest of the paperwork is not the bottleneck.

Provisional or full registration: which should a Nepali doctor apply for?

Many Nepali doctors are unsure whether to apply for provisional or full registration first, and the answer depends mainly on whether your internship is already complete and confirmed by the Nepal Medical Council at the point you apply. Provisional registration, at the lower £26 application fee, is generally for doctors who still need to complete part of their internship requirement under supervision, while full registration, at £481, is for doctors who can already evidence a complete, GMC-acceptable internship alongside their other documents. Applying for the wrong category can cost weeks of otherwise avoidable delay, so confirm your internship status precisely before submitting anything.

Most Nepali doctors report a total timeline of roughly twelve to eighteen months from the point they begin serious AKT preparation to the point they hold full GMC registration, though this varies considerably depending on how quickly EPIC/MyIntealth verification completes and whether a resit is needed at either stage. Sitting the CPSA in the UK: visas and travel for Nepali candidates

Because the CPSA takes place in Manchester, Nepali candidates need a UK visa purely to attend the assessment, even though this is not yet an employment visa. Most Nepali doctors travelling for the CPSA use a Standard Visitor visa, since the trip is for an examination rather than paid work. This is a different visa entirely from the one needed later, once a job offer is confirmed: at that stage, most Nepali doctors move onto the Health and Care Worker visa, a reduced-fee, sponsored route with no Immigration Health Surcharge, specifically designed for NHS-facing roles.

Because CPSA travel adds a real cost layer beyond the exam fee itself, most advisers suggest budgeting separately for flights from Kathmandu, UK accommodation for several days, and, ideally, a short CPSA-focused preparation course taken either online beforehand or in person just before your sitting. Treat this as part of your overall UKMLA budget rather than an afterthought, and check current UK Visas and Immigration guidance for Nepali nationals before booking travel, since visa requirements and processing times are reviewed periodically.

UKMLA vs NMLE: a genuine decision for Nepali graduates

A significant share of Nepali medical graduates weigh the UKMLA route against Nepal's own domestic licensing exam, generally referred to as the Nepal Medical Licensing Examination or NMLE, though it is also commonly called the NMCLE (Nepal Medical Council Licensing Examination). This exam, run by the Nepal Medical Council, is the mandatory gateway to practising medicine inside Nepal: candidates must complete at least six months of their compulsory internship before sitting it, the exam is reportedly conducted as a computer-based test covering the full MBBS syllabus, and a pass, together with completion of the full one-year internship, leads to registration to practise in Nepal. This domestic exam plays a broadly similar role for Nepali graduates to the one NEET PG plays for Indian graduates deciding whether to pursue postgraduate training at home, which is why our UKMLA vs NEET PG comparison is a useful parallel read even though Nepal's own exam is technically a different assessment with a different purpose (licensing to practise, rather than postgraduate specialty entrance).

NMLE opens the door to practising medicine and building a career inside Nepal's public and private hospital systems; the UKMLA route (AKT and CPSA) opens the door to GMC registration and NHS employment in the UK. Neither is inherently "better": the right choice depends on whether a Nepali graduate wants to build a career inside Nepal, pursue further postgraduate training domestically, or move towards NHS employment, UK-based specialty training and eventual UK consultant or GP status. Some Nepali doctors complete both NMLE and the UKMLA route, either because they are undecided or because they want to keep options open on both sides while deciding.

It is also worth remembering that satisfying Nepal's own requirements to practise, meaning your MBBS, completed internship and Nepal Medical Council registration, exists independently of whichever route you eventually choose next. In other words, meeting Nepal's domestic licensing requirements is not itself the UKMLA route, but rather the foundation that both the UKMLA route and NMLE-based domestic practice are built on top of. The choice between staying in Nepal and pursuing the UK route is genuinely about your career direction, not about which country's basic licensing you must satisfy first.

Is a Nepali MBBS actually recognised for UK practice?

This is one of the most common anxieties among Nepali doctors considering the UKMLA route, and the honest answer has two parts. First, a Nepali MBBS from a college listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools is generally accepted by the GMC as a valid primary medical qualification, meaning the degree itself is not the obstacle. Second, holding an accepted degree does not by itself entitle you to practise in the UK: you must still pass the AKT and CPSA (or hold a recognised exemption), complete an accepted internship, provide English language evidence and pass EPIC/MyIntealth verification. The question "is my Nepali MBBS valid in the UK" and the question "can I practise in the UK yet" have different answers, and conflating them is a common source of confusion for candidates from Nepal just as it is for candidates from India, Bangladesh and Sri Lanka.

What happens after you register: NHS jobs and career progression

Once you hold GMC registration with a licence to practise, you are legally able to apply for NHS posts, but registration itself is not a job offer, and Nepali doctors sometimes underestimate how much separate work the job search stage requires. Realistically, most Nepali IMGs move into their first NHS role either through direct trust-level applications, through NHS-facing recruitment campaigns aimed specifically at overseas doctors, or, in some cases, via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive post over the following years.

Salary expectations matter here too. Foundation-level and equivalent junior doctor posts sponsored under the Health and Care Worker visa typically start in the region of £32,000 per year, rising considerably as you move into specialty training and, eventually, consultant or GP-partner level roles, though exact figures depend on grade, specialty, location and whether the contract is a training or non-training post. Not every Nepali IMG follows the same entry point into NHS work: some pursue routes connected to the Foundation Programme, while others move straight into a suitable non-training NHS role, often described as a "trust grade" or "clinical fellow" post, and gain UK clinical experience before applying into formal specialty training later.

Common mistakes Nepali applicants make on this route

Several avoidable mistakes recur often enough among Nepali applicants that they are worth naming directly.

  • Confusing Nepal's NMC with India's NMC. Because both regulators share an abbreviation, some Nepali candidates assume documents, guidance or registration numbers from one apply to the other. They do not: always specify "Nepal Medical Council" in full on any GMC-facing form to avoid ambiguity.
  • Leaving EPIC/MyIntealth verification until after the AKT. Because your medical college's response time is unpredictable, delaying this step is one of the most common causes of a stalled application.
  • Assuming a Pearson VUE centre in Nepal will always be available. Test centre availability changes, so confirm directly with Pearson VUE before committing to a study timeline built around a specific location.
  • Booking a single UK trip without buffer time. Flight delays, illness or an unexpected CPSA resit can all disrupt a tightly scheduled Manchester visit, so build in contingency time and, where possible, flexible tickets.
  • Treating NMLE and the UKMLA route as interchangeable preparation. Although both test broad medical knowledge, the content emphasis, format and purpose differ enough that revision for one does not fully substitute for the other.
  • Underestimating total cost. Focusing only on the headline AKT and CPSA fees while forgetting English tests, EPIC/MyIntealth charges, flights, UK accommodation and visa fees leads to significant budget shortfalls partway through the process.
  • Applying for the wrong registration category. Submitting a full registration application before your Nepal Medical Council internship confirmation is genuinely complete can cause unnecessary rejection or delay.

Beyond the official GMC pages, Nepali doctors preparing for this route commonly draw on a mix of official and community resources. The GMC's Medical Licensing Assessment section remains the definitive source for anything related to eligibility, content and fees, and should be checked before relying on any secondary source, including this guide, for a figure that affects a real financial or career decision. Frequently asked questions

Do Nepali medical graduates have to take the UKMLA?

Yes. Doctors who qualified outside the UK, including Nepali MBBS graduates, must pass the two GMC-recognised assessments under the Medical Licensing Assessment framework, commonly called the AKT and the CPSA, before registering with the GMC and practising in the UK, unless they already hold a recognised exemption such as specialist or GP registration.

What is the UKMLA fee for Nepali doctors in 2026?

From 1 April 2026, the GMC charges £283 for the AKT and £1,036 for the CPSA, plus a registration application fee of £26 for provisional registration or £481 for full registration. Total exam and registration costs typically fall between roughly £1,345 and £1,800, excluding English test, travel and visa costs, so always confirm the current figures on gmc-uk.org.

Is the Nepal Medical Council the same organisation as India's NMC?

No, and this is a common source of confusion. Nepal's Nepal Medical Council (also abbreviated NMC) is Nepal's own statutory body established in 1964 that registers doctors and accredits medical colleges in Nepal. India's National Medical Commission, also often shortened to NMC, is a completely separate regulatory body for India. The two organisations share an abbreviation by coincidence, not by relationship, and neither has authority over the other's doctors.

Can Nepali doctors sit the AKT in Nepal?

This depends on current Pearson VUE test centre availability, which changes over time, so candidates should check the live Pearson VUE centre locator before assuming a Nepal-based sitting is available. Many Nepali candidates end up sitting the AKT at a centre in a neighbouring country such as India if no Nepal centre is listed at the time of booking. The CPSA, in contrast, is currently held only at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester, so a UK trip is unavoidable for that stage.

What is EPIC verification and do Nepali doctors need it?

EPIC, now delivered through the MyIntealth platform, is the primary source verification service run by ECFMG/Intealth that confirms your Nepali medical degree is genuine by checking directly with your medical college. GMC registration cannot proceed without a valid EPIC/MyIntealth verification report, so Nepali doctors should start this process early, ideally months before their planned AKT booking.

What English language test do Nepali doctors need for the UKMLA route?

Most Nepali doctors sit IELTS Academic, needing an overall score of 7.5 with at least 7.0 in each of the four components, or OET Medicine with at least grade B in every section. A small number of candidates may qualify for an exemption if their medical education was conducted and assessed substantially in English, though this is assessed case by case by the GMC.

Is a Nepali MBBS accepted by the GMC?

A Nepali MBBS from a medical college listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools is generally accepted as a valid primary medical qualification, since the GMC checks this listing as part of primary source verification. Acceptance of the degree itself is separate from having passed the UKMLA route and completed the required internship and registration with Nepal's own Nepal Medical Council.

How long does the whole UKMLA route take for a Nepali doctor?

Most Nepali candidates take roughly twelve to eighteen months from starting AKT preparation to receiving full GMC registration, once English test scores, EPIC/MyIntealth verification, AKT, CPSA, visa processing and the internship requirement are all accounted for. Well-organised candidates can sometimes move faster, while others take longer if a resit or a slow verification response is involved.

Do Nepali doctors need a UK visa to sit the CPSA?

Nepali nationals normally need a Standard Visitor visa to travel to the UK for the CPSA sitting in Manchester, since it is a short assessment visit rather than paid employment. A different visa, the Health and Care Worker visa, becomes relevant only later, once a Nepali doctor has a confirmed NHS job offer.

What is the difference between the UKMLA route and Nepal's own licensing exam?

Nepal's own licensing exam, generally referred to as the Nepal Medical Licensing Examination or NMLE (also called the NMCLE), is a domestic requirement set by the Nepal Medical Council that every Nepali MBBS graduate must pass to practise inside Nepal. The UKMLA route, meaning the AKT and CPSA, is a separate, UK-facing pathway to GMC registration and NHS employment. The two exams test different content, serve different countries, and are not interchangeable, though some Nepali doctors complete both to keep options open.

Can Nepali doctors get a discount on GMC fees?

The GMC offers a 50% discount on the annual retention fee, and in some cases the full registration fee, for doctors whose worldwide income falls below the GMC's income discount threshold, which rises to £38,500 from April 2026. Nepali doctors who are not yet earning at UK levels when they apply may be eligible, so it is worth checking the current criteria directly on gmc-uk.org.

What happens after a Nepali doctor passes the UKMLA route and registers with the GMC?

Once registered, most Nepali IMGs move into NHS employment, either directly into a suitable post or via routes connected to the Foundation Programme, before progressing into specialty training or a substantive NHS role and eventually applying for specialist or GP registration if they wish to practise independently in a defined specialty.
