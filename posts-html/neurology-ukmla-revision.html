Neurology UKMLA questions reward pattern recognition and a systematic approach to clinical presentation. Representing approximately 6–9% of AKT items, neurology spans acute emergencies such as stroke and status epilepticus through to chronic disease management in epilepsy, Parkinson's disease, and dementia. The NICE NG128 stroke guideline, NICE NG217 epilepsy guideline (updated January 2025), and the DVLA guidance on neurological conditions are particularly high-yield. This guide covers every major neurology UKMLA topic with comparison tables, management algorithms, and FAQs to consolidate your revision.

Stroke and TIA: Neurology UKMLA Core Knowledge

Stroke is a medical emergency. Neurology UKMLA questions on stroke test your ability to use assessment tools, understand the thrombolysis pathway, and initiate correct secondary prevention. FAST (Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 999) is the public-facing tool; ROSIER (Recognition of Stroke in the Emergency Room) is the validated clinical tool used in A&E, scoring for asymmetric facial/arm/leg weakness, speech disturbance, and visual field defect, with loss of consciousness and seizure activity subtracting one point each.

TIA vs Minor Stroke

TIA: focal neurological deficit lasting <24 hours with no infarct on diffusion-weighted MRI. Minor stroke: deficit resolves or nearly resolves but DWI shows infarct. Both require urgent assessment. ABCD2 score: Age ≥60 (1), BP ≥140/90 (1), Clinical features — unilateral weakness (2) or speech disturbance without weakness (1), Duration — 60+ minutes (2) or 10–59 minutes (1), Diabetes (1). ABCD2 ≥4 = high risk — specialist assessment within 24 hours. All TIA patients should receive dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin 300 mg + clopidogrel 300 mg loading) immediately if no haemorrhage and no indication for anticoagulation, continued for 21 days then clopidogrel alone. Carotid imaging (carotid Doppler/CTA) within 24 hours if anterior circulation TIA — carotid endarterectomy if symptomatic stenosis 50–99%.

Haemorrhagic Stroke

Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH): sudden-onset thunderclap headache, "worst headache of my life," reaching maximum intensity within seconds. Causes: ruptured berry aneurysm (80%), AVM, trauma. CT head (non-contrast): hyperdensity in basal cisterns. CT may miss 2–5% of SAH within 6 hours — LP at 12 hours to detect xanthochromia (yellow discolouration of CSF from haemoglobin degradation). Management: neurosurgical referral, nimodipine (calcium channel blocker — reduces vasospasm), neurovascular imaging (CTA or catheter angiography), aneurysm coiling (preferred) or clipping. Intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH): lenticulostriate vessel rupture — hypertension is the most common cause. Management: reverse anticoagulation (PCC for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors), BP control (target <140 mmHg within 1 hour), neurosurgical input for posterior fossa haemorrhage or hydrocephalus.

Status Epilepticus Management

Convulsive status epilepticus (CSE): seizure lasting >5 minutes, or two seizures without recovery of consciousness. Management by time: 0–5 minutes: position, ABC, recovery position, O2, IV/IO access, glucose check, BM; 5–10 minutes: IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) — repeat once if no IV access use buccal midazolam 10 mg or rectal diazepam 10 mg; 10–20 minutes: if seizure continues — IV levetiracetam 60 mg/kg (max 4500 mg) over 10 minutes, OR IV valproate 40 mg/kg over 15 minutes, OR IV phenytoin 20 mg/kg over 20 minutes (monitor ECG); 20–30 minutes: if refractory — anaesthetic referral, RSI with propofol or thiopental, transfer to ITU for continuous EEG monitoring.

Driving Rules After Seizure (DVLA)

First unprovoked seizure: must not drive for 6 months (Group 1 — car/motorcycle). Group 2 (HGV/PSV): 5 years seizure-free. Epilepsy (two or more seizures): must be seizure-free for 12 months before driving (Group 1); or 10 years seizure-free and off AEDs for Group 2. Provoked seizure (clear structural cause identified and treated, or metabolic): DVLA may allow earlier return to driving — seek DVLA advice. Seizure only during sleep (sleep seizures for 1 year before driving Group 1 if pattern established). Patients have a legal duty to notify DVLA; doctors have a duty to advise patients — and may breach confidentiality to inform DVLA if patient refuses and continues to drive.

Headache: Red Flags, Migraine, and Cluster

Red Flags: SNOOP Criteria

Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, malignancy); Neurological symptoms or signs; Onset sudden (thunderclap); Older age (>50, new headache); Previous headache history change (pattern change); Postural component (worse on lying down — raised ICP); Precipitated by Valsalva (cough, sneeze — suggests posterior fossa lesion). Multiple Sclerosis, MND, and Dementia

Multiple Sclerosis

MS is a chronic demyelinating disease of the CNS presenting in young adults (peak 20–40), more common in women and in those living further from the equator. Relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS) is the most common subtype. Clinical presentations: optic neuritis (unilateral visual loss, pain on eye movement, relative afferent pupillary defect), internuclear ophthalmoplegia (failure of adduction ipsilateral eye, nystagmus in abducting eye — MLF lesion), Lhermitte's phenomenon (electric shock down spine on neck flexion), trigeminal neuralgia, spastic paraparesis, bladder dysfunction, Uhthoff's phenomenon (worsening with heat). McDonald criteria 2017: evidence of demyelination disseminated in space and time — MRI showing T2 lesions in at least 2 of 4 CNS regions (periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord). CSF: oligoclonal bands in >90% (IgG bands in CSF not in serum). Evoked potentials: delayed VEP in optic neuritis. Acute relapse: IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3–5 days. Disease-modifying therapy (DMTs): interferon beta, glatiramer (first-line); natalizumab, ocrelizumab, alemtuzumab (high-efficacy).

Delirium vs Dementia

Delirium (acute confusional state): acute onset (hours-days), fluctuating consciousness, inattention, disorganised thinking, perceptual disturbances (hallucinations), reversible cause. Dementia: chronic, insidious onset (months-years), consciousness initially preserved, progressive, not fully reversible. Delirium can be superimposed on dementia (most common scenario in elderly inpatients). 4AT score screens for delirium (alertness, cognition [AMT4], attention [months backwards], acute onset/fluctuating). Causes of delirium: PINCH ME — Pain, Infection, Nutrition, Constipation, Hydration, Medication, Environment. Management: treat underlying cause, ensure orientation, avoid sedation (except extreme agitation — low-dose haloperidol), single room, familiar faces.

UMN vs LMN Facial Nerve Palsy

Bell's palsy (LMN VII palsy): all ipsilateral facial muscles weak including forehead (cannot raise eyebrow). Cause: idiopathic viral (HSV reactivation likely). Treatment: prednisolone 50 mg/day for 10 days within 72 hours of onset; eye care (lubricating drops, eye patch at night). UMN facial palsy (stroke, tumour): contralateral lower face weakness only — forehead is spared because it has bilateral cortical representation. Distinguishing forehead sparing (UMN) from forehead involvement (LMN/Bell's) is a classic UKMLA question. Other LMN VII causes: Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster of geniculate ganglion — ear pain, vesicles in ear canal, sensorineural hearing loss), parotid tumour, acoustic neuroma, sarcoidosis.

Spinal Cord Disease and Cranial Nerve Disorders

Spinal cord compression is a neurological emergency and a high-yield UKMLA topic, particularly in the context of metastatic malignancy. Presentation: back pain (the earliest and most common symptom), bilateral leg weakness (UMN signs), sensory level on the trunk, bladder and bowel dysfunction (urinary retention, constipation). Any patient with known cancer presenting with back pain should be treated as malignant spinal cord compression (MSCC) until proven otherwise. Management: immediate high-dose dexamethasone (16 mg IV stat); urgent MRI spine (whole spine); radiotherapy or surgical decompression depending on fitness and prognosis. Do not delay imaging — neurological outcome correlates directly with how quickly treatment is initiated.

Cauda equina syndrome: compression of the cauda equina nerve roots (below L1–L2) — most commonly by a central disc prolapse at L4/5 or L5/S1. Symptoms: bilateral leg pain/weakness, saddle anaesthesia (perianal, perineal, inner thigh), urinary retention (with overflow incontinence), faecal incontinence, loss of anal tone. This is a surgical emergency — immediate MRI spine and urgent neurosurgical referral. Any delay increases risk of permanent bladder and bowel dysfunction. Red flag features requiring same-day MRI: saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness, loss of bladder/bowel control, signs in two or more nerve root levels.

Cranial nerve palsies commonly tested: CN III (oculomotor) palsy — "down and out" eye, ptosis, dilated pupil; painful = posterior communicating artery aneurysm (surgical emergency); painless = diabetic mononeuropathy (pupil usually spared due to peripheral nerve supply to pupillary muscles). CN VI (abducens) palsy — failure of abduction, diplopia on lateral gaze; false localising sign in raised ICP. CN VII — Bell's palsy vs UMN (see neurology main body). CN VIII (vestibulocochlear) — acoustic neuroma presents with unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and vertigo; investigate with MRI internal auditory meati; manage with observation, stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife), or surgical resection. CN IX/X — glossopharyngeal and vagus nerve palsy: dysarthria, dysphagia, loss of gag reflex (bulbar palsy — LMN); pseudobulbar palsy (UMN) — dysarthria, dysphagia, brisk jaw jerk, emotional lability.

Neurological Emergencies: Raised Intracranial Pressure and Coning

Raised intracranial pressure (ICP) presents with headache (worse on waking, coughing, or bending forward — due to recumbency increasing venous pressure), nausea and vomiting, visual obscurations, papilloedema, and progressive conscious level decline. Cushing's triad (a late sign of severely raised ICP): hypertension, bradycardia, and abnormal respiratory pattern — indicates imminent coning (transtentorial herniation). Management principles: elevate head of bed to 30°, hyperventilate (reduces CO2, causes cerebral vasoconstriction, rapidly lowers ICP), IV mannitol 0.25–0.5 g/kg (osmotic diuretic), dexamethasone (for vasogenic oedema around tumours or abscesses, NOT for traumatic brain injury), neurosurgical referral. LP is absolutely contraindicated if raised ICP is suspected and there are focal neurological signs, altered consciousness, or papilloedema — risk of coning. Always obtain CT head first.

Temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis, GCA): affects patients over 50 (rarely under); unilateral throbbing temporal headache, jaw claudication (pathognomonic), scalp tenderness, temporal artery tenderness/absence of pulse, visual disturbance (amaurosis fugax — irreversible blindness if untreated). ESR typically above 50 mm/hr, CRP elevated. Diagnosis confirmed by temporal artery biopsy (skip lesions mean biopsy may be negative in 10–20%). Treatment: prednisolone 40–60 mg daily immediately (do NOT wait for biopsy — blindness can occur within hours); IV methylprednisolone 500 mg TDS if visual symptoms present. Taper steroids slowly over 1–2 years guided by ESR/CRP; tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor) is NICE approved for relapsing GCA to allow steroid sparing. Screen for osteoporosis and diabetes with long-term steroid use.

Neurological Assessment Tools and the CPSA

The CPSA tests practical neurological examination skills. The structured neurological assessment for the upper or lower limbs follows: inspection (wasting, fasciculations, tremor, involuntary movements), tone (spasticity, lead-pipe rigidity, cogwheel rigidity, flaccidity), power (MRC scale 0–5: 0=no movement, 1=flicker, 2=movement with gravity eliminated, 3=movement against gravity, 4=movement against resistance, 5=normal), reflexes (biceps C5/6, triceps C7, supinator C5/6, knee L3/4, ankle S1, plantar), sensation (light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception), and coordination (finger-nose test, heel-shin test, dysdiadochokinesis). A complete neurological examination also includes assessment of speech (dysarthria/dysphasia), cranial nerves, gait (broad-based=cerebellar; shuffling=Parkinsonian; steppage=foot drop; scissor=spastic diplegia), and Romberg test (positive=dorsal column or vestibular dysfunction).

Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) and Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) are brief cognitive screening tools. MMSE scores orientation, registration, attention/calculation, recall, and language (max 30 points; below 24 suggests cognitive impairment). MoCA is more sensitive for mild cognitive impairment (max 30; below 26 = impairment). The 4AT is a rapid delirium screening tool (max 12; score 4 or above = probable delirium). For the CPSA, candidates may be asked to administer or interpret abbreviated cognitive assessments — practise the AMT10 (Abbreviated Mental Test score) which tests 10 questions including date, year, age, address recall, recognition of two people, recall of date of World War I, and counting backwards from 20.

Frequently Asked Questions About Neurology in the UKMLA

What is the thrombolysis window for ischaemic stroke and what are the contraindications?

IV alteplase can be given within 4.5 hours of definite symptom onset (or last known well time). Absolute contraindications include haemorrhage on CT, BP >185/110 mmHg (must treat first), recent major surgery (<14 days), previous ICH, active bleeding, and platelet count <100 Ã— 10â¹/L. Relative contraindications include anticoagulation (INR >1.7 or DOAC use — specific reversal agent needed), recent MI (<3 months), and severe stroke (NIHSS >25 increases haemorrhage risk). Mechanical thrombectomy extends to 6–24 hours for large vessel occlusion if perfusion imaging shows viable penumbra (DAWN and DEFUSE-3 trial criteria).

How does valproate PREVENT programme affect prescribing?

Sodium valproate must not be used in women of childbearing potential (WOCBP) unless enrolled in the Valproate Pregnancy Prevention Programme (PREVENT). This requires annual review with a specialist, patient acknowledgment form, and two forms of effective contraception. The restrictions were strengthened by the MHRA following evidence of 10-fold increased neural tube defect risk and 30–40% risk of neurodevelopmental disorders in children exposed in utero. Topiramate received similar MHRA restrictions in 2024. For WOCBP with epilepsy, lamotrigine or levetiracetam are preferred alternatives. In the AKT, if a scenario describes a woman of childbearing potential being prescribed valproate without PREVENT, this is the incorrect management option.

What investigations are needed after a first seizure?

All patients with a first suspected seizure should be referred urgently to a specialist (ideally within 2 weeks). Investigations: EEG (may show epileptiform activity but a normal EEG does not exclude epilepsy); MRI brain (preferred over CT — detects structural causes including hippocampal sclerosis, cortical dysplasia, tumour); blood glucose (at time of seizure), Na, Ca, Mg, FBC, LFTs, U&Es (metabolic causes); ECG (cardiac syncope mimic); LP only if meningitis/encephalitis suspected. AED treatment is not routinely started after a single seizure — risk of recurrence must be weighed against medication side effects. Two unprovoked seizures or one seizure with high recurrence risk (structural lesion, epileptiform EEG) warrants AED initiation.

How do I differentiate Parkinson's disease from other Parkinsonian syndromes?

Idiopathic Parkinson's disease (IPD): asymmetric onset; resting tremor (pill-rolling, 4–6 Hz); bradykinesia; rigidity (cogwheel); postural instability late; excellent response to levodopa; non-motor features (anosmia, constipation, REM sleep behaviour disorder, depression). Red flags suggesting Parkinson's plus syndrome: symmetrical onset, early falls (PSP — progressive supranuclear palsy), early autonomic failure (MSA — multiple system atrophy), early dementia (DLB), poor or no levodopa response, cerebellar signs, eye movement abnormality (vertical gaze palsy in PSP). Drug-induced Parkinsonism: caused by dopamine antagonists (metoclopramide, haloperidol, prochlorperazine) — typically symmetrical, no tremor, responds to drug withdrawal. Levodopa + carbidopa (co-careldopa) or + benserazide (co-beneldopa) is first-line for IPD with functional disability.

What is Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome and how is it managed?

GBS is an acute demyelinating polyneuropathy typically triggered by infection (Campylobacter jejuni is most common; also CMV, EBV, Zika). Ascending flaccid paralysis with areflexia; paraesthesia; autonomic instability; respiratory failure in ~30% (monitor with spirometry — intubate if FVC <20 mL/kg or <15 mL/kg). Albuminocytological dissociation in CSF: raised protein (>0.45 g/L) with normal cell count. Nerve conduction studies show demyelination. Management: IVIg (0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days) or plasmapheresis (equally effective — not both). Supportive care: DVT prophylaxis, analgesia, cardiac monitoring, nutritional support. Steroids are NOT beneficial in GBS (unlike other inflammatory neuropathies). Recovery is over weeks to months; 80% make a good recovery.

Conclusion

Neurology UKMLA revision centres on mastering the acute management pathways (stroke thrombolysis, status epilepticus protocol), knowing the DVLA rules for epilepsy and stroke, applying the NICE NG217 epilepsy drug selection criteria, and recognising clinical patterns for dementia subtypes, MS, and peripheral neuropathies. The UMN vs LMN signs table and the LP findings table are essential clinical frameworks. Use the UKMLA syllabus to identify specific conditions listed and cross-reference with the preparation resources for neurology question practice.
