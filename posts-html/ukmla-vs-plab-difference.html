Understanding the UKMLA vs PLAB distinction is one of the most frequently asked questions from International Medical Graduates (IMGs) considering the UK medical registration pathway in 2025 and 2026. The short answer is that PLAB and the UKMLA are now fundamentally the same examination assessed against the same standard — but the terminology, history, and practical differences between the two pathways remain important for candidates to understand. Confusion about whether you are sitting a PLAB or a UKMLA exam, what the differences are in prerequisites and fees, and what the transition from the old PLAB system to the MLA framework means for your registration application is understandable given how quickly this landscape has changed since 2024.

This guide untangles the full picture: the history of PLAB, how and why the MLA framework was introduced, what changed in 2024, how PLAB and the UKMLA now relate to one another, what the change means for current and future IMG candidates, and whether there are any remaining practical differences between the two routes. We also address common misconceptions, including the belief that PLAB is being abolished, and provide a comprehensive comparison table covering more than ten dimensions of difference.

The History of PLAB: Before the UKMLA

The Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board (PLAB) test has been the primary route to GMC registration for International Medical Graduates since the 1970s. It was introduced to provide a standardised assessment of the clinical knowledge and professional skills of doctors trained outside the UK who wished to work in the NHS, at a time when the volume of IMG applications to the GMC was growing rapidly and the existing mutual recognition frameworks did not cover the majority of source countries.

For most of its history, PLAB consisted of two parts. PLAB 1 was a written multiple-choice examination of clinical knowledge, and PLAB 2 was an OSCE-style clinical assessment. Both were administered by the GMC, with PLAB 2 held at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester. PLAB was entirely separate from the assessments used by UK medical schools for their own graduation requirements — there was no formal content or standard alignment between PLAB and UK medical finals. This meant that the threshold for GMC registration was set by two different assessment systems that did not formally test the same things in the same way.

This inconsistency attracted increasing attention from medical education researchers, patient safety advocates, and professional bodies. The GMC's own analysis showed that the entry-level knowledge and skills tested by PLAB and UK medical school finals were not equivalently defined. A series of GMC consultations, Medical Schools Council discussions, and expert reviews during the 2010s and early 2020s led to the decision to create a single national medical licensing assessment — the Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA), popularly known as the UKMLA.

What Changed in 2024: The MLA Transition

The key transition date for the MLA framework is August 2024. From this point, all PLAB 1 examinations were aligned with the MLA Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) standard and assessed against the MLA Content Map. From the same date, PLAB 2 was formally aligned with the MLA Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) standard. This means that from August 2024 onward, IMGs who book and sit PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 are, in effect, sitting examinations that meet the UKMLA requirements — the MLA AKT and CPSA respectively.

For UK medical students, the transition happened through their medical schools: from the 2024–25 academic year onward, all UK medical school final assessments must incorporate the UKMLA AKT and CPSA as part of the graduation requirements. Medical schools that had been running pilot programmes since 2022 transitioned fully; those that had not been piloting joined the framework from 2024–25.

The significance of this change cannot be overstated: for the first time in the history of UK medical licensing, there is a single common standard — defined by the MLA Content Map — that applies equally to every route to GMC registration. Whether a doctor graduated from the University of Edinburgh, the University of Lagos, Bangalore Medical College, or Cairo University, they now meet the same licensing standard before being admitted to the UK medical register.

Who Sits the UKMLA?

The UKMLA in its native form — as an examination organised and administered by the medical school in partnership with the GMC and Medical Schools Council — is taken by UK medical students in their final year. All students enrolled in a GMC-approved UK undergraduate medical programme from the 2024–25 academic year onward must pass the UKMLA as a condition of graduation and as a prerequisite for provisional GMC registration. The assessment is coordinated by the student's institution; no separate application or booking is required by individual students beyond responding to their school's invitation.

UK medical graduates typically receive provisional registration upon successful graduation and UKMLA completion, proceed into the Foundation Programme (a two-year structured training programme), and receive full registration upon satisfactory completion of Foundation Year 1. This is the standard UK doctor training pathway and has not changed significantly in its broad structure with the introduction of the UKMLA — only the assessment component has been standardised.

Who Sits PLAB?

IMGs who wish to practise medicine in the UK must sit the PLAB route — which, as of August 2024, is assessed against the same MLA standard as the UKMLA. Any doctor who holds a primary medical qualification from outside the UK and who is not eligible for registration via a specialist route (such as having a recognised specialty qualification) must sit PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 to meet the knowledge and skills requirements for GMC registration.

The PLAB pathway is open to doctors from any country. There are no nationality or language-of-training restrictions. However, candidates must meet the GMC's eligibility requirements, which include: holding an acceptable primary medical qualification; demonstrating English language proficiency (IELTS Academic with an overall score of 7.5 and no component below 7.0, or OET with a minimum Grade B in all four sub-tests); and completing a recognised internship of at least twelve months following graduation from their primary medical programme. Only once these prerequisites are confirmed can a candidate book PLAB 1 and begin the formal examination pathway.

Is PLAB Being Abolished? The Future of the Pathway

No. Despite widespread speculation in IMG communities, PLAB is not being abolished. The PLAB examination continues to exist as the delivery mechanism through which IMGs access the MLA standard. What has changed is that the content of PLAB — the questions asked in PLAB 1 and the stations used in PLAB 2 — must now meet the MLA Content Map standard rather than the pre-2024 PLAB content framework. The name PLAB remains; the standard has been updated to align with the UKMLA.

The GMC has been clear on this point. In their official communications, they describe PLAB as the pathway by which IMGs meet the MLA requirements: PLAB 1 is the mechanism by which IMGs meet the AKT requirement; PLAB 2 is the mechanism by which they meet the CPSA requirement. For practical purposes, an IMG who passes PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 under the current framework has met the UKMLA standard. The examination brand persists, but it now points to the same destination as the UKMLA taken by UK graduates.

Which Route Is Harder? A Fair Assessment

A common question among candidates is whether the UKMLA route (for UK students) or the PLAB route (for IMGs) is harder. The honest answer is: neither is inherently harder in terms of the standard set — both are assessed against the same MLA Content Map at the same Foundation Year 1 level. The differences in observed pass rates (higher for UK students) reflect preparation context rather than assessment standard.

UK medical students have significant structural advantages: a five-year curriculum explicitly designed to prepare them for the UKMLA, institutional support including mock exams, faculty feedback, and OSCE practice facilities, familiarity with UK clinical guidelines and the NHS system, and the social and emotional support of their peer cohort. IMGs must self-direct their preparation, often from outside the UK, without access to the same institutional infrastructure, sometimes while working in clinical roles that leave limited time for dedicated study, and in some cases without recent exposure to the UK clinical context that the exam reflects.

These are preparation context differences, not assessment standard differences. An IMG who prepares thoroughly, using high-quality MLA-aligned resources, systematically studying the MLA Content Map and UK clinical guidelines, and practising OSCE skills regularly, can and does pass PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 at rates comparable to UK graduates. The gap in first-attempt pass rates is not inevitable — it reflects the average difference in preparation conditions, not any inherent difference in difficulty.

Transition Rules for Candidates Mid-Pathway

A common concern among IMGs who began their PLAB journey before August 2024 is what the transition means for their existing results. The GMC has handled this clearly: PLAB 1 results obtained before August 2024 retain their five-year validity and are recognised as meeting the MLA AKT requirement. PLAB 2 results obtained before August 2024 similarly retain their validity. Candidates who passed PLAB 1 under the old framework and are booking PLAB 2 after August 2024 will sit a PLAB 2 that is MLA CPSA-aligned, but their valid PLAB 1 result is still accepted.

Candidates who passed only one component before August 2024 do not need to re-sit that component — provided their result is within the five-year validity period. The GMC has not imposed any requirement to retake the newly aligned version of a component you have already passed. Always verify current guidance directly with the GMC if you are in this position, as policies may be updated.

Alternative Routes to GMC Registration

PLAB / UKMLA is not the only route to GMC registration for IMGs. The GMC also offers specialist registration pathways for doctors who already hold a recognised post-graduate specialty qualification. Doctors with MRCP(UK), MRCS, MRCGP, or equivalent qualifications from approved international bodies may be eligible for specialist or GP registration without taking PLAB. These routes are assessed by the GMC on a case-by-case basis and may require a Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration (CESR) application. For most IMGs who are earlier in their careers and do not yet hold a recognised specialty qualification, the PLAB route remains the most straightforward pathway to initial GMC registration.

Common Misconceptions About UKMLA vs PLAB

Several widespread misconceptions among candidates deserve direct clarification:

  • Misconception: PLAB is easier than UKMLA. False. Both are assessed against the identical MLA Content Map at the identical Foundation Year 1 standard. Observed pass rate differences reflect preparation context, not assessment difficulty.
  • Misconception: You can choose to sit the UKMLA instead of PLAB as an IMG. In practice, IMGs sit the PLAB route, which from August 2024 onward meets the UKMLA standard. There is no separate UKMLA booking system open to IMGs distinct from PLAB booking.
  • Misconception: PLAB results obtained before 2024 are no longer valid. False. Pre-August 2024 PLAB results retain their five-year validity and are recognised within the MLA framework.
  • Misconception: The UKMLA is a new exam entirely separate from PLAB. False. The UKMLA is a framework and standard; PLAB is the delivery mechanism for IMGs within that framework. They are not two separate competing examinations.
  • Misconception: UK medical students are exempt from the UKMLA. False. UK medical students must pass the UKMLA as a graduation requirement — they are not exempt. The difference is that their medical school coordinates and funds the assessment rather than students booking and paying individually.

Frequently Asked Questions About UKMLA vs PLAB

If I pass PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 in 2026, have I met the UKMLA requirement?

Yes. From August 2024 onwards, passing PLAB 1 meets the MLA AKT requirement and passing PLAB 2 meets the MLA CPSA requirement. These together satisfy the UKMLA's two-component licensing requirement for IMGs. You do not need to sit any additional exam labelled UKMLA — PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 in their current aligned forms are the UKMLA for IMGs. Your GMC registration application will confirm that you have met the MLA requirements through the PLAB route.

Can I switch from the PLAB route to the UKMLA route mid-journey?

In practice, there is no meaningful route-switching decision for IMGs, because the PLAB route is the UKMLA for IMGs. IMGs do not have access to the UK medical school UKMLA delivery system (coordinated by the Medical Schools Council for enrolled students). The only pathway available to IMGs is PLAB 1 (AKT) and PLAB 2 (CPSA) through the GMC booking system, which from August 2024 meets the MLA standard. There is no separate UKMLA for IMGs to switch to.

Will the GMC eventually drop the PLAB name entirely and call everything UKMLA?

This is possible in the long term, but there has been no formal announcement from the GMC about a timeline for retiring the PLAB name. As of 2026, the PLAB branding remains active in all GMC booking portals, communications, and official documents. The GMC has stated that PLAB meets the MLA requirements — the two exist in parallel terminology rather than one replacing the other at a branding level. Candidates should monitor GMC communications for any future changes to naming or structure.

Does the UKMLA / PLAB change affect doctors already registered with the GMC?

No. Doctors who are already fully registered with the GMC are not required to pass the UKMLA retrospectively. The UKMLA applies only to those seeking initial GMC registration. Existing registrants maintain their registration through the annual retention fee and continuing professional development requirements, not through re-sitting the licensing assessment. There is no grandfathering cliff or retrospective requirement for established GMC registrants.

What English language level do I need for PLAB?

IMGs sitting PLAB must demonstrate English language proficiency through either IELTS Academic (overall score of 7.5 with no individual component below 7.0 in Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking) or OET (Occupational English Test with a minimum Grade B in all four sub-tests). IELTS and OET results are valid for two years from the test date. English language proficiency must be demonstrated before a GMC Online account can be activated and a PLAB booking can proceed. UK medical students are not required to separately demonstrate English language proficiency as this is embedded in their degree programme.

Conclusion

The UKMLA vs PLAB question resolves, ultimately, to this: they are the same standard, delivered through different systems for different candidate groups. For UK medical students, the UKMLA is embedded in the final year of a GMC-approved degree, funded by the medical school, and coordinated by the institution. For IMGs, the same standard is accessed through the PLAB route — a self-funded, self-organised process that involves booking and sitting PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 through GMC Online. The content, the pass mark methodology, and the licensing outcome are identical across both routes.

This unification is the most significant development in UK medical licensing in a generation. It creates genuine equity of standard at the point of entry to the UK medical workforce, giving NHS employers, patients, and the wider public confidence that every newly registered doctor — wherever they trained — has demonstrated the same core level of safe practice. For IMGs navigating this pathway, the message is clear: prepare rigorously against the MLA Content Map, understand UK clinical guidelines, and invest in the OSCE preparation that the CPSA demands. The same standard that UK graduates meet through five years of structured training is achievable through disciplined, well-targeted self-directed preparation.
