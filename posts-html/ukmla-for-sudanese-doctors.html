The UKMLA for Sudanese doctors works exactly the same way it does for other international medical graduates: you need a recognised primary medical qualification (usually an MBBS from a Sudanese medical school), verification of that qualification through EPIC/MyIntealth, proof of English proficiency, and a pass in both parts of the UK Medical Licensing Assessment — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — before the General Medical Council (GMC) will grant registration. For Sudanese doctors, this route is achievable, but it comes with an extra layer of practical complexity since April 2023, when conflict in Sudan disrupted parts of the country's medical education and regulatory infrastructure. This guide walks through eligibility, current fees, the step-by-step process, and — importantly — what displaced or diaspora Sudanese doctors can do if their documents or medical school access have been affected.

None of this is designed to minimise what has happened in Sudan since 2023. It is written to be practically useful: many Sudanese doctors, including those who have been displaced within Sudan or abroad, are actively working through this exact process right now, and the GMC has specific support in place for doctors in these circumstances.

Key takeaways

  • Sudanese doctors with a recognised MBBS can sit the UKMLA and register with the GMC through the same route as other international medical graduates (IMGs); there is no separate "Sudan-specific" exam.
  • Current GMC fees (2026 cycle): AKT £283, CPSA £1,036, provisional registration application £26, full registration application £481, annual retention £481 (with licence) or £172 (without) — always confirm on gmc-uk.org before paying.
  • The Sudan Medical Council regulates registration and internship completion in Sudan; its operations were disrupted from mid-2023 but the Council has been resuming administrative functions, including a July 2026 announcement of an accelerated return to processing registrations and certificates.
  • EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification of your medical degree is required regardless of nationality, but Sudanese graduates should expect this may take longer if their medical school's administrative capacity has been affected.
  • Displaced and diaspora Sudanese doctors should look closely at the GMC's dedicated support for refugee and displaced doctors, which can help with fee waivers, staged payments and case-by-case document evidence where standard paperwork is hard to obtain.
  • Realistic total timeline from starting verification to full registration is roughly 9 to 18 months, and can run longer if documentation needs to be reconstructed.

Understanding the UKMLA and why it matters for Sudanese doctors

The UKMLA is the single licensing assessment that both UK medical students and international medical graduates must pass to be granted registration with a licence to practise by the GMC. It replaced the old PLAB test for IMGs from 2024 onward as the standard route into UK medical practice. For Sudanese doctors, passing the UKMLA is the exam-based half of the process; the other half is proving your qualification is genuine and that you meet the GMC's registration standards.

The UKMLA has two parts. The AKT is a computer-based knowledge test covering clinical scenarios similar to a UK final-year medical student's curriculum. The CPSA is a practical, circuit-based assessment of clinical and communication skills, delivered at GMC-approved test centres. Both must be passed, usually within a defined validity window of each other, before you can move on to applying for full registration.

This route sits alongside similar country-specific pathways already documented on this site, including UKMLA for Egyptian doctors, UKMLA for Jordanian and Iraqi doctors, and UKMLA for Gulf doctors. If you want the full picture of how different nationalities approach the same exam and registration system, the UKMLA for international medical graduates: country routes hub is a useful starting point, as is the neighbouring-region guide UKMLA for Ethiopian doctors.

Sudan's medical degree and regulatory landscape

Sudan trains doctors through a five- to six-year MBBS programme delivered by medical schools including the University of Khartoum Faculty of Medicine (the country's oldest medical school), the University of Gezira, and several other public and private institutions. On graduation, doctors historically completed a period of provisional registration and supervised internship (commonly a year of hospital-based houseman training plus a further period of service), followed by a licensing examination administered by the Sudan Medical Council before being granted full registration to practise independently in Sudan.

The Sudan Medical Council is the professional regulatory body responsible for registering doctors, dentists and pharmacists, confirming completion of internship training, issuing certificates of good standing, and running the national licensing examination for interns. This is broadly analogous to the GMC's role in the UK, though the two bodies operate independently and a Sudan Medical Council registration does not itself grant UK practising rights — it is, however, useful supporting evidence in a GMC application.

How the conflict since April 2023 has affected medical education and registration

Since fighting broke out in April 2023, Sudan's medical education and health system have been under severe strain. Independent academic research published in 2024 documented that a large majority of medical students were displaced from Khartoum, with many relocating either within Sudan or abroad, and that a substantial number of medical school buildings in Khartoum, Darfur and Kordofan were damaged, looted, or repurposed during the fighting. Separate reporting has described widespread disruption to teaching, examinations, and hospital-based training across the country.

The Sudan Medical Council's own administrative functions were also affected. According to public reporting, the Council paused confirming completion of some students' training from August 2023, which delayed graduates being able to formally begin their internship year, and the licensing examination for interns was itself suspended for a period before resuming in mid-2024 with support from diaspora medical associations. More recently, in mid-2026, Sudan's transitional government publicly announced steps to accelerate the Council's return to full operation, including faster processing of no-objection certificates for interns, permanent registration, specialist registration, and certificates of good conduct, alongside plans to expand online services.

The practical takeaway: if you graduated or were mid-training during this period, your registration or internship paperwork may be delayed, incomplete, or harder to obtain than it would have been before 2023. This is a genuine, documented disruption — not something to be embarrassed about when discussing your situation with the GMC or with EPIC/MyIntealth. The sections below cover exactly how to handle this.

Eligibility: what Sudanese doctors need for the UKMLA route

Eligibility for Sudanese doctors follows the GMC's general international medical graduate criteria. You will generally need each of the following.

1. A recognised primary medical qualification

An MBBS (or equivalent) from a Sudanese medical school that the GMC recognises as an acceptable primary medical qualification. Most established Sudanese medical schools, including the University of Khartoum and the University of Gezira, are listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, which the GMC references when assessing qualifications. Always check your specific medical school's current listing status, since directory entries and accreditation notes can change.

2. Sudan Medical Council registration (provisional or full)

Provisional or full registration with the Sudan Medical Council is not always a strict precondition for GMC registration, but it is commonly requested as supporting evidence of your standing as a doctor, and a certificate of good standing from the Council is frequently part of a complete application. If your registration certificate was lost, destroyed, or delayed due to the conflict, see the dedicated section below on alternative pathways.

3. Completion of internship (or evidence of your training stage)

The GMC's requirements focus on your primary medical qualification and its verification rather than mandating a specific internship structure identical to the UK's. That said, evidence of supervised clinical experience — internship certificates, hospital references, or Sudan Medical Council confirmation — strengthens your file and may be requested. If your internship was interrupted or delayed by the war, gather whatever documentation exists (hospital letters, partial completion certificates, supervisor references) and be transparent with the GMC about the circumstances.

4. EPIC/MyIntealth primary source verification

All international medical graduates, including Sudanese doctors, must have their primary medical qualification independently verified through EPIC, now delivered via the MyIntealth platform operated by Intealth (formerly ECFMG). This process confirms directly with your medical school that your degree is genuine. For a full walkthrough of how this system works, see the sibling explainer GMC EPIC verification process explained. Sudanese applicants should expect verification may take longer than average if their medical school's records office has reduced capacity, and should start this step as early as possible in their planning.

5. English language proficiency

Unless exempt, you will need to demonstrate English proficiency through IELTS Academic (usually requiring an overall band of 7.5 with no component below 7.0) or the Occupational English Test (OET), typically at Grade B across all four components. Test fees generally run around £180 to £230 depending on the test and centre — confirm current pricing directly with the test provider, since these are set independently of the GMC.

6. Passing the UKMLA (AKT and CPSA)

Both components must be passed, with the CPSA typically taken after a pass in the AKT, within the GMC's specified validity window between the two.

7. A complete, verifiable documents checklist

  • Passport and identity documents
  • Primary medical qualification certificate (MBBS)
  • Full academic transcript
  • Sudan Medical Council registration certificate or certificate of good standing, where obtainable
  • Internship or training completion evidence
  • English test certificate (IELTS/OET)
  • Evidence of any UK immigration status, if applicable (refugee status, visa, leave to remain)
  • Any additional supporting references from supervisors or employers

For the GMC's own general list of registration requirements applicable to all overseas-qualified doctors, see the related explainer GMC registration requirements for international doctors, and cross-check current criteria directly on gmc-uk.org.

Options for displaced and diaspora Sudanese doctors

A significant number of Sudanese doctors and medical students have been displaced since April 2023 — some within Sudan, many to neighbouring countries such as Egypt and Saudi Arabia, and others further afield. If this describes your situation, the standard document checklist above may be harder to complete in full, but it does not close off the UKMLA route. Here is what to actually do.

If your Sudan Medical Council paperwork is missing or delayed

Contact the Sudan Medical Council directly to request confirmation of your registration status or a replacement certificate. Given the Council's own disruption since 2023 and its ongoing recovery — including the mid-2026 government announcement of accelerated processing for registrations and certificates — response times may be longer than usual, so apply as early as possible and keep a clear record of every request you make. If the Council cannot immediately confirm your records, gather whatever secondary evidence you can: internship supervisor letters, hospital employment records, payslips, or diaspora medical association confirmations, and present these to the GMC alongside an honest explanation of the gap.

If you cannot easily reach your medical school for EPIC/MyIntealth verification

Verification depends on your medical school's records office being able to confirm your qualification. If your medical school's administration has been disrupted, relocated, or is operating with reduced capacity, contact MyIntealth support directly and explain the circumstances — processing timelines can sometimes be adjusted when an institution is known to be affected by conflict. It is worth starting this step months before you plan to apply for registration, precisely because delays are more likely.

The GMC's support for refugee and displaced doctors

If you have been granted refugee status, asylum, or another relevant UK immigration status, the GMC's dedicated assistance programme for refugee and displaced doctors is directly relevant to you. This programme can include waivers or reductions on certain fees (such as the international qualification assessment fee), staged payment plans for registration costs, and a named point of contact who understands the practical difficulties of assembling documents from a country in crisis. The site has a full explainer on this at UKMLA for refugee doctors: the GMC assistance programme and fee support — if any part of your situation involves displacement, asylum, or refugee status, read that guide alongside this one, since eligibility criteria and the application process for fee support are detailed there.

You can also contact the GMC's refugee doctor support team directly through the contact details published on gmc-uk.org to ask about your specific circumstances before you commit to fees or test bookings.

If you are studying or working outside Sudan already

Many displaced Sudanese doctors are currently working, studying, or completing internship-equivalent training in host countries such as Egypt or Saudi Arabia. This does not disqualify you from the UKMLA route. Your eligibility still rests on your original Sudanese primary medical qualification and its verification, plus whatever additional supervised clinical experience you can evidence from your time abroad. Keep detailed records of any clinical work undertaken outside Sudan, as this can strengthen your overall application even though it is not itself part of the GMC's core verification requirement.

If your documents or medical school access have been disrupted by the conflict, start the EPIC/MyIntealth process and any GMC enquiry as early as possible. Delays compound: a verification request that might take a few weeks in ordinary circumstances can take considerably longer when an institution has reduced capacity, so early action is the single most useful thing you can do.

Understanding the AKT: format and preparation

The AKT is a computer-based multiple-choice exam testing applied clinical knowledge across the breadth of the UK's Medical Licensing Assessment content map, covering presentations, conditions, and clinical management as they would be encountered by a newly qualified UK doctor. Sudanese medical curricula cover much of the same core clinical science and medicine, but the AKT's UK-specific clinical guidelines, NHS processes, and question style (single-best-answer, scenario-based) usually require dedicated preparation rather than relying on undergraduate revision alone.

Practical AKT preparation tips for Sudanese candidates

  • Work through the GMC's published UKMLA content map systematically rather than revising by specialty alone, since the AKT tests presentations and conditions across the whole map.
  • Use UK-specific guidelines (NICE, BNF drug doses) rather than Sudanese or regional protocols where prescribing or management questions are concerned, since the exam is written to UK clinical practice standards.
  • Practise timed question banks under exam conditions — question banks referenced across the wider IMG community can help you get used to the single-best-answer format.
  • Join or form a study group with other Sudanese or East African diaspora candidates preparing for the same sitting; shared revision reduces isolation, especially if you are studying while displaced or relocated.
  • Budget extra revision time if your undergraduate training was interrupted or compressed by the conflict — be honest with yourself about knowledge gaps rather than assuming continuity that may not exist.

Understanding the CPSA: format, travel and visas

The CPSA is the practical, skills-based half of the UKMLA. It assesses clinical examination technique, history-taking, communication, and professional skills across a circuit of stations at a GMC-approved test centre, almost always physically located in the UK.

What this means for Sudanese candidates specifically

Because the CPSA is sat in person in the UK, most Sudanese doctors will need to travel internationally to attend, and will require a UK visa unless they already hold a qualifying immigration status (such as a work visa, dependant visa, or refugee/leave-to-remain status already in the UK). Given the current cost and difficulty of some travel routes out of Sudan for those still resident there, and depending on your host country if displaced, plan your CPSA logistics with real care.

  • Confirm your CPSA test date and centre before booking any flights or accommodation.
  • Check current UK visitor visa requirements and processing times for Sudanese nationals, or for your country of current residence if displaced, well ahead of your test date — visa processing timelines can vary significantly and are outside the GMC's control.
  • Where possible, build in a buffer of several days before your test date to account for travel disruption, given that international routes involving Sudan or neighbouring transit points can be less predictable than standard travel corridors.
  • If you are already living in the UK (for example, as a refugee, asylum seeker with permission to work, or dependant of a UK visa holder), you will not need a separate visit visa for the CPSA itself, though you should still confirm your right-to-sit status with the test centre.

Practise clinical communication in English specifically for the CPSA format — history-taking, explaining diagnoses, and breaking bad news in a UK clinical communication style — since this differs somewhat from Sudanese clinical training environments, even where the underlying medicine is the same.

Budgeting realistically as a Sudanese candidate

Beyond the GMC's own fees, Sudanese doctors should budget for several costs the fee table above does not capture: international courier fees for sending physical transcripts where digital verification is not possible, translation and notarisation of documents that are not already in English, question bank subscriptions for AKT preparation, and — for most candidates — flights and UK accommodation around the CPSA sitting. For doctors currently displaced to Egypt, Saudi Arabia, or elsewhere, add the cost of travelling from your current location rather than assuming a direct Sudan-to-UK route, since many displaced doctors are no longer travelling from Khartoum at all. Setting aside a contingency of at least 15–20% above your itemised budget is sensible given how unpredictable some of these secondary costs can be.

What happens after GMC registration: entering the NHS

Once you hold full GMC registration with a licence to practise, you are legally able to apply for medical posts in the NHS, subject to the usual employment checks (right-to-work, occupational health clearance, references and, where relevant, a UK visa sponsored by an NHS employer). Sudanese doctors, like other IMGs, typically enter at a grade appropriate to their prior clinical experience — this could mean an FY2-equivalent post, a clinical fellow role, or a more senior post if you already have substantial post-internship experience recognised by the employing trust.

Many NHS trusts run structured induction programmes for internationally qualified doctors that cover UK-specific clinical documentation, prescribing systems, and multidisciplinary working — worth asking about specifically when interviewing, since these programmes can meaningfully ease the transition from a Sudanese hospital environment to an NHS one. If you are applying to the NHS as a refugee or displaced doctor, some trusts and charitable organisations run dedicated placement or mentoring schemes for doctors in exactly this situation, which is another reason to engage with the GMC's refugee and displaced doctor support contacts early — they can often point you toward these schemes directly.

It is also worth noting that GMC registration is only the regulatory gateway; securing an NHS job is a separate process involving standard job applications, interviews and visa sponsorship (where required) through the UK's points-based immigration system. Start researching NHS trusts, specialty interests, and visa sponsorship requirements well before you finish your UKMLA preparation, so you are not starting that research from zero once you are registered.

Step-by-step: the registration process for Sudanese doctors

  1. Check your eligibility. Confirm your medical school's status in the World Directory of Medical Schools and review the GMC's current eligibility requirements at eligibility.
  2. Start EPIC/MyIntealth verification early. Register on MyIntealth and initiate primary source verification of your MBBS as soon as possible — this is often the longest step, especially if your medical school's capacity is affected. See the detailed walkthrough at GMC EPIC verification process explained.
  3. Gather your Sudan Medical Council documentation. Request your registration certificate or certificate of good standing; if unobtainable, collect alternative supporting evidence and, where relevant, contact the GMC or its refugee support team to discuss your situation.
  4. Book and pass your English test. Sit IELTS Academic or OET and ensure your scores meet the GMC's current minimum requirements before applying.
  5. Register for and prepare for the AKT. Pay the AKT fee (£283), book your test date, and revise systematically against the UKMLA content map.
  6. Sit and pass the AKT. Results determine your eligibility to proceed to the CPSA within the GMC's validity window.
  7. Arrange CPSA logistics. Confirm your test centre and date, then sort visas, flights and accommodation in that order — never book travel before your CPSA slot is confirmed.
  8. Sit and pass the CPSA. Pay the CPSA fee (£1,036) and attend your practical assessment.
  9. Apply for GMC registration. Submit your provisional or full registration application (£26 or £481 respectively, depending on your route) along with all verified documents.
  10. Receive registration and a licence to practise. Once approved, you can begin applying for NHS posts, typically starting at Foundation Year 2-equivalent or a suitable clinical grade depending on your experience.
  11. Maintain annual retention. Pay your annual retention fee (£481 with a licence, £172 without) to keep your registration active.

Consult the site's Registration Guide pillar page for the general mechanics that apply to every nationality, and the general GMC registration requirements for international doctors post for a broader view of what every IMG, not just Sudanese doctors, needs to prepare.

Realistic timeline for Sudanese doctors

There is no single fixed timeline, since so much depends on document availability and personal circumstances, but a reasonable planning estimate is as follows.

  • Months 1–4: EPIC/MyIntealth verification initiated; Sudan Medical Council documentation requested; English test booked and sat.
  • Months 3–7: AKT preparation and sitting (allow longer if verification is delayed, since some candidates prefer to confirm verification is progressing before committing to exam fees).
  • Months 6–10: CPSA preparation, visa arrangements if required, and CPSA sitting.
  • Months 9–14: GMC registration application and processing.
  • Months 12–18+: Realistic outer bound if document verification or Sudan Medical Council confirmation is significantly delayed by the conflict's ongoing effects, or if you need to gather alternative evidence from scratch.

Doctors with straightforward, complete documentation may move through this faster; displaced doctors reconstructing records from a disrupted system should expect the process to sit toward the longer end of this range, and should not see that as unusual given the circumstances.

Documents checklist for Sudanese applicants

Wherever possible, gather these before starting your MyIntealth application: your original MBBS degree certificate and official academic transcripts; evidence of Sudan Medical Council provisional or full registration; internship completion records, or a letter from your training hospital confirming what was completed if records were disrupted; a valid passport; passport-style photographs meeting GMC specifications; and your English test certificate once sat. If any original documents were lost or are inaccessible due to displacement, contact your medical school and the Sudan Medical Council directly to request replacements or written confirmations, and keep a written record of every request in case you need to demonstrate to the GMC that you have made a genuine effort to obtain standard evidence before relying on an alternative pathway.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Waiting to start EPIC/MyIntealth verification. This is consistently the longest step; starting late is the single biggest cause of delay for Sudanese and other IMG applicants alike.
  • Booking CPSA travel before the test date is confirmed. Non-refundable flights booked speculatively can be a costly mistake if a slot changes.
  • Assuming Sudan Medical Council paperwork is a hard blocker. It is important supporting evidence, but the GMC assesses each application on its full evidence base — do not delay contacting the GMC simply because one document is missing.
  • Underestimating currency risk. Given SDG volatility, doctors who price their entire budget in SDG risk significant shortfalls if the exchange rate moves before they pay UK-based fees.
  • Not exploring refugee/displaced doctor support if eligible. Some doctors assume this support is only for certain nationalities named in older schemes; always check current eligibility directly with the GMC rather than assuming you do not qualify.
  • Relying solely on Sudanese undergraduate revision for the AKT. The exam tests UK clinical guidelines and NHS-specific processes, so dedicated UKMLA-focused preparation is necessary even for strong undergraduate students.
  • Leaving English test results until late in the process. Test slots and result processing can take time, and an out-of-date or expired certificate can delay a registration application.

Many Sudanese doctors currently going through this process are doing so while also managing the practical and emotional weight of displacement, family separation, or uncertainty about home. Frequently asked questions

Can Sudanese doctors sit the UKMLA and register with the GMC?

Yes. Sudanese doctors holding an MBBS from a recognised Sudanese medical school are eligible to sit the UKMLA (AKT and CPSA) and apply for GMC registration in the same way as other international medical graduates, provided they meet the qualification, verification and English-language requirements set by the GMC.

Does the Sudan Medical Council need to confirm my registration before I apply to the GMC?

The GMC primarily verifies your primary medical qualification through EPIC/MyIntealth with your medical school, not through the Sudan Medical Council directly. However, evidence of full or provisional registration with the Sudan Medical Council, and a certificate of good standing, are commonly requested as supporting evidence, so keep these documents safe or apply for replacements where possible.

What if my Sudanese medical school records were disrupted by the conflict?

If your medical school's administration was disrupted since April 2023, contact the GMC and your medical school directly and as early as possible, since EPIC/MyIntealth verification can take longer when institutions have limited capacity. The GMC's refugee and displaced doctor support route can also help if you have been granted asylum, refugee status, or another relevant UK immigration status.

What are the current GMC fees for Sudanese doctors taking the UKMLA?

For the 2026 cycle, the AKT fee is £283, the CPSA fee is £1,036, the provisional registration application fee is £26, and the full registration application fee is £481. There is also an annual retention fee of £481 with a licence to practise or £172 without one. Always confirm current figures on gmc-uk.org, as fees are reviewed periodically.

Is there financial help for Sudanese doctors who are refugees or asylum seekers in the UK?

Doctors granted refugee status, asylum, or certain other UK immigration statuses may be eligible for GMC fee waivers or reduced fees, staged payment plans, and dedicated support through the GMC's assistance programme for refugee and displaced doctors. This is separate from, but complementary to, the standard UKMLA route.

Do Sudanese doctors need to prove English language proficiency?

Yes, unless you qualify for an exemption. Most Sudanese applicants take IELTS Academic or OET, typically costing around £180 to £230 depending on the test and centre, and must meet the GMC's minimum scores in each component before applying for registration.

What is EPIC/MyIntealth and why does it matter for Sudanese graduates?

EPIC, now delivered through MyIntealth, is the primary source verification system the GMC uses to confirm that your medical degree is genuine by checking directly with the awarding institution. Sudanese graduates must complete this verification, though processing may take longer if a medical school's administrative capacity has been affected by the conflict.

How long does the whole UKMLA to GMC registration process take for a Sudanese doctor?

Allow roughly 9 to 18 months from starting document verification to receiving full GMC registration, covering EPIC/MyIntealth checks, English test booking, AKT and CPSA preparation and sitting, and registration processing. Displaced doctors or those awaiting Sudan Medical Council paperwork should budget extra time and start verification as early as possible.

Where do Sudanese doctors sit the CPSA?

The CPSA is held at GMC-approved test centres, mostly in the UK, so most Sudanese candidates need to travel and will require a UK visitor visa unless they already hold another UK visa route. Book flights and accommodation only after your CPSA slot is confirmed, and check current UK visa requirements for Sudanese nationals well in advance.

Can a Sudanese doctor still qualify if their internship was interrupted by the war?

An interrupted or delayed internship does not automatically disqualify you, but you will need updated documentation from the Sudan Medical Council or your training hospital confirming what was completed. Contact the GMC directly to discuss your specific circumstances, since assessment is done case by case.

Are fees different for Sudanese doctors compared with other IMGs?

No, standard GMC and UKMLA fees are the same for all international medical graduates regardless of nationality. The only differences arise if a Sudanese doctor qualifies for refugee or displaced-doctor fee support, which can reduce or waive certain charges.

What should displaced Sudanese doctors do first if they want to pursue UKMLA registration?

Start by gathering whatever documents you already have, contact your medical school and the Sudan Medical Council to request replacements or confirmations where possible, and reach out to the GMC's refugee and displaced doctor support team early. Beginning EPIC/MyIntealth verification as soon as possible is the most time-critical step.
