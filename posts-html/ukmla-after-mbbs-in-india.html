UKMLA after MBBS in India means making one central decision as soon as your internship ends: whether NEET PG, the UKMLA route to GMC registration, another country's licensing exam, or a mix of these is your next step, and then acting on that decision in the right order so you do not lose months to avoidable delay. For doctors who choose the UK route, the single most useful thing to know is that several steps, including EPIC/MyIntealth verification and early AKT preparation, can and should start before every piece of internship paperwork is finalised, not after. This guide sets out exactly what to do, in what order, in the weeks and months immediately after MBBS, and complements our fuller UKMLA for Indian doctors guide, which covers the eligibility, fees and registration mechanics in full depth once you have decided on this route.

Key takeaways

  • Right after MBBS, most Indian graduates are choosing between NEET PG (India's postgraduate route), the UKMLA route (AKT and CPSA, leading to GMC registration), other country routes such as the USMLE, or a period of Indian clinical work before deciding.
  • AKT preparation does not require a completed internship; many Indian doctors study for it during their compulsory rotating internship year.
  • EPIC/MyIntealth verification depends on your medical school's response time and is usually the slowest step in the whole route, so start it as early as your documents allow.
  • NExT, the planned unified Indian licensing exam, has been reported as deferred by roughly three to four years as of 2026, so NEET PG and FMGE remain the operative domestic exams for now.
  • There is no expiry date on the UKMLA route: Indian doctors can start it straight after internship or years later, as long as their documents remain obtainable.
  • Most doctors who commit immediately reach an NHS job offer roughly eighteen months to two years after MBBS completion.

Where you actually stand the moment MBBS finishes

Before deciding anything, it helps to be precise about what "finishing MBBS" actually means in India, because the timeline that follows depends on it. An Indian MBBS runs for four and a half years of academic study followed by a twelve-month compulsory rotating internship, sometimes called CRMI, which is mandatory both for the award of your MBBS degree and for full registration with the National Medical Commission (NMC) or your State Medical Council. In practice, this means most Indian graduates are not truly "finished" with MBBS, in the sense of holding a complete, registerable qualification, until the internship year itself is complete, not merely the academic years.

The internship year itself is structured rather than a single placement: it typically includes rotations through community medicine, general medicine, paediatrics and surgery, plus a short elective posting in a subject such as radio-diagnosis, forensic medicine, psychiatry or dermatology, according to the NMC's Regulations for Compulsory Rotating Internship. Only once this internship is complete, assessed and confirmed by your medical college does your degree, and your subsequent State Medical Council registration, become final. That registration certificate is one of the documents you will later be asked to produce for GMC registration, so it is worth treating internship completion as the real starting line for everything that follows, rather than your final MBBS exam.

What if your internship is delayed or extended?

Not every internship finishes exactly on schedule. Poor attendance, failed departmental assessments, or administrative issues at your medical college can trigger an extension, and if this happens to you, it genuinely does delay every downstream step described in this guide, since EPIC/MyIntealth verification and GMC registration both ultimately need a completed, confirmed internship. If your internship is extended, use the additional time productively rather than treating it purely as a setback: it is still a good window for AKT revision, English test preparation, and starting your EPIC/MyIntealth account, since none of those require your internship certificate in hand on day one. What matters is being honest with yourself early about a likely delay, rather than continuing to plan around an original completion date that has already slipped.

The real options on the table after MBBS

Once internship is complete, or nearly so, most Indian graduates are weighing some combination of the following paths, and it is worth being honest about all of them rather than assuming only one is realistic.

Option 1: NEET PG and Indian postgraduate training

NEET PG is India's entrance examination for postgraduate specialty training within the country, and it has a fixed annual cycle with a specific internship-completion cutoff each year, so timing matters if this is your primary goal. Registration typically opens mid-year, with the exam itself following some weeks later, and eligibility generally requires your compulsory rotating internship to be complete, or provably completing, by a fixed cutoff date set for that year's cycle. Because exact dates and cutoffs shift from year to year, and because different sources sometimes quote slightly different cutoff dates for the same cycle, always confirm the current year's exact dates directly through the National Board of Examinations in Medical Sciences rather than relying on a fixed date carried over from a previous year. After the exam, successful candidates go through a separate counselling and seat-allocation process, run partly at the national level and partly by individual states, before actually starting a postgraduate training post. If you are still deciding between NEET PG and the UKMLA route, our dedicated UKMLA vs NEET PG comparison is worth reading before you commit meaningful study time to either.

Option 2: The UKMLA route to GMC registration

This is the focus of this guide: passing the AKT and CPSA, completing EPIC/MyIntealth verification, meeting the English language requirement, and registering with the GMC to work in the NHS. Full mechanics, current fees in GBP and INR, and the step-by-step registration process are covered in detail in our UKMLA for Indian doctors guide; this article focuses specifically on sequencing and decision-making in the weeks right after MBBS, not on repeating that detail.

Option 3: Another country's licensing route

A smaller group of Indian graduates weigh the US route (the USMLE) or, less commonly, routes into Australia, Canada or the Gulf, alongside the UK option. If the US route is genuinely on your list, our UKMLA vs USMLE comparison lays out the practical differences in format, cost and typical timeline. Australia and Canada have their own separate licensing exams and assessment processes, generally referred to via the AMC and MCCQE routes respectively, and while fewer Indian graduates pursue these compared with the UK or US, our UKMLA vs AMC and MCCQE comparison is a useful starting point if either is genuinely under consideration. The Gulf region uses a different verification system entirely, called DataFlow, which some Indian doctors encounter later even after UK registration if they later consider Gulf-based practice; our Gulf countries DataFlow verification guide explains that process for anyone weighing options beyond the UK and US alone. The point of listing these alternatives is not to suggest you should chase every option at once, but to make clear that the UK route is a deliberate choice among several real ones, not the only path available to an Indian MBBS graduate.

Option 4: Clinical work in India first, decide later

Some doctors deliberately delay any postgraduate or overseas decision and take up clinical work in India for a year or two first, either to build confidence and clinical experience or simply because they are not yet certain which direction to take. This is a legitimate choice, not a wasted one: nothing about the UKMLA route requires you to start immediately after internship, and clinical experience gained in India can genuinely strengthen your performance in the CPSA's clinical stations later.

None of these four options need to be mutually exclusive forever. Many Indian doctors sit NEET PG in one cycle while quietly building AKT knowledge in the background, or work clinically for a year before committing fully to UKMLA preparation. The mistake to avoid is drifting for months without deciding anything, since several of the slowest UKMLA-route steps, described next, benefit enormously from an early start.

A simple decision framework for choosing your next step

Rather than treating this as one enormous, abstract decision, it helps to work through a short list of concrete questions, since the honest answers usually point fairly clearly toward one option over another.

  • Where do you actually want to be practising in five years? If the honest answer is "within India's hospital system," NEET PG deserves your primary focus. If it is "the UK, ideally in the NHS," the UKMLA route should be your priority, even if you keep NEET PG as a backup.
  • Can you fund roughly ₹3–4 lakh over the next year or so? If money is genuinely tight right now, starting with the lower-cost early steps, English testing and EPIC/MyIntealth verification, while you save toward the AKT and CPSA, is a reasonable staged approach rather than an all-or-nothing decision.
  • Are you prepared to relocate, at least initially? The UKMLA route ultimately leads to UK-based work; if relocation is not something you or your family can countenance right now, it is worth being honest about that before investing heavily in preparation.
  • Do you need a fixed decision this year, or can you keep both doors open? If NEET PG's cutoff and your own timeline allow it, sitting NEET PG while quietly starting EPIC/MyIntealth verification in the background is a legitimate way to delay a final choice without losing time on either route.

Working through these four questions honestly, ideally in writing rather than only in your head, tends to produce a clearer answer than simply weighing "UK versus India" as one abstract, undifferentiated decision.

Special situations: if your path is not the standard one

Not every Indian graduate reading this is a fresh intern deciding between two clean options, and it is worth addressing the less standard situations directly rather than assuming everyone starts from the same point.

You already completed an Indian postgraduate degree

Doctors who have already finished an MD, MS or diploma in India, and are now considering the UKMLA route mid-career, are not disadvantaged by that choice. Indian postgraduate experience is generally a genuine asset in later NHS job applications and, in some cases, may be relevant to specialist registration routes rather than starting again from a junior grade, though this depends heavily on your specialty and the specific GMC route you eventually pursue. Because specialist and GP registration criteria are more involved than standard AKT-and-CPSA registration, doctors in this position should read our guide on whether experienced doctors need to take the UKMLA before assuming they must sit both assessments regardless of their existing qualifications.

Several years have passed since your MBBS

There is no time limit on when an Indian graduate can begin the UKMLA route, and a meaningful share of successful applicants started years, not months, after graduating, often after a period of clinical work, family responsibilities, or an unrelated career detour. The main practical difference for this group is that some documents, particularly older internship records or contact details for verifying seniors, can take longer to track down, so it is worth starting the EPIC/MyIntealth request and document collection process a little earlier than a fresher graduate might need to, simply to allow for that extra administrative lag.

You are still deciding while working in a non-clinical or allied role

Some Indian MBBS graduates spend a period in research, public health, medical writing, or other non-clinical work before returning to a clinical pathway. This does not disqualify anyone from the UKMLA route, though the CPSA specifically tests live clinical and communication skills, so doctors returning from an extended non-clinical period often benefit from a deliberate clinical refresher, whether through short-term Indian clinical work or structured practice sessions, before booking their CPSA date.

What NExT means for your decision in 2026

Because so much online discussion of Indian medical licensing mentions the National Exit Test, or NExT, it is worth clarifying its current status so it does not distort your planning. NExT was originally proposed as a single unified exam that would eventually replace both the NEET-PG screening component and FMGE, the licensing exam for doctors who studied medicine abroad. However, multiple reports through 2026 indicate the NMC has deferred full NExT implementation by roughly three to four years, with mock testing reportedly planned before any real rollout, meaning NEET PG and FMGE continue to operate as before for the foreseeable future. Because this is a fast-moving domestic policy area rather than a GMC matter, always confirm the current position directly on nmc.org.in before assuming NExT changes anything about your own plans, rather than relying on any single blog post, including this one.

Practically, this means Indian graduates deciding their next step in 2026 should plan around NEET PG and the UKMLA route as they exist today, rather than waiting for NExT to arrive before making a decision. Waiting on a deferred exam is, in itself, a decision, and rarely the right one if your actual goal is UK-based practice.

What you can start during internship, and what has to wait

The single most valuable piece of sequencing advice for Indian graduates who already know they want the UKMLA route is this: several of the slowest steps do not require a completed internship, and starting them late is the most common source of lost time.

In other words, an Indian doctor who waits until their internship completion certificate is physically in hand before starting anything is very likely losing three to six months compared with a doctor who begins AKT revision, English test preparation and EPIC/MyIntealth document gathering during the final rotations of internship itself. Because your full registration application ultimately needs the completed internship confirmation, that final piece cannot be rushed, but everything upstream of it can run in parallel rather than in sequence.

Your realistic first 90 days after MBBS internship

Rather than a vague intention to "start preparing eventually," a concrete first-90-days plan tends to produce much better outcomes for Indian graduates who have decided on the UKMLA route.

  1. Weeks 1–2: Request your internship completion certificate and State Medical Council or NMC registration as soon as eligible, since these documents take time to process and are needed later.
  2. Weeks 1–4: Create your EPIC/MyIntealth account and begin the primary source verification request with your medical school, even before every other document is ready, because this step's timeline is the least within your control.
  3. Weeks 2–6: Book and begin structured preparation for IELTS Academic or OET Medicine, targeting a sitting within the next two to three months. See our English language requirements guide for the exact score thresholds.
  4. Weeks 4–12: Start structured AKT revision using a recognised question bank alongside the official MLA content map as your syllabus checklist, rather than general MBBS revision alone.
  5. By day 90: Have a provisional AKT booking target, a confirmed English test date or result, and an active EPIC/MyIntealth verification request in progress, so that by month four or five you are ready to sit the AKT rather than still assembling paperwork.

This sequencing mirrors what our broader UKMLA preparation guide for IMG candidates and the Preparation pillar recommend for IMGs generally, but it is worth restating specifically for the immediate post-MBBS moment, since this is exactly when Indian graduates are most likely to either lose momentum or, conversely, try to do everything at once and burn out.

Extending the plan to six months

Beyond the first 90 days, a realistic six-month view helps set expectations properly. By month four, most well-organised Indian graduates have a confirmed English test score and are deep into structured AKT revision, often layering in timed mock papers. By month five, an AKT sitting is commonly booked or already completed, and the EPIC/MyIntealth verification request, started back in month one, should be progressing, though its exact completion date remains largely outside your control. By month six, many candidates are turning attention to CPSA preparation and starting to research UK travel logistics for the Manchester sitting, even if that trip itself is still some months away. Treating months four to six as a distinct phase, rather than an open-ended "keep revising" period, helps avoid the common trap of AKT preparation quietly extending well beyond the point of diminishing returns.

Months seven to twelve: CPSA, registration and the job search

The second half of the first year typically shifts from knowledge revision to logistics and clinical polish. Months seven and eight are commonly spent on focused CPSA preparation, ideally including practice under timed, station-based conditions rather than passive revision alone. Around month nine or ten, most well-organised candidates travel to Manchester for the CPSA itself, often timing the trip once EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English test evidence are already finalised, so that a GMC registration application can be submitted almost immediately after a CPSA pass is confirmed. By month eleven or twelve, many Indian doctors are already submitting their GMC registration application and beginning early-stage NHS job research, so that the transition from "registered" to "actually working" does not itself become another lengthy, unplanned gap.

How much weekly study time is realistic?

Because most Indian graduates are also completing internship duties, working, or juggling family responsibilities during this period, an honest weekly study plan matters more than an ambitious but unsustainable one. Many successful AKT candidates report roughly eight to twelve focused hours per week during the early months, rising to fifteen to twenty hours in the final two months before their sitting, rather than attempting intensive full-time revision from day one and burning out well before the exam itself. Building in deliberate rest, rather than treating every evening and weekend as study time from the outset, is one of the more consistent pieces of advice from doctors who have already completed this route.

What if you do not pass the AKT or CPSA on your first attempt?

A resit is a normal, common part of this process, not a sign that the UKMLA route was the wrong choice. Because both assessments carry defined rules around attempt limits and reflection periods, it is worth understanding the resit framework before you sit either exam for the first time, rather than only researching it after a disappointing result. Our dedicated UKMLA resits guide covers the specific rules, typical waiting periods, and how to structure a focused resit preparation plan that targets the areas an initial attempt actually struggled with, rather than simply repeating the same revision approach a second time.

Budgeting before you have even decided

Because committing to the UKMLA route involves real cost, it helps to know roughly how much money keeps your options open even before you have fully decided, rather than discovering the full financial picture only once you are already partway through. English test registration (IELTS Academic or OET Medicine) typically costs somewhere in the range of £180 to £230, and EPIC/MyIntealth verification carries its own processing fee, both payable well before you sit the AKT itself. Setting aside a modest amount, often in the region of ₹20,000 to ₹30,000, simply to start your English test and EPIC/MyIntealth request is a reasonable early commitment that does not require you to have fully decided against NEET PG or any other option; it simply keeps the UKMLA route genuinely open while you continue weighing your choices. The full fee picture, including AKT, CPSA and GMC registration costs in both GBP and INR, is set out in detail once you are ready to commit fully, in our UKMLA for Indian doctors guide.

Getting reliable help without falling for exploitative agents

Because the UKMLA route has become popular enough among Indian graduates to attract a genuine industry of consultants, coaching centres and self-styled "placement agents," it is worth being deliberate about where you take advice from in these first few months. Legitimate question bank providers, revision course providers and English test preparation centres are widely available and generally transparent about pricing and what they actually offer. Be considerably more cautious of anyone promising guaranteed NHS job placement, guaranteed exam passes, or requiring large upfront payments before any concrete service has been delivered, since these are recurring patterns in complaints from Indian doctors who have gone through this route before you. The GMC itself does not charge, or authorise third parties to charge, any special "processing fee" beyond its own published fee schedule, so any request for an unusual additional payment tied to your GMC registration specifically is worth treating with real suspicion. When in doubt, cross-check any claim made by a paid adviser directly against the GMC's own published guidance before acting on it.

Talking to family and managing expectations early

Because the UKMLA route typically means relocating, at least initially, and asks for a meaningful financial and time commitment, having an honest conversation with family early tends to reduce stress later, particularly around the CPSA travel stage and any eventual visa and relocation decisions. Doctors who discuss realistic timelines, likely costs, and the possibility of a resit at either the AKT or CPSA stage with their family upfront generally report less pressure during the preparation period itself than those who present the plan only once it is already well underway. This is not a formal step in the process, but it is a practical one worth taking seriously in the same first few months covered by this guide.

Common mistakes in the first few months after MBBS

  • Waiting for "the right time" to start. Because EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English testing do not depend on your internship being finished, waiting until it is finished before starting either is one of the most common, and most avoidable, sources of delay.
  • Trying to prepare for NEET PG and the AKT with equal intensity at the same time. Both are demanding examinations; most doctors who succeed at either pick a primary focus rather than splitting attention evenly across both in the same cycle.
  • Assuming NExT changes your plans. Because NExT has been reported as deferred, doctors who delay decisions "until NExT arrives" are often waiting for something that will not affect their timeline for years.
  • Underestimating how long EPIC/MyIntealth verification takes. This step depends on your university's administrative response time, not your own effort, so starting it early is the only real lever you have.
  • Not requesting documents while still enrolled or recently graduated. Internship completion certificates and registration paperwork are usually far easier to obtain soon after graduation than years later, once staff and records have moved on.
  • Deciding in isolation. Talking to doctors who have already been through the UKMLA route, rather than relying solely on forum posts, tends to surface practical details, such as EPIC/MyIntealth quirks or CPSA travel logistics, that generic guides can miss.
  • Paying an agent for services you can do yourself. Requesting your own EPIC/MyIntealth verification, booking your own AKT and CPSA, and applying for your own GMC registration are all things an Indian doctor can do directly; some paid intermediaries add real value through structured coaching, but others simply repackage steps you could complete yourself at no extra cost.
  • Ignoring the internship's own completion requirements while focused on UKMLA planning. Because this guide focuses heavily on the UK route, it can be easy to under-prioritise your remaining internship assessments; remember that a delayed or incomplete internship stalls every UKMLA-route step just as much as any GMC-side delay would.

If you decide to gain Indian clinical experience first

Choosing to work clinically in India for a year or two before starting the UKMLA route is a genuinely reasonable decision, not a delay to be talked out of. Doctors who take this path should still consider starting EPIC/MyIntealth verification early, since the process does not require you to be actively preparing for the AKT, and having it already complete removes one dependency whenever you do decide to move forward. Beyond that, using the working period to build confidence in structured clinical examination and communication, the same skills tested in the CPSA, can genuinely help later, even though it is not a formal requirement.

If you decide on NEET PG instead, or alongside

Choosing NEET PG does not permanently close the UKMLA route. Some Indian doctors complete part or all of a postgraduate programme in India before later deciding to pursue GMC registration, often bringing valuable specialty experience to their eventual NHS application. If you are approaching this as a genuine dual-track decision rather than a final choice, our UKMLA vs NEET PG comparison covers the practical trade-offs, including how postgraduate training in India is viewed later if you do eventually pursue UK registration.

Frequently asked questions

What should I do immediately after finishing MBBS in India if I want to work in the UK?

Complete your compulsory rotating internship and State Medical Council or NMC registration first, since both are required before GMC registration, then start EPIC/MyIntealth verification and English language test preparation in parallel with your early AKT revision, rather than waiting until internship paperwork is fully settled to begin anything.

Can I prepare for the UKMLA AKT during my internship?

Yes. AKT preparation does not require a completed internship, so many Indian doctors study for the AKT during their compulsory rotating internship year, sitting the test once their internship finishes and their schedule allows dedicated revision and exam-day availability.

Do I have to choose between NEET PG and the UKMLA route right after MBBS?

Not immediately, but you should decide within a realistic window, because NEET PG has a fixed annual registration and exam cycle with an internship-completion cutoff, while the UKMLA route can be started at any time. Some doctors sit NEET PG while also progressing UKMLA preparation, but sitting both exams properly in the same year is demanding, so most advisers recommend choosing a primary focus early.

What is NExT and does it affect Indian MBBS graduates in 2026?

NExT (the National Exit Test) is a planned unified licensing exam intended eventually to replace NEET-PG screening and FMGE, but multiple 2026 reports indicate the NMC has deferred its rollout by roughly three to four years, meaning NEET PG and FMGE remain the operative exams for now. Always confirm the current position on nmc.org.in before assuming NExT applies to you.

When should I start EPIC or MyIntealth verification after MBBS?

As early as your medical school can confirm your degree details, ideally started in parallel with the final months of your internship rather than after it finishes, because verification depends on your university responding and is often the slowest step in the entire UKMLA route.

Is it too late to start the UKMLA route a few years after MBBS?

No. There is no expiry on when an Indian MBBS graduate can begin the UKMLA route, and many successful applicants start several years after graduating, sometimes after working in India first. The main practical consideration is making sure your internship, registration and any historical documents are still easy to obtain and verify.

Should I gain work experience in India before starting the UKMLA route?

There is no requirement to work in India first, but some doctors choose to gain a year or two of clinical experience before committing to UKMLA preparation, since it can strengthen confidence for the CPSA's clinical stations. This is a personal decision rather than a GMC requirement.

What is the very first document I should request after my internship ends?

Your internship completion certificate and State Medical Council or NMC registration certificate should be requested as soon as your internship formally ends, since these documents are frequently needed later for EPIC/MyIntealth verification and GMC registration, and delays in obtaining them from your medical college are a common source of lost time.

Can I sit NEET PG and still pursue the UKMLA route later?

Yes. Sitting NEET PG does not close off the UKMLA route later, and some doctors complete part or all of an Indian postgraduate programme before deciding to pursue GMC registration and NHS work. The routes are not mutually exclusive, only sequentially demanding if attempted at the same time.

How soon after MBBS can I realistically be working in the NHS?

Most Indian doctors who commit fully to the UKMLA route immediately after internship take roughly eighteen months to two years from MBBS completion to an NHS job offer, once EPIC/MyIntealth verification, English testing, AKT, CPSA, GMC registration and the NHS job search are all accounted for, though efficient candidates can move somewhat faster.

Does an Indian postgraduate degree help if I switch to the UKMLA route later?

Yes, generally. An MD, MS or diploma completed in India is usually a genuine asset in later NHS applications and, depending on your specialty, may be relevant to specialist registration routes, though you should confirm the specific route that applies to your qualification rather than assuming standard AKT-and-CPSA registration is your only option.

How much money should I set aside before I have even decided on the UKMLA route?

A modest amount, often roughly ₹20,000 to ₹30,000, is enough to start your English test registration and EPIC/MyIntealth verification request, both of which keep the route open without requiring you to have fully committed or ruled out other options such as NEET PG.
