Palliative Care and End-of-Life Medicine for the UKMLA: A Complete Revision Guide

Palliative care is a specialty that every doctor must practice, regardless of their field. The UKMLA AKT tests this area not only through symptom management questions but through scenarios involving advance care planning, consent, capacity, and ethical decision-making at the end of life. Candidates who master this topic demonstrate not just medical knowledge but the clinical and ethical reasoning that defines good doctoring.

This guide covers the complete UKMLA palliative care syllabus — symptom control, syringe drivers, recognition of dying, advance care planning, organ donation, and communication skills. Begin by understanding the exam format at our AKT guide and our UKMLA overview.

Principles of Palliative Care

The WHO (2020) definition of palliative care describes it as "an approach that improves the quality of life of patients and their families facing the problems associated with life-threatening illness, through the prevention and relief of suffering by means of early identification and impeccable assessment and treatment of pain and other problems, physical, psychosocial and spiritual."

Key principles that distinguish palliative care from purely curative medicine:

  • Palliative care is applicable earlier in illness — it should be introduced alongside curative or life-prolonging treatment, not only when curative options are exhausted
  • It affirms life and regards dying as a normal process
  • It neither hastens nor postpones death
  • It addresses pain and other distressing symptoms
  • It integrates physical, psychological, social, and spiritual care
  • It supports both patient and family, including bereavement
  • The goal is the best possible quality of life for both patient and family

The concept of goals of care conversations is central to modern palliative medicine — establishing with the patient and family what matters most, what they understand about their condition, and aligning medical treatment to their values. These conversations should happen early, be revisited regularly, and be documented.

Pain Management in Palliative Care — NICE NG31

The WHO Analgesic Ladder

The WHO analgesic ladder (1986, revised principles still applied) provides a step-wise framework for cancer pain management:

  • Step 1 — Non-opioid: Paracetamol 1g four times daily ± NSAID (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac — use with PPI, caution in renal impairment, cardiac disease)
  • Step 2 — Weak opioid: Add codeine 30–60mg four times daily (prodrug converted to morphine by CYP2D6 — ultra-rapid metabolisers at risk of toxicity, poor metabolisers get no analgesia) or tramadol. Dihydrocodeine alternative.
  • Step 3 — Strong opioid: Replace weak opioid with strong opioid (morphine, oxycodone, hydromorphone, fentanyl, buprenorphine). Continue Step 1 analgesics if appropriate. Add adjuvants throughout.

The principle is to prescribe analgesia by mouth, by the clock, by the ladder — regular dosing prevents pain returning (do not wait until pain is severe to medicate).

Opioid Prescribing in Practice

Immediate-release (IR) morphine (Oramorph 10mg/5ml solution or Sevredol tablets): Onset 20–30 minutes, duration 4 hours. Used for initiation and dose titration. Starting dose for opioid-naive patient: 2.5–5mg every 4 hours (lower in elderly, renal impairment). Titrate dose by 25–33% every 24–48 hours based on pain control and breakthrough use.

Modified-release (MR) morphine (MST Continus, Zomorph): Twice daily dosing, onset 1–2 hours. Converts to MR once total daily IR dose established. Calculate total daily dose of IR morphine → prescribe half as MR BD.

Breakthrough analgesia: Always prescribe alongside regular opioids. Dose = 1/6 of the total daily oral morphine equivalent. For example: total daily MR morphine 60mg → breakthrough IR morphine 10mg PRN every 1–4 hours. If patient using ≥3 breakthrough doses per 24 hours, increase regular dose by approximately total breakthrough used in that 24 hours.

Adjuvant Analgesics

  • Neuropathic pain (burning, shooting, lancinating, allodynia): Amitriptyline (10mg at night, titrate) or gabapentin (start 100–300mg three times daily, titrate) or pregabalin. Duloxetine (SNRI) — licensed for diabetic neuropathy and cancer pain. If one fails, try another class before combining.
  • Bone pain: NSAIDs (naproxen, diclofenac) ± dexamethasone (reduces oedema and inflammation). Bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid) reduce pain from bone metastases and pathological fracture risk. Denosumab. Radiotherapy is highly effective for localised bone metastases (single fraction commonly used — equivalent efficacy to multiple fractions for pain).
  • Raised intracranial pressure (headache, nausea, vomiting from cerebral oedema/metastases): Dexamethasone 8–16mg daily (reduces vasogenic oedema). Omeprazole gastroprotection. Whole-brain radiotherapy or stereotactic radiosurgery for multiple/single metastases.
  • Visceral pain: Antispasmodics — hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan) for bowel/bladder spasm. Octreotide for secretory diarrhoea and bowel obstruction symptoms.

Breathlessness

Dyspnoea is one of the most distressing symptoms in advanced illness. Causes include pleural effusion, lung metastases, pulmonary embolism, lymphangitis carcinomatosa, anaemia, COPD, cardiac failure, anxiety. The UKMLA tests symptom management, not the underlying aetiology alone.

Non-pharmacological measures: Fan directed at face (activates cold receptors on trigeminal nerve — reduces breathlessness perception significantly), cool room, open window, positioning (upright or supported), breathing control techniques (respiratory physiotherapy), occupational therapy (energy conservation, pacing).

Pharmacological management:

  • Low-dose opioids: Most evidence-based pharmacological treatment for dyspnoea. Oral or SC morphine (starting 2.5–5mg every 4 hours in opioid-naive) reduces the sensation of breathlessness without causing respiratory depression at appropriate palliative doses. Mechanism: reduces central response to hypercapnia, reduces anxiety.
  • Benzodiazepines: Lorazepam 0.5–1mg sublingual PRN for acute episodic breathlessness with anxiety. Midazolam SC for continuous subcutaneous infusion if distressing breathlessness at end of life. Not first-line — risk of respiratory depression if used without concurrent opioid; most effective as adjunct.
  • Oxygen: Indicated for hypoxic patients (SpO₂ <90%). Not routinely beneficial for normoxic patients with breathlessness — does not reduce dyspnoea sensation in non-hypoxic patients (Cochrane review). Can be burdensome and restricts mobility. Discuss realistic expectations with patient.

Secretions at End of Life (Death Rattle)

Noisy secretions from the upper airway and oropharynx due to inability to clear secretions in the unconscious/semiconscious patient. Present in up to 50% of dying patients. Distressing for families though usually not distressing for the patient (who is unconscious).

Management:

  • Repositioning (lateral or semi-prone) to allow drainage
  • Gentle mouth care (avoid deep suctioning — distressing and counterproductive)
  • Family education — explain that secretions cause noise but are unlikely to be distressing to patient
  • Antisecretory drugs: Hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan) 20mg SC PRN or by syringe driver — does not cross blood-brain barrier (no sedating effect), dries secretions. Glycopyrronium 200 micrograms SC PRN — alternative, longer duration. Hyoscine hydrobromide (scopolamine) — crosses BBB (mild sedation — may be benefit at end of life but choose consciously).

Agitation at End of Life (Terminal Restlessness)

Restlessness, agitation, confusion, and distress are common in the final hours to days of life. Causes include retained urine/faeces, pain, breathlessness, cerebral irritation (uraemia, hypercalcaemia, encephalopathy), drugs, fear, and existential distress. Reversible causes should be sought and treated where appropriate.

Pharmacological management:

  • Midazolam: Benzodiazepine, first-line for terminal agitation. SC via syringe driver, typically 5–30mg/24 hours (start low, titrate). PRN dose 2.5–5mg SC.
  • Levomepromazine (methotrimeprazine): Broad-spectrum antipsychotic with antiemetic and sedating properties. 6.25–25mg SC PRN, or by syringe driver for refractory agitation. Hypotensive — caution.
  • Haloperidol: For agitation with confusion/hallucinations, 0.5–1.5mg SC PRN. Less sedating than levomepromazine.
  • Consider ketamine (low-dose) for complex refractory pain with agitation in specialist palliative care setting.

Continuous Subcutaneous Infusion (CSCI) — Syringe Driver

When oral medications can no longer be taken (dysphagia, coma, intractable vomiting), a syringe driver (CSCI) delivers drugs continuously subcutaneously over 24 hours.

Setting Up a Syringe Driver

The McKinley T34 is the standard syringe driver in UK hospitals. Drugs are usually diluted in 0.9% sodium chloride or water for injection (check individual drug compatibilities — use water for injection for drugs that precipitate in saline, e.g., cyclizine).

Recognition of Dying — NICE NG142

NICE NG142 (Care of Dying Adults in the Last Days of Life, 2015) provides guidance on recognising dying and individualising care.

Clinical Signs of Dying

Recognition that a person may be in the last days or hours of life: clinical judgement based on — deteriorating despite disease-appropriate treatment, profound weakness, drowsiness, reduced oral intake, reduced urine output, mottling and cooling of peripheries, Cheyne-Stokes breathing, increased secretions. The multidisciplinary team should reach a consensus decision.

The Amber Care Bundle identifies patients in the last weeks to months of life where there is uncertainty about recovery, prompting proactive planning conversations.

Individualised End-of-Life Care Plan

NICE NG142 specifies that when a clinical decision is made that someone is dying:

  1. Communicate clearly with patient (if able to understand) and family/those important to them
  2. Develop an individualised care plan with the patient and family — document goals, preferences for place of death, wishes regarding resuscitation, spiritual needs, preferred name
  3. Review all medications — stop non-essential drugs (lipid-lowering agents, antihypertensives, preventive medications), continue essential symptom control drugs via syringe driver if needed
  4. Prescribe anticipatory medications PRN (see below)
  5. Assess needs four-hourly for: pain, breathlessness, nausea, secretions, agitation, hydration/nutrition (IV fluids if felt appropriate — decision with family, not routine)
  6. Do not impose eating/drinking but provide good mouth care
  7. Spiritual and psychological care for patient and family

Anticipatory Prescriptions

When a patient is recognised as dying, subcutaneous PRN prescriptions for common end-of-life symptoms should be written in advance so they can be administered promptly without a new prescription. Typical anticipatory prescriptions:

  • Pain/dyspnoea: SC morphine 2.5–5mg PRN hourly (or 1/6 total daily dose if already on opioids)
  • Agitation: SC midazolam 2.5–5mg PRN hourly
  • Nausea: SC haloperidol 1.5mg PRN 6-hourly OR SC levomepromazine 6.25mg PRN 6-hourly
  • Secretions: SC hyoscine butylbromide 20mg PRN 4-hourly

Advance Care Planning

Advance Decisions to Refuse Treatment (ADRT)

An ADRT (also called "advance directive" or "living will") is a legally binding document allowing a capacitated adult to refuse specified medical treatment in advance, to take effect if they subsequently lack capacity. Governed by the Mental Capacity Act 2005 (England and Wales).

Requirements for a valid ADRT refusing any treatment:

  • Adult aged ≥18 with capacity when made
  • Must specify the treatment being refused
  • Must specify the circumstances in which the refusal applies
  • Not made under undue influence

Additional requirements for an ADRT to refuse LIFE-SUSTAINING TREATMENT (e.g., CPR, artificial ventilation, artificial hydration/nutrition):

  • Must be in writing
  • Must be signed by the person (or by another in their presence if physically unable)
  • Must be witnessed (one witness, who must also sign)
  • Must include the statement: "even if my life is at risk" (or equivalent wording clearly indicating life-sustaining treatment is meant)
  • An ADRT refusing life-sustaining treatment can be overridden by a subsequent LPA for Health and Welfare (if the LPA has authority to give or withhold consent to life-sustaining treatment)

ReSPECT Form (Recommended Summary Plan for Emergency Care and Treatment)

ReSPECT replaced the DNACPR form in most UK trusts. It is a patient-held document summarising personalised recommendations for emergency care. Unlike a pure DNACPR, ReSPECT captures broader goals: clinical interventions that would and would not be appropriate, CPR recommendation, place of care preferences, and what matters most to the patient. It is a recommendation, not a legally binding document (unlike an ADRT).

DNACPR (Do Not Attempt Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

Key legal and ethical principles:

  • A DNACPR decision is a clinical recommendation — it does not require patient consent, but must involve discussion with the patient or proxy if the patient lacks capacity, unless this discussion would cause harm or is not practicable in an emergency
  • DNACPR cannot be placed without the knowledge of the patient unless there is a documented clinical reason why the conversation would cause harm
  • A DNACPR is not a blanket "do not treat" order — it applies only to CPR. All other treatments (antibiotics, fluids, analgesia, surgery) are assessed separately on clinical grounds
  • Family cannot override a clinical DNACPR decision, but should be involved in discussions
  • A patient with capacity can refuse CPR (this would be documented as an ADRT or DNACPR at the patient's request)

Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) for Health and Welfare

A Lasting Power of Attorney for Health and Welfare allows a nominated attorney (donee) to make decisions about health and welfare on behalf of a person who lacks capacity. The LPA must be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian. The attorney can consent to or refuse treatments on the person's behalf, including life-sustaining treatment (only if this power was explicitly granted in the LPA). An LPA for Property and Financial Affairs is separate and does not cover health decisions. Clinicians should request to see the registered LPA document, not just be told about it.

Best Interests Decision Making

When a patient lacks capacity and there is no valid ADRT or LPA, the clinical team makes a best interests decision under the MCA 2005. Best interests must consider: the patient's previously expressed wishes (verbal or written), their values and beliefs, the views of family and those important to them, and what a reasonable, objective assessment concludes is in the patient's best interest. For serious or contested decisions, an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) should be appointed or a Court of Protection application made.

Organ Donation in England

England introduced an opt-out system (Max and Keira's Law) in May 2020. Under deemed consent:

  • Adults who have not registered an objection to donation are considered to have consented — their organs can be used after death unless they are in an excluded group
  • Excluded from deemed consent: Under 18 years of age, those who lack capacity and have never had it in adulthood, those who lived in England for less than 12 months, those in regulated care (care homes, prisons) for most of the preceding 12 months, and those with registered objections on religious, cultural or personal grounds
  • Wales introduced opt-out in 2015; Scotland in 2021; Northern Ireland in 2023
  • Family are always consulted and their knowledge of the deceased's wishes respected — the specialist nurse for organ donation (SNOD) facilitates discussions

Donation categories: donation after brainstem death (DBD — ventilated patients with confirmed brainstem death) and donation after circulatory death (DCD — follows planned withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment). Living donation (primarily kidney, partial liver) also important.

Breaking Bad News — SPIKES Protocol

SPIKES is a structured 6-step protocol for delivering difficult news:

  • S — Setting up: Private room, patient can bring a support person, sit at same level, introduce everyone, avoid interruptions, switch off pagers where possible
  • P — Perception: Assess what the patient already knows. "What have you been told about your condition?" This prevents false assumptions and identifies misconceptions
  • I — Invitation: Ask how much information the patient wants to receive. "How much detail would you like about your results?" Some patients want everything; others prefer family to receive information first. Respect autonomy
  • K — Knowledge: Give information in clear, non-technical language. Use a "warning shot" — "I'm afraid the results are not what we hoped" before delivering the news. Give information in small steps and check understanding after each
  • E — Empathy and Emotions: Name the emotion — "It sounds like this is devastating news." Allow silence. Normalise distress. Don't rush to fill silence with information
  • S — Strategy and Summary: Explain the plan clearly. Provide written information. Arrange follow-up. Provide palliative care referral if appropriate. Ensure patient has support

Spiritual and Cultural Care

A person's spiritual and religious beliefs may profoundly influence their wishes at end of life — attitudes to analgesia, sedation, blood products, withdrawal of treatment, organ donation, autopsy, and funeral rites. Clinicians should ask sensitively about spiritual needs and involve hospital chaplaincy (who can support people of all faiths and none). Do not assume beliefs based on ethnicity or cultural background.

Bereavement support: Offer to families before and after death. Community bereavement services. Cruse Bereavement Support. Structured support groups. Complicated grief (prolonged, dysfunctional grief exceeding 6 months with significant impairment) may require specialist psychological input.

FAQ: Palliative Care for the UKMLA

What is the correct breakthrough dose formula for opioids?

Breakthrough (PRN) analgesia dose is calculated as 1/6 of the total daily oral morphine equivalent dose. For example: a patient on MST 30mg twice daily (total = 60mg oral morphine per 24 hours) should have a PRN dose of 60 ÷ 6 = 10mg oral morphine (or 5mg SC morphine, as SC = half the oral dose). If the patient is using more than 3 breakthrough doses in 24 hours, the total breakthrough used should be added to the background dose and the regular dose increased accordingly.

When is an ADRT legally binding for life-sustaining treatment?

An ADRT refusing life-sustaining treatment is legally binding under the Mental Capacity Act 2005 when it: is made by an adult aged ≥18 with capacity at the time; is in writing; is signed by the person (or by another at their direction, in their presence); is witnessed (witnessed signature required); and contains a specific statement that the decision stands even if life is at risk. Without all of these elements, an ADRT refusing life-sustaining treatment is not legally binding (though it may still be taken into account in best interests decisions). An ADRT refusing any other treatment (non-life-sustaining) does not require written form, though writing is strongly advisable.

What is the opt-out organ donation system introduced in England and who is excluded?

Max and Keira's Law introduced an opt-out (deemed consent) system in England in May 2020. Adults are deemed to have consented to organ donation after death unless they have actively registered an objection or fall into an excluded category. Exclusions: those under 18 years, people who lack capacity and have never had it as an adult, those residing in England for less than 12 months, those in regulated care (care home, prison) for most of the preceding 12 months, and those with registered objections (religious, cultural, or personal). Family members are always consulted and their knowledge of the patient's wishes is considered.

What drugs are commonly prescribed in a syringe driver for end-of-life care?

Common syringe driver drugs include: morphine (or alternative strong opioid) for pain and dyspnoea; midazolam for agitation and anxiety; haloperidol for nausea and mild agitation; levomepromazine for refractory nausea or agitation; hyoscine butylbromide for secretions and bowel colic. Drug compatibility must be checked before mixing — cyclizine is incompatible with 0.9% saline (use water for injection) and may precipitate at high morphine concentrations. A specialist palliative care pharmacist should be involved for complex combinations.

What is the difference between a DNACPR and an ADRT?

A DNACPR is a clinical recommendation made by the medical team (with patient/family involvement) that CPR should not be attempted in the event of cardiac or respiratory arrest — it is not legally binding in itself but represents good clinical decision-making. An ADRT is a legally binding document made by the patient (with capacity) in advance, which can refuse any treatment including CPR and other life-sustaining treatments. A clinically made DNACPR can be overridden by the patient with capacity or revoked; an ADRT that validly and specifically refuses CPR is legally binding and cannot be overridden by the clinical team or family.

What is the SPIKES protocol and when should it be used?

SPIKES is a structured framework for delivering bad news: Setting, Perception, Invitation, Knowledge, Empathy, Strategy and Summary. It is used whenever significant or distressing information must be communicated — a cancer diagnosis, prognosis conversations, withdrawal of treatment discussions, or notifying death to family. The key clinical skills tested are: tailoring information to what the patient already knows (Perception), respecting preferences for information (Invitation), using clear language without jargon (Knowledge), and acknowledging emotions before moving to a plan (Empathy). These scenarios appear in UKMLA OSCEs as well as AKT clinical reasoning questions.

What is the role of dexamethasone in palliative care?

Dexamethasone has multiple palliative indications: raised intracranial pressure (8–16mg daily to reduce vasogenic oedema around brain metastases), spinal cord compression (16mg daily as high-dose dexamethasone — urgent alongside radiotherapy), bone pain (anti-inflammatory adjuvant), appetite stimulation and general wellbeing (low dose 2–4mg daily — effect tends to wane after 4–6 weeks), superior vena cava obstruction (reduces oedema), airway obstruction, nausea (as antiemetic adjuvant in chemotherapy). Side effects: hyperglycaemia, insomnia (give in morning), mood changes, proximal myopathy, immunosuppression, Cushing features with prolonged use. Taper when stopping.

Conclusion: Palliative Care in the UKMLA

Palliative care in the UKMLA AKT is not merely about memorising drug doses — it tests the integration of ethical principles, legal frameworks, clinical symptom management, and communication skills. Candidates who approach end-of-life questions with a holistic framework — considering what matters most to the patient, applying the Mental Capacity Act correctly, and choosing evidence-based symptom management — will perform well across this domain.

The most tested areas are: opioid dose calculation and conversion, syringe driver drug compatibility, ADRT legal validity for life-sustaining treatment refusal, the opt-out organ donation system (recent and high-yield), and DNACPR principles. The SPIKES protocol is often tested in clinical communication scenarios within the UKMLA assessment framework.
