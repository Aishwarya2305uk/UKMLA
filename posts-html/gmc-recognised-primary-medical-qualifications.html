Every international medical graduate applying to the GMC eventually asks the same underlying question: is my primary medical qualification GMC accepted, or will my basic medical degree be rejected before I even reach the UKMLA? It is a reasonable worry, because unlike some regulators, the GMC does not publish a simple green-light list of approved universities that a candidate can scroll through and tick off. Instead, recognition works institution-by-institution and evidence-by-evidence, built around two checkable processes: confirming your medical school appears in the World Directory of Medical Schools, and independently verifying your specific certificate through the required verification route.

This guide is deliberately generic — it applies to a medical degree from any country, not one nationality's system in particular. If you trained in India specifically and want a country-specific walkthrough of MCI/NMC-era degrees, private versus government colleges, and the EPIC process as it applies to Indian graduates, see the site's dedicated guide on whether an Indian MBBS is valid for UK registration, and the companion piece on GMC registration and credential verification for Indian applicants. What follows here is the underlying framework that applies to every source country: what a primary medical qualification (PMQ) actually means in GMC terms, how to check whether your own qualification is recognised, what happens when a qualification needs additional evidence, and the misconceptions that cause the most unnecessary anxiety among IMGs worldwide.

The single most important thing to understand before reading any further is this: the GMC does not maintain a fixed "approved country list" in the way some people expect. There is no page where a candidate types in "Nigeria" or "Philippines" or "Egypt" and receives a blanket yes or no. Instead, the GMC's acceptability test looks at the specific institution that awarded your degree and the specific graduation year, checked against the World Directory of Medical Schools, and then verifies your individual certificate through the required verification service. Understanding this process — rather than searching for a nonexistent universal ruling — is what actually moves a candidate from uncertainty to a documented, confirmed answer.

Is My Primary Medical Qualification GMC Accepted? The Quick Answer

A primary medical qualification is GMC accepted when it satisfies two independent tests, both of which the GMC checks directly rather than taking on trust. First, the awarding institution must appear in the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) for the relevant period, since WDOMS listing status can and does change over time, and the GMC checks the listing as it applied when the qualification was awarded. Second, the specific degree certificate, transcripts, and (where applicable) internship or clinical training completion must be independently verified through the GMC's required verification route — most commonly the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC) service operated via Intealth (the organisation that also runs the Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates, ECFMG), or an equivalent recognised verification pathway.

When both conditions are met, the qualification is accepted as a valid primary medical qualification for GMC registration purposes. Meeting these two conditions does not, on its own, grant a licence to practise — it only confirms that the underlying degree clears the first gate. A doctor whose PMQ is accepted must still pass the UKMLA (the Applied Knowledge Test and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment), provide accepted evidence of English language proficiency, and submit a complete registration application before receiving full registration with a licence to practise.

What Counts as a Primary Medical Qualification (PMQ)?

A primary medical qualification is the GMC's term for the basic, undergraduate-level medical degree that first qualifies someone as a doctor in their home education system — commonly titled MBBS, MBChB, MD, or an equivalent local designation, depending on the country. It is "primary" in the sense that it is the foundational qualification, as distinct from postgraduate specialist or GP qualifications that a doctor might acquire later in their career. Every applicant for GMC registration, whether applying via the UKMLA route or an alternative pathway, must provide evidence of a PMQ as one of the core building blocks of their application.

Importantly, the GMC's PMQ requirement is not simply about holding a piece of paper with the word "doctor" printed on it. The qualification must represent a complete, recognised programme of undergraduate medical education — meaning a full course of study (typically five to six years internationally, depending on the country's system) that included both the theoretical curriculum and supervised clinical training, culminating in an actual degree conferred by a recognised institution. A partial qualification, an intercalated degree taken mid-programme, or a qualification from a programme that was never completed does not meet the bar.

PMQ vs Postgraduate Qualifications

It is worth being explicit about a distinction that trips up some applicants: a PMQ is not the same thing as a postgraduate qualification such as a Membership of the Royal Colleges equivalent, a Fellowship, or a specialist certification obtained after years of practice. The GMC assesses these separately, under different rules, when a doctor applies for registration in a specialist or GP register rather than (or in addition to) the standard register route most IMGs use. A doctor with an impressive postgraduate qualification but no verifiable PMQ still cannot register, because the PMQ is the foundational evidence every application requires — postgraduate credentials build on top of it, they do not substitute for it.

Why "Approved List" Thinking Leads Applicants Astray

A great deal of anxiety among IMGs stems from assuming that the GMC operates the same way as some other regulators — publishing a definitive list of accepted countries or a fixed roster of approved universities. In practice, the GMC's own published guidance frames acceptability in terms of institutional listing and verification, not a static "approved nations" register. This matters because the same country can contain both WDOMS-listed institutions in good standing and institutions with lapsed, disputed, or historically inconsistent listing status. Consequently, two graduates from the same country, even the same city, cannot always assume identical outcomes purely because they share a nationality — the relevant unit of analysis is the specific medical school and the specific graduation period, not the country as a whole.

This is, in fact, a reassuring structure once it is properly understood, rather than a source of extra worry. It means that a candidate's eligibility is a checkable, objective fact about their own institution and graduation year — not a subjective judgement about which countries the UK considers prestigious enough. The practical task, therefore, is never "does the UK accept degrees from my country," but rather "is my specific medical school listed for my specific graduation year, and can my specific certificate be verified." Every section that follows in this guide exists to help answer exactly that pair of questions.

The World Directory of Medical Schools: How It Works

The World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS) is a free, publicly searchable database of the world's medical schools, jointly maintained by the World Federation for Medical Education (WFME) and FAIMER (a division of Intealth). It lists thousands of undergraduate medical education programmes worldwide, both currently operating and historical, and records key details such as the institution's official name, country, and the years for which it holds recognised, listed status.

For GMC purposes, WDOMS listing is the first and most fundamental test applied to any overseas PMQ. The GMC's published guidance on acceptable overseas qualifications states that a primary medical qualification must have been awarded by an institution listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools. This is a necessary, though not on its own sufficient, condition — WDOMS listing establishes that the institution is a genuine, recognised medical school; it does not, by itself, verify that any particular individual's degree from that school is genuine, which is what the separate primary source verification step exists to confirm.

How to Search WDOMS for Your Own Medical School

  • Go to the official search tool at search.wdoms.org — this is the free, official interface; avoid unofficial mirror sites or forum-shared spreadsheets, since listing data changes and outdated copies can be misleading.
  • Search using the exact institutional name on your degree certificate, not necessarily the informal or commonly used name for your school. Many university systems, especially those with affiliated or constituent college structures, issue degrees under a specific college name that differs from the parent university's name — and it is the awarding institution's precise name that matters for the WDOMS check.
  • Check the listed years of operation and recognition against your own graduation year specifically. A school's listing status is not necessarily constant across its entire history — some institutions have had gaps in formal listing at certain points while continuing to operate and graduate students, so the years matter as much as the name.
  • Note the exact spelling and any historical name changes. Some medical schools have changed names, merged, or been renamed by government reorganisation over the decades; WDOMS entries sometimes record this history, and it is worth checking former names if a search under your degree certificate's current name returns nothing.

It is worth pausing on one especially important clarification the World Directory's own operators make explicit: a listing in WDOMS confirms that a medical school exists and has been through the Directory's own inclusion process — it does not, by itself, constitute recognition, accreditation, or endorsement by the World Directory, WFME, FAIMER, or the GMC. Being listed is the necessary starting point the GMC checks, but the GMC still applies its own separate judgement about whether a specific qualification meets its standards, and it still requires the qualification to be independently verified before accepting it. Treat WDOMS listing as confirmation you have cleared the first hurdle, not as a guarantee of the final outcome.

What if My Medical School Has Multiple Names or Campuses?

Many countries operate university systems where a single parent university oversees several affiliated or constituent colleges, each of which may (or may not) hold its own separate WDOMS listing. In these systems, what matters for GMC purposes is the WDOMS status of the specific institution that actually delivered your training and issued your degree certificate — not the parent university's listing status considered in isolation. If your degree certificate names a specific college or campus, search for that exact name first; if the search returns no clear result, try the parent university's name as a secondary check, and consider contacting your registrar's office for clarification on which entity is the "awarding institution" for WDOMS purposes.

Primary Source Verification and EPIC: What It Actually Checks

Even once a medical school's WDOMS listing is confirmed, the GMC still requires each individual applicant's own qualification to be independently verified — a process generally referred to as primary source verification. For the vast majority of overseas applicants, this verification is carried out through the Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials (EPIC), a service historically run by the Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates (ECFMG) and now operated under Intealth, the umbrella organisation that also runs ECFMG and FAIMER. Since 2024, much of the applicant-facing verification workflow has migrated to Intealth's newer MyIntealth platform, though "EPIC verification" remains the term most GMC guidance, applicants, and third-party resources use for this step — the underlying purpose has not changed even as the specific technical platform has been updated.

Primary source verification means exactly what it sounds like: rather than accepting your degree certificate and transcripts at face value, Intealth/ECFMG contacts your medical school directly — the primary source — to confirm that the specific documents you have submitted genuinely match the institution's own internal records. This is a materially more rigorous check than simply reviewing a scanned certificate, and it exists specifically to prevent fraudulent or altered qualifications from entering medical registers internationally, not just in the UK.

What the Verification Process Involves

  1. Create an account with the verification service (currently via MyIntealth) and provide your personal and educational details exactly as they appear on your original documents, since mismatches between your application and your certificate can cause delays.
  2. Upload the required evidence of your primary medical qualification. The GMC's guidance is specific about document quality: you must upload a clear, legible scanned copy of your PMQ certificate, free of blur, glare, or shadows, that clearly shows the degree awarded and the awarding institution's name.
  3. The verification service contacts your medical school directly to confirm your degree, graduation date, and (where applicable) any required internship or supervised practice period, checking these details against the school's own records rather than relying on the documents you provided alone.
  4. Your medical school responds, confirming or querying the details. This step is the single biggest source of variable timing in the entire process, because response speed depends entirely on your specific institution's administrative office, not on you or on any GMC processing queue.
  5. The GMC receives confirmation that your qualification has been successfully verified, and this becomes part of your registration application evidence.

Because step four above is genuinely unpredictable, the single most effective action any IMG can take to shorten their overall registration timeline is to start primary source verification as early as possible — ideally in parallel with UKMLA preparation rather than after it. Doctors who wait until they have passed both components of the exam before initiating verification frequently find that the verification step, not the exam itself, becomes the longest-running item on their critical path to registration.

Why Verification Timing Varies So Much Between Countries and Institutions

A frequent source of frustration for IMGs is discovering that a colleague from a different medical school completed verification in a matter of weeks, while their own request has taken several months with no clear resolution. This variation is real, and it is worth understanding why, rather than assuming something has gone uniquely wrong with an individual application. Verification speed depends on factors specific to each institution: whether the school has a dedicated, responsive records office; whether records from the relevant graduation era have been digitised or must be retrieved from physical archives; how many similar verification requests the school receives and how it prioritises them; and, in some cases, national holidays, administrative restructuring, or temporary staffing gaps at the institution. None of these factors reflect on the applicant personally or on the underlying validity of their qualification — they are purely a function of institutional administrative capacity, which is exactly why starting the process early, and following up periodically rather than waiting passively, is worthwhile regardless of which country or school is involved.

What if My Medical School Isn't Easily Found or Verification Stalls?

Occasionally, an applicant discovers that their medical school's WDOMS status is unclear, that a name search returns no results, or that verification requests seem to have stalled with no response. This is considerably less common than anxious online forum discussions sometimes suggest, but when it does happen, it is solvable with a methodical approach rather than guesswork or panic.

Steps to Take if WDOMS Listing Is Unclear

  • Re-check the exact institutional name and try historical or alternative spellings. Transliteration differences, government-mandated renaming, or mergers between institutions can all cause a straightforward name search to miss a genuine, valid listing.
  • Contact your medical school's registrar or academic affairs office directly. Most institutions that have gone through any WDOMS listing history can confirm their own accreditation status and, in many cases, provide a letter or documentation useful for a GMC enquiry.
  • Raise a pre-application enquiry directly with the GMC. Rather than submitting a full, fee-bearing application and risking an unfavourable outcome or lengthy delay, applicants with a genuinely uncertain WDOMS situation can ask the GMC directly, in advance, whether their specific qualification is likely to be acceptable. This produces a tailored answer far more reliable than general online guidance, which cannot capture every institution's specific edge case.
  • Keep dated, written records of every enquiry and response. Correspondence with your medical school and with the GMC becomes valuable supporting evidence later if any part of your eligibility is questioned during the formal application, so retain copies rather than relying on memory of phone calls or informal replies.

Steps to Take if Verification Is Delayed

If your medical school has confirmed WDOMS listing but the actual EPIC/Intealth verification request appears to have stalled, a few practical actions tend to help. First, contact your medical school's registrar directly, rather than only the verification service, since delays are frequently on the institution's side rather than the platform's. Second, keep your own MyIntealth or EPIC account details and application reference numbers organised, since follow-up queries move faster when you can cite a specific case reference. Third, if a genuinely extended delay (several months with no institutional response at all) arises, consider raising the specific situation with the GMC's registration enquiries team, since in rare cases alternative forms of evidence or an alternative verification route may be available — this is assessed case by case, not by a fixed published rule, so a direct enquiry is the only reliable way to find out what applies to your specific circumstances.

It is worth stating plainly: taking these steps early, ideally before booking any exam sitting or making significant financial commitments to preparation courses, avoids the single costliest mistake IMGs make in this area — investing heavily in UKMLA preparation before confirming that the underlying qualification question is settled. Common Misconceptions About PMQ Recognition

Because the GMC's process differs from what many applicants expect based on other countries' systems, a handful of specific misconceptions recur constantly across IMG forums and informal advice networks. Addressing them directly here prevents unnecessary worry that has no basis in how the process actually works.

"There Is a Fixed List of Approved Countries"

This is the single most persistent misconception, and it is not accurate. The GMC does not publish a country-level approved list; acceptability is determined institution-by-institution through WDOMS listing and then verified certificate-by-certificate through primary source verification. Two graduates from the same country can have genuinely different outcomes if they attended different institutions with different listing histories — the relevant unit of assessment is always the specific medical school and graduation year, never the country as a whole.

"Older Degrees Are Automatically Disadvantaged"

Some applicants assume that a degree awarded many years ago, or awarded under a domestic regulatory system that has since been reformed or replaced, is somehow devalued for GMC purposes. In general, this is not the case: what matters is whether the institution held valid WDOMS listing status covering the specific year of graduation, not which domestic regulatory body happened to oversee medical education in that country at that time. A subsequent change in a country's own medical education governance does not retroactively alter the GMC's assessment of a degree that was validly awarded under the rules in place at the time.

"A Prestigious or Government Institution Automatically Counts for More"

The GMC's test is WDOMS listing status, not institutional prestige, reputation, or public-versus-private ownership. A less internationally famous institution that holds valid WDOMS listing carries exactly the same standing, for PMQ-recognition purposes, as a widely known flagship university with the same listing status. Applicants should resist the temptation to assume that attending a smaller or less well-known school disadvantages their application — the checkable fact is the listing itself, not the school's public profile.

"My Admission Route or Entrance Exam Score Matters"

Whatever domestic entrance examination or admissions process a country uses to allocate medical school places — however competitive or well regarded — has no bearing on GMC recognition once a degree has actually been awarded by a WDOMS-listed institution. The GMC's acceptability assessment does not review entrance exam scores, admissions rankings, or the selectivity of the admissions process; it reviews the awarding institution's listing status and the authenticity of the individual's specific qualification.

"If My School Is Listed, Registration Is Guaranteed"

This is the opposite misconception, and it is equally important to correct. WDOMS listing and successful primary source verification confirm that a candidate's primary medical qualification is GMC accepted as a foundational qualification — they do not, by themselves, grant registration or a licence to practise. A doctor whose PMQ clears both checks must still pass the UKMLA, provide accepted evidence of English language proficiency, and submit a complete registration application. Recognition of the degree and registration of the person are two genuinely separate steps, and conflating them leads some candidates to either under-prepare for the exams (assuming degree recognition was the hard part) or to over-worry about degree recognition long after it has effectively been settled.

How PMQ Recognition Fits Into the Wider Registration Journey

Confirming that a primary medical qualification is accepted is only the first stage of a longer registration pathway, and it is worth being explicit about where it sits relative to everything else an IMG must complete. Once WDOMS listing and primary source verification are confirmed, candidates still need to satisfy the GMC's English language requirement — most commonly demonstrated through IELTS or OET, each with its own accepted score thresholds and validity windows, covered in detail in the site's comparison of OET versus IELTS for GMC registration. Only once the qualification, English language, and (where applicable) internship requirements are all satisfied does a candidate move on to sitting and passing the UKMLA itself, followed by submitting the full registration application with all supporting evidence. Leaving it unresolved, or relying on secondhand assumptions about how the process works, is the single most common source of avoidable delay in the entire pathway. PMQ Recognition for UK Medical Students vs IMGs

Although this guide is primarily written for internationally trained doctors, it is worth being clear about how the PMQ requirement applies differently to UK-trained medical students, since the two audiences often research this topic side by side and can otherwise come away confused about which parts apply to them.

UK medical students graduating from a GMC-recognised UK medical school automatically satisfy the primary medical qualification requirement without any WDOMS search or primary source verification process — a UK degree from a UK medical school is accepted as a matter of course, since UK medical schools' curricula and quality assurance are already directly overseen by the GMC. For UK students, the PMQ question essentially resolves itself as a consequence of where they trained; there is no separate recognition step to complete. Once a UK medical student is in their final year, the relevant documentation is generally coordinated through their medical school, and the GMC has increasingly moved towards provisional registration processes that begin before graduation itself.

For IMGs, by contrast, PMQ recognition is never automatic and never assumed — it requires the active WDOMS and verification steps set out throughout this guide, regardless of how well regarded a candidate's home country's medical education system may be internationally. This is not a judgement about the quality of overseas medical training; it reflects the simple fact that the GMC has direct oversight of UK medical schools' curricula in a way it structurally cannot have over the thousands of medical schools operating outside the UK, which is exactly why an independent, evidence-based verification process exists for overseas qualifications specifically.

Why Precise Verification Matters for Patient Safety and Trust

It is worth briefly explaining why the GMC applies such a rigorous, evidence-based process to PMQ recognition rather than adopting a lighter-touch, list-based approach — because understanding the rationale makes the process feel less like arbitrary bureaucracy and more like a considered, patient-safety-driven system. Medical qualifications sit at the foundation of public trust in a doctor's competence; a regulator that accepted degree certificates at face value, without independent verification against the awarding institution's own records, would be vulnerable to fraudulent or falsified qualifications entering the medical register. Primary source verification exists specifically to close that gap, and it is applied consistently to every overseas applicant, regardless of nationality or the perceived standing of their home country's medical education system.

This same logic explains why WDOMS itself exists as an international, multi-regulator resource rather than a UK-specific tool: medical regulators in the United States, Australia, Canada, and many other countries rely on the same underlying directory and closely related verification principles when assessing their own international medical graduates, precisely because a shared, internationally recognised reference reduces duplicated effort and increases consistency across borders. An applicant who understands how the UK's system works has, in the process, learned a broadly transferable framework relevant to evaluating other major destination countries as well, since the same two questions — is the school internationally listed, and can the specific qualification be independently verified — recur in some form across nearly every serious medical regulator worldwide.

Practical Checklist Before You Apply

Bringing together every element covered in this guide, the following sequence reflects the order in which an IMG should realistically approach PMQ recognition, to avoid the common mistake of investing heavily in exam preparation before the underlying qualification question is settled.

  1. Identify the exact awarding institution name as it appears on your original degree certificate, checking for affiliated-college structures where the certificate-issuing body may differ from the parent university's name.
  2. Search the World Directory of Medical Schools at search.wdoms.org using that exact name, and confirm the listed years of recognition explicitly cover your graduation year.
  3. If the listing is unclear, contact your medical school's registrar for accreditation documentation, and consider a pre-application enquiry directly with the GMC for a definitive, tailored answer.
  4. Register with the required verification service (currently MyIntealth, historically referred to as EPIC) and begin primary source verification as early as possible, in parallel with other preparation rather than after it.
  5. Gather supporting documents, including your degree certificate, complete academic transcripts, and any internship or supervised-practice completion evidence your specific registration route requires.
  6. Address English language requirements alongside qualification verification, rather than treating them as a later, separate stage — see the comparison of OET and IELTS routes for GMC registration for how to choose between the two accepted tests.
  7. Only then commit fully to structured UKMLA preparation, with the confidence that the underlying qualification question has already been resolved rather than left as an open risk hanging over exam planning and financial commitments.

Sequencing the process this way — qualification recognition first, verification and English evidence running in parallel, exam preparation building on a settled foundation — consistently produces a smoother, less stressful path towards full GMC registration than tackling these stages in an ad hoc order dictated by whichever step happens to feel most urgent at the time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is my primary medical qualification GMC accepted if my country isn't on a specific approved list?

The GMC does not maintain a fixed country-level approved list, so there is no list to check your country against. Acceptability depends on whether your specific medical school is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year and whether your individual qualification can be independently verified — this is assessed institution-by-institution, not country-by-country.

How do I check if my medical school is listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools?

Search for your medical school by its exact awarding institution name at the official World Directory of Medical Schools tool, search.wdoms.org, and confirm the years of recognised listing cover your specific graduation date. If your certificate names an affiliated college rather than a parent university, search using that specific name first.

What is EPIC verification and do I definitely need it?

EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) is the primary source verification service, historically run by ECFMG and now operated under the Intealth umbrella (increasingly via the MyIntealth platform), that confirms your degree certificate genuinely matches your medical school's own records. Almost all applicants with a primary medical qualification awarded outside the UK need to complete this verification step as part of their GMC registration application.

What happens if my medical school won't respond to a verification request?

Contact your medical school's registrar or academic affairs office directly to follow up, since delays are usually institutional rather than a fault in the verification platform itself. If a significant delay persists with no response, raise the specific situation directly with the GMC, since alternative evidence routes are occasionally considered on a case-by-case basis.

Does an older medical degree count for less than a recent one?

No. What matters is whether your medical school held valid World Directory of Medical Schools listing status covering the specific year you graduated, not how long ago that was or which domestic regulatory body oversaw medical education in your country at the time. A subsequent change to your country's own medical education governance does not retroactively affect a degree that was validly awarded under the rules in place when you graduated.

Is a private medical college's degree treated differently from a government college's degree?

No. GMC acceptability depends entirely on the awarding institution's World Directory of Medical Schools listing status, not on whether the institution is privately run or government-funded. A WDOMS-listed private institution carries exactly the same standing as a WDOMS-listed government institution for primary medical qualification purposes.

Does WDOMS listing alone guarantee my registration will be approved?

No. WDOMS listing and successful primary source verification confirm that your primary medical qualification is accepted as a foundational qualification, but they do not by themselves grant registration. You must still pass the UKMLA, provide accepted evidence of English language proficiency, and submit a complete registration application before receiving a licence to practise.

Conclusion

Whether a primary medical qualification GMC accepted status applies to your own degree comes down to two checkable facts, not a guessing game: does your medical school appear in the World Directory of Medical Schools for your graduation year, and can your specific certificate be independently verified through the required verification route. There is no shortcut around either step, and there is no fixed country-level list that can answer the question faster than checking your own institution directly. IMGs who confirm these two points early, in parallel with English language preparation, consistently move through the wider registration journey with far less uncertainty than those who leave the qualification question unresolved until later.
