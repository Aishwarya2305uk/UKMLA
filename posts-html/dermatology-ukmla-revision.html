Dermatology UKMLA questions test four core competencies: describing skin lesions accurately using precise terminology, recognising common inflammatory dermatoses and their stepped management, identifying skin cancer red flags and applying 2WW referral criteria, and spotting drug reactions and systemic disease skin manifestations. Although candidates rarely spend extended time on dermatology wards, dermatology UKMLA AKT questions appear consistently across systems — rashes occur in infectious disease, rheumatology, oncology, and paediatrics. This guide covers every high-yield dermatology UKMLA topic with glossaries, management tables, and FAQs aligned to NICE and BAD (British Association of Dermatologists) guidelines.

Skin Lesion Vocabulary: Primary and Secondary Lesions

Dermatology UKMLA questions frequently require accurate lesion description before diagnosis. The AKT may use photographs and ask candidates to identify the morphological type. Systematic description covers: type (primary/secondary), size, shape, colour, border, distribution, surface characteristics, and associated features.

Eczema: Stepped Management

Atopic eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterised by itchy, dry, inflamed skin. It follows a relapsing-remitting course. Associations: asthma, allergic rhinitis, food allergies (filaggrin gene mutations increase susceptibility). NICE (NG190, 2023) recommends a stepped approach based on severity.

StepSeverityTreatment
Step 1Mild (dry skin, infrequent itching, small area)Emollients (apply liberally multiple times daily; leave-on and wash-off); avoid soap; mild potency topical corticosteroid (hydrocortisone 1%) for flares
Step 2Mild-moderate (frequent itching, redness, some sleep disturbance)Moderate potency TCS (clobetasone butyrate 0.05%) for face/flexures; moderately potent or potent TCS for body; topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) for face/sensitive areas
Step 3Moderate (daily itching, widespread, sleep disturbance)Potent TCS (betamethasone valerate 0.1%) for body; wet wrap therapy; bandaging; sedating antihistamines (chlorphenamine) short-term for sleep; refer to specialist
Step 4Severe (continuous itching, widespread, severe sleep loss)Very potent TCS (clobetasol propionate 0.05%) short-term; dupilumab (IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor — NICE approved for adults and adolescents with moderate-severe uncontrolled AD); tralokinumab; systemic immunosuppressants (ciclosporin, methotrexate, azathioprine)

Topical corticosteroid (TCS) potency ladder: mild (hydrocortisone 1%), moderate (clobetasone butyrate 0.05%), potent (betamethasone valerate 0.1%, mometasone furoate 0.1%), very potent (clobetasol propionate 0.05%). Side effects of TCS: skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasia, purpura, perioral dermatitis, steroid acne. Use lowest effective potency; never use potent/very potent on face; fingertip unit (FTU) guides quantity — 1 FTU = 500 mg = adequate for area of 2 adult palms.

Psoriasis: Types and Management

Psoriasis is a chronic immune-mediated inflammatory skin disease with systemic associations (psoriatic arthritis in ~30%). Chronic plaque psoriasis (psoriasis vulgaris) is the most common type — well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery-white scale on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, and lumbosacral area. Nail changes: pitting, onycholysis, oil-drop sign, subungual hyperkeratosis. Auspitz sign: pinpoint bleeding when scale removed. Koebner phenomenon: new psoriasis at sites of skin trauma.

Skin Cancer: Diagnosis, Referral, and Management

Skin cancer is the most common cancer in the UK. NICE NG12 sets out 2WW referral criteria. The ABCDE criteria for melanoma recognition: Asymmetry, Border (irregular), Colour (two or more colours or uneven), Diameter (>6 mm), Evolution (changing over time). Any suspicion of melanoma warrants a 2WW referral. Dermoscopy by trained clinicians improves diagnostic accuracy.

Autoimmune Blistering Disorders and Inflammatory Conditions

Autoimmune blistering disorders are a well-defined area of dermatology UKMLA questions. Bullous pemphigoid (BP): the most common autoimmune blistering disorder; affects elderly patients; IgG antibodies against BP180 and BP230 in the basement membrane zone causing subepidermal blistering. Clinical features: tense, intact bullae on erythematous or urticarial base; pruritic; inner thighs, flexures, abdomen. Nikolsky's sign is negative (unlike pemphigus). Diagnosis: skin biopsy for H&E (subepidermal blister with eosinophil infiltrate) and direct immunofluorescence (linear IgG and C3 at BMZ). Treatment: superpotent topical corticosteroids (clobetasol propionate) for localised disease; oral prednisolone for extensive; doxycycline + nicotinamide (steroid-sparing); rituximab for refractory. Pemphigus vulgaris (PV): IgG antibodies against desmoglein 1 and 3 (keratinocyte cell adhesion molecules) causing intraepidermal blistering. Clinical features: flaccid, easily ruptured bullae; mucosal involvement (mouth ulcers often first); Nikolsky's sign positive; predominantly in middle-aged adults; Jewish and Middle Eastern populations at higher risk. Diagnosis: biopsy shows intraepidermal acantholysis (loss of intercellular adhesion, "tombstone" cells); DIF shows intercellular IgG (chicken-wire pattern). Treatment: oral prednisolone high-dose; immunosuppressants (azathioprine, mycophenolate); rituximab increasingly first-line for moderate-severe PV.

Dermatitis herpetiformis (DH): extremely itchy vesicular rash on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees, buttocks, scalp); associated with coeliac disease (virtually all patients have gluten-sensitive enteropathy); IgA deposits in dermal papillae on DIF (granular pattern, unlike BP which is linear). Treatment: gluten-free diet (resolves DH and coeliac enteropathy over months-years); dapsone for rapid symptom control (check G6PD before starting — haemolytic anaemia risk). Lichen planus: pruritic, violaceous, flat-topped polygonal papules; Wickham's striae (white lacy network on surface); predilection for wrists, ankles, lower back; mucosal involvement (white lacy patches in mouth — buccal mucosa, risk of SCC); nail involvement (pterygium, thinning). Cause: unknown, possibly T-cell mediated; associated with HCV infection; drug-induced LP (gold, antimalarials, thiazides, beta-blockers). Treatment: topical corticosteroids; systemic steroids for widespread; retinoids, tacrolimus for mucosal involvement.

Leg Ulcers: Venous, Arterial, and Neuropathic

Leg ulcers are a common clinical problem encountered by foundation doctors, particularly in elderly patients. Accurate classification guides management, and misclassification (e.g. applying compression to an arterial ulcer) can cause serious harm. Venous leg ulcers: most common type (70%); occur in the gaiter area (medial ankle, above medial malleolus); irregular shape; sloping edges; base may be granulating or sloughy; surrounded by lipodermatosclerosis (indurated, hyperpigmented skin), varicose eczema, haemosiderin deposition. ABPI (ankle-brachial pressure index) measures arterial supply: normal 0.9–1.3; mild arterial disease 0.7–0.9; significant 0.5–0.7; critical ischaemia below 0.5. Management of venous ulcers: compression bandaging (four-layer bandage system or equivalent — do NOT apply if ABPI below 0.8); leg elevation; wound debridement; treat underlying venous insufficiency (duplex USS; varicose vein treatment). Arterial ulcers: punched-out appearance; painful; on pressure points (heels, toes, malleoli); cold, pale, pulseless limb; ABPI below 0.5. Management: vascular surgical referral, angioplasty or bypass, wound care. Neuropathic ulcers: painless ulcers on pressure areas (metatarsal heads, heel) in diabetics or those with peripheral neuropathy; well-defined edges; surrounded by callus; deep, may probe to bone (osteomyelitis risk). Management: offloading (total contact cast, special footwear), glycaemic control, wound care, antibiotics if infected, consider MRI for osteomyelitis.

Pressure ulcers: caused by prolonged pressure on bony prominences impairing microcirculation; at risk patients include elderly, immobile, incontinent, malnourished, unconscious. National Pressure Ulcer Advisory Panel (NPUAP) classification: Grade 1 — non-blanching erythema, intact skin; Grade 2 — partial-thickness skin loss, shallow ulcer; Grade 3 — full-thickness skin loss, subcutaneous visible; Grade 4 — full-thickness tissue loss, bone/tendon/muscle visible. Prevention: pressure-relieving mattresses and cushions, two-hourly repositioning, skin inspection, nutritional support, incontinence management. Treatment: wound debridement, appropriate dressings (moisture-balancing, antimicrobial), negative-pressure wound therapy for complex wounds, surgical debridement and flap repair for severe ulcers. Document and report all new pressure ulcers as a patient safety incident — grade 3 and 4 pressure ulcers acquired in hospital are classified as serious incidents.

Hair and Nail Disorders in the UKMLA

Hair disorders are testable in the dermatology UKMLA AKT, particularly alopecia. Androgenetic alopecia (male-pattern/female-pattern hair loss): most common cause; androgen-dependent miniaturisation of hair follicles; in men — bitemporal recession and vertex thinning (Hamilton-Norwood classification); in women — diffuse crown thinning with frontal hairline preserved (Ludwig classification). Treatment: topical minoxidil (men and women); finasteride 1 mg daily (men only — inhibits 5-alpha reductase; avoid in women of childbearing potential); hair transplant surgery. Alopecia areata: autoimmune, T-cell mediated attack on hair follicles; well-circumscribed patches of non-scarring hair loss; exclamation mark hairs at periphery; associated with thyroid disease and vitiligo. Alopecia totalis (entire scalp) or universalis (entire body) in severe cases. Treatment: potent topical corticosteroids; intralesional triamcinolone (for localised patches); DPCP (contact immunotherapy); baricitinib (JAK inhibitor) is NICE approved for severe alopecia areata. Telogen effluvium: diffuse hair shedding 2–3 months after a trigger (surgery, illness, childbirth, crash dieting, hypothyroidism, iron deficiency); hair count on pull test positive (>6 telogen hairs per pull); self-limiting; treat underlying cause. Tinea capitis: dermatophyte infection of the scalp; children; scaly patches, broken hairs, lymphadenopathy; Wood's lamp fluorescence (Microsporum species); requires systemic antifungal (oral terbinafine or griseofulvin) — topical alone is ineffective for scalp.

Nail disorders: onychomycosis (tinea unguium) — distal/lateral subungual infection most common; dystrophic, thickened, discoloured nails; confirm with nail clippings for MC&S before treating (to avoid unnecessary long courses of systemic antifungals); treatment: oral terbinafine 250 mg daily for 6 weeks (fingernails) or 12 weeks (toenails). Psoriatic nail disease: pitting, onycholysis, oil-drop sign, subungual hyperkeratosis, splinter haemorrhages. Beau's lines: transverse grooves across the nail plate following severe systemic illness (the nail temporarily stops growing). Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails): iron deficiency anaemia. Leukonychia (white nails): hypoalbuminaemia (liver disease, nephrotic syndrome). Terry's nails (proximal white, distal normal band): hepatic cirrhosis, heart failure. Clubbing: COPD, IPF, cyanotic heart disease, IBD, hepatic cirrhosis, mesothelioma, bronchiectasis — increased nail-fold angle (>180°) and fluctuation of the nail bed.

Frequently Asked Questions About Dermatology in the UKMLA

How do UKMLA AKT questions use dermatology photographs?

The AKT includes single-best-answer questions with clinical images, including dermatology photographs. The approach is the same as in clinical practice: describe the lesion systematically (distribution, morphology, colour, borders, secondary changes) before trying to name the diagnosis. Common AKT dermatology images include psoriasis plaques (silver scale on extensor surfaces), eczema (flexural, excoriated), impetigo (golden crust), shingles (dermatomal vesicles), melanoma (ABCDE changes), and BCC (pearly nodule with rolled edge). Practise describing images aloud during revision — it trains pattern recognition and reinforces terminology.

What are the 2WW referral criteria for skin cancer in NICE NG12?

NICE NG12 criteria for suspected skin cancer 2WW referral: melanoma — any lesion with two or more ABCDE features, a dermoscopically suspicious lesion, or any nodule without obvious benign cause; SCC — firm scaly or ulcerated nodule, especially in sun-damaged or immunosuppressed skin; BCC — pearly nodular lesion with telangiectasia. Urgent non-2WW referral: actinic keratosis (routine unless rapidly changing). Note that 2WW for skin cancer applies when the clinician suspects cancer — not for every pigmented lesion. Dermatoscopy by trained primary care clinicians can reduce unnecessary referrals.

What is Stevens-Johnson Syndrome and how is it different from TEN?

Both SJS and TEN (toxic epidermal necrolysis) are severe cutaneous adverse drug reactions characterised by mucosal involvement, target lesions or maculae, and epidermal detachment. The distinction is the percentage of body surface area affected: SJS involves <10% BSA detachment, SJS/TEN overlap involves 10–30%, and TEN involves >30%. Nikolsky's sign is positive in TEN (lateral pressure causes skin to shear). Both require immediate drug withdrawal and urgent specialist management — ophthalmology is critical (corneal scarring can cause blindness). Mortality of TEN is 20–30%; SCORTEN score is used for prognosis (0–1 = 3.2% mortality; ≥5 = 90%). Commonest culprit drugs: allopurinol, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, sulfonamides, NSAIDs. HLA-B*1502 screening before carbamazepine in East Asian patients reduces risk.

What is the difference between erysipelas and cellulitis?

Both are bacterial skin infections presenting with erythema, warmth, swelling, and pain. The key distinction is depth of infection: erysipelas involves the superficial dermis and lymphatics — the border is sharply demarcated and raised, and it is commonly caused by Streptococcus pyogenes. Cellulitis involves the deep dermis and subcutaneous tissue — the border is poorly defined and not raised. Erysipelas more commonly affects the face (classic butterfly distribution) or lower legs. Both respond to penicillin-based antibiotics, but erysipelas is more reliably treated with penicillin V (streptococcal), while cellulitis covering staphylococcal cover (flucloxacillin) is preferred. Bilateral leg erythema should raise the question of venous eczema/stasis dermatitis rather than cellulitis, which is almost always unilateral.

How do I manage acne in a woman of childbearing potential in the UKMLA?

This is a classic UKMLA question combining dermatology and prescribing safety. In a woman of childbearing potential with moderate acne: avoid isotretinoin unless all REMS conditions are met (two forms of contraception, monthly pregnancy tests, specialist prescribing). Oral tetracyclines (doxycycline, lymecycline) are safe if not pregnant but must be stopped if pregnancy is suspected (tooth discolouration in fetus; use after 12 weeks). COCP with anti-androgenic progestogen (co-cyprindiol/Dianette) is NICE-recommended for women seeking contraception alongside acne treatment, but carries a higher VTE risk than standard COCP and should only be used for acne, not as a first-line contraceptive. Topical options (BPO, adapalene, clindamycin) are generally safe in pregnancy. Azelaic acid is safe in pregnancy. Always check pregnancy status before prescribing systemic acne treatments.

Conclusion

Dermatology UKMLA revision rewards systematic learning of lesion terminology, management ladders, and red flag recognition. Mastering the primary and secondary lesion glossary enables accurate description in both AKT written questions and CPSA clinical skills. The stepped management tables for eczema and psoriasis, the skin cancer referral criteria from NICE NG12, and the drug reaction comparison table are particularly high-yield. Systemic disease skin manifestations — from pyoderma gangrenosum in IBD to heliotrope rash in dermatomyositis — appear regularly across integrated AKT questions. Use the UKMLA syllabus to confirm which skin conditions are listed, and supplement your revision with the preparation resources including photo-based question banks.
