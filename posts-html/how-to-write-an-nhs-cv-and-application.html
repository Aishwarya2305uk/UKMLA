Learning how to write an NHS CV and application that actually gets shortlisted is a genuinely different skill from writing a CV or cover letter for almost any other job market in the world, and it is a skill that is rarely taught explicitly to either UK medical graduates or international medical graduates (IMGs). Most candidates arrive at their first NHS vacancy assuming that a polished, traditional CV and a warm covering letter will do the talking, only to discover that the vacancy asks for neither in the conventional sense — instead, it asks them to complete a structured online application form, write a supporting statement mapped against a list of criteria they have never seen laid out so explicitly before, and submit everything through a portal called NHS Jobs or Trac Jobs rather than by email.

This guide is a dedicated, practical deep-dive into exactly that process: how the real online application system works, how to read and respond to a person specification line by line, how to write a supporting statement that a busy shortlisting panel will actually credit with points, the mistakes IMGs make most often when they assume a UK-style CV works the same way it does back home, and how references and the wider application timeline typically unfold once a form is submitted. It is written for both UK medical graduates applying for their first substantive post and IMGs applying for their first NHS role, since both audiences make different mistakes but face the same underlying system.

Unlike the wider overview of the pathway from UKMLA success to a first NHS post, this article does not cover visa sponsorship, GMC registration, or the broader job-search timeline in detail — those topics are covered fully elsewhere on this site. What follows is focused entirely on the mechanics of the CV, the application form, and the supporting statement itself: the single stage of the process where strong candidates are most often filtered out for reasons that have nothing to do with their clinical ability. Once an application has actually been submitted, the next hurdle is the interview itself, and candidates should also budget time to work through a structured plan for preparing for that stage well before any invitation arrives.

Quick Answer: What Actually Gets an NHS Application Shortlisted

An effective NHS CV and application is one that mirrors the exact wording of the job's person specification, provides a specific, evidenced example against every essential criterion, and is submitted through the trust's own NHS Jobs or Trac Jobs portal rather than sent as a generic emailed CV. Shortlisting panels typically score applications criterion by criterion against the person specification, not by forming a general impression of the candidate, so the single highest-leverage action any applicant can take is to address each listed requirement explicitly and in the panel's own language.

In practice, this means three things matter far more than they would for a CV submitted to an employer outside the NHS: matching the specific vocabulary of the advert and person specification, filling in every structured section of the online form completely rather than referring the reader to an attached CV, and writing a supporting statement that reads as a direct, evidenced response to the criteria rather than a general summary of a career to date. Candidates who understand this — and who invest the extra hour or two it takes to tailor each application individually — are shortlisted at a noticeably higher rate than equally qualified candidates who submit a generic, unedited application to every vacancy they see. Getting this stage right matters just as much for a candidate weighing up a locum shift against a substantive post as it does for someone applying to a Trust Grade role directly, since both routes still rely on a structured application being read and scored by a real panel.

Understanding the NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs Application System

A well-prepared NHS CV and application starts with understanding the software and process that almost every NHS trust in England actually uses to recruit, before a single word of a supporting statement is written. Unlike private-sector recruitment in many countries, where a candidate might simply email a CV and cover letter to a named hiring manager, the overwhelming majority of NHS vacancies — from Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow posts through to consultant roles — are advertised and processed through one of two linked online systems.

NHS Jobs: The National Vacancy Portal

NHS Jobs (jobs.nhs.uk) is the national vacancy website used by NHS trusts across England to advertise almost every post they recruit for. Every serious applicant should treat it as their primary search tool, setting up saved search alerts for relevant grades and specialties so that new vacancies are flagged the moment they are posted, since popular posts can close for applications within days. Full guidance on registering an account, searching vacancies, and using the online application form is available directly from the NHS Jobs applicant guidance pages, and candidates should refer to this directly for the latest version of the form fields and submission process, since the platform is updated periodically.

Critically, NHS Jobs is not simply a job board that links out to an external application process — for the majority of vacancies, the entire application is completed within the NHS Jobs system itself. A candidate creates a single NHS Jobs account, builds out a structured employment history, education history, and referee details once, and then applies to individual vacancies using that same core profile, supplemented by a vacancy-specific supporting statement. This is a meaningfully different model from applying to jobs in most other sectors, where each employer might use entirely separate software or accept a simple emailed CV.

Trac Jobs: The System Many Trusts Actually Recruit Through

Trac Jobs (trac.jobs) is a separate but closely related recruitment platform used by a large number of NHS trusts to manage the underlying recruitment workflow — shortlisting, interview scheduling, and pre-employment checks — even when the vacancy itself is first advertised on NHS Jobs. In many cases, a candidate finds a vacancy through NHS Jobs, clicks through, and is redirected to complete the actual application on a Trac Jobs-branded form instead. The two systems are closely integrated in practice, and candidates should not be confused or concerned if a vacancy discovered on NHS Jobs redirects them to a Trac-branded application page — this is standard practice for many trusts rather than a sign of anything unusual. Trac Jobs publishes its own applicant guidance covering account creation, the structure of the online form, and how to track an application's progress once submitted, and it is worth reading in full before submitting a first application. Candidates who have already worked through the eligibility requirements for practising in the UK will find the account-creation stage of either system straightforward, since much of the same identity and qualification information is simply re-entered into the recruitment platform.

Because both systems share broadly the same underlying structure — employment history, education and qualifications, structured supporting information boxes, and referee details — the practical advice in this guide applies equally whether a specific trust happens to use NHS Jobs directly or redirects applicants to Trac Jobs.

Why This Is Not "Just Upload Your CV"

The single most important structural fact to understand about NHS recruitment is this: in the majority of NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs applications, an uploaded CV is either optional, supplementary, or entirely absent from the process — the application itself is the structured online form, and it is this form, not any attached document, that shortlisting panels are trained to score. Some vacancies do request a CV upload as one component of the application, and where this is the case it should certainly be included and kept current. However, even when a CV upload is offered, panels overwhelmingly base their shortlisting decision on the structured supporting information boxes completed within the form itself, not on a generic CV attached alongside it.

This is the single biggest adjustment most new applicants — both UK graduates and IMGs — need to make. A CV formatted beautifully in Word, however impressive the underlying career history it describes, will not by itself score points against a person specification if the structured boxes in the online form are left thin, generic, or simply reference "see attached CV" instead of directly answering what was asked. Treat the online form itself, not any attached document, as the primary artefact a panel will actually read and score.

Mapping Your Experience Against the Person Specification

The single most important, and most frequently skipped, technique behind any successful NHS CV and application is deliberately mapping your own experience against the job's person specification before writing a word of your supporting statement. Every substantive NHS vacancy is published with a person specification — a formal document, usually attached to or embedded within the job advert, listing the specific attributes, qualifications, skills, and experience the post requires.

What a Person Specification Actually Contains

A person specification is typically laid out as a table or structured list divided into several standard categories: qualifications, experience, skills and knowledge, and sometimes personal attributes or values. Crucially, almost every person specification separates its requirements into two columns or two labelled groups: essential criteria and desirable criteria. Essential criteria are the non-negotiable requirements a candidate must be able to evidence to be shortlisted at all — for example, "full GMC registration with a licence to practise" or "evidence of managing acutely unwell patients." Desirable criteria are requirements that strengthen an application but whose absence does not automatically disqualify a candidate — for example, "prior UK clinical experience" or "an additional postgraduate qualification."

Shortlisting panels commonly use a formal scoring matrix, awarding points for each essential and desirable criterion a candidate clearly evidences, and candidates who fail to evidence even one essential criterion are frequently screened out at this stage regardless of how strong the rest of their application is. This is precisely why simply describing a career history in a narrative CV format is a much weaker strategy than directly and explicitly evidencing each listed criterion in turn.

The Criterion-by-Criterion Mapping Technique

The practical technique that consistently produces stronger applications is straightforward, though it takes real time and discipline to do properly for every application rather than only occasionally. Before writing anything, list every essential and desirable criterion from the person specification down one column of a working document. Next to each one, write down the single most specific, concrete example from your own training or clinical experience that directly evidences it — not a vague description of your general competence, but an actual instance: a specific clinical scenario, a specific audit or quality-improvement project, a specific example of leading or contributing to a team.

Only once every criterion has a mapped example should the supporting statement itself be drafted, and the statement should then be structured so that each criterion and its evidence is addressed in turn, ideally using language that echoes the exact wording of the person specification rather than a loose paraphrase. For example, if a person specification lists "demonstrates effective communication with patients and colleagues from a range of backgrounds" as an essential criterion, a strong statement does not simply assert "I am a good communicator" — it describes a specific instance where the candidate communicated a complex diagnosis to a patient with a language barrier, or de-escalated a difficult conversation with a distressed relative, naming the specific skill the person specification is asking about along the way.

This mapping exercise is genuinely the single highest-leverage thing any candidate can do before submitting an NHS application, and it is the technique most consistently skipped by both UK graduates in a hurry and IMGs unfamiliar with the convention. Doing it properly for every application, rather than reusing one generic statement across multiple vacancies, is what separates candidates who are shortlisted consistently from those who are not. The same disciplined, criterion-led approach that serves candidates well during structured UKMLA exam preparation transfers directly to this stage — both reward candidates who work systematically through a defined checklist rather than relying on general ability alone.

Writing a Strong Supporting Statement

The supporting statement, sometimes labelled "supporting information," "personal statement," or simply a large free-text box within the online form, is the single most important piece of writing in the entire application. This is where a candidate makes the direct, explicit case for why they meet the person specification, and it is the section shortlisting panels read most carefully when deciding who to invite to interview.

Structuring the Statement Around the Person Specification, Not a Chronology

The most common and most damaging mistake in a supporting statement is structuring it chronologically, as though narrating a CV in prose — "I graduated in 2021, then worked as a house officer, then rotated through general surgery..." This kind of narrative is easy to write quickly, but it forces the panel to do the work of matching the candidate's experience against the person specification themselves, which busy panels reading dozens of applications are unlikely to do generously. A far stronger structure follows the person specification's own categories directly, often using the exact subheadings from the specification itself (for example, "Clinical Experience," "Communication Skills," "Teaching and Audit," "Commitment to the Specialty") and addressing each one with a short, specific paragraph.

Within each section, the strongest statements follow a simple pattern: state the specific example, describe the action taken, and — critically — state the outcome or what was learned. This mirrors the structured, evidence-based reasoning that NHS interview panels also expect at interview stage, and preparing a supporting statement this way doubles as useful preparation for the interview itself.

Being Specific Rather Than General

A recurring weakness in weaker applications is vague, unfalsifiable language — phrases like "I am a hard-working and compassionate doctor with excellent communication skills" that could be written by literally any candidate and that provide the panel with nothing concrete to score. Strong statements replace every such general claim with a specific, checkable example: not "I have good time-management skills" but "I independently managed a caseload of twelve inpatients during a short-staffed weekend on-call shift, prioritising two acutely unwell patients for senior review while safely managing routine tasks for the remainder of the ward." Specificity is not merely a matter of style — it is what actually earns scoring points against a person specification criterion, since a panel scoring "evidence of managing clinical priorities" needs an actual instance to award marks against, not an adjective.

Keeping the Statement Within the Word Limit and Format the Form Expects

Most NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs supporting statement boxes impose a strict word or character limit, and exceeding it typically results in the text being truncated on submission rather than rejected outright — meaning a candidate who writes past the limit may simply lose their concluding paragraph without any warning. Always check the stated limit before drafting, write the statement in a separate document first, and trim it to fit comfortably within the limit before pasting it into the form, checking the pasted version for formatting loss, since some systems strip bullet points, bold text, or line breaks on submission.

Similarly, some forms provide multiple separate labelled boxes — for example, distinct boxes for "clinical experience," "additional information," and "reason for application" — rather than one single free-text field. Where this is the case, resist the temptation to write one continuous statement and paste the same text into every box; instead, tailor the content of each box specifically to what its label is asking for, since panels notice, and mark down, an application that visibly repeats the same paragraph across multiple sections.

Preparing the CV Itself and Completing the Structured Sections

Even where the supporting statement carries most of the scoring weight, the remaining sections of an NHS CV and application — the structured employment history, education details, and any CV uploaded alongside them — still need to be completed carefully and consistently.

Employment History With No Unexplained Gaps

NHS application forms consistently ask for a full employment history presented in a structured format, typically month and year for each start and end date, with no unexplained gaps. This is treated seriously: an unexplained gap of even a few months can trigger a query from the recruiting team or, in some cases, count against a candidate at shortlisting, since NHS recruitment governance places real weight on a transparent, continuous employment record as part of safe-recruitment practice. Any genuine gap — for further study, family leave, visa processing delays, or a period between posts while relocating — should be stated honestly and briefly explained, rather than left blank or glossed over.

Education, Qualifications, and Registration Details

Dates of primary medical qualification, postgraduate qualifications, and GMC registration status (including the exact GMC reference number where already registered, or realistic expected registration date where an application is submitted just ahead of full registration) should be stated precisely and should match supporting documents exactly, in line with the requirements set out in the site's own step-by-step registration walkthrough. Even small inconsistencies between an application form and the certificates later provided for verification can slow down or complicate the pre-employment checks stage.

Formatting a CV That Complements, Rather Than Duplicates, the Form

Where a CV upload is requested alongside the structured form, it should be treated as a complementary document, not a substitute for completing the form's own sections in full. A clear, well-organised CV — reverse-chronological work history, clearly dated, with a concise summary of clinical skills, procedures, audits, and publications — remains a useful supporting document, particularly at interview stage when a panel may refer back to it. However, candidates should never leave key structured form fields sparse on the assumption that "it's all on my CV," since many shortlisting processes score the form fields directly without a panel member necessarily opening the attached CV at all during the initial screening pass.

Common Mistakes IMGs Make With NHS Applications

International medical graduates bring genuinely strong clinical training to their NHS CV and application, but a recurring pattern of avoidable, non-clinical mistakes costs many IMG candidates interview invitations they would otherwise have earned. Understanding these mistakes in advance is one of the most effective ways an IMG candidate can improve their shortlisting rate without changing anything about their underlying clinical competence.

Assuming a Home-Country CV and Cover-Letter Format Transfers Directly

Many IMGs arrive at their first NHS application having already prepared a polished CV and covering letter in a format that works well in their home country's job market — often a narrative, achievement-led CV paired with a warm, personalised cover letter addressed to a named recruiter. This format, however well executed, does not map onto how NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs applications are actually scored, since — as covered above — the structured supporting information boxes and explicit person-specification mapping matter far more than a standalone CV or cover letter ever will in this specific system. IMGs who simply attach their existing CV and skip the structured boxes, assuming the CV "speaks for itself," are shortlisted at a markedly lower rate than those who take the time to complete every structured section properly.

Writing a Generic, Non-Tailored Supporting Statement

A second, closely related mistake is writing one generic supporting statement and reusing it, largely unchanged, across many different vacancies. This is understandable — tailoring a statement properly for every single application is genuinely time-consuming, particularly when applying to a large number of Trust Grade posts across several trusts simultaneously. However, panels can usually tell within a few sentences when a statement has not been tailored to the specific post and specialty advertised, since a generic statement rarely echoes the particular wording of that vacancy's own person specification. Investing the extra time to adjust the statement's structure and examples to match each specific advert, even when applying to superficially similar posts, consistently produces a materially better shortlisting rate than submitting the same statement everywhere.

Underestimating How Literally the Essential Criteria Are Applied

Some IMG candidates assume that strong underlying clinical experience will compensate for failing to explicitly address one or two essential criteria, on the assumption that a panel will infer the missing evidence from the overall strength of the application. In practice, many NHS shortlisting processes are run to a strict, auditable scoring matrix specifically to ensure fairness and consistency across a large volume of applicants, and a criterion that is not explicitly evidenced in the application is frequently scored as not met, regardless of whether the underlying experience genuinely exists. The practical lesson is straightforward: never assume a criterion is "obviously" met and can be left unaddressed — state it directly, even where it feels repetitive to do so.

Overlooking UK-Specific Terminology and Systems

IMG applications are also frequently weakened by unfamiliarity with UK-specific clinical and administrative terminology that a person specification or supporting statement is implicitly expected to reference — terms such as clinical governance, multidisciplinary team working, safeguarding, or incident reporting through a system such as Datix. Using the precise UK terminology the NHS system expects, rather than an equivalent term from a different health system, signals system-readiness to a panel far more effectively than describing the same underlying concept in different language.

Not Allowing Enough Time for a First Application

Finally, many IMGs — often juggling GMC registration paperwork, visa research, and the wider practical demands of relocating to the UK simultaneously — underestimate how long a properly tailored first application actually takes to prepare, particularly the person-specification mapping exercise described earlier in this guide. A rushed application completed in the final hour before a closing deadline is rarely as strong as one drafted, reviewed, and refined over several days. Building in deliberate time for a first application, rather than treating it as a final administrative step to complete quickly, pays off directly in shortlisting outcomes. Candidates who worked through a structured plan of UKMLA preparation built specifically around the IMG pathway already know the value of this kind of early, deliberate planning — the same discipline applies just as directly to the application stage.

Common Mistakes to Avoid: A Checklist

  • Relying on an uploaded CV alone instead of completing every structured supporting information box in the online form.
  • Writing a generic statement and reusing it, largely unedited, across multiple different vacancies and specialties.
  • Structuring the supporting statement chronologically as a narrative CV, rather than mapping it directly onto the person specification's own categories.
  • Leaving essential criteria unaddressed on the assumption a panel will infer them from general clinical strength.
  • Using vague, unfalsifiable language ("hard-working," "excellent communicator") instead of specific, evidenced examples.
  • Leaving unexplained gaps in the employment history section of the form.
  • Exceeding the stated word or character limit, risking silent truncation of the statement on submission.
  • Notifying referees at the last minute, rather than early enough for them to respond within a realistic timeframe.
  • Ignoring UK-specific terminology such as clinical governance, multidisciplinary working, and incident reporting.
  • Submitting the same application under time pressure without reviewing it against the original advert and person specification a second time before final submission.

References and the Wider Application Timeline

Once the writing stage of an NHS CV and application is complete, understanding how references and the broader recruitment timeline work helps candidates plan realistically, since this stage often takes longer than applicants expect and is frequently misunderstood by first-time applicants.

How Many References Are Required and From Whom

NHS applications typically require a minimum of two or three professional references, and — critically — most trusts require these references to cover a specific recent period of employment, commonly the most recent three years, with no gaps in referee coverage across that period. This means a candidate cannot simply nominate two favourite former colleagues; the referees must, between them, account for the relevant recent period continuously. IMGs relocating from overseas should identify referees from their most recent posts abroad in advance, confirm current, reliable contact details, and — since UK reference request formats and structured reference forms may be unfamiliar to an overseas referee — consider briefing referees on what a UK employer's reference request typically looks like, so they are not caught off guard when the request arrives.

When References Are Requested

References are usually requested only after a candidate has been shortlisted and, in many cases, only once a conditional offer has already been made following a successful interview, rather than at the point of initial application. Because reference verification is consistently one of the slower stages of the pre-employment process — particularly where a referee is based overseas and unfamiliar with the request — proactively notifying referees the moment an application is submitted, rather than waiting until a reference request actually arrives, can meaningfully shorten the overall time between a conditional offer and a confirmed start date.

The Structured Application-to-Offer Timeline

NHS recruitment follows a broadly predictable sequence once an application is submitted, though exact timings vary between trusts and specialties. The table below sets out the typical stages.

Responding promptly to any request for further information at this stage — whether from the recruitment team or from a referee needing a nudge — measurably shortens the time between a conditional offer and an agreed start date.

Tracking Application Status

Both NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs allow candidates to log back into their account and check the current status of a submitted application, which typically moves through labelled stages such as "submitted," "under review," "shortlisted," or "unsuccessful." Checking this status periodically, rather than waiting solely for an email notification, is a sensible habit, since notification emails occasionally arrive later than the status update itself or, in rare cases, are filtered into a spam folder — a particular risk for candidates applying from an overseas email provider unfamiliar to some trusts' email filtering systems.

Tailoring Applications for Different Types of NHS Posts

While the core principles above apply to any NHS vacancy, it is worth briefly noting how the emphasis shifts slightly depending on the type of post being applied for, since a Trust Grade application and a substantive training-programme application are not scored in quite the same way.

For Trust Grade and Clinical Fellow posts — the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs — panels place particular weight on evidence of safe, supervised clinical practice and a realistic understanding of the post's clinical scope, alongside straightforward logistics such as visa status and availability to start. For posts that form part of a formal national training programme, by contrast, the supporting statement and any accompanying portfolio typically need to evidence a more explicit trajectory of professional development — audit, quality-improvement projects, teaching experience, and research or publications — since these posts are assessed against nationally standardised person specifications designed to differentiate between a larger and more competitive applicant pool. Understanding which category a specific vacancy falls into before drafting a supporting statement helps a candidate calibrate how much weight to give to service-delivery experience versus academic and developmental evidence.

Locum and bank work, discussed in detail in the site's dedicated comparison between locum and substantive NHS posts, typically involves a lighter-touch application process than a substantive Trust Grade or training post, since it is usually arranged either directly through a trust's own staff bank or through a specialist locum agency rather than a full competitive shortlisting exercise — but the same underlying principle of evidencing specific, relevant experience clearly still applies wherever a structured application or registration form is involved.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a traditional CV for an NHS job application?

In most cases, an uploaded CV is either optional or a supplementary document rather than the primary basis for shortlisting. The structured supporting information boxes within the NHS Jobs or Trac Jobs online application form — mapped directly against the job's person specification — are what most shortlisting panels actually score, so a candidate should never rely on an attached CV alone, even where a vacancy allows or requests one.

What is a person specification and why does it matter so much?

A person specification is the formal document listing the essential and desirable criteria a candidate must evidence for a specific NHS post, usually attached to or embedded in the job advert. It matters because most shortlisting panels use it as a direct scoring matrix, awarding points for each criterion a candidate clearly and specifically evidences, meaning an application that fails to address even one essential criterion explicitly risks being screened out regardless of the candidate's overall clinical strength.

What is the difference between NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs?

NHS Jobs is the national vacancy portal most trusts use to advertise posts, while Trac Jobs is a closely integrated recruitment platform many trusts use to manage the underlying application, shortlisting, and interview workflow. A candidate frequently finds a vacancy on NHS Jobs and is redirected to a Trac Jobs-branded form to complete the actual application, which is standard practice rather than anything unusual.

How long should a supporting statement be for an NHS application?

Most NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs supporting statement fields impose a specific word or character limit stated on the form itself, and exceeding it risks the text being silently truncated on submission. Candidates should draft the statement in a separate document first, confirm it fits comfortably within the stated limit, and check formatting carefully after pasting it into the online form, since some systems strip bullet points or line breaks.

How many references does an NHS application require?

Most NHS trusts require a minimum of two or three professional references covering a specific recent period of employment, commonly the most recent three years, with no gaps in referee coverage across that period. References are typically requested only after shortlisting, often following a successful interview and conditional offer, rather than at the point of initial application.

Why do IMG applications get rejected even with strong clinical experience?

The most common reason is structural rather than clinical: many IMGs submit a home-country-style CV and generic cover letter without completing the structured supporting information boxes properly, or without explicitly mapping their experience against every essential criterion in the person specification. Since many NHS shortlisting processes score applications against a strict criterion-by-criterion matrix, a criterion that is not explicitly evidenced in writing is frequently scored as not met, regardless of the underlying clinical strength genuinely present.

Can I reuse the same supporting statement for multiple NHS job applications?

It is possible to reuse a broad structure, but reusing the same statement largely unchanged across different vacancies is a common and costly mistake. Shortlisting panels can usually tell when a statement has not been tailored to the specific post's own person specification and wording, and applications tailored individually to each vacancy are shortlisted at a noticeably higher rate than generic, unedited submissions.

Conclusion

Writing an effective NHS CV and application is a learnable, structured skill rather than a matter of luck or connections, and the candidates who consistently get shortlisted are simply the ones who have understood how the NHS Jobs and Trac Jobs system actually works: complete the structured form fully, map every essential and desirable criterion from the person specification to a specific, evidenced example, write a tailored supporting statement rather than a generic one, and prepare referees and documentation early rather than at the last minute. Both UK graduates and IMGs make avoidable mistakes at this stage, but IMGs in particular benefit from consciously setting aside home-country CV conventions in favour of the NHS's own criterion-based approach.
