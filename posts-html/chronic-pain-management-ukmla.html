Chronic Pain Management in the UKMLA: What to Revise

Chronic pain UKMLA questions catch out more candidates than almost any other prescribing topic, largely because the "obvious" answer — starting or escalating an opioid — is very often the wrong one. Chronic pain affects an estimated one in three to one in two adults in the UK to some degree, and the AKT expects candidates to reason through assessment, classification, and management with a framework that has shifted substantially in the last few years. This guide sets out exactly what the exam tests: the WHO analgesic ladder and why it no longer applies neatly to non-cancer pain, the 2021 NICE guideline that surprised a generation of prescribers by recommending against long-term opioids and gabapentinoids for chronic primary pain, how to recognise and treat neuropathic pain, the principles of safer opioid prescribing, and the biopsychosocial model that now underpins UK chronic pain services.

This is deliberately a different topic from end-of-life symptom control — for cancer pain, syringe drivers, and terminal care prescribing, see our separate guide to palliative and end-of-life medicine. This article instead focuses on the ambulatory, non-cancer chronic pain that GPs, physicians, and pain clinics manage for years or decades — low back pain, fibromyalgia, chronic headache, and neuropathic pain syndromes — which is tested extensively across AKT prescribing and general medicine questions.

Chronic Pain UKMLA Quick Answer

Chronic pain is pain that persists or recurs for longer than three months, beyond the normal time expected for tissue healing. The UKMLA divides it conceptually into chronic secondary pain (where an underlying condition such as osteoarthritis, diabetic neuropathy, or endometriosis directly causes or is strongly associated with the pain) and chronic primary pain (pain with no clear underlying cause, or pain that is disproportionate to any identifiable tissue damage, such as fibromyalgia or non-specific chronic low back pain). This distinction matters enormously for management, because NICE's 2021 guideline, NG193, recommends against starting most standard analgesics — including opioids, NSAIDs, paracetamol, and gabapentinoids — for chronic primary pain specifically, favouring instead supervised exercise, psychological therapy (ACT or CBT), acupuncture, and, if a drug is genuinely needed, an antidepressant rather than an analgesic. Candidates who assume "more pain equals stronger opioid" will consistently choose the wrong answer on this topic; the exam increasingly rewards recognising when to withhold pharmacological escalation.

Three points are worth holding in mind before working through the detail below. First, the WHO analgesic ladder remains examinable but is now understood as a framework for cancer pain, not a template to apply automatically to chronic non-cancer pain. Second, NICE NG193's recommendations against long-term opioids and gabapentinoids for chronic primary pain are a genuinely important, sometimes counter-intuitive change that the exam is likely to test directly, precisely because it contradicts older teaching and instinctive practice. Third, neuropathic pain has its own separate NICE pathway (NG193 and the earlier CG173 principles) with specific first-line drug choices that differ from nociceptive pain — mixing these two frameworks up is one of the most common errors candidates make.

The WHO Analgesic Ladder: Origins and Modern Critique

What the Ladder Originally Described

The World Health Organization analgesic ladder was published in 1986 as a three-step framework for treating cancer pain in resource-limited settings, and it remains one of the most widely taught concepts in medicine. The ladder proposes stepwise escalation:

  • Step 1 (mild pain): Non-opioid analgesia — paracetamol and/or an NSAID
  • Step 2 (moderate pain): Add a weak opioid such as codeine or tramadol to Step 1 therapy
  • Step 3 (severe pain): Substitute a strong opioid — morphine, oxycodone, or similar — while continuing Step 1 agents and adding adjuvants as required

The underlying principle — analgesia given regularly ("by the clock"), by the simplest effective route ("by mouth"), and escalated in a structured way ("by the ladder") — transformed cancer pain control worldwide and remains a core part of palliative prescribing, covered in detail in our companion guide on palliative care revision for the UKMLA.

Why the Ladder Does Not Transfer Cleanly to Chronic Non-Cancer Pain

The ladder was designed and validated for a specific population: patients with progressive, usually terminal, cancer pain, where the goal is comfort over a foreseeable and often shortening timeframe, and where concerns about long-term opioid tolerance, dependence, and diversion are proportionately less pressing. Applying the same "escalate the opioid dose until pain improves" logic to chronic non-cancer pain — which may persist for thirty or forty years of a patient's life — produces a very different risk-benefit calculation.

Three problems became apparent as the ladder was extrapolated into general chronic pain practice through the 1990s and 2000s:

  • Opioids lose analgesic benefit over time in chronic non-cancer pain while risks continue to accumulate — tolerance develops, meaning higher doses are needed for the same effect, without a corresponding gain in function or quality of life for many patients.
  • The ladder has no adequate "step 4." For cancer pain, escalation has a ceiling defined by symptom control and life expectancy. For chronic primary pain with an unclear or absent tissue-damage mechanism, there is no dose of opioid that reliably "fixes" the pain, because the pain is not simply a nociceptive signal proportional to tissue injury.
  • It ignores the biopsychosocial contributors to pain (see below) — mood, sleep, deconditioning, fear-avoidance behaviour, and social context — that drive chronic pain far more than they drive acute or cancer pain, and that a purely pharmacological ladder cannot address.

Modern teaching therefore treats the WHO ladder as historically important and still valid for its original purpose — cancer and palliative pain — while explicitly rejecting its automatic extension to chronic primary pain. Some clinicians and researchers have proposed a "fourth step" incorporating interventional techniques (nerve blocks, spinal cord stimulation) or, more influentially, argue the ladder should be replaced altogether for chronic non-cancer pain by a biopsychosocial, multimodal framework — which is exactly the direction NICE took in 2021. Recognising which framework applies to which scenario is one of the highest-yield discriminators in this area of the exam.

NICE NG193: The 2021 Guideline That Changed Chronic Primary Pain Prescribing

What NG193 Covers and Why It Surprises Candidates

NICE published guideline NG193, "Chronic pain (primary and secondary) in over 16s: assessment of all chronic pain and management of chronic primary pain," on 7 April 2021. It is one of the most consequential and, for many candidates, one of the most counter-intuitive guidelines in the whole UKMLA syllabus, because it recommends against starting the very drug classes that decades of prior teaching treated as the default response to pain.

NG193 first sets out how to assess chronic pain in general — distinguishing chronic secondary pain (where pain arises from or is strongly associated with an identifiable underlying condition, such as osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, endometriosis, or diabetic neuropathy) from chronic primary pain (pain persisting for more than three months that cannot be explained by another condition, or that is out of proportion to the degree of tissue damage identified, such as fibromyalgia, chronic primary headache, complex regional pain syndrome, or non-specific chronic low back pain). This assessment step matters because the guideline's management recommendations that follow apply specifically to chronic primary pain — chronic secondary pain should be managed by treating the underlying condition, alongside any of the general supportive approaches described below.

What NICE Recommends Against Starting for Chronic Primary Pain

For adults with chronic primary pain, NG193 recommends that clinicians should not initiate the following medicines, on the basis that the evidence does not show they improve quality of life, pain, or function, while all carry a real risk of harm:

  • Opioids
  • Paracetamol used alone for the specific purpose of treating chronic primary pain
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Antiepileptic drugs, including gabapentinoids (gabapentin and pregabalin) — with a narrow exception for use within a clinical trial for complex regional pain syndrome
  • Antipsychotics
  • Local anaesthetics (again, other than in a clinical trial for complex regional pain syndrome)
  • Ketamine
  • Corticosteroid injections (with or without local anaesthetic) into a trigger point for chronic primary pain

This is a genuinely unusual recommendation in UK clinical guidance, because it advises against initiating widely-used, familiar analgesics — not on cost grounds, but because the trial evidence available to the guideline committee did not demonstrate a net benefit for chronic primary pain specifically, while dependence, tolerance, and adverse-effect risks were well established. Candidates should note carefully that this recommendation applies to chronic primary pain, not to acute pain, cancer pain, or chronic secondary pain with a clear structural cause — prescribing an NSAID for an acute exacerbation of osteoarthritis, or an opioid for post-operative pain, is unaffected by NG193.

What NICE Recommends Instead

In place of the medicines above, NG193 recommends a small number of non-pharmacological interventions with an evidence base for improving quality of life in chronic primary pain:

Recommended interventionWhat NICE advisesKey caveat

Psychological therapyAcceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) or cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) as part of a pain management programmeDelivered by a trained practitioner; addresses how a person relates to and copes with pain, not merely distraction

The inclusion of an antidepressant as the recommended first-line drug — rather than an analgesic — is itself a frequently examined point, precisely because it looks counter-intuitive to candidates who have not seen the guideline before. The rationale is that these particular antidepressants have evidence for modulating central pain processing independent of any effect on mood, and NICE explicitly states they should be considered even when the person has no depression or anxiety. This is a distinct pharmacological rationale from using an antidepressant to treat comorbid low mood, and the exam may test whether candidates understand the difference.

Shared Decision-Making Is Central to NG193

NG193 places heavy emphasis on shared decision-making rather than presenting these recommendations as a rigid, one-size-fits-all algorithm. Clinicians are expected to discuss with the patient why long-term opioids, gabapentinoids, and other agents are not being started or are being reviewed for discontinuation, to acknowledge that the person's pain is real, and to explain the evidence honestly rather than simply withholding treatment without explanation. For patients already established on long-term opioids or gabapentinoids before the guideline was published, NG193 does not recommend abrupt discontinuation; instead, it supports a structured, collaborative conversation about the risks and benefits of continuing, alongside a supervised, gradual reduction plan where appropriate, which is covered further in the opioid safety section below.

Recognising and Treating Neuropathic Pain

What Distinguishes Neuropathic from Nociceptive Pain

Neuropathic pain arises from a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system itself, rather than from ongoing stimulation of intact pain receptors by tissue damage (nociceptive pain). Recognising the difference clinically is essential, because the two categories often respond to entirely different drug classes.

Typical descriptors and features that point towards a neuropathic mechanism include:

  • Burning, shooting, stabbing, or electric-shock-like quality
  • Allodynia (pain triggered by a normally non-painful stimulus, such as light touch or clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia (an exaggerated pain response to a normally mildly painful stimulus)
  • Numbness, tingling, or altered sensation in the same distribution as the pain
  • A distribution that follows a dermatome, peripheral nerve territory, or a stocking-glove pattern (as in diabetic peripheral neuropathy)
  • Common underlying causes: diabetic peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, trigeminal neuralgia, sciatica with nerve root involvement, chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy, and central post-stroke pain

Validated screening tools such as the DN4 or painDETECT questionnaires can support this distinction in both clinical practice and, occasionally, in AKT clinical vignettes, but the exam more commonly tests recognition through the descriptive language used in a scenario stem — candidates should be alert to words like "burning," "electric," or "pins and needles" as a strong signal to consider a neuropathic mechanism and therefore a different first-line drug choice.

First-Line Drug Treatment for Neuropathic Pain

NICE's guidance on neuropathic pain (originally set out in CG173 and incorporated into the current NG193 pathway for pain not meeting the definition of chronic primary pain) recommends offering one of the following as first-line pharmacological treatment, with the choice guided by comorbidities, side-effect profile, and patient preference:

  • Amitriptyline — a tricyclic antidepressant, typically started at a low dose (for example 10mg at night) and titrated; sedating, so often useful when sleep is also disturbed, but caution is needed in older adults and in cardiac disease due to anticholinergic and QT-prolonging effects
  • Duloxetine — a serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor with a specific licence for diabetic peripheral neuropathic pain; a useful choice where a sedating drug is undesirable
  • Gabapentin — titrated gradually against tolerability and effect; requires dose adjustment in renal impairment
  • Pregabalin — similar mechanism and place in therapy to gabapentin, twice-daily dosing, also requires renal dose adjustment

Crucially, if the first drug tried is not effective or is not tolerated at an adequate dose, NICE recommends switching to one of the other three first-line options, rather than adding a second drug from this list on top of the first — combining two of amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin from the outset is not recommended, since this increases the side-effect burden without clear evidence of added benefit. Tramadol is reserved for acute rescue therapy only, not for long-term neuropathic pain management, and strong opioids are not recommended as routine treatment for neuropathic pain outside specialist pain-service oversight. For trigeminal neuralgia specifically, carbamazepine is the recommended first-line agent rather than the drugs listed above, reflecting a different underlying mechanism and evidence base.

Specialist Referral

Referral to a specialist pain service or the relevant condition-specific specialty should be considered when neuropathic pain fails to respond to first-line drug treatment, when the diagnosis is uncertain, when there is significant impact on function or mental health, or when interventional options (nerve blocks, spinal cord stimulation) may be appropriate. Complex regional pain syndrome, in particular, warrants early specialist input given its distinct diagnostic criteria and management pathway.

Opioid Safety: Dependence, Tolerance, and Safer Prescribing

Why Long-Term Opioid Risk Became a UK Prescribing Priority

Concern about the harms of long-term opioid prescribing for chronic non-cancer pain has grown substantially across the UK over the past decade, driven by rising rates of opioid dependence, overdose, and prescription-related harm identified in Public Health England's landmark 2019 review of prescribed medicine dependence. That review found that a substantial proportion of adults in England had been prescribed an opioid in the preceding year, with prescribing rates for strong opioids having risen markedly over the previous decade — a pattern now understood to reflect the ladder-based, escalate-until-comfortable approach described earlier being applied well beyond its original cancer-pain context.

Key concepts the exam expects candidates to distinguish clearly:

  • Tolerance: a physiological adaptation in which increasing doses of an opioid are required to achieve the same analgesic effect over time. Tolerance develops predictably with continued use and is not, by itself, evidence of addiction.
  • Physical dependence: the body's physiological adaptation to the continued presence of an opioid, such that abrupt cessation produces a withdrawal syndrome (sweating, agitation, diarrhoea, tachycardia, piloerection, yawning, dilated pupils). Physical dependence can occur in any patient taking regular opioids for more than a few weeks, again regardless of any addictive behaviour.
  • Addiction (opioid use disorder): a behavioural pattern characterised by compulsive use despite harm, loss of control over use, and continued use despite negative consequences — a distinct phenomenon from tolerance or physical dependence, though the three frequently coexist in long-term opioid use.
  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia: a paradoxical state in which long-term opioid exposure increases sensitivity to pain, so that the drug intended to relieve pain begins to worsen it — an important and often under-recognised reason why escalating an opioid dose in chronic non-cancer pain can make the clinical picture worse rather than better.

Principles of Safer Opioid Prescribing

UK guidance now converges on a consistent set of principles for opioid prescribing in chronic non-cancer pain, reflected across NG193, subsequent Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) safety communications, and Faculty of Pain Medicine guidance:

  • Avoid initiating long-term opioids for chronic primary pain in line with NG193, given the lack of demonstrated benefit for quality of life or function.
  • Where opioids are used for chronic secondary pain, set clear functional goals with the patient before starting, use the lowest effective dose, and review regularly rather than prescribing indefinitely on repeat without reassessment.
  • Avoid escalating doses indefinitely in the absence of a clear, sustained functional benefit — dose escalation without benefit should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis and treatment plan, not simply a higher prescription.
  • Be alert to high-dose thresholds — UK guidance highlights increased overdose risk at total daily doses at and above 120mg oral morphine equivalent, and recommends specialist review before prescribing at or above this level for chronic non-cancer pain.
  • Avoid co-prescribing opioids with benzodiazodiazepines or gabapentinoids where possible, since the combination substantially increases the risk of fatal respiratory depression — a repeatedly tested prescribing-safety point.
  • Plan for gradual dose reduction (deprescribing) collaboratively with patients established on long-term opioids where the balance of risk and benefit has shifted, using shared decision-making, a slow taper individualised to the patient, and support for withdrawal symptoms — never an abrupt, unplanned cessation.
  • Provide honest information about dependence risk before initiating any opioid, as part of informed consent, and document this discussion.

This prescribing-safety thread connects directly to the wider UKMLA syllabus on medicines safety — for a broader treatment of prescribing errors, dose calculation pitfalls, and the exam's approach to safe prescribing scenarios more generally, see our guides on core pharmacology and prescribing safety for the UKMLA and on recognising and avoiding common prescribing errors. Gabapentinoid safety in particular has become a distinct prescribing-safety topic in its own right: pregabalin was reclassified as a Schedule 3 controlled drug in the UK in 2019 because of rising rates of misuse and its contribution to drug-related deaths, especially when combined with opioids — a fact that reinforces why NG193 advises against initiating gabapentinoids for chronic primary pain.

Withdrawal and Deprescribing in Practice

When a patient and clinician agree to reduce a long-term opioid, UK guidance recommends a gradual, individualised taper — commonly reducing the total dose by around 10% every one to four weeks, though the exact rate should be adjusted to the patient's tolerance of the process and can be slowed further if withdrawal symptoms emerge. Abrupt cessation is avoided wherever possible because it produces an unpleasant, and clinically unnecessary, withdrawal syndrome and increases the risk the patient will seek opioids from other sources. Patient-facing withdrawal symptoms to anticipate and explain in advance include anxiety, sweating, gastrointestinal upset, myalgia, and disrupted sleep — all of which are self-limiting over days to a couple of weeks once a dose reduction step has been made, and which typically settle further with each subsequent, smaller step.

The Biopsychosocial Model of Pain

What the Model Proposes

The biopsychosocial model, first articulated for pain by Gate Control Theory pioneers and subsequently formalised by Loeser and others, holds that the experience of chronic pain is shaped by the interaction of biological factors (tissue pathology, nociceptive and neuropathic mechanisms, genetics), psychological factors (mood, beliefs about pain, coping style, fear of movement or re-injury, catastrophising), and social factors (work, relationships, financial stress, cultural attitudes to illness, access to support). This stands in contrast to a purely biomedical model, which assumes pain intensity is directly and reliably proportional to identifiable tissue damage — an assumption that holds reasonably well for acute pain but breaks down substantially for chronic pain, where the correlation between imaging findings (for example, degenerative changes on a lumbar spine MRI) and reported pain severity is often weak.

NICE NG193's recommendations reflect this model directly: exercise addresses deconditioning and fear-avoidance; psychological therapy (ACT or CBT) addresses unhelpful beliefs, catastrophising, and coping strategies; and the guideline's overall framing explicitly moves away from a purely pharmacological, biomedical response to chronic primary pain.

Key Concepts Within the Model

  • Fear-avoidance behaviour: a cycle in which pain leads to fear of movement, leading to avoidance of activity, leading to deconditioning and muscle weakness, which in turn increases pain and disability, reinforcing the fear — supervised graded exercise programmes are designed specifically to interrupt this cycle
  • Pain catastrophising: a cognitive-affective pattern of magnifying the threat value of pain, feeling helpless in the face of it, and ruminating on it — strongly associated with worse pain outcomes and a key target of CBT and ACT
  • Central sensitisation: a state in which the central nervous system becomes hyper-responsive to pain signals following prolonged nociceptive input, amplifying pain perception independent of ongoing peripheral tissue damage — thought to underlie much of fibromyalgia and other chronic primary pain conditions
  • Yellow flags: psychosocial risk factors for the development of chronic, disabling pain (as opposed to "red flags," which indicate serious underlying pathology) — including negative beliefs about pain, fear-avoidance, low mood, and problems at work — recognising these early is part of good assessment practice and is directly examinable

Applying the Model in Practice

In clinical terms, applying the biopsychosocial model means that a chronic pain consultation should explore not only the pain's location, quality, and severity, but also its impact on sleep, mood, work, and relationships; the patient's own beliefs about the cause and likely course of their pain; and any fear of movement or activity. Multidisciplinary pain management programmes, which combine physiotherapy-led exercise, psychological therapy, and patient education, are the practical expression of this model and are the setting in which NG193's recommended interventions are typically delivered together, rather than as isolated referrals.

Assessment of Chronic Pain in Practice

History and Examination

NG193 recommends a comprehensive assessment for anyone presenting with chronic pain, covering the nature, intensity, and impact of the pain; the effect on sleep, mood, and daily function; any factors that improve or worsen the pain; the person's understanding of their pain and expectations of treatment; and the presence of any physical or mental health comorbidities that could be contributing. This structured assessment feeds directly into the primary-versus-secondary classification described earlier, and should always screen for red flags — features suggesting serious underlying pathology, such as unexplained weight loss, new neurological deficit, or night pain unrelieved by rest — which mandate further, urgent investigation rather than a routine chronic pain pathway.

Validated Assessment Tools

Several structured tools support consistent assessment and monitoring in both clinical practice and, on occasion, AKT vignettes: the Brief Pain Inventory for pain severity and interference with daily activities; the DN4 and painDETECT questionnaires for identifying a likely neuropathic component; and generic outcome measures such as the EQ-5D for tracking quality of life over time. None of these tools alone makes a diagnosis, but they support the shared decision-making and outcome-tracking that NG193 emphasises throughout.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does NICE recommend against all opioids for every type of pain?

No. NICE NG193's recommendation against initiating opioids applies specifically to chronic primary pain — pain with no clear underlying cause, such as fibromyalgia or non-specific chronic low back pain. Opioids remain appropriate for acute pain, cancer pain, and palliative care, and may still have a limited, carefully monitored role in some chronic secondary pain where an underlying condition is being actively treated. The guideline's concern is specifically that long-term opioids do not reliably improve quality of life in chronic primary pain while carrying real risks of tolerance, dependence, and hyperalgesia.

Why does NICE recommend an antidepressant rather than a painkiller for chronic primary pain?

NICE NG193 recommends amitriptyline, citalopram, duloxetine, fluoxetine, paroxetine, or sertraline as first-line pharmacological treatment for chronic primary pain because these drugs have evidence for modulating central pain-processing pathways independent of any effect on mood — the recommendation applies even when the patient has no depression or anxiety. This is a different pharmacological rationale from prescribing an antidepressant purely to treat low mood, and the choice reflects a lack of demonstrated benefit from standard analgesics, opioids, or gabapentinoids in this specific pain category.

What is the difference between chronic primary pain and chronic secondary pain?

Chronic secondary pain is pain that persists for more than three months and is caused by, or strongly associated with, an identifiable underlying condition — for example osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, endometriosis, or diabetic peripheral neuropathy — and is managed primarily by treating that underlying condition. Chronic primary pain persists for more than three months with no clear underlying cause identified, or is disproportionate to any observable tissue damage, as in fibromyalgia or non-specific chronic low back pain; NICE NG193's specific recommendations against opioids, gabapentinoids, and other standard analgesics, and in favour of exercise, psychological therapy, and acupuncture, apply to this chronic primary pain category.

What is the first-line drug treatment for neuropathic pain?

NICE recommends offering one of amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin as first-line pharmacological treatment for neuropathic pain, with the choice guided by comorbidities, tolerability, and patient preference. If the first drug tried is ineffective or not tolerated at an adequate dose, the recommended approach is to switch to a different one of these four drugs rather than adding a second one on top, since combining them increases side-effect burden without clear added benefit. Trigeminal neuralgia is treated differently, with carbamazepine as first-line therapy, reflecting its distinct underlying mechanism.

What is the difference between tolerance, dependence, and addiction in opioid prescribing?

Tolerance is a physiological adaptation in which higher opioid doses are needed over time to achieve the same analgesic effect. Physical dependence is the body's adaptation to continued opioid exposure, such that abrupt stopping causes a withdrawal syndrome; it can occur in any patient on regular opioids for more than a few weeks, regardless of behaviour. Addiction (opioid use disorder) is a distinct behavioural pattern of compulsive use despite harm and loss of control over use. All three can coexist in long-term opioid use, but tolerance and physical dependence alone are not evidence of addiction.

Is gabapentin or pregabalin a controlled drug in the UK?

Yes. Gabapentin and pregabalin were reclassified as Schedule 3 controlled drugs in the UK in April 2019, following evidence of rising misuse and their contribution to drug-related deaths, particularly when combined with opioids. This reclassification, combined with NICE NG193's recommendation against initiating gabapentinoids for chronic primary pain, reflects a broader UK prescribing shift towards caution with this drug class outside its well-evidenced indications, such as first-line neuropathic pain treatment or epilepsy.

What non-drug treatments does NICE recommend for chronic primary pain?

NICE NG193 recommends supervised group exercise programmes tailored to the person's needs, psychological therapy in the form of acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) or cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), and a defined course of acupuncture, as the evidence-based non-pharmacological options for chronic primary pain. These interventions reflect the biopsychosocial model of pain, addressing deconditioning, fear-avoidance, and unhelpful pain-related beliefs directly, rather than relying solely on pharmacological suppression of the pain signal.

Conclusion

Chronic pain UKMLA questions reward candidates who can do three things: correctly classify pain as primary or secondary (and neuropathic or nociceptive within secondary pain), apply the right guideline to the right category rather than defaulting to the WHO analgesic ladder's escalate-the-opioid logic, and reason explicitly about opioid safety and the biopsychosocial model rather than treating pain purely as a pharmacology problem. NICE NG193's 2021 recommendation against initiating opioids, gabapentinoids, and other standard analgesics for chronic primary pain — in favour of exercise, psychological therapy, and acupuncture — is one of the most important, and most counter-intuitive, guideline changes candidates need to know cold. Revising this topic alongside related content on prescribing safety, neuropathic pain drug choice, and the biopsychosocial model, rather than in isolation, will make these AKT questions far more predictable to answer correctly.
