Transgender Health in the UKMLA: What the Content Map Covers

Transgender health UKMLA content is one of the clearest examples of how the exam has grown to reflect everyday NHS practice rather than a narrow list of diseases. The GMC's updated MLA content map changes, which apply to all Applied Knowledge Test and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment sittings from September 2026 onwards, formally add "gender dysphoria and/or incongruence" to the list of examinable conditions. This is not a stray footnote. It sits alongside a wider expansion of the map from roughly 311 to 430 conditions, and it appears in the same revision as new content on genetics and genomics, updated sepsis recognition standards, and a stronger emphasis on the wider social context of a patient's presentation. This guide sets out, in strictly clinical and curriculum terms, what a foundation doctor is realistically expected to know: how to take a respectful and clinically accurate history, what gender-affirming hormone therapy means for a non-specialist prescriber or lab-result interpreter, how NHS referral pathways to Gender Identity Clinics work, and the general principles of inclusive communication that apply across every patient group the UKMLA examines.

None of this requires specialist endocrinology knowledge, and none of it asks a foundation doctor to manage gender-affirming treatment independently. What it does require is the same baseline competence expected for any patient group: an accurate history, safe prescribing awareness, correct interpretation of investigations, and a referral to the right specialist service when that is what the patient needs. This article works through each of those requirements in turn, in the same structured, exam-focused format used across the rest of the site's clinical systems revision content.

Quick Answer: What Does the UKMLA Actually Test on Transgender Health?

Transgender health UKMLA questions are built around four realistic foundation-doctor scenarios: taking a respectful and accurate history from a transgender patient presenting with an unrelated problem, recognising that a patient may already be established on gender-affirming hormone therapy and knowing what that means for other prescribing decisions and laboratory interpretation, knowing when and how to refer a patient with gender dysphoria or incongruence to an NHS Gender Identity Clinic (GIC), and demonstrating the communication skills — correct name, correct pronouns, non-judgemental questioning — that Good Medical Practice expects for every patient, not only transgender patients.

The content map does not ask candidates to become specialists in gender-affirming endocrinology or surgery. Those areas remain the preserve of NHS Gender Identity Clinics and specialist multidisciplinary teams. The exam instead tests whether a newly qualified doctor can recognise the clinical relevance of a patient's transgender status where it genuinely matters — for example when interpreting a full blood count or estimating renal function — and can otherwise treat the presenting complaint exactly as they would for any other patient, without unnecessary or irrelevant questioning about gender identity.

Transgender Health for UK Medical Students Versus IMGs

Transgender health UKMLA preparation looks slightly different depending on a candidate's route into the exam, even though the content map and marking standard are identical for everyone. UK medical students will typically have encountered at least some formal teaching on transgender health during clinical placements, communication skills sessions, or ethics and law modules, even if this varied significantly between medical schools before the topic became a distinct content map entry. For these candidates, the main revision task is consolidation: making sure the referral pathway, the physiological effects of hormone therapy, and the communication principles are held together as a single coherent topic rather than scattered across old lecture notes from several different years of training.

International medical graduates preparing for the UKMLA registration process come from a very wide range of health systems, some of which provide extensive undergraduate teaching on transgender health and gender-affirming care, and others where the topic barely features in the medical curriculum at all. IMGs should not assume that clinical experience gained elsewhere automatically transfers to UK-specific referral pathways, terminology, or the GMC's professionalism expectations. The safest approach for an IMG is to treat this exactly like any other UK-specific process topic — for example how eligibility and registration work — and learn the NHS referral route, the relevant Good Medical Practice principles, and the specific vocabulary (gender dysphoria, gender incongruence, Gender Identity Clinic) as new, UK-specific knowledge rather than assuming it generalises automatically from training received in another country.

Both groups benefit from the same core preparation: reading the GMC's own content map wording, understanding the referral pathway at a practical level, and rehearsing the communication skills in mock CPSA stations rather than only reading about them. Because the CPSA marks observed behaviour rather than recalled facts, passive reading alone is a weaker preparation strategy for this topic than for purely knowledge-based AKT content — a distinction worth remembering when allocating revision time across the different Applied Knowledge Test and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment components.

Safeguarding, Mental Health and Wider Wellbeing Considerations

Transgender health also intersects with mental health and safeguarding content already present elsewhere in the UKMLA content map, and candidates should be prepared for questions that combine the two rather than treating transgender health as a purely endocrine or administrative topic.

Recognising Distress and Signposting Support

Research consistently associated with this patient group, and reflected in NHS guidance, shows elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and minority stress compared with the general population, often linked to experiences of discrimination or delayed access to care rather than being an inherent feature of being transgender. A foundation doctor encountering a transgender patient who discloses low mood, anxiety, or distress should apply the same structured mental health assessment used for any patient — asking about safety, support networks, and functional impact — while being alert to the possibility that minority stress or long waiting times for specialist gender services may be a contributing factor. The correct response remains generic good practice: assess risk, signpost to appropriate mental health support, and refer onward through standard mental health pathways where indicated, rather than assuming a transgender patient's mental health presentation must automatically be caused by gender-related distress.

Safeguarding Principles Apply Equally

Where a UKMLA scenario involves a young person exploring their gender identity, standard child and adolescent safeguarding and consent principles apply in full, including Gillick competence for consent-related questions and the involvement of parents or carers as clinically and legally appropriate. Specialist gender services for children and young people operate under distinct, carefully governed NHS pathways separate from adult Gender Identity Clinics, and a foundation doctor's role remains the same as with any paediatric presentation requiring specialist input: recognise the need for specialist assessment, involve the wider clinical and safeguarding team as appropriate, and refer through the correct specialist route rather than attempting an independent assessment of gender-related questions in a child.

Avoiding Diagnostic Overshadowing

A specific pattern worth revising is diagnostic overshadowing — the risk of attributing a new symptom to a patient's transgender status or mental health history rather than properly investigating it. UKMLA scenarios sometimes test whether a candidate correctly investigates a physical symptom on its own clinical merits rather than assuming it is somehow explained by the patient being transgender. The safe answer is almost always to investigate the presenting symptom using the same systematic approach applied to any other patient, and only consider hormone therapy or gender-related factors once relevant, straightforward differential diagnoses have been appropriately addressed.

Practising Transgender Health Scenarios for the CPSA

Because the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment marks observed communication behaviour, candidates benefit from rehearsing transgender health scenarios as mock stations rather than only reading about the theory. A useful practice structure mirrors the approach recommended for any other communication-skills station on the site's UKMLA preparation guidance.

A Simple Station Framework

  • Open the consultation by confirming how the patient would like to be addressed if there is any uncertainty from the notes, then move straight into the presenting complaint
  • Take a structured history relevant to the presenting problem, including a full medication history that would naturally capture any hormone therapy
  • Avoid introducing questions about gender identity, transition status, or anatomy unless they are directly relevant to the problem being assessed
  • If hormone therapy is relevant to the presentation or the investigations being requested, ask about it factually and briefly, in the same tone used for any other long-term medication
  • Where the scenario involves gender dysphoria or a request for referral, acknowledge the patient's experience, explain the referral pathway into the NHS Gender Identity Clinic, and check the patient's understanding and agreement before closing the consultation

Examiners marking this kind of station are typically looking for the same generic communication domains marked in every other CPSA station — rapport, structure, clarity, and safety-netting — rather than a separate transgender-specific mark scheme. Candidates who over-focus on gender identity as the central theme of the consultation, when the presenting complaint is unrelated, tend to lose marks for structure and relevance in the same way they would in any station where they fail to prioritise the patient's actual problem.

Why Transgender Health Was Added to the MLA Content Map

The GMC reviewed the original MLA content map through 2024 and 2025, publishing a revised version in October 2025 that applies to all MLA exams and assessments taken from September 2026 onwards. Alongside the addition of gender dysphoria and/or incongruence as an examinable condition, the review expanded descriptors for topics such as human factors and quality improvement, microbiology, and social and population health, and added clinical imaging as a distinct area. Transgender health did not appear in isolation — it forms part of a broader move to make sure the content map reflects conditions and consultation types that newly qualified doctors genuinely encounter across NHS primary and secondary care.

For UKMLA candidates, the practical implication is straightforward: transgender health is no longer an optional extra that some medical schools happen to teach and others do not. It is now core content, examinable in the same way as any other condition on the map, and candidates should revise it with the same rigour they would apply to any other new topic area — reading the source guidance, understanding the clinical reasoning behind each step, and practising the communication skills that the CPSA specifically assesses.

How This Fits the Wider 2026 Content Map Expansion

The 2026 content map update is best understood as part of a single, coordinated revision rather than a set of unrelated additions. The GMC's stated aim was to bring the map closer to the reality of a foundation doctor's caseload, which is why several disparate-seeming topics landed in the same review cycle. Alongside transgender health, the update strengthened content on consent and mental capacity — relevant here because gender dysphoria referrals, like many specialist referrals, depend on a patient's capacity to understand and consent to the referral and subsequent treatment pathway. It also expanded guidance on the social determinants of health, recognising that a patient's social context, discrimination, and access to services can materially affect their presentation and outcomes, a principle that applies directly to some transgender patients' experience of healthcare.

Candidates preparing for the 2026 sitting onwards should treat transgender health as one thread within this larger fabric rather than a stand-alone topic to memorise in isolation. Questions may combine it with communication skills stations, prescribing safety questions, or generic consent scenarios, so revision should integrate it with those themes rather than treating it as a separate silo.

Respectful and Clinically Accurate History-Taking

History-taking is where most UKMLA transgender health questions are likely to sit, because it is where the CPSA can directly assess communication skills in a scored, observed station. The core principle examined is simple: a doctor should gather the information relevant to the presenting complaint, using the patient's correct name and pronouns, without asking intrusive or irrelevant questions about a patient's gender identity, transition status, or anatomy unless that information is clinically necessary for the problem being assessed.

Using the Correct Name and Pronouns

Using a patient's correct name and pronouns is a matter of basic clinical professionalism, not a political position. Good Medical Practice requires doctors to treat patients as individuals and to respect their dignity, and this principle applies identically to a transgender patient as it does to any patient whose name has changed for any other reason, or whose title or preferred form of address differs from what is recorded in an old set of notes. In practice, this means checking how a patient wishes to be addressed at the start of a consultation if there is any uncertainty, using that name and pronoun consistently throughout the encounter, and correcting an accidental slip briefly and moving on rather than dwelling on it.

Where a patient's electronic record has not yet been updated to reflect a name change, the safe and professional approach is to acknowledge this administrative gap directly with the patient, apologise if the mismatch causes any embarrassment, and continue the consultation using the name the patient has given. UKMLA scenarios testing this skill typically reward candidates who resolve the naming issue quickly, briefly, and without making it the focus of the consultation, then move on to addressing the presenting complaint.

Only Asking What Is Clinically Relevant

A recurring theme in professionalism-based UKMLA scenarios is the distinction between clinically relevant history and irrelevant, intrusive questioning. If a transgender patient presents with, for example, a chest infection, a broken wrist, or a routine medication review, their transgender status and the details of any transition-related care are very unlikely to be relevant to the immediate problem and should not be probed unless the patient raises it or it has a specific bearing on management. Conversely, if a patient is having blood taken for renal function, having hormone levels checked, or being assessed for a condition where hormone therapy is directly relevant (see the section below), the history must include accurate information about current hormone therapy in exactly the same way it would for any other regularly prescribed medication.

The safe clinical rule taught across the CPSA is this: ask what you need to know to manage the presenting problem safely, in the same structured way you would take any other history, and do not ask questions purely out of curiosity or discomfort with an unfamiliar topic. Foundation doctors who default to a comprehensive, respectful, structured history — covering presenting complaint, relevant systems, medications, and social context — will naturally avoid both under-asking (missing a clinically relevant detail) and over-asking (intrusive questioning with no clinical justification).

Confidentiality and Sensitive Information

Information about a patient's transgender status, transition history, or gender-affirming treatment is sensitive personal information and is subject to the same confidentiality obligations as any other health information under Good Medical Practice and UK data protection law. It should not be disclosed to family members, colleagues not involved in care, or recorded in ways that are more visible than clinically necessary, without the patient's consent. UKMLA questions occasionally test this directly — for example, a scenario where a relative asks a doctor to confirm a patient's transgender status, which the doctor should decline to do without the patient's explicit permission, exactly as they would for any other confidential health information.

Gender-Affirming Hormone Therapy: What a Non-Specialist Doctor Needs to Know

The UKMLA does not expect candidates to prescribe or titrate gender-affirming hormone therapy — that specialist role sits with endocrinologists and Gender Identity Clinic teams. What it does expect is awareness that a patient may be established on hormone therapy, and an understanding of the practical downstream effects this has on other prescribing decisions and the interpretation of routine investigations. This is analogous to how the exam treats other long-term specialist-initiated therapies: a foundation doctor is expected to know that the therapy exists, recognise it in a drug history, and adjust their own generalist practice accordingly, without needing to manage the specialist therapy itself.

Recognising Hormone Therapy in a Drug History

Gender-affirming hormone therapy typically falls into two broad categories relevant to non-specialist recognition. Patients transitioning from male to female physiology may be prescribed oestrogen, usually alongside an anti-androgen such as a gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) analogue or, less commonly now, an older anti-androgen agent. Patients transitioning from female to male physiology are typically prescribed testosterone, usually as a regular intramuscular injection, transdermal gel, or implant. A thorough medication history, taken in the way it would be taken for any patient, should identify these agents by name, route, and approximate dose, exactly as it would for any other regularly prescribed hormonal medication such as combined oral contraception or thyroid replacement therapy.

The exam-relevant point is that a foundation doctor does not need to know how to initiate or adjust these therapies. They do need to record them accurately in a drug history, recognise that stopping such therapy abruptly is not usually appropriate without specialist input, and know that if a patient is unwell or requires new medication, potential interactions and relevant physiological effects should be considered in the same systematic way as with any other long-term hormonal treatment.

Effects Relevant to Other Prescribing Decisions

Hormone therapy has physiological effects that can influence decisions a foundation doctor makes in unrelated contexts. Oestrogen-based therapy is associated with an increased risk of venous thromboembolism, a factor that is clinically relevant when assessing any patient — transgender or otherwise — for surgery, prolonged immobility, or other VTE risk factors, and when completing a standard VTE risk assessment on admission. This is not a special rule invented for transgender patients; it is the same principle applied to any patient on oestrogen-containing medication, including combined hormonal contraception or hormone replacement therapy, and the UKMLA expects candidates to apply it consistently regardless of why a patient is taking oestrogen.

Testosterone therapy can affect haematocrit and haemoglobin, and may be relevant when interpreting a full blood count or considering polycythaemia in an unrelated clinical context. As with oestrogen, this is simply an application of standard clinical reasoning — a raised haemoglobin or haematocrit in a patient on testosterone therapy should prompt the same systematic thinking as it would in any patient, taking the medication history into account rather than assuming pathology where a physiological explanation is more likely.

Interpreting Laboratory Results Where Hormone Therapy Is Relevant

Several routine investigations use reference ranges that differ between typical adult male and female physiology — for example, haemoglobin, haematocrit, and creatinine (and therefore estimated glomerular filtration rate calculations that depend on it). A patient on long-term gender-affirming hormone therapy may have results that sit outside the reference range historically associated with their sex recorded at birth, and closer to the range typically associated with the hormonal environment their current therapy produces. The safe, exam-appropriate approach is straightforward: interpret results in the context of the patient's current hormonal status and clinical picture rather than assuming an abnormal result is necessarily pathological, and seek senior or specialist advice where interpretation is genuinely uncertain — exactly the same safety-netting principle applied to any ambiguous laboratory result in the UKMLA.

NHS Referral Pathways: Gender Identity Clinics

When a patient presents with gender dysphoria or gender incongruence — that is, distress or discomfort arising from a mismatch between their gender identity and the sex they were assigned at birth — the correct clinical response for a generalist doctor is referral to a specialist NHS Gender Identity Clinic (GIC), not an attempt to manage the assessment or treatment independently. Understanding this referral pathway, and recognising when it is (and is not) the appropriate answer, is directly examinable.

How the Referral Pathway Works

In current NHS practice, a patient experiencing gender dysphoria or incongruence can be referred to an NHS Gender Identity Clinic by their GP or another NHS healthcare professional, and in many services can also self-refer. Adult gender services generally require the patient to be aged 18 or over and registered with a GP. Once referred, the patient is placed on a waiting list and subsequently undergoes assessment by the specialist multidisciplinary team, who determine whether the patient meets the diagnostic criteria for gender dysphoria or incongruence — a prerequisite for accessing gender-affirming treatments such as hormone therapy or surgical referral through NHS pathways.

Following assessment, the GIC works with the patient on an individualised treatment pathway that may include hormone therapy, voice and communication therapy, and psychological support, according to the patient's wishes and needs. GPs continue to play a supporting role throughout this pathway, including shared-care prescribing arrangements and phlebotomy monitoring as advised by the specialist service — a collaborative model similar to shared care arrangements used for other specialist-initiated long-term therapies in NHS practice.

When Referral Is the Correct UKMLA Answer

UKMLA scenarios testing this pathway typically present a patient describing persistent distress related to gender identity, sometimes alongside an unrelated presenting complaint. The correct answer in these scenarios is usually to acknowledge the patient's experience, ask sensitively about how it is affecting them, and offer or arrange referral to the local Gender Identity Clinic through the standard GP-referral (or self-referral, where available) route — not to attempt a diagnostic assessment or discuss hormone therapy initiation directly, since this exceeds a generalist doctor's scope of practice. This mirrors how the exam treats referral decisions for any other specialist service: recognise the presentation, understand the appropriate pathway, and refer promptly and respectfully rather than attempting specialist management outside one's competence.

Supporting the Patient While They Wait

NHS Gender Identity Clinic waiting times can be long, and UKMLA scenarios may test what a doctor should do for a patient during this period. The generalist responsibilities are consistent with good practice for any patient awaiting specialist assessment: continuing to provide routine care for unrelated health needs without unnecessary delay, signposting to appropriate support services if the patient is distressed, and, where hormone therapy has already been initiated by a specialist service, providing agreed shared-care prescribing or monitoring support as directed by that service rather than independently altering the regimen.

General Principles of Non-Judgemental, Inclusive History-Taking

Although transgender health is now its own line in the content map, the communication skills it draws on are the same generalist skills examined throughout the CPSA for every patient group. This is a deliberate design choice by the GMC: rather than testing a separate, isolated "transgender consultation skill," the exam applies the standard principles of respectful, patient-centred communication to a specific clinical context.

Avoiding Assumptions

A safe consultation avoids assuming a patient's gender identity, relationship structure, or reproductive plans based on their appearance, name, or the sex recorded in old notes. Where any of this information is clinically relevant — for example, when taking a sexual health history or discussing fertility preservation before starting hormone therapy or receiving certain cancer treatments — it should be asked about directly, factually, and without embarrassment, in the same manner any other relevant clinical history is taken.

Fertility Considerations

Fertility preservation is a genuinely examinable point of clinical fact: gender-affirming hormone therapy can affect fertility, and patients should be counselled about fertility preservation options before starting treatment wherever possible. This counselling is ordinarily undertaken by the specialist Gender Identity Clinic team as part of the assessment and treatment pathway, but a foundation doctor should be aware that the discussion is a standard part of that pathway, in case a patient asks about it or a scenario tests recognition of this safeguard.

Applying the Same Standard Across All Patient Groups

The underlying principle tested across the UKMLA's professionalism and communication content is consistency: the standard of respectful, non-judgemental, patient-centred communication expected with a transgender patient is the same standard expected with every patient, regardless of ethnicity, religion, disability, sexual orientation, or any other characteristic. Candidates who default to structured, respectful, clinically focused questioning — checking preferred name and pronouns where uncertain, asking only what is clinically relevant, and treating sensitive information with the same confidentiality as any other health information — will apply this standard correctly regardless of the specific patient group presented in a given scenario.

Working Within a Multidisciplinary Team

Transgender health, like many areas of specialist care, is managed through a multidisciplinary team that may include the GP, the Gender Identity Clinic team, endocrinology, and allied health professionals such as speech and language therapists providing voice therapy. A foundation doctor's role within this team is generally to provide safe day-to-day generalist care, recognise when specialist input is required, and communicate clearly with the specialist team when a shared-care arrangement exists — the same team-based principle examined across the rest of Good Medical Practice's guidance on working with colleagues.

How to Revise This Topic for the UKMLA

Because transgender health is a newly added topic area, candidates should not assume their medical school curriculum has already covered it in depth, particularly if they trained before the 2026 content map took effect. The most efficient revision approach mirrors how the site recommends approaching any newly added content map topic:

  • Read the GMC's own content map descriptor for the condition directly, rather than relying solely on secondary summaries
  • Focus revision time on history-taking and communication skills, since this is where CPSA marks are most likely to be allocated
  • Learn the referral pathway into NHS Gender Identity Clinics at a practical level — who can refer, and what happens after referral — rather than memorising local service names or waiting-time figures, which vary by region and change over time
  • Understand the physiological basis for why hormone therapy affects VTE risk and full blood count interpretation, rather than memorising isolated facts, since the AKT tends to test underlying clinical reasoning
  • Practise applying the same respectful communication framework to transgender patients that is already taught for other CPSA communication stations, rather than treating it as an entirely new skill

Transgender health revision does not need to be lengthy to be effective, and as with any topic newly added to the content map, question writers are likely to start conservatively, testing recognition and safe generalist behaviour rather than obscure detail. Candidates who can demonstrate safe, respectful, clinically grounded practice — knowing what to ask, what to refer, and what not to attempt without specialist input — are well placed to answer these questions correctly, even without highly detailed subject-specific knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is transgender health a new topic on the UKMLA?

Yes. The GMC's updated MLA content map, which applies to all MLA exams and assessments taken from September 2026 onwards, formally adds "gender dysphoria and/or incongruence" to the list of examinable conditions, as part of a wider expansion of the content map from around 311 to 430 conditions.

Do I need to know how to prescribe hormone therapy for the UKMLA?

No. Gender-affirming hormone therapy is initiated and managed by specialist NHS Gender Identity Clinic teams and endocrinologists. The UKMLA expects foundation doctors to recognise that a patient may be on hormone therapy, understand its relevance to other prescribing decisions and laboratory interpretation, and know when to seek specialist advice — not to initiate or titrate the therapy itself.

How should I refer a patient with gender dysphoria in an NHS setting?

The standard pathway is referral by a GP or another NHS healthcare professional to the patient's local NHS Gender Identity Clinic; many services also accept self-referral. Adult services generally require the patient to be 18 or over and registered with a GP. The clinic's multidisciplinary team then carries out assessment and, where appropriate, agrees an individualised treatment pathway with the patient.

Why does hormone therapy matter if I am not managing it directly?

Because it affects other aspects of care a generalist doctor is responsible for. Oestrogen-based therapy increases venous thromboembolism risk, which is relevant to VTE risk assessments and peri-operative planning, while testosterone-based therapy can raise haemoglobin and haematocrit, which is relevant to interpreting a full blood count. Recognising these effects, rather than prescribing the hormone therapy itself, is what the UKMLA examines.

What communication skills are examined in transgender health CPSA stations?

The same generalist communication skills examined across the rest of the CPSA: using a patient's correct name and pronouns, asking only what is clinically relevant to the presenting complaint, avoiding assumptions based on appearance or old records, and maintaining confidentiality of sensitive personal information, consistent with the standards set out in Good Medical Practice for every patient group.

Is this topic examined differently for IMGs compared with UK medical students?

No — the content map applies identically to every MLA candidate from September 2026 onwards, regardless of route into the exam. IMGs who trained in health systems where transgender health was not part of the curriculum should treat this as core new content requiring the same dedicated revision as any other newly added topic, using GMC and NHS sources directly rather than assuming prior clinical training has already covered UK-specific referral pathways.

Where can I find the GMC's official source for this content?

The GMC publishes the full, current MLA content map, including the addition of gender dysphoria and/or incongruence, on its own website. Candidates should always check the live GMC content map directly for the most current wording, since content maps are reviewed periodically and specific descriptors can be refined between publications.

Does transgender health appear in the AKT, the CPSA, or both?

Both. The Applied Knowledge Test can examine the underlying clinical knowledge — for example why hormone therapy affects VTE risk or full blood count interpretation, and the structure of the NHS referral pathway — while the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment examines the communication behaviours directly, such as using correct names and pronouns and taking a relevant, non-intrusive history. Candidates should prepare for both formats rather than assuming transgender health is a knowledge-only or communication-only topic.

What should I do if I am unsure how to address a patient during a UKMLA station or in real practice?

The safe approach, both in the exam and in real clinical practice, is to ask the patient directly and briefly at the start of the consultation if there is any uncertainty, then use their stated name and pronouns consistently for the remainder of the encounter. This is the same general communication principle applied whenever a doctor is unsure how a patient wishes to be addressed, and it is treated as routine good practice rather than an exceptional accommodation.

Conclusion

Transgender health UKMLA content reflects a genuine, GMC-published expansion of the exam's scope rather than a marginal addition, and it is best revised the same way any other newly added condition is revised: understand the underlying clinical reasoning, learn the correct referral pathway, and practise the respectful, non-judgemental communication skills that Good Medical Practice already expects for every patient group. Foundation doctors are not expected to become specialists in gender-affirming care — that responsibility sits with NHS Gender Identity Clinics and specialist multidisciplinary teams — but they are expected to take an accurate history, recognise the practical implications of hormone therapy for prescribing and laboratory interpretation, and refer appropriately and promptly when specialist assessment is needed. Candidates who build this topic into their wider revision of communication skills, consent, and social determinants of health will be well prepared for however the 2026 content map update is examined.
