Good Medical Practice and the UKMLA: Everything You Need to Know About GMP 2024

Good Medical Practice (GMP) 2024 is one of the most heavily tested frameworks in the UKMLA, appearing in both Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) questions and Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) stations that probe your ability to handle ethically complex, real-world clinical scenarios. Updated and effective from 30 January 2024, the revised GMP replaces the 2013 edition and reorganises the duties of a doctor into four clear domains that map directly onto the type of judgement calls you will be expected to make as a newly qualified doctor in the UK. This guide breaks down every corner of GMP 2024 in the context of UKMLA preparation, covering consent, capacity, confidentiality, raising concerns, fitness to practise, and the professional and legal frameworks that underpin each domain.

What Is Good Medical Practice 2024 and Why Did It Change?

The GMC published a substantially revised edition of Good Medical Practice in January 2024 following a wide-ranging consultation that began in 2021. The core driver was to make the guidance clearer, more inclusive, and better aligned with the realities of contemporary medical practice — including team-based care, digital medicine, social media, and a renewed commitment to equality, diversity and inclusion.

The four domains of the new GMP are:

  • Domain 1: Knowledge, skills and development
  • Domain 2: Patients, partnership and communication
  • Domain 3: Colleagues, culture and safety
  • Domain 4: Trust and professionalism

Each domain contains a set of numbered obligations. The language has shifted deliberately from the older formulation of "duties" towards obligations that feel more active and reflective. Critically for UKMLA candidates, the 2024 GMP makes explicit duties that were previously implied — for example, actively contributing to an inclusive workplace culture, and using social media responsibly. These additions translate into new question themes you will encounter in the exam.

Domain 1: Knowledge, Skills and Development

Maintaining Competence

GMP 2024 requires doctors to recognise and work within the limits of their competence. For UKMLA purposes, this manifests in scenarios where a junior doctor is asked to perform a procedure they are not trained for, or where a colleague appears to be practising beyond their skill level. The correct response in such scenarios invariably involves seeking appropriate supervision, being honest about limitations, and escalating when patient safety is at risk.

The obligation extends to keeping knowledge up to date. The GMC links this to continuing professional development (CPD), which is now explicitly framed not merely as a personal obligation but as something that benefits patients and the wider healthcare system. In UKMLA scenarios, this theme often appears as questions about appraisal, revalidation, or what to do when a colleague admits they have not attended any CPD in several years.

Teaching, Training and Supervision

GMP 2024 strengthens the obligation to contribute to teaching and training. Doctors who supervise others must ensure those they supervise are competent for the tasks delegated to them, and must not put pressure on trainees to work beyond their abilities. This is an important UKMLA theme: delegation is only appropriate when the person receiving the task has the skills, support, and authority to carry it out safely. The delegating doctor retains overall responsibility. A common UKMLA question type asks what the correct response is when you, as a foundation doctor, are asked to sign off on a task you were not present for, or when a senior delegates a procedure without checking your competence first.

Continuous Quality Improvement

Domain 1 also covers participation in audit, quality improvement, and research. GMP 2024 makes explicit that doctors should engage with these activities and use findings to improve care. Research-related questions in the UKMLA may probe knowledge of research ethics, informed consent for research participation (which is governed by additional standards beyond clinical consent), and the duty to report adverse events or errors in clinical trials.

Domain 2: Patients, Partnership and Communication

Domain 2 is the richest domain for UKMLA question writers because it covers the full landscape of patient-centred care, communication, and the legal and ethical frameworks that govern consent and decision-making. This domain alone accounts for a substantial proportion of ethics and law scenarios in both the AKT and the CPSA.

Person-Centred Care and Shared Decision-Making

The 2024 GMP places significant emphasis on shared decision-making. This is not simply about giving patients information — it requires a two-way dialogue in which the doctor understands what matters to the patient and uses that to guide clinical recommendations. The Montgomery ruling (Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board [2015] UKSC 11) is the legal cornerstone here. The Supreme Court held that a doctor must take reasonable care to ensure the patient is aware of any material risks involved in any recommended treatment and of any reasonable alternative treatments. A risk is material if a reasonable person in the patient's position would be likely to attach significance to it, or if the doctor is or should reasonably be aware that this particular patient would attach significance to it.

This was a seismic shift away from the previous Bolam standard for consent, which asked only what a reasonable body of doctors would consider adequate disclosure. Montgomery puts patient values at the centre of the consent process, and GMP 2024 is fully aligned with this principle. UKMLA candidates must be comfortable applying Montgomery in clinical scenarios — for example, a patient with a needle phobia asking about the risks of surgery under general anaesthesia, or a sportsperson asking about the risk of nerve damage from a procedure that might end their career.

Consent and Mental Capacity

Consent is valid only when it is informed, voluntary, and given by a person with capacity. The Mental Capacity Act (MCA) 2005 governs assessment of decision-making capacity in England and Wales. There is a two-stage test:

Stage 1 — Is there an impairment or disturbance in the functioning of the mind or brain? This can be permanent (e.g. dementia) or temporary (e.g. acute confusion, intoxication, severe pain).

Stage 2 — Does that impairment mean the person is unable to make the specific decision at the relevant time? A person lacks capacity if they cannot do one or more of the following:

  • Understand the information relevant to the decision
  • Retain that information long enough to make a decision
  • Use or weigh that information as part of the decision-making process
  • Communicate their decision (by any means)

The MCA 2005 contains five key principles that must be applied in every capacity assessment:

  1. A person must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established that they lack it
  2. A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision unless all practicable steps to help them have been taken without success
  3. A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because they make an unwise decision
  4. An act done or a decision made under the Act for a person who lacks capacity must be done or made in their best interests
  5. Before doing so, regard must be had to whether the purpose for which it is needed can be as effectively achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the person's rights and freedom of action

These principles appear repeatedly in UKMLA scenarios. A patient refusing surgery is not automatically lacking capacity — the MCA specifically states that a person has the right to make unwise decisions. Equally, just because someone has a diagnosis of schizophrenia or a learning disability does not mean they lack capacity for a specific decision.

Best Interests Decision-Making

When a patient genuinely lacks capacity, decisions must be made in their best interests. The MCA provides a checklist for determining best interests:

  • Consider whether and when the person might regain capacity
  • Involve the person as much as possible in the decision
  • Consider the person's past and present wishes, feelings, beliefs and values
  • Consider the views of carers, family, and others with an interest in the person's welfare
  • Not be motivated by a desire to bring about the person's death (where the decision concerns life-sustaining treatment)

In UKMLA scenarios involving patients who lack capacity, you will often be asked who the decision-maker is. The answer depends on the legal framework. If there is a valid Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) for health and welfare, the attorney can make decisions within the scope of the LPA. If there is no LPA, the treating clinician is the decision-maker for the specific decision, working in consultation with those close to the patient. Where there is no one appropriate to consult, an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) must be appointed for serious medical decisions.

Advance Decisions to Refuse Treatment (ADRT)

An advance decision is a legally binding refusal of specific treatment, made in advance. For an advance decision to be valid, it must be made by a person aged 18 or over with capacity at the time, it must specify the treatment being refused, and it must be applicable to the current circumstances. If the advance decision concerns the refusal of life-sustaining treatment, it must be in writing, signed, witnessed, and include a statement that it applies even if life is at risk.

UKMLA candidates commonly struggle with the distinction between an advance decision (legally binding refusal) and an advance statement (a record of preferences and values that is not legally binding but must be taken into account in best interests decisions). Confusing these two is a frequent error — always check whether the document is specifically refusing a treatment or merely expressing a general preference.

Gillick Competence and Fraser Guidelines

When dealing with patients under 18, the principles of Gillick competence apply. Following Gillick v West Norfolk and Wisbech AHA [1986], a child under 16 can consent to treatment if they have sufficient maturity and intelligence to understand the nature and implications of the proposed treatment. This is an assessment of the individual child, not a fixed age threshold.

The Fraser guidelines specifically relate to contraceptive advice and treatment for under-16s. Under Fraser, a doctor can provide contraceptive advice and treatment to a person under 16 without parental consent or notification if:

  • The young person understands the advice
  • The young person cannot be persuaded to inform or allow a parent to be informed
  • The young person is very likely to begin or continue having sexual intercourse with or without treatment
  • Their physical or mental health will suffer unless they receive contraceptive advice or treatment
  • It is in their best interests to receive such advice or treatment without parental consent

Important: Gillick competence applies to consent; it does not automatically mean a competent child can refuse treatment. The courts have repeatedly held that where a competent young person refuses life-saving treatment, the court can override that refusal in the child's best interests.

Using Interpreters and Communication Aids

GMP 2024 explicitly addresses the duty to communicate effectively with patients, including the use of professional interpreters. Using family members as interpreters — particularly children — is strongly discouraged and potentially harmful. In UKMLA scenarios involving patients with limited English proficiency, the correct response is always to request a professional interpreter. This may mean delaying a non-urgent consultation, or using a telephone or video interpretation service urgently. Using a child family member to interpret consent discussions is never appropriate.

Domain 3: Colleagues, Culture and Safety

Teamwork and Respecting Colleagues

Domain 3 reflects the reality of modern medicine as a team endeavour. GMP 2024 requires doctors to treat colleagues with respect, to contribute positively to team culture, and to challenge discriminatory behaviour. UKMLA scenarios in this domain often present as interpersonal conflicts, colleagues behaving inappropriately, or workplace dynamics that could compromise patient safety or staff wellbeing.

Patient Safety and a Just Culture

One of the most significant additions to GMP 2024 is its explicit endorsement of a Just Culture approach to patient safety. A just culture recognises that errors occur within systems and contexts, not simply because of individual incompetence or malice. It distinguishes between acceptable human error (which should prompt system learning), at-risk behaviour (which requires coaching and support), and reckless behaviour (which warrants disciplinary response).

In UKMLA scenarios, a just culture manifests in questions about what to do after a near-miss or patient safety incident. The correct response centres on reporting through incident reporting systems (such as the NHS Datix system), supporting the individuals involved, and learning systemically rather than seeking to blame individuals. Questions may also ask about the culture expected of a leader or senior doctor — for example, a consultant who responds to errors by berating junior staff is demonstrating a culture incompatible with GMP 2024 and patient safety.

Duty of Candour

The duty of candour is one of the most frequently examined topics in UKMLA ethics sections. There are two distinct but complementary duties:

1. Statutory Duty of Candour (Regulation 20, Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014) — This applies to organisations (NHS trusts and other registered providers). When a notifiable safety incident occurs, the organisation must notify the patient or their representative as soon as reasonably practicable, provide a truthful account, apologise, and offer support. This is a legal requirement regulated by the CQC.

2. Professional Duty of Candour (GMP 2024, Domain 3) — This applies to individual doctors. GMP states that you must be open and honest with patients when things go wrong. Specifically, you must:

  • Tell the patient (or those close to them) when something has gone wrong
  • Apologise to the patient — importantly, an apology is not an admission of legal liability
  • Explain what has happened and what further treatment or support is available
  • Report the incident through appropriate channels

UKMLA candidates frequently confuse the statutory and professional duties or fail to appreciate that an apology is appropriate and required even before a full investigation is completed. A common wrong answer in UKMLA questions is to delay telling the patient until the investigation is concluded — GMP requires openness as soon as a harm is identified.

Raising Concerns

GMP 2024 makes raising concerns an explicit professional obligation. If you believe that patient safety is, or may be, compromised by poor practice, inadequate resources, or other factors, you must raise your concerns promptly and through appropriate channels. You must not be deterred by the prospect of upsetting colleagues or damaging working relationships.

The infrastructure for raising concerns in the NHS includes:

  • Freedom to Speak Up Guardians — present in every NHS organisation, they provide confidential support to staff who want to raise concerns about patient safety or workplace culture
  • Line managers and clinical leads — the first point of contact for most concerns
  • The GMC — if concerns relate to the fitness to practise of a doctor and local processes have failed
  • The CQC — for concerns about organisational standards
  • NHS England National Patient Safety Team — for systemic patient safety issues

The Public Interest Disclosure Act (PIDA) 1998 (as amended by the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013) provides legal protection for whistleblowers — individuals who make qualifying disclosures in good faith. A qualifying disclosure is one that the worker reasonably believes tends to show one or more of: a criminal offence, a breach of a legal obligation, a miscarriage of justice, danger to health and safety, damage to the environment, or cover-up of any of these. Workers who make protected disclosures cannot lawfully be dismissed or subjected to detriment because of them. UKMLA scenarios may ask what legal protections apply to a nurse or doctor who has raised concerns about patient safety and is subsequently threatened with dismissal.

Critically, if you raise a concern and it is not acted on, you must escalate further. GMP 2024 is explicit that raising a concern once and then doing nothing further is not sufficient if the risk to patients remains. In UKMLA scenarios, a foundation doctor who raises concerns to their registrar, is dismissed, and then does nothing further is not acting in accordance with GMP.

Inclusive Workplaces

A significant new emphasis in GMP 2024 is the active promotion of inclusive, respectful workplace cultures. Doctors must not discriminate against colleagues or patients on grounds including age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, or sexual orientation (the nine protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010). GMP 2024 goes further, requiring doctors to actively challenge discriminatory behaviour when they witness it, rather than simply avoiding discrimination themselves. UKMLA scenarios in this theme may involve microaggressions, bystander responses, or what to do when a colleague makes a discriminatory comment about a patient or staff member.

Domain 4: Trust and Professionalism

Honesty and Probity

Domain 4 covers the overarching qualities of honesty, integrity, and professional behaviour that underpin public trust in medicine. GMP 2024 requires doctors to be honest in all professional dealings — this includes being honest with employers (for example, on job applications), with the GMC, with colleagues, and with patients. It explicitly covers financial probity, conflicts of interest, and gifts.

On the topic of gifts, GMP 2024 confirms that doctors should refuse gifts, hospitality, or other benefits that might influence or be seen to influence their clinical decisions. Small tokens of genuine appreciation are generally acceptable, but anything of significant value, or anything that creates an expectation of benefit for the donor, should be declined. UKMLA scenarios often present as a consultant receiving pharmaceutical company hospitality or a patient offering a large cash gift — the correct answer consistently involves declining gracefully while preserving the therapeutic relationship.

Social Media and Online Behaviour

GMP 2024 addresses social media as a distinct area of professional conduct for the first time in a substantive way. The GMC's supplementary guidance on social media reinforces that the standards expected of a doctor apply equally in online spaces. Specifically:

  • Doctors must not post anything on social media that breaches patient confidentiality, even if the patient is not named
  • Doctors must not make derogatory comments about patients, colleagues, or their employer online
  • Doctors should be aware that professional and personal personas are not neatly separable online
  • Doctors must not use social media to pursue personal or professional relationships with patients

UKMLA candidates should be aware that even anonymised case descriptions posted publicly can breach confidentiality if they contain enough detail to allow identification of the patient (or by the patient themselves). The correct approach is to seek explicit consent before sharing any clinical information, even in educational contexts.

Fitness to Practise and Self-Reporting

GMP 2024 requires doctors to be honest with the GMC and with employers about anything that might affect their fitness to practise. This includes:

  • Criminal convictions and cautions (including those received outside the UK)
  • Findings of fact by other regulatory bodies
  • Health conditions that may affect the ability to practise safely
  • Disciplinary proceedings at previous employers

The duty to self-report is one of the more anxiety-provoking aspects of the GMC's framework for junior doctors, and it generates a distinct UKMLA question theme. If a foundation doctor is arrested (even if not charged), receives a caution for a minor offence, or is found to have fabricated a section of a portfolio, they have a duty to inform the GMC and their employer. Failure to self-report is itself a fitness to practise concern. UKMLA scenarios may present as a doctor being offered a conditional caution by the police and asking what professional obligations this triggers.

Professional Boundaries

GMP 2024 is explicit that doctors must not enter into sexual or romantic relationships with patients or their close relatives. This prohibition applies regardless of who initiated the contact and regardless of whether the clinical relationship is ongoing or recently concluded. The doctor-patient relationship creates an inherent power imbalance that makes genuine consent to such relationships difficult or impossible. UKMLA questions may ask about the appropriate response when a patient makes a romantic advance, or what to do if a colleague confides that they are in a relationship with a current patient.

Confidentiality: The Duty and Its Exceptions

Confidentiality is a cornerstone of the doctor-patient relationship and one of the most frequently examined areas in UKMLA ethics. The general rule is that patient information shared in the context of a clinical relationship is confidential and must not be disclosed without the patient's consent.

When Disclosure Without Consent Is Justified

There are specific, well-defined circumstances in which disclosure without consent is permitted or required:

1. Public interest / prevention of serious harm — If disclosure is necessary to prevent serious harm to the patient or a third party, and the benefit clearly outweighs the harm of breaking confidentiality. This requires careful judgement: the risk must be serious, real, and imminent. Anonymous disclosure should be considered first. A classic UKMLA scenario involves a patient who discloses to their GP that they intend to harm a named individual. While English law does not have a formal equivalent of the US Tarasoff duty (Tarasoff v Regents of University of California, 1976 — which imposed a legal duty to warn identifiable third parties at risk), GMP and the GMC's confidentiality guidance permit disclosure in such circumstances where the risk meets the threshold, and some argue there is an emerging equivalent duty in UK law. The UKMLA expects candidates to recognise that serious, credible, imminent threats to identified individuals justify disclosure to police or the at-risk person.

2. DVLA reporting — Doctors have a duty to advise patients with notifiable conditions (such as epilepsy, visual impairment meeting DVLA thresholds, certain cardiac conditions, or serious alcohol dependency) that they must inform the DVLA and must not drive. If the patient refuses to inform the DVLA despite medical advice, and the doctor believes they are continuing to drive, disclosure to the DVLA is justified in the public interest. The GMC's supplementary guidance on confidentiality and driving sets out a step-by-step process that UKMLA candidates must know: advise the patient, encourage self-reporting, document advice, and if self-reporting is refused and driving continues, inform the DVLA (and tell the patient you are doing so).

3. Knife crime / Serious Violence Duty — Under the Health and Care Act 2022, specified health authorities have a duty to cooperate in multi-agency strategies to prevent serious violence. In practice, for UKMLA purposes, if a patient presents with a stab wound or gunshot wound, the doctor should consider whether disclosure to the police is in the public interest. This does not create an automatic duty to disclose, but it requires active consideration.

4. Sharing information with the care team — Patients who share information in a clinical context generally understand that this information will be shared with other members of the treating team. This is implied consent rather than an exception to confidentiality. However, information should be shared on a need-to-know basis, and information should not be shared with individuals outside the treating team without explicit consent.

5. Sharing information with relatives — A patient's relatives, partner, or friends do not automatically have a right to access clinical information. If a patient has capacity, information can only be shared with relatives if the patient consents. If the patient lacks capacity, information may be shared with those close to the patient to the extent necessary to make a best interests decision. If a patient asks you not to share information with a relative, you must respect that request.

6. Deceased patients — The duty of confidentiality continues after death, though it weakens over time. Disclosure may be justified to assist a coroner's investigation, for research purposes (with appropriate ethical approval), or where it is in the public interest. Family members do not have an automatic right to access the deceased person's medical records.

Applying GMP in UKMLA Scenarios: Common Errors and How to Avoid Them

Error 1: Conflating Capacity with Competence

The MCA 2005 replaced the old common law concept of "competence" in England and Wales. Always use the language of the MCA: a person has or lacks capacity for a specific decision at a specific time. Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific — a person may lack capacity to consent to surgery but retain capacity to refuse a blood test. Avoid applying a global or permanent assessment of capacity without evidence for that specific decision.

Error 2: Thinking an Apology Equals an Admission of Liability

This is one of the most persistent myths in medical culture. An apology after an adverse event is required by GMP and by the statutory duty of candour. It is not an admission of legal liability, and NHS bodies are encouraged to apologise promptly. In UKMLA scenarios, candidates who choose the option of waiting for legal advice before speaking to the patient are choosing incorrectly.

Error 3: Underestimating the Threshold for Breaking Confidentiality

Many candidates break confidentiality too readily in UKMLA scenarios. The threshold for disclosure in the public interest is high — the harm must be serious, real, and not adequately addressed by other means. Curiosity from relatives, general concern about a patient's lifestyle, or vague discomfort about a situation does not justify breach of confidentiality. Always consider whether anonymisation or seeking consent could achieve the same goal.

Error 4: Failing to Escalate Concerns About Colleagues

Candidates frequently choose the option of speaking to a colleague informally about concerns (which is sometimes appropriate as a first step) but stop there, without recognising that if the concern is not resolved or is sufficiently serious, formal escalation is required. GMP 2024 is clear: patient safety obligations override collegial loyalty.

Frequently Asked Questions About GMP and the UKMLA

Does GMP 2024 apply to foundation doctors?

Yes. GMP 2024 applies to all doctors registered with the GMC, including those in foundation training. The four domains apply from day one of working as a doctor. Foundation doctors are expected to understand and apply GMP principles, even though their level of clinical responsibility differs from that of a consultant. UKMLA scenarios will present you with situations appropriate to a foundation doctor and expect you to respond in accordance with GMP.

What is the difference between a best interests decision and substituted judgement?

The MCA 2005 uses a best interests standard for decision-making when a patient lacks capacity. This asks what outcome would be best for the patient, taking into account their known wishes, values, and preferences. In contrast, substituted judgement — which is used in some US jurisdictions — asks what decision the patient would have made if they had capacity. English law does not use substituted judgement as the formal test, though in practice the distinction is subtle: the patient's likely wishes under best interests are heavily weighted. UKMLA answers should align with the MCA best interests framework.

Can a Lasting Power of Attorney refuse life-saving treatment on behalf of a patient?

A health and welfare LPA can make decisions about life-sustaining treatment, but only if the LPA document explicitly grants this authority (the donor must specifically tick the box about life-sustaining treatment when creating the LPA). Even then, the attorney must act in the patient's best interests. An attorney cannot demand treatment that is not clinically appropriate, and cannot consent to actions that would constitute murder or assisted suicide. If there is doubt about whether an LPA covers the decision in question, or whether the attorney is acting in the patient's best interests, the Court of Protection can be asked to adjudicate.

How does the duty to raise concerns interact with loyalty to colleagues?

GMP 2024 is unambiguous: the duty to protect patients takes priority over collegial loyalty. However, the manner in which concerns are raised matters — GMP encourages a graduated approach, starting with speaking directly to the colleague concerned (unless this would put patients at risk or is otherwise inappropriate), then escalating to a line manager or clinical lead, and ultimately to the GMC or CQC if local processes fail. Raising concerns is not about punishing colleagues; it is about protecting patients and supporting a culture of safety. Candidates should be aware that UKMLA scenarios will often offer a range of options from completely ignoring the concern to going straight to the GMC — the correct answer is almost always a proportionate, escalating response.

What should I do if a patient tells me they intend to harm someone?

First, explore the seriousness and specificity of the threat. Is the threat serious? Is it directed at a specific identifiable person? Is it credible and imminent? If yes, you should consider disclosure, first by attempting to encourage the patient to contact appropriate services themselves, and then, if necessary, by disclosing to the police and/or the at-risk individual. You should inform the patient that you intend to break confidentiality before doing so (unless this would increase the risk). Document your reasoning carefully. The GMC's confidentiality guidance sets out that disclosure in the public interest is justified when the risk of harm to others is serious enough to outweigh the patient's right to confidentiality. Note: the threshold is high — generalised statements of anger or frustration do not automatically justify disclosure.

Conclusion: Mastering GMP for UKMLA Success

Good Medical Practice 2024 is not merely a document to memorise — it is the ethical and professional framework within which every UKMLA scenario is set. The four domains provide a structure for thinking through complex situations systematically: Does this scenario raise questions about clinical competence or training (Domain 1)? Does it involve consent, capacity, or communication (Domain 2)? Does it require raising concerns, addressing safety, or applying the duty of candour (Domain 3)? Or does it touch on honesty, confidentiality, social media, or fitness to practise (Domain 4)? Applying this framework to practice questions will sharpen your ability to identify the ethical core of a scenario and select the response that best reflects the values of a safe, compassionate, and professionally sound doctor.
