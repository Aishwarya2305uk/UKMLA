The GMC revalidation process is how every licensed doctor in the UK proves, on an ongoing basis, that they remain up to date and fit to practise — and it is one of the least understood parts of a doctor's career for newly registered doctors, whether they qualified at a UK medical school or came through the international medical graduate (IMG) route. Registration and licensing get most of the attention during exam preparation and the early months of practice, but revalidation is the mechanism that keeps that licence live for the rest of a doctor's working life. Understanding it early — ideally from the very first week of practice — makes the difference between a straightforward five-yearly renewal and a stressful scramble to reconstruct a portfolio from memory.

This guide is written for doctors who have just been through GMC registration and are now asking a different question: what happens next? It explains, in plain terms, why revalidation exists, how the appraisal-based cycle actually works day to day, what a newly registered doctor should start doing immediately (not in year four), and what the process looks like for the minority of doctors who do not have a clear route to a Responsible Officer. Both UK graduates entering Foundation Year 1 and IMGs starting their first UK post will find the same underlying process applies to them — the difference lies mainly in how quickly each group typically finds themselves inside a formal appraisal structure.

Quick Answer: What Is the GMC Revalidation Process?

Revalidation is the ongoing, generally five-yearly confirmation that a licensed doctor remains up to date and fit to practise medicine in the UK. It is separate from registration itself: registration is the one-off decision that a doctor meets the standard required to join the medical register and hold a licence; revalidation is the recurring check that the doctor has kept that standard current. Most doctors revalidate through a Responsible Officer — a senior doctor at their employing or contracting organisation — who reviews their annual appraisals and makes a recommendation to the GMC. Doctors without a clear employer link to a Responsible Officer may instead need to go through the GMC's own assessment route, described later in this article.

In practice, revalidation is built from small, cumulative pieces of evidence rather than a single high-stakes exam. Every year, a doctor takes part in an appraisal — a structured, one-to-one conversation with a trained appraiser — that reviews their practice against the GMC's core professional standards, set out in the GMC's Good Medical Practice framework. Roughly every five years, those annual appraisals are drawn together and a recommendation is made to the GMC about whether the doctor should be revalidated. For a doctor only a few months into their first UK post, the immediate takeaway is simple: start keeping records of your practice now, because the evidence collected in year one is just as relevant to a revalidation recommendation as the evidence collected in year five.

Why Revalidation Exists

Understanding why the GMC revalidation process exists in the first place makes every subsequent requirement easier to accept as reasonable rather than bureaucratic. Revalidation exists to give patients, employers and the public ongoing assurance that a licensed doctor remains safe and competent — assurance that a one-off registration decision, made at a single point in time, cannot provide on its own. Medicine changes continuously: guidelines are updated, new evidence emerges, and a doctor's own scope of practice can shift substantially over a five-year period. A system that only ever checked competence once, at the point of joining the register, would leave a significant gap in patient safety over the following decades of a career.

How Revalidation Differs from Registration

Registration and revalidation answer two different questions, and it helps to be explicit about the distinction, because the two processes are easy to conflate when a doctor is newly qualified or newly arrived in the UK. Registration answers: does this doctor currently meet the standard required to practise medicine in the UK? That question is answered once, at the point a doctor joins the register, whether via provisional registration and a Foundation Programme, full registration after satisfying the relevant requirements, or one of the other routes onto the register available to IMGs. Revalidation answers a different question: is this doctor still up to date and fit to practise, several years into their career, based on how they have actually been practising since? That question has to be asked repeatedly, because the answer can change over time in ways a single registration decision could never capture.

It is worth noting that revalidation is also conceptually distinct from a fitness to practise investigation, even though both processes ultimately protect patients and relate to the same overarching standards. Revalidation is a routine, cyclical, largely evidence-based process built around appraisal; it is not, in itself, a disciplinary mechanism. Concerns that arise through revalidation can, in some circumstances, feed into a separate fitness to practise process, but the two are administered differently and exist for different primary purposes — readers wanting the full picture of how concerns are formally investigated should see the companion guide on how GMC fitness to practise investigations work.

The Patient Safety Rationale

The core justification for revalidation is straightforward: patients and employers benefit from an ongoing, structured check that a doctor's knowledge, skills and professional behaviour remain current, rather than relying solely on the assumption that a qualification obtained years earlier still reflects present-day competence. Revalidation supports this in several concrete ways. It gives every doctor a formal, protected space — the annual appraisal — to reflect honestly on their own practice, including areas that went well and areas that need development. It creates a paper trail of colleague feedback, patient feedback, complaints, compliments and continuing professional development (CPD) that a Responsible Officer can review holistically rather than in isolation. And it gives employing organisations a structured mechanism for identifying doctors who may need additional support, long before any single incident would otherwise trigger concern.

For a newly registered doctor, this rationale matters practically as much as philosophically: understanding that revalidation is designed as an ongoing developmental process, not a one-off hurdle, should change how a doctor approaches their first appraisal. It is not something to pass or fail in the way an exam is passed or failed — it is closer to a recurring professional check-in, most of which is entirely within the doctor's own control to prepare for well.

A Distinct Decision From the One-Off Registration Decision

Because revalidation and registration are separate decisions made at separate points, a doctor's registration status does not automatically confirm anything about their revalidation status, and vice versa. A doctor can hold full registration and a licence to practise while still being some years away from their first revalidation submission date. Equally, revalidation itself does not re-examine whether a doctor still meets the original registration requirements — it examines whether their subsequent practice, since registering, has remained consistent with Good Medical Practice. Keeping these two processes conceptually separate from the outset avoids a common source of confusion for newly registered doctors, who sometimes assume revalidation is simply "registration renewal." It is not a renewal of the original decision; it is an entirely separate, ongoing confirmation layered on top of it.

How the Appraisal-Based Revalidation Cycle Works

The GMC revalidation process is built around a repeating annual cycle of appraisal, which is then reviewed cumulatively roughly every five years to produce a revalidation recommendation. Understanding the internal structure of that annual cycle is the single most useful thing a newly registered doctor can do, because it clarifies exactly what evidence needs to be collected, and when.

The Annual Appraisal

Most licensed doctors working within a designated body — typically an NHS trust, health board, or other substantive employer — take part in an annual appraisal with a trained appraiser, usually a senior doctor. The appraisal is a structured, confidential, one-to-one conversation that reviews the doctor's practice over the preceding year against the domains set out in Good Medical Practice: knowledge, skills and performance; safety and quality; communication, partnership and teamwork; and maintaining trust. Rather than being an abstract discussion, the appraisal is built around a defined set of supporting information the doctor brings to the meeting.

At the end of the appraisal, the doctor and appraiser agree a personal development plan for the year ahead — areas to focus CPD on, skills to develop, or objectives arising from feedback received. This forward-looking element is not a formality; it forms part of the continuous thread of evidence a Responsible Officer later reviews when making a revalidation recommendation, since it shows whether a doctor is actively engaging with their own professional development year on year, rather than treating each appraisal as an isolated event.

What Counts as Supporting Information

The specific categories of supporting information a doctor is expected to bring to appraisal are broadly consistent across specialties and employing organisations, although the exact volume and format can vary. As a general guide, doctors are expected to gather and reflect on:

  • Continuing professional development (CPD) — courses, conferences, e-learning and other structured learning completed during the year
  • Quality improvement activity — audits, case reviews, or other structured reflections on how care is delivered in the doctor's own practice
  • Significant events — a reflective account of any significant event the doctor was involved in, including what was learned and what, if anything, changed as a result
  • Feedback from colleagues — typically gathered periodically (not necessarily every single year) via a structured multi-source feedback exercise
  • Feedback from patients — similarly gathered periodically via structured patient feedback exercises, where the doctor's role involves direct patient contact
  • Complaints and compliments — a record of any formal complaints made about the doctor's practice, and how they were handled, alongside any compliments received
  • A review of the previous year's personal development plan — showing whether the objectives set at the last appraisal were actually met

None of this needs to be extensive or polished in year one. The purpose of supporting information is honest, evidenced reflection — a short, genuine reflective account of a significant event is worth far more to an appraiser than a lengthy but superficial one. Newly registered doctors sometimes worry that they have "nothing to bring" to their first appraisal because they have not yet accumulated years of material; in reality, even a few months of practice generates enough genuine material — a memorable case, a piece of structured feedback, a completed e-learning module — to make for a substantive, useful first appraisal.

From Annual Appraisal to the Five-Year Recommendation

Roughly every five years, a doctor reaches their revalidation submission date — the point by which their Responsible Officer must make a formal recommendation to the GMC about whether they should be revalidated. That recommendation is not based on a single new assessment; it is based on the cumulative pattern shown across the doctor's annual appraisals during that cycle. A Responsible Officer reviewing a doctor ahead of their submission date is essentially asking whether the doctor has engaged consistently and appropriately with appraisal, whether any significant concerns have arisen and, if so, whether they were satisfactorily addressed, and whether the overall pattern of evidence supports continued fitness to practise.

There are three broad outcomes the GMC can reach following a Responsible Officer's recommendation. Where the evidence supports it, the GMC revalidates the doctor: their licence continues uninterrupted, a new revalidation submission date is set, and the doctor carries on practising exactly as before. Where there are gaps — for example, a period of leave, a change of employer partway through the cycle, or incomplete supporting information — the GMC can defer the decision, giving the doctor additional time to address the gap rather than treating it as a failure. Only in more serious circumstances, where significant concerns about a doctor's fitness to practise are unresolved, does revalidation intersect with a formal fitness to practise process rather than simply being deferred. For the overwhelming majority of doctors who engage consistently with annual appraisal, revalidation is a straightforward, low-friction renewal — the deferral and non-engagement scenarios are the exception, not the norm, but they are far easier to avoid than to resolve retrospectively, which is exactly why early engagement matters so much.

Revalidation for Doctors in Training

Doctors still in a training programme — including UK graduates in Foundation Year 1 and Foundation Year 2, and IMGs who have entered a UK training pathway — generally have their engagement with appraisal reviewed through their training programme's own annual review process rather than a separate standalone employer appraisal, since training programmes already build in a structured annual review of progress. The underlying principle is the same: a trainee is expected to engage with reflective practice and professional development throughout training, and that engagement feeds into their overall revalidation record in essentially the same way a substantive post's annual appraisal does. A newly registered Foundation doctor does not need a separate appraisal process bolted on top of their existing training reviews — but they do need to understand that the reflective habits built during training are not solely for the benefit of the training programme; they are also, quietly, building the foundation of their long-term revalidation record.

What a Newly Registered Doctor Should Start Doing From Day One

The single most common mistake newly registered doctors make with revalidation is treating it as a distant concern — something to think about in year three or four, once a first appraisal is imminent. This is a mistake because the raw material for a strong appraisal record — reflections, feedback, evidence of learning — is generated continuously throughout practice, and is far easier to capture in real time than to reconstruct months or years later from memory.

Start a Reflective Log Immediately

The most practical single habit a newly registered doctor can build is keeping a simple, ongoing reflective log from their very first week of practice, well before any formal appraisal is scheduled. This does not need to be sophisticated — a private, dated running document noting memorable cases, things that went well, things that could have gone better, and any learning points arising from each, is more than sufficient as a starting point. Many NHS employers and royal colleges provide structured e-portfolio systems for exactly this purpose, and a newly registered doctor should ask their induction team or supervisor which system applies to their post as early as possible, rather than waiting until it becomes urgent.

The habit matters more than the format. A doctor who writes two or three brief, honest reflections a month from day one will arrive at their first formal appraisal with a genuinely useful body of material, distributed naturally across the whole year rather than reconstructed retrospectively in the fortnight before the appraisal date — a pattern appraisers can generally recognise, and one that produces weaker, less specific reflections regardless.

Understand Good Medical Practice Early

Because every appraisal — and, cumulatively, every revalidation recommendation — measures a doctor's practice against the GMC's Good Medical Practice framework, it is worth becoming genuinely familiar with that framework early, rather than treating it as background reading to be skimmed once before an appraisal. Good Medical Practice sets out the professional standards the GMC expects of every licensed doctor, organised around domains covering knowledge and skills, safety, communication and maintaining trust. A newly registered doctor who understands these domains from the outset is better placed to recognise, in real time, which everyday incidents and pieces of feedback are actually relevant reflective material for their portfolio — rather than only recognising this in hindsight.

Keep Structured Records, Not Just Memories

Beyond a personal reflective log, a newly registered doctor should get into the habit of formally retaining specific categories of evidence as they occur, rather than relying on memory:

  • Certificates or completion records for any CPD, courses, or e-learning undertaken
  • Copies of any structured feedback received, whether from formal multi-source feedback exercises or informal written feedback from colleagues and supervisors
  • A brief written note at the time of any significant event, complaint, or notable compliment — even if the doctor does not yet know whether it will be formally relevant
  • Records of any audits, quality improvement projects, or case-based discussions participated in
  • A running note of objectives discussed with supervisors, and progress against them

This is exactly the kind of supporting information described earlier that feeds directly into annual appraisal. Building the habit of capturing it as it happens — rather than trying to remember it — is, in practical terms, the single highest-leverage thing a newly registered doctor can do for their long-term revalidation record.

Ask Early Who Your Responsible Officer Is

A newly registered doctor starting a substantive post should ask, during induction, who their organisation's Responsible Officer is and how the local appraisal process works — including how appraisers are allocated and when the first appraisal is likely to be scheduled. Most doctors employed in a substantive NHS or equivalent post will have a clear route to a Responsible Officer through their employer's designated body, and simply confirming this early removes any uncertainty. It is doctors in more fragmented working patterns — locum work without a single substantive employer, for example — who are more likely to need to actively establish this, which is covered in the next section.

The Responsible Officer's Role in Revalidation

For the majority of licensed doctors, the Responsible Officer is the central figure in the entire revalidation process — the person who translates a doctor's annual appraisal record into a formal recommendation the GMC actually acts on.

Who the Responsible Officer Is

A Responsible Officer is generally a senior, licensed doctor connected to the organisation — known as a designated body — where a doctor has their main, substantive practice, most commonly their employer. Every doctor connected to a designated body has an identifiable Responsible Officer with statutory responsibility for overseeing their revalidation. In a large NHS trust, the Responsible Officer role is typically held by a senior figure such as the medical director, and in practice much of the day-to-day appraisal administration is handled by a wider appraisal team acting on the Responsible Officer's behalf, even though the formal recommendation itself is the Responsible Officer's responsibility.

What the Responsible Officer Actually Does

Across the five-year cycle, the Responsible Officer's core function is to oversee the annual appraisal system within their organisation, ensure doctors connected to them are engaging with appraisal appropriately, and — critically — review the cumulative pattern of appraisals ahead of each doctor's revalidation submission date to decide what recommendation to make to the GMC. That recommendation is not made in isolation from the doctor: any significant concerns are generally expected to have been raised with the doctor well before the recommendation is due, giving them a genuine opportunity to respond or address the concern, rather than the recommendation coming as a surprise.

It is worth being clear that the Responsible Officer's recommendation is a recommendation, not the final decision — the GMC itself makes the ultimate revalidation decision, informed by that recommendation. In the overwhelming majority of cases the two align, but the distinction matters conceptually: the Responsible Officer is not personally granting or withholding a doctor's licence, they are providing the structured, evidence-based assessment the GMC's own decision relies on.

Changing Employer or Designated Body

A newly registered doctor's link to a Responsible Officer is not fixed for their entire career — it follows their designated body, which typically changes whenever they change substantive employer. Doctors rotating through Foundation and specialty training posts, in particular, may find their formal designated body and Responsible Officer link is managed through their training programme's host organisation rather than each individual rotational placement. This is generally handled administratively by the training programme rather than requiring the doctor to personally re-establish the link at every rotation, but it is still worth confirming at the start of a new post, especially for IMGs unfamiliar with how UK training rotations are administered, simply to avoid any uncertainty building up unnoticed across a training programme.

What Happens Without a Responsible Officer

The standard GMC revalidation process described above assumes a doctor has a clear connection to a designated body and a Responsible Officer, but that assumption does not hold for every licensed doctor. Not every licensed doctor has a straightforward link to a Responsible Officer through a single substantive UK employer. This is a genuinely relevant scenario for some newly registered doctors — particularly certain locums working across multiple organisations without one main substantive post, doctors between UK posts, or doctors practising in settings that do not have a designated body in the usual sense.

Who This Applies To

Doctors most likely to find themselves without a clear Responsible Officer connection include those doing exclusively locum work spread across several organisations rather than holding one substantive post, doctors who hold a licence but are not currently working in the UK, and doctors in other atypical working arrangements that do not map cleanly onto a single designated body. If a newly registered doctor is unsure whether they have a Responsible Officer, the GMC's own online account clearly shows this connection, and it is worth checking directly rather than assuming.

The Alternative Route

The GMC does provide an alternative route for doctors without a Responsible Officer or an approved "suitable person" to make a recommendation on their behalf. In broad terms, doctors in this position are expected to arrange their own appraisal with a suitably qualified appraiser who meets the GMC's requirements, and to submit an annual return to the GMC directly, along with supporting evidence of their engagement with revalidation and their continued fitness to practise. Because this route does not have the same built-in recommendation process as the standard employer-based model, the GMC may also require the doctor to complete a separate assessment to independently confirm they remain fit to practise.

The precise procedural detail of this alternative route — including exactly what the annual return must contain and how any required assessment is structured — is set out directly by the GMC and is genuinely worth reading in full rather than relying on a summary, since it is updated from time to time and applies to a relatively small, specific group of doctors. Any newly registered doctor who suspects they may fall into this category — most commonly locums without a single substantive UK post — should consult the GMC's own guidance on managing registration and revalidation directly and, where uncertain, contact the GMC to confirm which route actually applies to their specific circumstances.

Why This Matters More for Some IMGs

This scenario is worth flagging specifically for IMGs, because the path from registration into a substantive UK post is not always immediate. An IMG who has completed GMC registration but has not yet secured a substantive training or employed post — for example, while still working through the wider pathway from the UKMLA into an NHS role — should understand that holding a licence without a connected designated body can, in principle, place them in the "no Responsible Officer" category described above. In practice, most newly registered doctors move relatively quickly into a substantive post with a clear Responsible Officer link, but for those in a longer gap between registration and employment, it is worth being aware that revalidation obligations do not simply pause during that period — they follow a different route instead.

Revalidation and Other Regulatory Processes

Revalidation does not operate in isolation from the GMC's other regulatory functions, and newly registered doctors benefit from seeing how it fits alongside them.

Revalidation and Fitness to Practise

As already noted, revalidation and fitness to practise investigations are distinct processes with different triggers and purposes. Revalidation is cyclical and evidence-based, built around routine annual appraisal; a fitness to practise investigation is typically triggered by a specific concern raised about a doctor, from any source, and follows an entirely separate investigatory process. That said, information arising through appraisal can, in specific circumstances, be relevant to a fitness to practise matter, which is one more reason honest, well-documented reflective practice serves a doctor well in both contexts. Readers wanting a full explanation of how the separate fitness to practise process actually works should see the companion article on GMC fitness to practise explained for new doctors.

Revalidation Across an Expanding Regulated Workforce

It is also worth understanding that the GMC's regulatory remit has expanded in recent years to cover roles beyond doctors, including physician associates and anaesthesia associates. The revalidation process described throughout this article applies specifically to licensed medical doctors — the parallel governance and oversight arrangements that apply to these newer regulated roles are a distinct topic, covered in the companion article on GMC regulation of physician associates, which is worth reading for doctors working alongside these roles who want clarity on where the two systems of oversight differ.

Common Pitfalls Newly Registered Doctors Should Avoid

A handful of avoidable mistakes account for most of the friction newly registered doctors encounter with the GMC revalidation process, and all of them are easier to avoid from the outset than to fix retrospectively.

Waiting Until the First Appraisal to Start Reflecting

Treating the first scheduled appraisal as the starting point for reflective practice, rather than the first check-in on reflective practice that has already been ongoing, is the single most common and most avoidable mistake. Reflections written from memory, months after the fact, are consistently weaker and less specific than reflections captured close to the event — and appraisers can generally tell the difference.

Losing Track of Employer or Designated Body Changes

Doctors who move between posts, particularly across a training rotation or between locum placements, sometimes lose track of exactly which organisation currently holds responsibility as their designated body. This matters because gaps or ambiguity in this link can complicate an otherwise straightforward revalidation recommendation. Confirming the current designated body and Responsible Officer connection at the start of every new post is a simple, low-effort habit that prevents this.

Assuming Registration and Revalidation Are the Same Process

As covered earlier, conflating registration with revalidation is a common early misunderstanding. A doctor's registration status confirms they met the standard to join the register at a single point in time; it says nothing, on its own, about where they currently stand in their revalidation cycle. Newly registered doctors should treat the two as entirely separate ongoing responsibilities from day one.

Not Checking GMC Online Regularly

A doctor's GMC Online account is the authoritative source for their own revalidation submission date, their recorded Responsible Officer connection, and any formal notices the GMC has issued about their revalidation. Checking this account periodically, rather than relying solely on reminders from an employer's appraisal team, is a simple safeguard against any administrative gap going unnoticed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the GMC revalidation process for newly registered doctors?

The GMC revalidation process is the ongoing, generally five-yearly confirmation that a licensed doctor remains up to date and fit to practise, based primarily on their annual appraisals. For a newly registered doctor, the process begins immediately in the sense that reflective practice and evidence-gathering should start from day one, even though the first formal appraisal and the first five-year recommendation both come later.

How often does GMC revalidation happen?

Revalidation recommendations are generally made on a five-yearly cycle, with each doctor given a specific revalidation submission date that can be checked in their GMC Online account. That five-yearly recommendation is built from the doctor's annual appraisals completed throughout the cycle, rather than a single standalone assessment carried out only once every five years.

What is the difference between GMC registration and revalidation?

GMC registration is the one-off decision that a doctor meets the standard to join the medical register and hold a licence to practise; revalidation is the separate, recurring confirmation that a doctor remains fit to keep that licence, based on their ongoing practice. A doctor can be fully registered and licensed while still being years away from their next revalidation submission date, since the two processes track different questions.

Do doctors without a substantive UK post still need to revalidate?

Yes. Doctors without a clear link to a Responsible Officer — for example, certain locums working without one main substantive employer — still have revalidation obligations, but they generally follow a different route involving their own arranged appraisal and a direct annual return to the GMC, sometimes alongside a GMC-run assessment. Doctors in this position should consult the GMC's own guidance directly to confirm exactly what is required for their specific circumstances.

What happens at an annual appraisal?

An annual appraisal is a structured, confidential conversation with a trained appraiser that reviews a doctor's practice over the preceding year against the Good Medical Practice framework, using supporting information the doctor has gathered — including CPD records, reflections on significant events, and colleague or patient feedback. The appraisal concludes with an agreed personal development plan for the following year, which itself becomes part of the evidence reviewed at the next appraisal.

What should a newly registered doctor do to prepare for revalidation from day one?

A newly registered doctor should start an ongoing reflective log immediately, well before any formal appraisal is scheduled, noting memorable cases and learning points as they happen rather than reconstructing them later. They should also retain records of CPD completed, feedback received, and any significant events or complaints, and confirm early in a new post who their Responsible Officer is and how the local appraisal system works.

Who makes the final decision on GMC revalidation?

The GMC itself makes the final revalidation decision, but that decision is informed by a formal recommendation from the doctor's Responsible Officer, based on the doctor's cumulative appraisal record across the cycle. In the great majority of cases the GMC's decision follows the Responsible Officer's recommendation directly, since the recommendation is itself built on a structured review of the doctor's evidenced practice.

Conclusion

The GMC revalidation process is not a one-off exam to prepare for at the last minute — it is a continuous professional habit that starts the day a doctor is first licensed and runs for the rest of their career. For newly registered doctors, whether UK graduates just beginning Foundation training or IMGs settling into their first UK post, the most valuable single action is starting a genuine reflective log immediately, understanding how annual appraisal builds toward the five-yearly recommendation, and confirming early who their Responsible Officer is — or, for the smaller number of doctors without one, understanding that a separate GMC-run route exists and reading that guidance directly. Treated this way, revalidation becomes a straightforward, low-stress renewal built on evidence gathered naturally throughout the year, rather than a scramble reconstructed from memory once a submission date is looming.
