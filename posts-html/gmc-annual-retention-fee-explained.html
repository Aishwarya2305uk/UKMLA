The GMC annual retention fee is the yearly charge every doctor with a licence to practise in the UK must pay to remain on the medical register, and understanding it properly matters just as much once you are registered as passing the exams did to get there in the first place. Unlike the one-off registration fee paid when you first join the register, the annual retention fee is a recurring obligation that continues for as long as you hold UK registration — whether you are working full-time in an NHS hospital, taking a career break, on parental leave, or living and working overseas while keeping your registration active for a future return. For UK medical graduates who progress from provisional to full registration, and for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) who have just paid substantial fees to sit the AKT and CPSA and register for the first time, this fee is easy to overlook amid the excitement of finally being registered — but missing it can have consequences that are disproportionately serious compared with the size of the bill itself.

This guide is deliberately narrow in scope. It does not cover what happens when you want money back from the GMC — for that, see our separate guide to the GMC registration refund policy, which explains the £110 scrutiny fee, refund timelines, and partial retention fee refunds on voluntary removal. Nor does it explain the different categories of registration itself — for that distinction, see our guide to the types of GMC registration. What follows here is focused entirely on the ongoing annual charge itself: what it is, why it exists, roughly how much it currently costs, how you can pay it, what happens if you don't, and which doctors may qualify for a reduced rate.

Because the GMC reviews its fee schedule periodically — sometimes annually — every figure in this guide should be treated as a snapshot rather than a permanent number. We link directly to the GMC's own Fees for doctors page throughout, and we strongly recommend checking that page directly before you budget for, or pay, your own annual retention fee.

The Quick Answer: What the GMC Annual Retention Fee Is

In short: the GMC annual retention fee (often abbreviated ARF) is the yearly payment that every doctor holding registration and a licence to practise must make to the General Medical Council to remain on the medical register. Following the fee changes that came into force on 1 April 2026 under the General Medical Council Registration Fees Regulations 2026, the standard annual fee for full registration with a licence to practise rose to £408, while newly qualified doctors in their first year on the register pay a reduced rate of £157. These are the headline figures at the time of writing, but because the GMC reviews and can adjust its fee schedule with each annual regulations update, you should always confirm the exact current amount on the GMC's own Fees for doctors page before relying on a specific number.

The fee can be paid annually in a single payment or spread across the year by Direct Debit, in either quarterly or ten-monthly instalments, at no extra cost. Doctors whose gross taxable income falls below a set threshold can apply for a 50% income discount on the fee. And, critically, non-payment without an approved reason does not simply result in a late fee or a warning — it can lead to automatic removal from the register, a process the GMC calls administrative erasure, which means the doctor is no longer legally permitted to practise medicine in the UK until registration is restored.

That single paragraph captures the shape of everything covered in more detail below: what the fee funds, how the current amount is set, the ways you can pay it, what genuinely happens if you don't, and who may be entitled to a reduced rate. Each of the audiences reading this guide — UK graduates newly on the register and IMGs who have just completed the registration process — face broadly the same rules here, since the annual retention fee applies identically regardless of where a doctor originally qualified.

What the GMC Annual Retention Fee Actually Funds

Understanding why the fee exists in the first place makes the rest of this guide easier to follow, and it also explains why the GMC treats non-payment so seriously rather than as a minor billing lapse.

The GMC's Statutory Role

The General Medical Council is the independent statutory regulator for doctors, physician associates and anaesthesia associates practising in the UK. Its core functions include maintaining the medical register itself, setting the standards doctors are expected to meet, overseeing medical education and training (including the content of the UKMLA), handling fitness-to-practise concerns, and running the revalidation system that confirms doctors remain up to date and fit to practise throughout their careers. None of this work is funded by general taxation — the GMC is a registered charity that operates independently of government, and its running costs are met almost entirely by the fees doctors themselves pay.

Why the Fee Is Doctor-Funded Rather Than Government-Funded

This funding model is deliberate, not incidental. A regulator that depended on government money for its day-to-day budget would be vulnerable to political interference in how it investigates concerns about individual doctors or sets professional standards. By contrast, a profession that funds its own regulator through a retention fee preserves the GMC's independence from ministerial control, which is itself considered a safeguard of public trust in the register. In practical terms, this means the annual retention fee you pay each year goes directly toward maintaining the register, running fitness-to-practise investigations and hearings, supporting revalidation, and overseeing medical education standards — including, for UKMLA candidates specifically, the ongoing development and quality assurance of the exam itself.

What This Means for Doctors Preparing for or Just Past the UKMLA

For candidates who have spent months preparing for the AKT and CPSA, it can feel like the financial commitment ends once registration is granted. In reality, registration is the start of an ongoing financial relationship with the regulator, not a one-off transaction. Budgeting for the GMC annual retention fee as a recurring yearly cost — much like a professional subscription or membership fee — avoids the unpleasant surprise of an unexpected bill landing a year after registration, at a point when a newly registered doctor may be settling into a first UK post, managing relocation costs, or juggling other financial pressures.

How Much Is the GMC Annual Retention Fee Currently?

This is the section most readers come to this guide for, so it is worth being precise about both the current figures and the necessary caveat that accompanies them.

Current Standard Rate

Following the General Medical Council Registration Fees Regulations 2026, which came into effect on 1 April 2026, the standard annual retention fee for a doctor holding full or specialist/GP registration with a licence to practise is £408 per year. This is the rate that applies to the great majority of working doctors, both UK graduates and IMGs, once they have completed their first year on the register.

Reduced Rate for Newly Qualified Doctors

Doctors in their first year of registration — typically those who have just completed the UKMLA pathway and joined the register, whether as a UK graduate moving from provisional to full registration or an IMG registering for the first time — pay a lower introductory rate, currently £157 for that first year. This reduced first-year rate recognises that newly qualified doctors are often at the most financially constrained point of their early career, having just paid registration and, for IMGs, exam and verification fees on top.

Why the Fee Changes From Year to Year

The GMC's retention fee is not set arbitrarily. Each year, the regulator reviews its operating costs — including the volume and complexity of fitness-to-practise casework, the costs of running revalidation, and the costs of maintaining and developing the register and its underlying IT systems — against its income, which comes almost entirely from registration and retention fees. When costs rise faster than efficiency savings can offset, the fee is adjusted accordingly, usually by a modest amount year on year rather than a dramatic jump. Doctors who want to understand exactly how a given year's fee was calculated, rather than simply what the figure is, can consult the GMC's own "How we use your fee" page, which breaks down where retention fee income is spent across the organisation's core functions.

How UK Graduates and IMGs Experience the Fee Differently

Although the GMC annual retention fee itself is the same regardless of where a doctor originally qualified, the practical experience of paying it differs somewhat between UK medical graduates and IMGs, largely because of timing and context rather than the rule itself.

UK Medical Graduates

UK graduates typically first encounter the retention fee shortly after converting from provisional registration (held during the Foundation Year 1 training year) to full registration, which usually happens around the end of FY1. Because this transition tends to happen alongside a stable NHS salary and an established UK bank account, UK graduates are often better placed to set up a Direct Debit early and treat the fee as a routine annual deduction, similar to professional indemnity cover or a professional body subscription.

International Medical Graduates

IMGs face a somewhat different practical picture. Many pay their first annual retention fee at the same time as, or shortly after, completing full registration — often before they have started a substantive UK post, and sometimes before they have opened a UK bank account or received their first UK salary. This timing mismatch is one of the more common reasons IMGs miss or delay their first retention fee payment, not through any intention to avoid it, but simply because the administrative sequencing of "register, then find a job, then get paid, then set up UK banking" does not always align neatly with the GMC's payment deadline. IMGs who are still finalising visa, housing, or banking arrangements in the weeks after registration should treat setting up their retention fee payment method as a priority alongside those other tasks, not something to defer until settled — a point covered further in the non-payment section below. For a full breakdown of every cost an IMG pays across the entire pathway — from EPIC verification through to the AKT, CPSA and initial registration fee — see our complete 2026 cost guide for IMGs.

Doctors Working or Living Outside the UK

A related point applies to both UK graduates and IMGs who move abroad after registering: the retention fee obligation does not pause simply because a doctor is no longer physically working in the UK. A doctor who keeps their UK registration active — for example, to preserve the option of returning to NHS practice later, or because their registration underpins a role that involves periodic UK-based work — remains liable for the annual retention fee for as long as that registration is held, regardless of country of residence. Doctors who genuinely do not intend to return to UK practice, and who want to stop this ongoing liability, should consider requesting voluntary removal from the register rather than simply allowing payments to lapse — a distinction explained in detail in the refund policy guide linked above, since voluntary removal can produce a partial refund of any unused portion of a fee already paid, whereas non-payment leads to erasure with no refund at all.

Payment Options for the GMC Annual Retention Fee

The GMC offers more than one way to pay the annual retention fee, and choosing the right one can make a genuine practical difference, particularly for doctors managing tight monthly budgets or irregular income.

Paying Annually in a Single Payment

The simplest option is to pay the full annual retention fee as a single payment, either by debit/credit card through GMC Online or via a one-off Direct Debit collection, at the point your registration year renews. This suits doctors who prefer to deal with the fee once a year and move on, and it avoids any ongoing instalment administration.

Paying by Direct Debit Instalments

Doctors, physician associates and anaesthesia associates can instead choose to spread the annual retention fee across the year through a Direct Debit instalment plan, selecting either quarterly instalments or ten monthly instalments. Crucially, it costs exactly the same to pay by instalments as it does to pay in one go — there is no surcharge or hidden interest for spreading the payments, which makes the instalment route a straightforwardly useful option for doctors who prefer to manage cash flow in smaller, more predictable amounts rather than a single larger annual debit.

Setting Up a Direct Debit

To pay by instalments, you need a UK bank account capable of accepting Direct Debit collections. When setting up the instalment plan, you can choose from five available collection dates each month — the 1st, 7th, 14th, 21st or 28th — allowing you to align the debit with your own salary or income schedule rather than an arbitrary GMC-set date. Direct Debit instructions must generally be set up by a fixed annual deadline (commonly the end of October ahead of the new registration year beginning in December) — check the exact current deadline on the GMC's how to pay your fee page, since missing the setup window for one payment style can mean defaulting to the annual lump-sum method for that year instead.

A Practical Note for IMGs Without a UK Bank Account Yet

Because Direct Debit instalments require a UK bank account, IMGs who have only just arrived in the UK, or who are still registering from overseas, may need to pay the first year's fee as a single card payment before a UK account is fully operational, then switch to Direct Debit instalments in a later year once UK banking is in place. This is a normal and common sequencing issue, not a problem specific to any individual applicant, but it is worth planning for rather than discovering at the point of payment.

Updating Your Payment Details

Whichever method you choose, keeping your payment details current on GMc Online is essential — a Direct Debit instruction tied to a closed bank account, or a card payment method tied to an expired card, is one of the most common reasons a routine annual payment fails, sometimes without the doctor immediately noticing. Since a failed payment starts the same non-payment countdown described in the next section, checking that your payment details on file are current is a five-minute task worth doing whenever your banking arrangements change, not just when a renewal reminder lands.

What Happens If You Don't Pay: Non-Payment and Administrative Erasure

This is the section of the guide that matters most for any doctor who is temporarily out of the UK, on leave, between jobs, or simply distracted by a busy rotation when a renewal notice arrives — because the consequences of missing the GMC annual retention fee are more serious, and more automatic, than many doctors initially assume.

The Legal Basis for Non-Payment Removal

The Medical Act 1983, as amended, gives the GMC's Registrar the statutory authority to remove a doctor from the register administratively if that doctor fails to pay their annual retention fee. This process is known as administrative erasure, and it is distinct from any fitness-to-practise sanction — it is a straightforward, largely automatic consequence of an unpaid fee, not a disciplinary judgement about a doctor's clinical competence or conduct.

How the Process Unfolds

The GMC does not erase a doctor from the register the moment a payment is missed. In practice, the GMC begins sending payment reminders in the weeks before the fee is due — commonly starting around 31 days ahead of the payment deadline — giving doctors a window to pay, query, or resolve any issue with their payment method before the deadline passes. If no payment is received and no approved exemption or extension applies, the doctor's registration is ultimately removed once the non-payment period runs its course. At that point, the doctor is no longer on the medical register.

What Removal Actually Means in Practice

The practical consequence of administrative erasure is significant and immediate: a doctor who is not on the register cannot legally practise medicine in the UK. Continuing to work as a doctor after being removed from the register for non-payment would, in effect, mean practising without a licence — a breach of the Medical Act itself, not merely an administrative irregularity. This is precisely why the GMC treats non-payment so firmly rather than allowing an informal grace period to drift on indefinitely: the register exists to give patients, employers and the public certainty about who is currently entitled to practise, and an unpaid, lapsed registration undermines that certainty just as much as any other gap in the register would.

No Refund for Non-Payment Removal

As covered in more detail in our dedicated guide to the GMC registration refund policy, no refund is given when a doctor's registration is removed for failure to pay a required fee. This differs meaningfully from voluntary removal, where a doctor who proactively asks to come off the register partway through a paid year may be entitled to a partial refund of the unused portion of that year's fee. A doctor who simply allows their registration to lapse through non-payment receives nothing back — the removal is treated as a regulatory consequence of the doctor's own default, not a request the doctor initiated.

Restoring Your Registration After Non-Payment Erasure

If a doctor is removed from the register for non-payment and later wants to return to UK practice, restoration is possible but requires a fresh application process — it is not simply a case of paying the missed fee and resuming as before. Restoration typically involves completing an online application, paying both the outstanding renewal fee and a separate restoration fee, providing an up-to-date statement from a Responsible Officer confirming recent practice or fitness to return, and passing identity and fitness-to-practise checks again. Because the exact restoration fee and evidentiary requirements can vary depending on how long a doctor has been off the register and their individual circumstances, any doctor facing this situation should contact the GMC directly and consult the current guidance on the Fees for doctors page rather than assume the process mirrors an ordinary annual renewal.

Why This Matters Especially for Doctors Who Are Temporarily Out of the UK

The single most common scenario that catches doctors out is exactly the group this guide is written for: doctors who are temporarily overseas, on extended parental or carer's leave, between UK posts, or focused on further study or specialty training abroad, who assume their registration obligations somehow pause while they are not actively working in the UK. They do not. The annual retention fee continues to fall due on schedule regardless of whether you are currently employed as a doctor in the UK, working abroad, on leave, or between contracts — the obligation attaches to holding registration, not to being in active UK employment at that moment. Doctors in any of these situations should treat updating their contact details and payment method with the GMC, before departure or before going on leave, as a standing task — not an afterthought to handle "when I'm back."

Keeping the GMC Informed of Your Contact Details

Separately from non-payment, failing to maintain an effective means of contact with the Registrar is itself a distinct ground for administrative erasure, and it compounds the payment risk described above — a doctor who has moved, changed email addresses, or let their GMC Online contact details go stale may simply never see the payment reminders that would otherwise have prompted timely action. Keeping your registered contact details current, particularly your email address and postal address, is therefore not just good administrative practice but a direct safeguard against both payment-related and contact-related removal.

Reduced and Discounted Fee Categories

Not every doctor pays the full standard rate. The GMC operates a small number of genuine, published discount categories worth knowing about before you assume the standard fee automatically applies to you.

The Income Discount

Doctors whose gross taxable income during their registration year falls below the GMC's published income threshold can apply for a 50% discount on their annual retention fee. This discount exists specifically to avoid the retention fee becoming a disproportionate burden for doctors in genuinely low-income circumstances — for example, those working part-time, taking an extended unpaid career break, or in the early stages of a role with limited hours. The discount can be applied for online through GMC Online, and doctors can typically apply in advance of a registration year based on an estimated income, then confirm at the end of that year whether their actual income remained below the threshold. Because the exact income threshold is reviewed periodically alongside the standard fee itself, always check the current figure on the GMC's own Fees for doctors page before assuming your own income qualifies.

The Newly Qualified First-Year Rate

As covered above, doctors in their first year of registration pay a reduced rate rather than the full standard fee — a form of built-in discount that applies automatically based on registration date rather than requiring a separate application. This is particularly relevant for UKMLA candidates moving straight from exam success into their first year of practice, since it directly reduces the cost of that first, often financially tightest, year on the register.

Other Circumstances Worth Checking

Beyond the income discount and first-year rate, the GMC periodically reviews whether other specific circumstances — such as doctors who are not currently practising at all, or particular categories of registration without a licence to practise — attract a different fee structure. Because these categories can change and are genuinely dependent on individual registration status, doctors who believe their circumstances might qualify for anything other than the standard rate should check directly with the GMC or consult the current fee schedule rather than assume eligibility based on a general description in this guide.

Budgeting for the Fee Alongside Other UKMLA and Registration Costs

For candidates who have just come through the UKMLA pathway, it is worth placing the annual retention fee in the wider context of the costs already paid to get to registration in the first place, so the recurring nature of this particular fee doesn't come as a surprise.

A Recurring Cost, Not a One-Off

The AKT fee, the CPSA fee, and the initial registration fee are all one-off costs paid once during the pathway to becoming a licensed doctor in the UK. The GMC annual retention fee is different in kind — it recurs every single year for as long as you hold registration, which for most doctors means for the rest of their working life, and in many cases beyond, if registration is kept active into retirement for any reason. Budgeting for it as an ongoing annual line item, rather than treating registration as a single finished transaction, avoids the fee catching newly registered doctors off guard a year after they assumed the costs of becoming a doctor were behind them.

How It Compares to the Rest of the Pathway's Costs

In pure cost terms, the annual retention fee is modest compared with the AKT, CPSA, and EPIC verification fees IMGs typically pay to reach registration in the first place — but its recurring nature means it accumulates over a career in a way none of those one-off costs do. A doctor practising in the UK for several decades will pay the retention fee, in some form, every single year of that career, making it, in cumulative terms, one of the largest total costs of a UK medical career even though any single year's payment is comparatively small. This is precisely why understanding payment options, discount eligibility, and the consequences of non-payment matters more than the headline figure alone might suggest.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the GMC annual retention fee?

The GMC annual retention fee is the yearly payment every doctor with a licence to practise must make to the General Medical Council to remain on the UK medical register. It funds the GMC's regulatory functions, including maintaining the register, running fitness-to-practise investigations, and overseeing revalidation and medical education standards, and it applies equally to UK graduates and international medical graduates once registered.

How much is the GMC annual retention fee right now?

Following the fee regulations that took effect on 1 April 2026, the standard annual retention fee for full registration with a licence to practise is £408, with newly qualified doctors in their first year paying a reduced rate of £157. Because the GMC reviews its fees periodically, always confirm the current figure on the GMC's own Fees for doctors page before budgeting for or paying your own fee.

Can I pay the GMC annual retention fee in instalments?

Yes. Doctors can pay by Direct Debit in either quarterly or ten monthly instalments, choosing a collection date of the 1st, 7th, 14th, 21st or 28th of the month. It costs exactly the same to pay by instalments as it does to pay the full amount in one go, so instalments are simply a cash-flow choice rather than a cheaper or more expensive option.

What happens if I don't pay my GMC annual retention fee?

If the fee goes unpaid without an approved reason, the GMC can remove the doctor from the register through a process called administrative erasure, under powers granted by the Medical Act 1983. A doctor removed from the register in this way cannot legally practise medicine in the UK until their registration is restored, and no refund is given for a removal caused by non-payment.

Does my GMC annual retention fee still need to be paid if I am working or living abroad?

Yes. The obligation to pay the annual retention fee attaches to holding UK registration, not to being actively employed in the UK at that moment. Doctors who are temporarily overseas, on leave, or between UK posts but who keep their registration active remain liable for the fee each year, and should keep their payment method and contact details current with the GMC regardless of where they are living or working.

Can I get a discount on my GMC annual retention fee?

Yes, in specific circumstances. Doctors whose gross taxable income falls below the GMC's published threshold can apply for a 50% income discount on the fee, and doctors in their first year of registration automatically pay a reduced introductory rate rather than the standard fee. Check the GMC's current fee schedule to confirm the exact income threshold and rates that apply.

What is the difference between the GMC annual retention fee and the initial registration fee?

The initial registration fee is a one-off charge paid when you first join the medical register, while the annual retention fee is a recurring yearly charge paid to remain on the register for every subsequent year you hold registration. Can I get my GMC annual retention fee back if I come off the register?

It depends on how you come off the register. If you voluntarily request removal partway through a year you have already paid for, you may be entitled to a refund of the unused portion of that year's fee. If your registration is instead removed for failing to pay the fee in the first place, no refund is given at all. Conclusion

The GMC annual retention fee is a modest but genuinely recurring obligation that continues for as long as a doctor holds UK registration — currently around £408 a year for most doctors, with a reduced first-year rate and a 50% income discount available in the right circumstances, though every figure should be verified against the GMC's current fee schedule before you rely on it. The real risk this guide has tried to highlight is not the size of the fee itself, but the consequence of letting it lapse: non-payment can lead to automatic removal from the register, with no refund and a genuine restoration process required to return to practice. Whether you are a UK graduate settling into your first post, or an IMG newly registered and still finding your feet with UK banking and administration, treat the retention fee as a standing annual commitment from day one rather than something to think about only when a reminder arrives.
