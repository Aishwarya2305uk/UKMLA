Types of GMC registration confuse almost everyone who first encounters them, including UK medical students who assume "registration" is a single event rather than a ladder with several distinct rungs. In reality, the General Medical Council maintains four categories a doctor's name can sit under at any given time — provisional registration, full registration, registration with entry on the Specialist Register, and registration with entry on the GP Register — and each one means something different about what that doctor is legally permitted to do. A newly qualified doctor starting Foundation Year 1, a consultant leading a hospital department, and a GP partner running their own practice are all "GMC registered," yet each one sits under a different one of the types of GMC registration, and only one of them holds a licence that lets them work entirely independently in their chosen field. Learning to tell these types of GMC registration apart is one of the first pieces of GMC terminology every doctor working toward, or already holding, UK registration eventually needs to master.

This guide exists because the site's companion post on GMC registration requirements for international doctors already covers the process of getting registered — eligibility, primary source verification, English testing, the UKMLA, and the application itself. That guide answers "how do I register." This one answers a different, equally common question: once registered, what type of registration does a doctor actually hold, what does each type allow them to do, and how does a doctor move from one type to the next over the course of a career? Understanding the distinction between provisional and full registration, and between full registration and registration on the Specialist or GP Register, matters just as much for a UK graduate approaching their Foundation Programme as it does for an international medical graduate (IMG) planning a multi-year route to consultant or GP partner status.

A second, closely related source of confusion runs throughout this topic: registration and licensing are not the same thing. A doctor can be registered with the GMC without holding a licence to practise, and it is specifically the licence — not registration on its own — that permits prescribing, signing statutory certificates, and working clinically in the UK. This guide sets out both concepts side by side, in plain terms, for both UK medical students and IMGs, alongside a clear, structural overview of the different types of GMC registration and how a doctor typically moves between them.

Quick Answer: The Four Types of GMC Registration

The GMC recognises four main types of registration: provisional registration, full registration, full registration with entry on the Specialist Register, and full registration with entry on the GP Register. Provisional registration is held almost exclusively by UK medical graduates during Foundation Year 1 (F1), under close supervision. Full registration is the standard licence to practise held by the great majority of working doctors in the UK, including nearly all IMGs from the point their GMC registration is granted. Entry on the Specialist Register or GP Register sits on top of full registration and confirms a doctor has met the recognised standard for independent practice in a medical specialty or in general practice — it is generally required before a doctor can take up a substantive consultant post or become a GP partner or principal in the NHS.

Each of these four types of GMC registration answers a slightly different question about a doctor's status. Provisional and full registration describe the general legal authority to practise medicine and the level of supervision that applies. Specialist and GP registration describe something additional and more specific: recognition that a doctor has completed the training, or an equivalent standard, expected of a consultant-level specialist or an independent GP. A doctor can hold full registration for an entire career without ever entering the Specialist or GP Register — plenty of staff-grade, trust-grade, and locally employed doctors do exactly this — but no doctor can enter the Specialist or GP Register without first holding full registration. Keeping these four types of GMC registration distinct in your mind — rather than treating "GMC registered" as one flat status — is the single most useful mental model this guide can offer.

Provisional Registration: The Foundation Year 1 Licence

Provisional registration is a limited category of GMC registration held almost exclusively by newly qualified UK medical graduates during their first postgraduate year, Foundation Year 1 (F1), and it permits practice only within an approved F1 training programme under defined supervision. It exists because a newly graduated doctor, however well they performed academically and clinically at medical school, has not yet demonstrated the specific competencies the GMC requires before granting the full, unrestricted authority to practise that experienced doctors hold. Provisional registration is deliberately transitional: no doctor is expected to remain on it for more than a single year. Of all the types of GMC registration, this is the only one designed from the outset to be temporary rather than a stable, long-term status.

A provisionally registered doctor holds a licence to practise alongside their provisional registration, and this combination is what actually authorises them to work clinically as an F1 doctor — registration and licensing operate together here just as they do at every other stage. Provisional registration is granted automatically to graduates of UK medical schools once their degree is confirmed, generally shortly before they start their F1 post, and applications can be submitted up to three months in advance of the F1 start date.

What Provisional Registration Allows and Restricts

Among the types of GMC registration compared in this guide, provisional registration is by far the most tightly restricted, and understanding exactly what it does and does not allow prevents a common early misunderstanding for new F1 doctors.

A provisionally registered doctor may only work in posts that form part of an approved Foundation Year 1 programme, and only under supervision appropriate to that stage of training. This is a genuine legal restriction, not merely a professional convention: a provisionally registered doctor cannot lawfully take up a post that requires full registration, however capable they may be in practice. The restriction exists specifically because F1 is the year in which a doctor is formally assessed against the outcomes the GMC requires before granting full registration — outcomes covering safe prescribing, clinical decision-making across a range of common presentations, and professional behaviours consistent with Good Medical Practice.

Consequently, the F1 year functions as a supervised bridge between undergraduate training and independent practice, rather than simply the first year of a job. The doctor's educational supervisor and the wider Foundation Programme structure exist specifically to support and assess the doctor against these outcomes, and successful completion is what unlocks the next registration type: full registration.

Who Holds Provisional Registration

Because the four types of GMC registration are not evenly distributed across the profession, it helps to know in advance which doctors typically hold each one before assuming a given rule applies to your own situation.

In practice, provisional registration is held overwhelmingly by graduates of UK medical schools moving directly into F1. IMGs occasionally hold provisional registration too, in specific and less common circumstances — for example, an IMG who secures an F1-equivalent post through a particular route may be provisionally registered for that year in the same way a UK graduate would be. However, the great majority of IMGs who pass the UKMLA and complete the registration process described in the site's guide to GMC registration requirements for international doctors move straight to full registration, since they are not entering an F1 post and have typically already completed an internship or equivalent supervised period in their own country. This is one of the clearest structural differences between the UK-graduate route and the IMG route through the same overall registration system, and it is worth understanding early, since it changes which of the types of GMC registration a new doctor should expect to encounter first.

For UK medical students specifically, the practical sequence is to sit finals, receive confirmation of the primary medical qualification from the medical school, apply for provisional registration with a licence to practise in the months before graduation, and then begin F1 once that provisional registration is confirmed. Anyone approaching this stage should read the site's dedicated guide to the Foundation Programme after the UKMLA for the full detail on how the UKMLA, provisional registration, and the F1 application process fit together in sequence, since that guide covers the F1 application timeline and structure in far more depth than is useful to repeat here.

Full Registration: The Standard Licence to Practise

Of the four types of GMC registration set out in this guide, full registration is by far the most common, and it is the status the overwhelming majority of doctors practising in the UK hold at any given time.

Full registration is the standard category of GMC registration held by the majority of working doctors in the UK, and it is what most people mean when they refer generically to "being GMC registered." A doctor with full registration and a licence to practise may work in any medical post that does not specifically require entry on the Specialist Register or GP Register — this covers the entire range of non-consultant, non-GP-partner clinical roles across the NHS and private practice, including senior trust-grade and staff-grade posts, registrar-level training posts, and locally employed doctor roles.

For UK graduates, full registration is granted automatically once F1 is satisfactorily completed and the Foundation Programme confirms the required outcomes have been met — this typically happens at the point of moving from F1 into F2, and the GMC's own guidance on joining the register sets out the exact application mechanics for this transition. For IMGs, full registration is generally granted directly, at the point their registration application is approved, without an intervening period of provisional registration, since the eligibility criteria for IMG registration already require evidence broadly equivalent to what UK provisional registration and F1 are designed to establish.

What Full Registration Permits

Full registration is, in practical terms, the broadest of the types of GMC registration in scope, since it covers every clinical role that does not specifically require Specialist or GP Register entry.

Full registration with a licence to practise permits independent practice across the full range of medical work that does not require Specialist or GP Register entry. This is a broad category: it includes core and specialty training posts (ST1 upward, including registrars working toward a CCT), staff-grade and specialty doctor posts, locally employed doctor (LED) and trust-grade posts, and any substantive post that does not carry a contractual requirement for consultant or GP-partner status specifically. In practice, this means a fully registered doctor without Specialist or GP Register entry can still practise clinically, prescribe, hold a training number, and progress through a full specialty training programme — the Specialist/GP Register requirement only bites at the point a doctor seeks to take up the specific, substantive consultant or GP partner/principal post at the end of that training.

Doctors who entered the UK through a locally employed doctor post, rather than a numbered specialty training post, hold exactly the same full registration as their training-post colleagues — the distinction between LED roles and formal training numbers is an employment and career-structure distinction, not a registration-category distinction. The site's dedicated guide to the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration explains how LED posts fit into the wider registration and career picture, and how a doctor working as an LED can still ultimately reach the Specialist or GP Register via the CESR or CEGPR routes covered in that guide.

Full Registration Without Specialist or GP Registration

It is entirely normal, and common, for a doctor to hold full registration for years — or an entire career — without ever entering the Specialist Register or GP Register. Doctors in staff-grade, specialty doctor, and many trust-grade posts frequently make a deliberate career choice to remain in these roles rather than pursue the specific route onto the Specialist or GP Register, and full registration alone is entirely sufficient, permanent, and legally valid for this kind of practice. There is no expiry date or automatic downgrade attached to full registration; a doctor holds it indefinitely, alongside their licence to practise, for as long as they remain in good standing and complete revalidation as required. Unlike some of the other types of GMC registration covered in this guide, full registration does not carry any implicit expectation of further progression — it is a stable, permanent endpoint for many doctors, not merely a waypoint.

Specialist Register and GP Register: Registration for Independent Specialty Practice

The remaining two types of GMC registration covered in this guide — the Specialist Register and the GP Register — sit apart from provisional and full registration in an important way: they are not alternatives to full registration, but an additional layer built on top of it.

Entry on the Specialist Register or the GP Register is an additional registration status, held on top of full registration, that confirms a doctor has met the standard the GMC requires for independent practice in a specific medical specialty or in general practice. Neither register can be entered without full registration already in place, and neither is a separate "licence to practise" in its own right — a doctor's licence to practise remains the same underlying authorisation throughout; what changes on entering the Specialist or GP Register is the additional confirmation of specialty-level competence recorded against that doctor's name.

The practical significance of Specialist or GP Register entry is primarily contractual and structural rather than a change in day-to-day clinical authority. NHS employers generally require Specialist Register entry before appointing a doctor to a substantive consultant post, and GP Register entry before a doctor can become a GP partner, principal, or take up certain independent GP roles. A doctor without Specialist or GP Register entry can still work extensively in that specialty — as a registrar, a staff-grade doctor, or a locum, for example — but cannot generally be appointed to the specific substantive consultant or GP partner post that formally requires register entry.

Routes Onto the Specialist Register

Moving onto the Specialist Register, one of the more advanced types of GMC registration covered here, can happen through either of two genuinely different routes, depending on how and where a doctor trained.

The standard route onto the Specialist Register is completion of an approved UK specialty training programme, culminating in the award of a Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT). A CCT confirms a doctor has completed the full, GMC-approved curriculum for a given specialty, and it leads to automatic entry on the Specialist Register once awarded. This is the route the majority of UK-trained specialists and most IMGs who enter UK specialty training via the standard route will eventually follow.

A second route exists for doctors who have not followed the standard UK specialty training programme but can demonstrate they have reached an equivalent standard through other means — for example, overseas specialty training, research, or a combination of UK and non-UK clinical experience. This route is called the Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration, or CESR, and it is a common and legitimate pathway for many experienced IMGs and locally employed doctors who did not enter a UK training number but have nonetheless accumulated specialist-level experience and evidence. Because CESR involves a detailed, evidence-based application process quite different from the straightforward CCT route, the full mechanics — what evidence is required, how the application is assessed, and realistic timelines — are covered in depth in the site's guide to the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration, rather than repeated here.

Routes Onto the GP Register

The equivalent standard route onto the GP Register is completion of GP specialty training, which in the UK typically runs for three years following F2 and leads to a CCT in General Practice. Doctors who complete this training and are awarded the CCT are entered on the GP Register in the same way CCT holders in other specialties enter the Specialist Register.

For doctors who have not completed UK GP specialty training but can demonstrate equivalent experience — commonly IMGs with substantial general practice experience gained overseas, or doctors who have worked extensively in UK general practice without a training number — the equivalent evidence-based route is the Certificate of Eligibility for General Practice Registration, or CEGPR. As with CESR, the detailed evidence requirements and process for CEGPR are covered in the site's dedicated guide to the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration, which this guide deliberately does not duplicate.

Why Some Doctors Never Need the Specialist or GP Register

Not every doctor's intended career requires Specialist or GP Register entry, and it is worth stating this plainly since it is a frequent point of confusion for both UK graduates and IMGs at earlier career stages. Doctors who trained overseas in a recognised specialty may, in some circumstances, be exempt from needing to sit the full UKMLA in the first place — a genuinely separate question from registration category, covered in the site's guide to UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors. Meanwhile, doctors who are content to remain in staff-grade, specialty doctor, or non-partner GP roles indefinitely do not need Specialist or GP Register entry at all, since full registration already permits this kind of practice. The decision to pursue CCT, CESR, or CEGPR is therefore a career decision layered on top of registration status, not a mandatory next step every doctor must eventually take — and recognising this removes a good deal of unnecessary pressure for doctors who are simply unsure which of the types of GMC registration their own career actually requires.

Registration vs Licence to Practise: The Distinction Almost Everyone Misses

Every one of the types of GMC registration described so far can, in principle, exist with or without an accompanying licence to practise, which is why this distinction deserves its own dedicated section rather than a passing mention.

Registration and licence to practise are related but conceptually separate things: registration places a doctor's name on the GMC register, while a licence to practise is the specific additional permission required to actually work as a doctor, prescribe medicines, and sign certain statutory documents in the UK. It is entirely possible to be registered without holding a licence to practise, and understanding why this distinction exists resolves a large share of the confusion doctors — both UK graduates and IMGs — encounter around GMC terminology.

A helpful way to think about the difference: registration is the GMC's confirmation of who a doctor is and what qualifications and standing they hold — their name, their qualification, and their registration category (provisional, full, Specialist, or GP). A licence to practise is a separate, additional status confirming that a doctor is currently required to meet, and is meeting, the ongoing obligations of practising medicine in the UK — principally, participation in revalidation. A doctor can therefore be registered but not licensed, for example if they have retired from clinical practice, moved into a purely non-clinical or academic role that does not require prescribing or treating patients, or chosen to give up their licence while remaining on the register for professional or historical reasons.

What a Licence to Practise Actually Authorises

Because a licence to practise sits alongside, rather than inside, the four types of GMC registration, it is worth spelling out exactly what holding one adds in practical, day-to-day terms.

Holding a licence to practise, alongside the relevant registration category, is what specifically authorises a doctor to prescribe medicines, sign death certificates and other documents that legally require a licensed doctor's signature, and hold certain NHS and clinical posts that explicitly require a current licence. A registered-but-unlicensed doctor cannot lawfully do any of these things, however long they have held GMC registration or however extensive their prior clinical experience. This is why job applications, locum agencies, and NHS employers routinely ask specifically about licence-to-practise status, not just registration status — the two answers can legitimately differ for the same doctor at different points in their career.

For almost every doctor actively working clinically in the UK — including the great majority of both UK graduates and IMGs at every career stage covered in this guide — registration and licensing go together as a single combined status, and this guide's earlier sections describe them together for exactly that reason. The distinction becomes practically important chiefly at the margins: doctors stepping back from clinical practice, doctors moving abroad temporarily, doctors in purely research or administrative roles, and doctors considering whether to give up their licence while remaining registered.

Revalidation and Maintaining a Licence to Practise

A licence to practise is not granted once and then held indefinitely without further obligation; doctors who hold one must take part in revalidation, a recurring process through which the GMC confirms a doctor remains up to date and fit to practise. Revalidation typically involves regular appraisals with an employer or a designated body, alongside supporting evidence submitted periodically to the GMC. A doctor who does not engage with revalidation risks having their licence withdrawn, even though their underlying registration may be unaffected in the short term. Because revalidation is a substantial process in its own right, with its own timeline and evidence requirements, the full mechanics are covered separately in the site's guide to the GMC revalidation process explained, which this guide intentionally does not repeat.

Giving Up or Losing a Licence Without Losing Registration

A doctor can choose to give up their licence to practise voluntarily — commonly on retirement, when moving into a role that genuinely does not require one, or when relocating outside the UK for an extended period — while remaining on the register in a non-licensed capacity. This is a deliberate, formal choice made through GMC Online, and it differs materially from losing registration entirely, which is a far more serious step generally associated with fitness-to-practise proceedings rather than a routine administrative choice. A doctor who has voluntarily given up their licence can typically apply to restore it later if they return to clinical practice, subject to meeting the GMC's requirements at that time, including evidence of continued fitness to practise.

How a Doctor Moves Through the Registration Types Over a Career

Having covered each of the four types of GMC registration individually, it is worth stepping back and tracing how a doctor typically moves between them over the course of a full career.

Doctors typically progress through these registration categories in a broadly predictable sequence: provisional registration during F1 for UK graduates (or full registration directly for most IMGs), then full registration from F2 or from initial IMG registration onward, and finally, for those who pursue it, entry on the Specialist Register or GP Register once specialty training or an equivalent recognised route is complete. Setting this sequence out explicitly, for both audiences this guide serves, makes clear why the four categories exist as a ladder rather than four unrelated labels.

The Typical UK Graduate Pathway

A UK medical student typically holds no GMC registration at all until shortly before graduation, at which point they apply for provisional registration with a licence to practise, timed to take effect at the start of their F1 post. Successful completion of F1 leads to full registration, generally granted automatically as the doctor moves into F2. From that point, the doctor holds full registration for the remainder of their career unless and until they complete CCT in a chosen specialty or in general practice, at which point they are entered on the Specialist Register or GP Register respectively. A UK graduate who chooses a staff-grade or non-partner GP career path may simply remain on full registration indefinitely, by choice, without ever needing Specialist or GP Register entry.

The Typical IMG Pathway

The IMG route through these types of GMC registration is worth tracing in full, since it skips one stage entirely and can proceed through several different onward routes depending on a doctor's prior training.

An IMG's pathway differs at the very first step: rather than provisional registration, most IMGs move directly to full registration once their GMC registration application — covering eligibility, EPIC verification, English language evidence, and UKMLA success, as set out in the site's guide to GMC registration requirements for international doctors — is approved. From that point, an IMG's pathway toward the Specialist Register or GP Register can run through several different routes depending on how and where they trained: entering UK specialty training from scratch and completing a CCT in the normal way; working as a locally employed doctor while separately building a CESR or CEGPR portfolio based on their prior overseas training and experience; or, for a smaller number of doctors trained in a recognised specialty overseas, potentially qualifying for the UKMLA exemption route covered in UKMLA exemptions for specialist and GP doctors before this sequence even begins. The site's guide to the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration sets out the CESR and CEGPR routes in full detail, since they are the routes most relevant to IMGs who did not enter a numbered UK training post.

Where New Registrants Often Start

Regardless of which of the types of GMC registration a new doctor is moving into, the first few weeks of UK clinical practice bring a separate set of practical adjustments worth preparing for in advance.

Doctors of any background arriving from overseas, or UK graduates about to start F1, benefit from understanding what to expect in their first weeks and months of UK clinical practice, independent of registration category specifically. The site's GMC welcome to UK practice induction guide covers this practical settling-in period, and is worth reading alongside this guide precisely because registration category and day-to-day induction into UK clinical culture are two different things that new doctors sometimes conflate.

Why the Distinction Between Registration Types Matters in Practice

Understanding which of the four types of GMC registration a doctor holds, and whether a licence to practise currently accompanies it, has concrete consequences well beyond terminology. Job applications and locum bookings routinely specify a minimum registration requirement — a post advertised as requiring "full registration with a licence to practise" cannot lawfully be filled by a provisionally registered doctor, however strong their underlying clinical ability. Substantive consultant and GP partner posts specifically require Specialist or GP Register entry as a contractual precondition, meaning a highly experienced doctor without register entry may be entirely unable to apply for such a post, regardless of years of relevant clinical experience.

For IMGs in particular, misunderstanding these categories can lead to two common, avoidable mistakes. The first is assuming full registration alone is sufficient for any consultant-level post, when in fact Specialist or GP Register entry is generally required separately. The second is assuming CESR or CEGPR is unavailable or unrealistic without first understanding the evidence-based nature of those routes, and consequently either over-committing to a lengthy UK training programme unnecessarily or under-preparing an achievable CESR/CEGPR application. Reading the registration-type ladder set out in this guide alongside the detailed CESR/CEGPR mechanics in the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration guide is the most reliable way to avoid both errors.

For UK medical students, the practical takeaway is more immediate: knowing that provisional registration is strictly time-limited to F1, and that full registration follows automatically on satisfactory completion, removes a common source of anxiety around whether moving between these types of GMC registration is something that needs separately, actively pursuing at each stage. In the great majority of cases, the transition from provisional to full registration is a straightforward administrative step that follows naturally from satisfactory F1 progress, not a competitive or uncertain application.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the different types of GMC registration?

The different types of GMC registration recognised by the GMC are: provisional registration, full registration, full registration with entry on the Specialist Register, and full registration with entry on the GP Register. Provisional registration applies only during Foundation Year 1 for UK graduates; full registration is the standard status held by most working doctors, including nearly all IMGs; and Specialist or GP Register entry sits on top of full registration, generally required before taking up a substantive consultant or GP partner post.

What is the difference between provisional and full GMC registration?

Of all the types of GMC registration, these two are the ones most frequently confused by newly qualified doctors, so the distinction is worth restating plainly here.

Provisional registration permits practice only within an approved Foundation Year 1 programme, under supervision, while a doctor works toward the outcomes required for full registration. Full registration removes this restriction and permits independent practice across any post that does not specifically require Specialist or GP Register entry, provided a licence to practise is also held.

Do international medical graduates get provisional registration?

Most IMGs move directly to full registration once their GMC registration application is approved, rather than passing through provisional registration first, since they are not typically entering a UK Foundation Year 1 post and have generally already completed an internship or equivalent supervised period in their home country. Provisional registration is held almost exclusively by UK medical graduates entering F1, with only limited exceptions for IMGs in specific F1-equivalent circumstances.

Is a doctor with full registration allowed to become a consultant?

Full registration alone is generally not sufficient to take up a substantive consultant post in the NHS; this specific role generally requires entry on the Specialist Register, awarded on completion of a Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT) or through the equivalent CESR route for doctors who did not complete standard UK specialty training. A doctor can work extensively within a specialty on full registration alone — for example as a registrar or staff-grade doctor — without yet holding Specialist Register entry.

Can a doctor be registered with the GMC but not have a licence to practise?

Yes. Registration and licensing are separate statuses, and a doctor can hold any of the four registration categories without a current licence to practise — for example, after retirement, when moving into a non-clinical role, or after voluntarily giving up their licence. It is specifically the licence to practise, not registration alone, that permits prescribing, signing certain statutory documents, and working clinically in the UK.

How do I move from full registration to the Specialist Register or GP Register?

The standard route is completing an approved UK specialty or GP training programme and being awarded a CCT, which leads to automatic entry on the relevant register. Doctors who trained differently — commonly IMGs and locally employed doctors — can instead apply through the CESR or CEGPR routes, which assess equivalent overseas or non-standard experience against the same recognised standard; the full mechanics of these routes are covered in the site's dedicated guide to the locally employed doctors' route to GMC registration.

Does every doctor eventually need to be on the Specialist Register or GP Register?

No. Many doctors remain on full registration for their entire careers in staff-grade, specialty doctor, or non-partner GP roles without ever entering the Specialist Register or GP Register, and this is an entirely valid, permanent registration status. Specialist or GP Register entry only becomes a practical necessity for a doctor who specifically wants to take up a substantive consultant post or become a GP partner or principal.

Conclusion

To summarise the different types of GMC registration covered in this guide: provisional, full, Specialist Register, and GP Register describe a genuine progression rather than four interchangeable labels, and understanding which one a doctor holds, alongside whether a licence to practise currently accompanies it, resolves most of the confusion that surrounds GMC terminology for UK graduates and IMGs alike. Provisional registration is a short, F1-specific stage; full registration is the durable status nearly every working doctor holds; and Specialist or GP Register entry is an additional, specialty-specific recognition that most doctors only need if their career is heading toward a substantive consultant or GP partner post.
