Sepsis Recognition and Management in the UKMLA: The Essential Guide

Sepsis UKMLA revision is one of the highest-yield investments a candidate can make, because sepsis sits at the intersection of almost every clinical domain the exam tests — acute medicine, paediatrics, obstetrics, primary care, and professional decision-making all converge on a single question: has this patient recognised, and are they being treated fast enough? Sepsis kills an estimated 48,000 people in the UK every year, and a meaningful proportion of those deaths are judged, in retrospect, to have involved a recognisable delay in escalation. The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) reflects that reality directly, testing not just the definition of sepsis but a candidate's ability to spot it in atypical presentations, apply the NEWS2 score correctly, and initiate the Sepsis Six bundle within the correct timeframe.

This guide is a dedicated, exam-focused deep dive into sepsis specifically — distinct from the broader infection and antimicrobial content covered in our infection and microbiology revision guide, which surveys pneumonia, UTIs, tuberculosis, HIV and STIs alongside a brief sepsis section. Here, sepsis gets the depth it deserves on its own: population-specific recognition in adults, the frail elderly, children, and pregnant women; the mechanics and pitfalls of NEWS2 scoring; the exact composition and timing of the Sepsis Six; and the specific exam traps that catch out otherwise well-prepared candidates. It is written for both UK medical students sitting the UKMLA as part of their final assessments and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the Applied Knowledge Test as a stand-alone qualifying exam.

Sepsis has also become more prominent within the GMC's own assessment blueprint. The GMC's updated Medical Licensing Assessment (MLA) content map — which applies to all exams and assessments sat from September 2026 onwards — explicitly lists updated sepsis guidance among the new and expanded areas of content, alongside genetics and genomics, transgender health, and social determinants of health. Sepsis was previously under-mapped to contexts such as child health and primary care, meaning candidates were not always assessed on recognising it outside acute and emergency settings. The updated map corrects this gap deliberately, reflecting real-world clinical practice in which sepsis can present anywhere, not only in the emergency department. For a full breakdown of everything that changed in the update, see our dedicated piece on the 2026 content map revision.

Quick Answer: What Do You Need to Know About Sepsis for the UKMLA?

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, and the UKMLA expects you to recognise it early using a validated scoring system, understand that presentations vary markedly by population, and know exactly what the Sepsis Six bundle involves and when each element must be completed. In UK hospitals and ambulance services, NEWS2 is the standard tool for detecting physiological deterioration and escalating suspected sepsis in patients aged 16 and over who are not currently or recently pregnant. Once sepsis is suspected, the Sepsis Six — three tests and three treatments — should be completed within one hour of recognition: take blood cultures, measure serum lactate, and monitor urine output; give oxygen, give intravenous fluids, and give intravenous antibiotics.

The exam consistently tests the gap between textbook sepsis and real sepsis. A normal or even low temperature does not exclude sepsis — hypothermia can be a marker of severe sepsis, particularly in older or immunocompromised patients. Tachycardia in a young, anxious-looking patient is frequently the correct answer precisely because it looks like it could be dismissed as anxiety; the UKMLA rewards candidates who investigate rather than reassure. NICE's suspected sepsis guideline (NG51) and the UK Sepsis Trust's Sepsis Six framework remain the two authoritative sources underpinning almost every sepsis question you will see, and both are referenced throughout this guide. Together they form the backbone of sound sepsis UKMLA revision, since AKT questions are written to be consistent with exactly this guidance rather than any looser or more generic international standard.

What Is Sepsis? Definition and Pathophysiology

Sepsis is formally defined, under the international Sepsis-3 consensus definitions, as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. This definition deliberately moves away from older concepts based purely on the systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) criteria, because SIRS criteria (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, and white cell count derangements) proved too non-specific — many patients with SIRS never develop organ dysfunction, while some patients with sepsis and organ dysfunction do not meet SIRS thresholds at all. The UKMLA follows this same shift in thinking: rather than testing whether a patient meets a checklist of inflammatory markers, questions test whether you recognise organ dysfunction developing in the context of infection.

The underlying pathophysiology is a cascade in which the immune system's response to infection becomes harmful in itself. Pro-inflammatory mediators (cytokines such as TNF-alpha, interleukin-6, and interleukin-1) are released in excess, causing widespread vasodilation, increased capillary permeability, and activation of the coagulation cascade. The net effect resembles distributive shock: blood pressure falls because vascular tone is lost, tissue perfusion falls despite normal or even elevated cardiac output, and oxygen delivery to tissues becomes inadequate at a cellular level even before global haemodynamic collapse is obvious. This is why serum lactate — a marker of anaerobic metabolism and tissue hypoperfusion — is such a valuable early warning sign, often rising before blood pressure falls.

Septic shock is a more severe subset, defined as sepsis with persisting hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain a mean arterial pressure of 65 mmHg or above, and a serum lactate greater than 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Septic shock carries a hospital mortality rate above 40%, considerably higher than sepsis without shock, and its presence should trigger immediate escalation to critical care wherever this is available.

Why Early Recognition Matters So Much

Mortality from sepsis rises steeply with every hour that appropriate antibiotics are delayed once septic shock is established, even though more recent evidence has refined exactly how aggressively that "golden hour" framing should be applied to every patient. This is precisely why the UK Sepsis Trust and NICE both frame the Sepsis Six as a one-hour bundle rather than a same-shift or same-day target: the interventions are simple, cheap, and low-risk, and the cost of delay is measured in lives. For the UKMLA, the practical implication is that "sepsis" as an answer option should trigger urgency in your reasoning — if a question stem is even mildly suggestive of sepsis, the correct answer is very rarely "wait and reassess in four hours."

Recognising Sepsis: Red Flags and Population-Specific Presentations

Sepsis recognition is not a single checklist — the same underlying physiological derangement looks different depending on age, baseline health, and pregnancy status, and the UKMLA deliberately tests this variation. This is arguably the single most important section for anyone revising sepsis UKMLA content, since population-specific recognition (rather than a generic adult-only checklist) is what separates a secure pass from a near-miss on this topic. NICE's NG51 guideline (and its successor guidelines) set out specific "red flag" criteria for high-risk sepsis in different populations, and candidates are expected to apply the correct set for the patient in front of them rather than a single generic list.

Red-Flag Features in Adults

NICE identifies a set of high-risk criteria that should prompt immediate senior review and treatment as if the patient has sepsis until proven otherwise. These include: objective evidence of new altered mental state; respiratory rate of 25 breaths per minute or more, or a new need for 40% oxygen or more to maintain saturations; heart rate above 130 beats per minute; systolic blood pressure of 90 mmHg or less (or a drop of more than 40 mmHg from normal); not passed urine in the previous 18 hours, or for catheterised patients, passing less than 0.5 ml/kg of urine per hour; mottled or ashen appearance; cyanosis of skin, lips, or tongue; a non-blanching rash of the skin; and recent chemotherapy within the last six weeks. Any one of these features, in the presence of suspected infection, should be treated as a medical emergency, with the Sepsis Six started immediately.

A second, "moderate to high risk" tier includes features such as relative hypotension, tachycardia (heart rate 91–130, or 100–130 in specific contexts), respiratory rate 21–24, impaired immune function (including diabetes, recent trauma or surgery, or long-term steroid use), and history suggestive of new infection. These patients require a face-to-face clinical assessment by a competent and experienced clinician within one hour, and a structured decision on whether to treat as sepsis.

Recognising Sepsis in the Frail Elderly

Older, frail patients frequently do not present with the "classic" picture of fever, tachycardia, and an obvious source. Instead, sepsis in this group commonly presents with non-specific deterioration: acute confusion or worsening of baseline cognitive impairment, reduced mobility or a fall, reduced oral intake, generalised weakness, or simply "not being themselves" according to a carer or family member. Fever is frequently blunted or entirely absent in frail elderly patients because of an impaired febrile response, and a normal or even low temperature must never be used to rule out sepsis in this group. Baseline heart rate and blood pressure are also often altered by comorbidity and polypharmacy (beta-blockers blunting the tachycardic response, for example), which can mask warning signs that would be obvious in a younger patient. This is precisely why NEWS2 is scored against absolute physiological thresholds rather than a patient's own individual "normal" — clinical judgement and any noted change from baseline must always be layered on top of the numerical score.

The UKMLA frequently tests this population by presenting an elderly care-home resident with vague symptoms — new confusion, reduced appetite, a fall — without a dramatic fever or rigors, and expects candidates to recognise sepsis as a strong differential rather than defaulting to a diagnosis of "urinary tract infection, treat with oral antibiotics and reassess," particularly if any red-flag features are present.

Recognising Sepsis in Children

Paediatric sepsis recognition uses age-banded physiological thresholds, because normal heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure vary substantially across infancy and childhood. NICE's paediatric sepsis guidance (covering children under 16 and reflected in resources such as the NHS "Spotting the Sick Child" framework and Sepsis Trust paediatric screening tools) identifies red-flag features including: abnormal behaviour (does not wake, or if woken does not stay awake; weak, high-pitched, or continuous cry); grunting, marked chest indrawing, or a respiratory rate more than 60 breaths per minute; reduced skin turgor; a non-blanching rash; bulging fontanelle in an infant; neck stiffness; status epilepticus or a new seizure; and cyanosis of skin, lips, or tongue.

A key exam trap specific to children is that early sepsis can look remarkably like a common, self-limiting viral illness — a feverish, slightly lethargic toddler is an everyday presentation, and the skill being tested is distinguishing the rare but dangerous case from the common benign one. Features that should heighten suspicion of serious bacterial infection or sepsis in a child include: temperature over 38°C in an infant under 3 months (which mandates full septic screen regardless of how well the infant otherwise looks); persistent tachycardia or tachypnoea beyond what fever alone would explain; reduced urine output reported by parents (fewer wet nappies); non-blanching rash (a classic red flag for meningococcal sepsis specifically); and parental concern that "this is different" from previous illnesses — a soft but genuinely useful clinical sign that the UKMLA expects candidates to take seriously rather than dismiss as unquantifiable.

Recognising Sepsis in Pregnant and Recently Pregnant Women

Pregnancy and the postpartum period produce a distinct set of recognition challenges, and NICE issues separate guidance (NG255) specifically for suspected sepsis in pregnant or recently pregnant people, precisely because the standard NEWS2 tool is not validated for this group. Normal physiological changes of pregnancy — a resting heart rate 10–15 beats per minute higher than non-pregnant baseline, a lower blood pressure in the second trimester, and a mild physiological leucocytosis — can either mask early sepsis or, conversely, cause over-triggering of adult scoring systems not designed for pregnancy. A Modified Early Obstetric Warning Score (MEOWS) or equivalent maternity-specific track-and-trigger tool should be used instead of standard NEWS2 in this population.

Specific red flags in maternal sepsis include: fever with rigors; abdominal or pelvic pain, particularly in the postpartum period (raising concern for endometritis or retained products of conception); offensive vaginal discharge or lochia; diarrhoea or vomiting that could indicate an intra-abdominal source; reduced fetal movements (a fetal, not maternal, marker of compromise that must be taken seriously); and any breach of the standard obstetric early-warning parameters. Common causative organisms in maternal sepsis include Escherichia coli (chorioamnionitis, urinary source), Group A Streptococcus (a particularly aggressive and rapidly progressive cause of maternal sepsis and one of the leading causes of direct maternal death in the UK), and Group B Streptococcus. The UKMLA expects candidates to identify maternal sepsis as a time-critical obstetric emergency requiring the same urgency as the Sepsis Six in any other population, adapted to use pregnancy-appropriate antibiotics and involving obstetric and, where relevant, anaesthetic and critical care teams early.

NEWS2: The National Early Warning Score 2

NEWS2 (National Early Warning Score 2) is the standardised physiological track-and-trigger scoring system used across NHS acute hospitals and ambulance services to detect clinical deterioration, including deterioration caused by sepsis, in adult patients. NICE recommends that NEWS2 be used to assess adults aged 16 and over with suspected sepsis, provided they are not currently or recently pregnant, in acute hospital and ambulance settings.

How NEWS2 Is Calculated

NEWS2 assigns points across six physiological parameters, plus an additional two points if the patient requires any supplemental oxygen to maintain their target saturation. The parameters scored are: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation (using either Scale 1 for most patients or Scale 2 for patients with hypercapnic respiratory failure, such as some patients with severe COPD, where a lower target saturation range is deliberately accepted), whether the patient is on air or supplemental oxygen, systolic blood pressure, pulse rate, level of consciousness or new confusion, and temperature. Each parameter is scored from 0 to 3 points depending on how far it deviates from a normal physiological range, and the points are summed to give an aggregate score.

A crucial nuance the UKMLA tests directly: a single parameter scoring the maximum of 3 points triggers an urgent clinical review even if the aggregate score is otherwise low, because an isolated extreme derangement (for example, a respiratory rate of 25 or above scoring 3 points on its own) can be clinically significant even when averaged out by normal values elsewhere. Relying solely on the total aggregate score, and ignoring an individual red-flag parameter, is a recognised and specifically tested error.

NEWS2's Role in Escalating Suspected Sepsis

NEWS2 does not diagnose sepsis on its own — it is a general deterioration score used across all acutely unwell patients, not a sepsis-specific tool. Its role in the sepsis pathway is to flag physiological deterioration promptly and trigger an appropriately urgent clinical assessment, at which point the clinician then applies specific sepsis screening (looking for a source of infection alongside the physiological derangement, and checking against the NICE red-flag criteria described above) to decide whether sepsis is the likely cause and whether the Sepsis Six should begin. In practice, a high NEWS2 score in a patient with a plausible source of infection should prompt the clinician to actively consider and screen for sepsis, not simply to "monitor more closely."

The exam frequently uses NEWS2 as the vehicle for testing prioritisation: given several deteriorating patients or a single evolving vignette, candidates must correctly interpret rising NEWS2 scores and identify the point at which escalation — calling a senior, activating a sepsis pathway, or initiating an early warning "sepsis six" trolley or box — becomes mandatory rather than optional. NEWS2 is not validated for use in children (paediatric early warning scores, such as PEWS, are used instead) or in pregnant and recently pregnant women (where MEOWS or an equivalent obstetric tool is used instead), and the UKMLA does test awareness of these population-specific exceptions.

The Sepsis Six: Composition and Timing

The Sepsis Six is a bundle of six time-critical interventions developed and championed by the UK Sepsis Trust, designed to be simple enough that any competent healthcare professional can begin them immediately, without waiting for senior review or a definitive diagnosis. It is conventionally taught as "three tests, three treatments," and NICE guidance reinforces the same one-hour timing principle for high-risk patients.

Take Three: Investigations

The three investigations must be obtained promptly, ideally before or alongside — but never at the expense of delaying — the three treatments. Blood cultures should be taken before antibiotics wherever this is feasible, using aseptic technique, typically two sets from peripheral sites, with additional cultures from any indwelling lines if line infection is suspected; however, blood cultures must never be allowed to delay antibiotic administration beyond the target window if venous access or culture collection is proving difficult. Serum lactate should be measured (venous lactate is acceptable and widely used; arterial sampling is not required routinely) because it is one of the most sensitive available markers of tissue hypoperfusion and helps identify patients who are more unwell than their blood pressure alone suggests, as well as providing a baseline against which the response to treatment can be judged with a repeat lactate. Monitor urine output closely — this may mean starting strict fluid balance charting, catheterising the patient where indicated, and treating reduced urine output as an early marker of renal hypoperfusion and a target for ongoing fluid resuscitation.

Give Three: Treatments

Give oxygen to correct hypoxia, generally targeting a saturation of 94% or above in patients not at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure, and titrating to a lower target range (typically 88–92%) in those who are. Give intravenous fluids: a crystalloid bolus (commonly 500 ml of 0.9% sodium chloride or a balanced crystalloid such as Hartmann's solution, given over 15 minutes) is used for patients with hypotension or other evidence of hypoperfusion, with the patient reassessed after each bolus rather than fluids simply being infused on autopilot — NICE's updated guidance places specific emphasis on reassessment between boluses to avoid fluid overload, particularly in patients with cardiac or renal impairment. Give intravenous antibiotics: broad-spectrum antibiotics, chosen according to local hospital policy and the likely source of infection, should be administered as soon as possible, and NICE and the UK Sepsis Trust both frame this as a within-one-hour target for patients meeting high-risk criteria.

The overarching principle the UKMLA rewards is that all six elements should be completed within one hour of sepsis being recognised or strongly suspected, not one hour from arrival, not one hour from a senior reviewing the patient, and not sequentially over the course of a shift. In practice this means several elements happen simultaneously — a nurse can be obtaining blood cultures while a doctor prescribes antibiotics and fluids are being drawn up — rather than being treated as a strict checklist to work through one item at a time. Antibiotics should not be delayed to wait for a lactate result or a completed septic screen if the patient meets high-risk criteria; the correct sequence in a genuine emergency is to treat first and complete the full diagnostic picture in parallel.

After the First Hour: Reassessment and De-escalation

The Sepsis Six is the beginning of management, not the end. Patients should be reassessed after initial treatment — repeating lactate, reviewing the response to fluid boluses, and involving critical care early if there is no improvement or if septic shock is present. Antibiotics chosen empirically in the first hour should be reviewed at 24–48 hours in light of culture results and clinical response, and de-escalated or narrowed wherever possible, consistent with antimicrobial stewardship principles ("start smart, then focus"). Identifying and controlling the source of infection — draining an abscess, removing an infected line, or relieving an obstructed urinary tract, for example — remains essential and should not be forgotten once the initial bundle is complete.

Common UKMLA Exam Traps in Sepsis Questions

Certain recurring patterns catch out candidates who know the guidelines in the abstract but have not trained themselves to apply that knowledge under exam pressure. Recognising these traps in advance is one of the most efficient ways to convert borderline marks into secure ones, and it is where most focused sepsis UKMLA question practice pays off fastest.

"Normal Temperature" Does Not Exclude Sepsis

Perhaps the single most exploited exam trap is the assumption that sepsis requires a fever. Many question stems deliberately present a patient with a normal, or even low, temperature alongside other signs of deterioration — tachycardia, tachypnoea, confusion, hypotension — precisely to test whether candidates fall back on the outdated assumption that "no fever means no infection." Hypothermia is itself a recognised feature of severe sepsis, particularly in the very young, the frail elderly, and the immunosuppressed, and can indicate a more severe physiological insult than a modest fever would. The correct exam approach is to weight the overall clinical and physiological picture, not any single parameter, when sepsis is a plausible differential.

Tachycardia Dismissed as "Anxiety"

A second recurring trap presents a young patient — often with vague symptoms and a mildly raised heart rate — in a context (a busy ward, an anxious-sounding history, a recent stressful event) that tempts candidates toward "anxiety" or "pain" as an explanation for tachycardia, without adequately excluding sepsis as a cause. While anxiety and pain are genuine and common causes of tachycardia, and the differential should always be considered proportionately, the UKMLA specifically rewards candidates who look for a source of infection and other red-flag features before accepting a benign explanation, rather than anchoring on the first plausible cause. If a stem includes any suggestion of infection (a recent procedure, a urinary catheter, a wound, recent travel, immunosuppression) alongside tachycardia, sepsis should be actively screened for, not assumed away.

Relying on the Aggregate NEWS2 Score Alone

As covered above, a single parameter scoring 3 points mandates urgent review regardless of the total score — a nuance the exam tests directly by presenting scenarios where the total NEWS2 score looks reassuringly low but one parameter (commonly respiratory rate or new confusion) is markedly abnormal. Candidates who only sum the total and miss the individual trigger will select the wrong (typically too passive) management option.

Waiting for Lactate or Cultures Before Starting Treatment

Another common trap presents a scenario in which venous access is difficult, or a lactate result is delayed, and offers an answer option of "wait for the lactate result before giving antibiotics." This is incorrect: the "give three" elements of the Sepsis Six, particularly antibiotics, should not be delayed pending investigation results in a high-risk patient. The correct answer is almost always to proceed with empirical treatment immediately while investigations are obtained in parallel.

Treating Sepsis as an Emergency-Department-Only Problem

Because sepsis has historically been most strongly associated with emergency medicine and intensive care in teaching, some candidates default to assuming sepsis questions will only be set in those contexts. The updated GMC content map specifically corrects this assumption by extending sepsis mapping into primary care and child health contexts, and the UKMLA increasingly presents sepsis scenarios in general practice, postnatal wards, paediatric assessment units, and community settings. Candidates should be equally alert to sepsis red flags regardless of the clinical setting described in the stem.

Assuming One Screening Tool Fits All Populations

NEWS2 is validated for non-pregnant adults aged 16 and over; it is not the correct tool for children (who require age-appropriate paediatric early warning scores) or for pregnant and recently pregnant women (who require an obstetric-specific tool such as MEOWS). A stem describing a pregnant woman or a child, followed by an answer option suggesting a standard NEWS2 score should be calculated and acted upon in isolation, is testing exactly this population-specific knowledge gap.

Sources of Infection and Empirical Treatment Principles

Specific local antibiotic policies vary between trusts, and the UKMLA does not expect memorisation of every trust's precise empirical regimen. Candidates should instead understand that empirical antibiotic choice in sepsis is guided by the most likely source of infection, patient allergy status, and recent microbiology results, providing broad-spectrum cover until a source and organism are identified. Common sources tested include: urinary tract infection progressing to urosepsis or pyelonephritis with bacteraemia; pneumonia; intra-abdominal infection such as cholangitis, diverticulitis, or a perforated viscus; skin and soft tissue infection, including cellulitis and the surgical emergency of necrotising fasciitis; line-associated bloodstream infection in patients with central venous access; and, in the maternal context, chorioamnionitis or postpartum endometritis.

Identifying and controlling the source is a core management principle that sits alongside the Sepsis Six rather than replacing it — antibiotics alone will not resolve sepsis caused by an undrained abscess or an obstructed, infected urinary tract, and source control (drainage, removal of an infected device, or relief of obstruction) should be pursued as soon as it is safely possible once initial resuscitation is under way.

Sepsis Recognition for UK Medical Students

For UK medical students, sepsis recognition is typically taught and assessed through direct clinical exposure — observing NEWS2 charting on the wards, seeing sepsis pathways activated in real time, and encountering deteriorating-patient simulation scenarios during clinical placements. The UKMLA translates this practical exposure into structured written and simulated scenarios: an Applied Knowledge Test question stem describing vital signs and asking for the NEWS2 score or the next management step, or a Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) station simulating a deteriorating ward patient where a candidate must demonstrate the ABCDE approach, recognise the need to escalate, and communicate concerns clearly to a senior colleague using a structured tool such as SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation).

UK medical students should ensure they are equally comfortable applying sepsis recognition principles across all four population groups discussed in this guide — not just the adult ward patient most commonly encountered on medical and surgical placements, but also the paediatric and obstetric presentations that may receive comparatively less clinical exposure time during undergraduate training, yet are tested with equal weight in the UKMLA.

Sepsis Recognition for IMGs Preparing for the AKT

For international medical graduates, sepsis pathways and specific scoring tools such as NEWS2 may differ from systems used in a candidate's home country, even where the underlying clinical principles of early recognition and prompt treatment are universal. IMGs preparing for the AKT should familiarise themselves with the exact NEWS2 scoring bands and thresholds, the UK-specific terminology used in NICE guidance and Sepsis Six materials, and the structure of NHS escalation pathways, since AKT questions are written against this specific national framework rather than international equivalents such as the quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (qSOFA) score used more prominently elsewhere.

qSOFA, while a globally recognised sepsis screening tool, is not the primary tool referenced in current NICE guidance for UK acute hospital and ambulance settings — NEWS2 holds that position. IMGs who trained using qSOFA should make a deliberate effort to reframe their sepsis knowledge around NEWS2's specific parameters and thresholds before sitting the AKT, since a technically correct qSOFA-based answer may not align with the NEWS2-based marking scheme the exam uses.

How Sepsis Connects to Wider UKMLA Content

Sepsis recognition rarely appears in complete isolation from the rest of the UKMLA syllabus — it intersects with several other high-yield content areas worth revising alongside it. The GMC's 2026 content map update, discussed at the start of this guide, bundled updated sepsis guidance together with several other newly expanded topics, and candidates preparing broadly for the AKT should treat these as a linked revision cluster rather than isolated single-topic questions. Genetics and genomics content has been similarly expanded, reflecting the growing role of genomic medicine in mainstream practice, and transgender health is another newly formalised area within the same content map revision. A further practical connection worth revising alongside sepsis is toxicology and poisoning, since drug overdose and poisoning can both mimic and precipitate sepsis-like physiological instability, and distinguishing between the two is a recurring theme in acute deterioration scenarios.

Building Sepsis Into Your Wider Revision Strategy

Sepsis revision is most effective when treated as three linked layers rather than one single topic to memorise. The first layer is recognition: internalising the red-flag criteria for each population group covered in this guide, so that a question stem's specific physiological details trigger the correct level of suspicion automatically, rather than requiring conscious recall of a checklist under time pressure. The second layer is scoring and escalation: being completely fluent in NEWS2's parameters, thresholds, and response bands, and knowing precisely when a different tool (paediatric early warning scores, or an obstetric equivalent such as MEOWS) should be substituted instead. The third layer is management: knowing the Sepsis Six by heart, in both its "take three, give three" structure and its one-hour timing requirement, and understanding that these interventions run in parallel rather than in strict sequence.

Practising timed AKT-style questions that combine all three layers — a vital signs table to interpret, a scoring decision to make, and a management step to select — is the most realistic way to prepare, since this is exactly how the exam presents sepsis content. Candidates should also actively practise applying this knowledge across all four population contexts, since it is common to over-prepare for the adult ward scenario while under-preparing for the paediatric, obstetric, and frail elderly variants that the updated content map now weights more heavily. Frequently Asked Questions About Sepsis in the UKMLA

What is the definition of sepsis used in current UK guidance?

Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, following the international Sepsis-3 consensus definitions. This moves away from older definitions based purely on systemic inflammatory response criteria, focusing instead on evidence of organ dysfunction developing in the context of infection. Septic shock is a more severe subset requiring vasopressors to maintain adequate blood pressure alongside a persistently raised lactate despite fluid resuscitation.

What score is used to identify deteriorating adult patients with suspected sepsis in UK hospitals?

NEWS2 (National Early Warning Score 2) is the standard tool recommended by NICE for detecting deterioration in adults aged 16 and over with suspected sepsis, provided they are not currently or recently pregnant, in acute hospital and ambulance settings. It scores six physiological parameters plus supplemental oxygen use, with an aggregate score guiding the urgency of clinical review, though any single parameter scoring the maximum 3 points also independently triggers an urgent review regardless of the total.

What are the six elements of the Sepsis Six bundle and when should they be completed?

The Sepsis Six comprises three investigations (take blood cultures, measure serum lactate, and monitor urine output) and three treatments (give oxygen, give intravenous fluids, and give intravenous antibiotics). All six elements should ideally be completed within one hour of sepsis being recognised or strongly suspected, with several elements carried out simultaneously rather than working through them one at a time, and treatment should not be delayed to wait for investigation results in a high-risk patient.

Can a patient have sepsis with a normal temperature?

Yes. A normal or even low body temperature does not exclude sepsis, and hypothermia can itself be a marker of severe sepsis, particularly in frail elderly, very young, and immunosuppressed patients. Sepsis should be considered on the basis of the overall clinical and physiological picture — including heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, mental state, and evidence of a source of infection — rather than the presence or absence of fever alone.

Why is NEWS2 not used for pregnant women or children?

NEWS2 was developed and validated for non-pregnant adults, and the significant physiological changes of pregnancy (including a higher baseline heart rate and altered blood pressure) mean it is not an accurate deterioration tool for pregnant or recently pregnant women, who should instead be assessed using a maternity-specific early warning tool such as MEOWS. Children have different age-banded normal physiological ranges throughout infancy and childhood, so paediatric-specific early warning scores are used instead of NEWS2 in this population.

Does the updated GMC content map really include sepsis as a new topic?

The GMC's updated MLA content map, which applies to exams and assessments from September 2026 onwards, explicitly lists updated sepsis guidance among its newly integrated and expanded content areas, alongside genetics and genomics, transgender health, and social determinants of health. Sepsis had previously been under-mapped to contexts such as primary care and child health, and the update corrects this so that sepsis can now be tested in any clinical context, not only acute or emergency settings.

What is the difference between sepsis and septic shock?

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Septic shock is a more severe subset of sepsis in which the circulatory and cellular metabolic abnormalities are profound enough to substantially increase mortality — defined as sepsis with a persisting requirement for vasopressors to maintain a mean arterial pressure of 65 mmHg or above, together with a serum lactate greater than 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Septic shock carries a hospital mortality rate exceeding 40% and requires immediate escalation to critical care wherever available.

Conclusion

Sepsis recognition and management sits among the most consistently tested, and most consequential, topics across the entire UKMLA syllabus. Mastering sepsis UKMLA content pays off precisely because it rewards the same skills the exam is ultimately designed to certify: recognising deterioration early, applying a validated tool correctly, and acting without unnecessary delay. Whether the scenario in front of you involves a frail care-home resident with vague confusion, a feverish infant, a postpartum woman with abdominal pain, or a tachycardic young adult whose symptoms look deceptively like anxiety, the same underlying discipline applies — screen actively for sepsis, apply the correct population-specific tool, and initiate the Sepsis Six within the hour once it is suspected. Build this topic into your revision as a connected cluster alongside NEWS2, the wider infection syllabus, and the GMC's 2026 content map changes, rather than as an isolated fact set, and you will be well placed to handle it in whatever form the exam presents it.
