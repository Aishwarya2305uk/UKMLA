Obstetrics gynaecology UKMLA questions span acute emergencies requiring immediate action, long-term gynaecological conditions, and the application of guideline-based contraceptive and screening pathways. This system is consistently well-represented in the AKT and CPSA, covering obstetric emergencies such as postpartum haemorrhage and eclampsia, gynaecological cancers with 2WW referral thresholds, and the updated UKMEC 2025 criteria for contraception. This guide consolidates the high-yield obstetrics gynaecology UKMLA content you need, with tables, clinical algorithms, and FAQs based on NICE, RCOG, and FSRH guidelines.

Pre-eclampsia, Eclampsia, and HELLP Syndrome

Pre-eclampsia is defined as new-onset hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions >4 hours apart) after 20 weeks of pregnancy with significant proteinuria (≥0.3 g/24h, PCR ≥30 mg/mmol, or 2+ on dipstick confirmed) or end-organ dysfunction. Risk factors: nulliparity, previous pre-eclampsia, obesity (BMI >35), multiple pregnancy, CKD, diabetes, hypertension, antiphospholipid syndrome, age >40, family history. Aspirin 150 mg nightly from 12 weeks in high-risk patients (NICE definition: ≥1 high-risk factor or ≥2 moderate-risk factors) reduces risk by ~15%.

Management: antihypertensives (labetalol first-line, then nifedipine MR, then methyldopa if both fail; avoid ACE inhibitors and ARBs in pregnancy); corticosteroids (betamethasone) if <34 weeks for fetal lung maturity; magnesium sulphate 4 g IV loading dose then 1 g/hour infusion for eclampsia prophylaxis (in severe pre-eclampsia) and treatment. Monitor urine output, reflexes (loss of patellar reflex = early toxicity), and respiratory rate on magnesium. Calcium gluconate 10 mL of 10% IV is the antidote for magnesium toxicity. Definitive treatment: delivery. Deliver at 37 weeks if mild-moderate pre-eclampsia; immediate delivery (regardless of gestation) if severe features (BP >160/110, haemolysis, thrombocytopaenia, hepatic involvement, eclampsia, pulmonary oedema).

Eclampsia: tonic-clonic seizure in a woman with pre-eclampsia. Management: left lateral position, ABC, magnesium sulphate 4 g IV over 5 minutes (repeat 2 g if seizure recurs), airway management, deliver after stabilisation. HELLP syndrome (Haemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets): variant of severe pre-eclampsia; right upper quadrant/epigastric pain, nausea, malaise; LDH elevated, liver enzymes elevated, platelets <100 Ã— 10â¹/L. Risk of DIC, liver rupture. Management: immediate delivery, blood transfusion, platelet transfusion if <20 or bleeding.

Gestational Diabetes and Antenatal Screening

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is glucose intolerance first recognised in pregnancy. Risk factors: BMI >30, previous GDM, previous macrosomic baby (>4.5 kg), family history T2DM, South Asian/Black/Middle Eastern ethnicity. Screening: OGTT (75 g at 24–28 weeks) — diagnostic if fasting glucose ≥5.6 mmol/L or 2-hour glucose ≥7.8 mmol/L (NICE NG3). Management: dietary advice, blood glucose monitoring; metformin if targets not met; insulin if metformin insufficient or contraindicated. Targets: fasting <5.3 mmol/L, 1-hour post-meal <7.8 mmol/L, 2-hour post-meal <6.4 mmol/L. Complications: macrosomia, shoulder dystocia, neonatal hypoglycaemia, stillbirth, NICU admission. Monitor with growth scans from 28–32 weeks.

Antenatal screening programme (UK): first trimester combined screening (11+0 to 13+6 weeks) — nuchal translucency USS + maternal age + PAPP-A + free beta-hCG gives trisomy 21/18/13 risk. High risk (≥1:150): offer NIPT or diagnostic testing (CVS/amniocentesis). Anomaly scan at 18+0 to 20+6 weeks: structural survey including cardiac, spine, brain, abdominal wall, limbs. Hb and blood group at booking; repeat at 28 weeks. Group B Streptococcus (GBS): not routinely screened in UK (unlike USA); treat intrapartum if risk factors (previous GBS baby, preterm, prolonged rupture of membranes, maternal fever).

Emergency Contraception

Levonorgestrel EC (Levonelle 1500 micrograms): effective within 72 hours of unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI); effectiveness decreases with time. Ulipristal acetate (ellaOne 30 mg): effective within 120 hours of UPSI; more effective than LNG-EC in hours 72–120 and in heavier women. Copper IUD: most effective EC (failure rate <0.1%); insert within 120 hours of UPSI or within 5 days of calculated ovulation; also provides ongoing contraception. After LNG-EC: advise patient to use barrier method until next period. After ulipristal: avoid any hormonal contraception for 5 days (reduces effectiveness). EC does not cause abortion — it prevents implantation or ovulation.

Cervical Screening, STIs, and Gynaecological Cancers

Cervical Screening Programme

England moved to HPV primary testing in 2019. Women aged 25–49: HPV test every 3 years. Women aged 50–64: every 5 years. Result: HPV not detected — return to routine screening. HPV detected but negative cytology — repeat HPV in 12 months. HPV detected and abnormal cytology — colposcopy referral. High-grade cytology (HSIL) regardless of HPV: colposcopy. HPV is the cause of virtually all cervical cancers; types 16 and 18 are high-risk. HPV vaccination: Gardasil 9 offered to boys and girls aged 12–13 (school year 8) — protects against HPV 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58.

Gynaecological Cancer Referral and Presentations

Ovarian cancer: insidious onset; bloating, abdominal distension, urinary frequency, early satiety. NICE: CA-125 first-line test if symptoms (bloating, pelvic/abdominal pain, early satiety, urinary frequency) ≥12 times/month. If CA-125 ≥35 IU/mL: USS pelvis. If USS abnormal: 2WW referral. Endometrial/uterine cancer: postmenopausal bleeding (PMB) is the most common presentation (90% of uterine cancers present with PMB). Urgent 2WW: postmenopausal bleeding; unexplained vaginal discharge in post-menopausal women. USS to assess endometrial thickness; if >4 mm post-menopause or suspicious — hysteroscopy and biopsy. Risk factors: obesity, unopposed oestrogen (HRT without progestogen), tamoxifen, nulliparity, PCOS, Lynch syndrome. Treatment: total abdominal hysterectomy + bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (TAH + BSO); radiotherapy for advanced disease.

Menopause, PID, and Endometriosis

Menopause and HRT

Menopause is defined as 12 months of amenorrhoea in a woman over 45. Perimenopause: irregular cycles, vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats), mood changes, sleep disturbance. Diagnosis is clinical if ≥45; FSH not routinely needed (levels fluctuate). FSH >30 IU/L on two occasions 4–6 weeks apart confirms menopause in women under 45 (premature ovarian insufficiency). HRT: systemic oestrogen (with progestogen if intact uterus) is most effective for vasomotor symptoms. Benefits: reduces fracture risk, may reduce cardiovascular risk if started within 10 years of menopause/age <60. Risks: combined HRT slightly increases breast cancer risk (similar to 1–2 years of delay in menopause); VTE risk increased with oral oestrogen (transdermal avoids first-pass, lower VTE risk). Contraindications: current/recent breast cancer, undiagnosed vaginal bleeding, active liver disease, known thrombophilia (use transdermal with caution). NICE (NG23): HRT should be offered to women with menopausal symptoms after discussing risks and benefits; do not routinely limit duration.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease and Endometriosis

PID: infection of upper genital tract (uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries). Usually ascending from cervicovaginal flora; often polymicrobial (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, anaerobes). Symptoms: lower abdominal/pelvic pain, dyspareunia, abnormal vaginal discharge, fever, cervical motion tenderness (CMT — excitation). Management: empirical antibiotics — outpatient: ofloxacin 400 mg BD + metronidazole 400 mg BD for 14 days; inpatient (severe): IV cefoxitin or ceftriaxone + doxycycline + metronidazole. Complications: ectopic pregnancy risk (Ã—6), infertility, chronic pelvic pain, tubo-ovarian abscess (USS-guided drainage or laparoscopy). Endometriosis: endometrial tissue outside the uterus (most commonly on ovaries, peritoneum, POD). Symptoms: cyclical pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, deep dyspareunia, dyschezia, subfertility. Diagnosis: laparoscopy (gold standard); USS can identify endometriomas (chocolate cysts). Medical management: COCP (continuous), Mirena IUS, GnRH analogues (with add-back HRT), dienogest. Surgical: laparoscopic ablation/excision of deposits.

Anti-D Rh prophylaxis: all Rh-negative women with threatened miscarriage at or after 12 weeks (or with heavy bleeding/pain before 12 weeks), ectopic pregnancy, termination, or invasive procedures should receive anti-D 250 IU IM to prevent Rh sensitisation. Ectopic pregnancy: implantation outside the uterine cavity (most commonly in fallopian tube — ampullary portion 70%). Risk factors: previous ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, IVF, IUD (not cause but raises relative risk of ectopic if contraceptive fails), smoking. Presentation: unilateral pelvic pain, amenorrhoea, vaginal bleeding; shoulder-tip pain (diaphragmatic irritation from haemoperitoneum) indicates rupture — surgical emergency. Investigations: urine hCG; serum beta-hCG (doubles every 48h in viable IUP; plateaus/falls in ectopic); TVUSS (gestational sac outside uterus, free fluid in pouch of Douglas). Management: surgical (salpingectomy or salpingotomy) for haemodynamically unstable or large ectopic; methotrexate IM for stable, small ectopic (serum hCG <5000 IU/L, no cardiac activity, patient compliant for follow-up); expectant management for serum hCG <1000 IU/L and falling.

Normal Labour, Induction, and Operative Delivery

Understanding the stages of normal labour is essential for obstetrics gynaecology UKMLA questions. First stage: onset of regular contractions to full cervical dilation (10 cm). Latent phase: from onset of labour to 4 cm dilation; active phase: 4–10 cm dilation (expected rate approximately 0.5–1 cm/hour; refer to obstetrician if progress is slow). Second stage: full dilation to delivery of the baby. Third stage: delivery of the placenta (managed actively with oxytocin 10 IU IM to reduce PPH risk; expect placental delivery within 30 minutes). The partogram (labour record) documents cervical dilation, fetal head descent, CTG features, maternal observations, and urine output — a deviation from the expected curve triggers review.

Induction of labour (IOL): indications include post-dates pregnancy (offered at 41 weeks in UK), pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, IUGR, PPROM (prelabour rupture of membranes at term), maternal request. Methods: prostaglandin E2 (dinoprostone) pessary or gel to ripen the cervix (Bishop score used to assess cervical favourability; score below 6 = unfavourable cervix requiring cervical ripening); if cervix favourable — amniotomy (ARM) Â± Syntocinon (IV oxytocin infusion). Contraindications to IOL: previous uterine scar with unfavourable cervix (relative — risk of uterine rupture with prostaglandins), placenta praevia, cord presentation, malpresentation. Caesarean section categories: category 1 (immediate threat to life — within 30 minutes); category 2 (maternal/fetal compromise, not immediately life-threatening — within 75 minutes); category 3 (elective timing needed, no immediate compromise — within 24 hours); category 4 (at time to suit patient and team).

Instrumental delivery: ventouse (vacuum cup) and forceps are used to expedite delivery in the second stage. Indications: presumed fetal compromise, maternal exhaustion, failure to progress, medical conditions where pushing is contraindicated (e.g. raised ICP, recent cardiac surgery). Ventouse: less maternal trauma but higher failure rate and risk of neonatal cephalhaematoma and retinal haemorrhages. Forceps: higher maternal perineal trauma but lower failure rate; Kielland's forceps for rotational delivery. Both require the head to be at or below the level of the ischial spines, membranes ruptured, bladder emptied, adequate analgesia, and full informed consent.

Subfertility and Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome

Subfertility is defined as failure to conceive after 12 months of regular unprotected sexual intercourse (offer investigation after 6 months if woman is over 36 or known risk factors). Causes: anovulation (35%) — PCOS, hypothalamic dysfunction, hyperprolactinaemia, premature ovarian insufficiency; tubal damage (25%) — PID, endometriosis, previous ectopic; male factor (30%) — semen analysis (volume, count, motility, morphology); unexplained (10%). Initial investigations: semen analysis; mid-luteal progesterone (day 21 in a 28-day cycle — level above 30 nmol/L confirms ovulation); FSH, LH, oestradiol, AMH (anti-MÃ¼llerian hormone — reflects ovarian reserve); USS pelvis; consider hysterosalpingography (HSG) or laparoscopy for tubal assessment. Treatment: anovulatory infertility — clomifene citrate (anti-oestrogen; ovulation induction; increases multiple pregnancy risk); letrozole (aromatase inhibitor — now preferred for PCOS-related anovulation); gonadotrophins; IVF if tubal factor or failed oral ovulation induction.

Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS): the most common endocrine disorder in women of reproductive age, affecting 5–10%. Rotterdam criteria (2 of 3 needed): oligo/anovulation; clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism (acne, hirsutism, raised free testosterone/DHEAS); polycystic ovaries on USS (12 or more follicles per ovary measuring 2–9 mm, or increased ovarian volume above 10 mL). Associated with insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular risk, and endometrial hyperplasia/cancer (due to unopposed oestrogen from anovulation). Management: weight loss (improves insulin sensitivity and restores ovulation in overweight patients); metformin (improves insulin sensitivity, may restore ovulation and menstrual regularity); COCP (manages hyperandrogenism, acne, hirsutism, and provides endometrial protection); clomifene/letrozole for fertility; eflornithine (cream) or laser for unwanted facial hair. Screen for IGT/T2DM with fasting glucose or OGTT, lipid profile, and BP annually.

Safeguarding, Mental Health in Pregnancy, and Domestic Violence

Perinatal mental health is a critical area of obstetrics gynaecology UKMLA revision. Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS): 10-item validated questionnaire scored 0–30; score 10 or above suggests postnatal depression (PND); score 13 or above indicates major depression; question 10 (thoughts of self-harm) scores separately and requires immediate assessment regardless of total score. PND affects approximately 10–15% of new mothers; risk factors include previous depression, poor social support, adverse life events, and complicated delivery. Treatment: psychological therapies (CBT, counselling) for mild-moderate; antidepressants (sertraline is first-line in breastfeeding — lowest breast milk transfer) for moderate-severe. Postnatal psychosis (puerperal psychosis): rare (1–2/1000); onset within 2 weeks of delivery; confusion, hallucinations, delusions, rapidly fluctuating mood — psychiatric emergency requiring inpatient admission (mother and baby unit). All women booking for antenatal care should be screened for mental health history, domestic violence, and substance misuse using NICE-recommended questions. Healthcare professionals have a duty to safeguard — make a referral to children's social care if there is concern that an unborn or newborn child is at risk, even if the mother does not consent.

Frequently Asked Questions About Obstetrics and Gynaecology in the UKMLA

What is the first-line management of primary postpartum haemorrhage?

The first step is to call for help and activate a major haemorrhage protocol. Simultaneously: establish large-bore IV access (two wide-bore cannulae), take blood (FBC, clotting, U&E, group and crossmatch), give IV fluids, and perform uterine massage. The most common cause of PPH is uterine atony (70%) — treat with uterine massage, bimanual compression, and uterotonics: oxytocin 10 IU IV bolus (first-line), then ergometrine 500 micrograms IV (avoid in hypertension), then carboprost 250 micrograms IM (avoid in asthma), then misoprostol 1000 micrograms PR. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV should be given as early as possible (within 3 hours of PPH onset). If medical management fails: intrauterine balloon tamponade (Bakri balloon), B-Lynch brachysystole suture, uterine artery ligation, interventional radiology, and finally hysterectomy as last resort.

What are the UKMEC 4 contraindications to the combined oral contraceptive pill?

UKMEC 4 conditions (absolute contraindications to COCP) include: current or history of venous thromboembolism (VTE); migraine with aura (stroke risk); ischaemic heart disease or stroke; valvular heart disease with complications; current breast cancer; active viral hepatitis; cirrhosis (severe decompensated); smoking ≥35 years old with ≥15 cigarettes/day; uncontrolled hypertension (≥160/100); postpartum (breastfeeding <6 weeks or not breastfeeding <3 weeks). UKMEC 2025 added enhanced VTE risk conditions — women with multiple VTE risk factors should be classified as UKMEC 4 for CHC.

When is magnesium sulphate used in obstetrics?

Magnesium sulphate has two main obstetric indications: (1) Neuroprotection for the preterm fetus — given to women in established preterm labour <30 weeks gestation to reduce the risk of cerebral palsy in the neonate; (2) Eclampsia prophylaxis and treatment — in severe pre-eclampsia (as prophylaxis) and in eclampsia (to stop and prevent recurrent seizures). The loading dose is 4 g IV over 5–20 minutes followed by a maintenance infusion of 1–2 g/hour. Signs of toxicity: loss of patellar reflexes (first sign, at ~3.5–5 mmol/L), respiratory depression (>5 mmol/L), cardiac arrest (>7.5 mmol/L). Antidote: calcium gluconate 10 mL of 10% IV. Renal function must be monitored as magnesium is renally excreted.

How do I interpret a CTG for the UKMLA?

CTG (cardiotocography) interpretation uses the DR C BRAVADO mnemonic: Define Risk, Contractions, Baseline Rate (normal 110–160 bpm), Rhythm, Accelerations (normal: ≥2 in 20 minutes, amplitude ≥15 bpm for ≥15 seconds), Variability (normal: 5–25 bpm), Decelerations. Reassuring features: baseline 110–160, variability 5–25, accelerations present, no decelerations. Non-reassuring: one abnormal feature. Abnormal/pathological: two or more non-reassuring or one abnormal feature (late decelerations — after contraction peak — most concerning, suggesting fetal hypoxia; variable decelerations — cord compression; prolonged deceleration >3 min). If pathological CTG: fetal scalp blood sampling (pH <7.20 = deliver; pH 7.20–7.25 = repeat; pH >7.25 = reassuring) or immediate delivery if acute bradycardia.

What are the risk factors for pre-eclampsia and who should receive aspirin prophylaxis?

NICE classifies risk as high or moderate. High-risk factors (one required): hypertensive disease in previous pregnancy, CKD, autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome), type 1 or 2 diabetes, chronic hypertension. Moderate-risk factors (two or more required): first pregnancy, age ≥40, BMI >35 at first visit, family history of pre-eclampsia, multiple pregnancy, interpregnancy interval >10 years. Aspirin 150 mg nightly from 12 weeks (until delivery) is recommended for all women with one high-risk factor or two or more moderate-risk factors. Regular BP monitoring and urine dipstick at each antenatal visit. Fetal growth scans and uterine artery Doppler if high risk. Low-dose aspirin reduces pre-eclampsia incidence by ~15% and preterm pre-eclampsia by ~60% in high-risk women.

Conclusion

Obstetrics gynaecology UKMLA revision requires integrating acute emergency management (the obstetric emergencies table is exam-essential) with guideline-based contraceptive prescribing using UKMEC 2025, antenatal screening pathways, and gynaecological cancer referral thresholds. The magnesium protocol for eclampsia, the miscarriage management table, and the CTG interpretation framework are particularly high-yield for both AKT and CPSA stations.
