The realistic cost of GMC registration in 2026 is roughly £700 to £1,400 all-in once you add the GMC's own application fee to the primary source verification and English test fees you need alongside it. On the GMC side alone, provisional registration costs just £26, while full registration — the route almost every international medical graduate (IMG) takes — costs £481, both effective from 1 April 2026. Layer on EPIC/MyIntealth verification (typically US$250-450, roughly £200-350) and an English test such as IELTS Academic UKVI (around £200) or OET Medicine (around £290-320), and the true one-off cost of getting onto the register climbs well past the headline GMC figure. This post is deliberately narrow: it covers only the registration application itself and the costs that sit directly around it, not the UKMLA exam fees (AKT £283, CPSA £1,036) and not the recurring annual retention fee (£481 or £172 a year) you pay after you're registered — both are explained here precisely so you stop conflating three separate budget lines that are easy to mix up.

Key takeaways

  • EPIC/MyIntealth verification (required for most IMGs) typically costs US$250-450 (roughly £200-350) once you include account setup, portfolio establishment, and per-credential verification fees.

The GMC charges a fee at the point you submit your registration application — this is the fee most people mean when they ask about "the cost of GMC registration." As of 1 April 2026, the GMC's official fee schedule sets these amounts:

Provisional registration is the route UK medical graduates take when they move straight into Foundation Year 1 under structured supervision — it costs only £26 because the scope of practice is limited and time-bound. Why the registration application fee is deliberately small compared with everything else

It surprises a lot of candidates that the GMC's own administrative fee (£481, or £26 for provisional) is often smaller than what they pay for verification and English testing combined. That's because the GMC outsources primary source verification to Intealth/ECFMG rather than running it in-house, and the English test is run by entirely separate examination bodies (IDP/British Council for IELTS, Cambridge Boxhill Language Assessment for OET). The GMC fee covers processing your application and registration decision — it does not cover verifying your medical degree is genuine or proving your English is at the required standard.

Registration cost vs exam cost vs annual retention fee: the distinction that matters most

This is the core confusion this post exists to resolve. There are three genuinely separate cost categories in the UKMLA-to-GMC-registration journey, and none of them substitute for each other:

1. UKMLA exam fees — paid to sit the assessment

The AKT (Applied Knowledge Test) costs £283 and the CPSA (Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment) costs £1,036, both from 1 April 2026. These are examination fees, paid when you book each component, and they have nothing to do with the GMC registration application itself — you can, in theory, pass both components and never apply for registration, though in practice almost everyone proceeds straight to registration afterward. For a full breakdown of exam-specific costs by IMG pathway, see UKMLA fees for IMGs 2026.

2. GMC registration application fee — paid once, to join the register

This is the £26 (provisional) or £481 (full) fee covered above. It's a one-off charge tied to the act of registering, not to the exam itself and not to staying registered afterwards. You pay this only after you've passed the relevant exams (or hold an accepted equivalent) and completed verification.

3. Annual retention fee — paid every year you stay on the register

Once registered, you must pay an annual fee to remain on the register, due on the anniversary of your registration. From 1 April 2026, this is £481 per year with a licence to practise, or £172 per year without a licence. This is a recurring cost that has nothing to do with the one-off application fee — many doctors mistakenly assume the £481 they paid to register covers them indefinitely, when in fact £481 (or £172) becomes an annual bill from that point forward. There is a discounted annual fee of £184 (with licence) or £66 (without) for doctors who moved from provisional to full registration in the past four years, or who passed their primary medical qualification within the past five years.

English test fees: IELTS Academic UKVI vs OET Medicine

The GMC requires evidence of English language proficiency before granting registration, unless you qualify for an exemption (for example, having trained in a majority English-speaking country as defined by the GMC, or holding recent, extensive experience working in English). Most IMGs meet this requirement with either IELTS Academic (UKVI version) or OET Medicine.

Because English test results are typically only accepted by the GMC within a set validity window (commonly around two years, though you should confirm the current rule), timing your test close to when you'll actually submit your registration application avoids paying for a result that expires before you use it.

Worked example: a full cost walkthrough for a typical IMG applicant

Numbers on a table are useful, but seeing them applied to one realistic candidate makes the picture concrete. Consider a doctor who qualified outside the UK, has already passed both UKMLA components, and is now moving through verification toward full registration in 2026.

Step 1: exam fees, already paid

This candidate paid £283 for the AKT and £1,036 for the CPSA when booking each exam — a combined £1,319 spent well before registration itself even becomes relevant. These fees are sunk costs by the time registration planning starts, but they belong in the same overall "cost of becoming a GMC-registered doctor" figure that many readers actually have in mind when they search for "cost of GMC registration."

Step 2: verification, running in parallel

While waiting on exam results, or shortly after passing, the candidate opens a MyIntealth account (US$110), establishes an EPIC portfolio (US$35), and submits their primary medical qualification and an internship completion certificate for verification (2 x US$110 = US$220). No courier fee is needed because the medical school accepts electronic verification requests. Total EPIC cost: US$365, roughly £285 at typical exchange rates.

Step 3: the English test

The candidate sits IELTS Academic (UKVI), paying approximately £200, and achieves the GMC's required score comfortably, avoiding a costly resit.

Step 4: notarisation and translation

Because one supporting certificate was issued in a language other than English, the candidate pays for a certified translation (£45) and has their identification documents notarised as part of the MyIntealth identity-confirmation step, which is already included in the US$110 account fee above.

Step 5: the registration application itself

With verification complete and English requirements met, the candidate applies for full registration and pays £481 — or, since they qualified within the past five years and are moving from provisional to full registration, they qualify for the fixed-term discount and pay only £184 instead.

Payment methods and timing for GMC registration fees

The GMC's registration application fee is paid online, in full, at the point you submit your application through GMC Online — there is no instalment option for this one-off fee. Card payment is the standard method; check current guidance on gmc-uk.org for accepted card types and any regional restrictions. The annual retention fee, once you're registered, can usually be paid by direct debit, which spreads the recurring cost more predictably across the year rather than requiring a single lump payment on your registration anniversary.

Timing matters financially as well as procedurally. Because English test results and some verified credentials have validity windows, paying for these too early risks the result or verification expiring before you actually submit your registration application — meaning you pay twice. A sensible sequence is: pass the UKMLA components, begin EPIC verification promptly (it can take several weeks), sit your English test with enough runway that the result is still valid when you expect to apply, and only then submit and pay the registration application fee itself. For a realistic timeline across the whole process, see how long does GMC registration take.

Currency and exchange rate timing

Because Intealth's EPIC fees are billed in US dollars and the GMC's fees are in GBP, the exact converted cost for candidates paying from India, Nigeria, Pakistan, or elsewhere shifts with the exchange rate on the day of payment. If you're budgeting well in advance, build in a buffer of 5-10% above the current converted estimate to absorb reasonable currency movement between now and when you actually pay.

Choosing between IELTS Academic and OET Medicine on cost grounds

Cost alone shouldn't decide which English test you sit, but it's a legitimate factor when the two options are otherwise close. IELTS Academic UKVI is generally the cheaper option at around £200, and it's widely recognised beyond medicine, which can be useful if you're also applying for visas or other UK processes that ask for a general English qualification. OET Medicine costs more (roughly £290-320 for all four sub-tests) but is built specifically around healthcare scenarios, and some candidates find the clinical context easier to perform well in, which can be worth the higher fee if it reduces your chance of a costly resit. If you're confident in one skill area but weaker in another, OET's option to resit individual sub-tests rather than the whole exam can also work out cheaper than a full IELTS resit, depending on which skill needs improving.

What happens financially if you fail to meet the required score

Both IELTS and OET require you to pay the full fee again for any resit — there's no discounted "retake" rate for either test as there sometimes is for other professional exams. If your first attempt narrowly misses the GMC's required score in only one component, it's worth checking whether the test provider allows a single-skill retest (OET does) before assuming you must repeat everything. Planning your first attempt with a buffer above the minimum required score, rather than sitting right at the boundary, is the most reliable way to avoid this added cost altogether.

Fee waivers and discounts: what actually reduces the cost

The GMC runs several discount schemes that materially change the real cost of registration for eligible doctors — worth checking carefully before you assume you must pay the full headline figure.

Fixed-term discount on the registration application fee

Doctors moving from provisional to full registration, or who passed their primary medical qualification within the past five years, can pay a discounted full registration application fee of £184 instead of £481. This is one of the most valuable, and most overlooked, discounts — many newly-qualifying IMGs don't realise they may already meet the five-year qualification window.

Income-based discount (below the £38,500 threshold)

The GMC offers a 50% discount on the annual retention fee for doctors whose worldwide income is below the current income discount threshold, set at £38,500 from 1 April 2026 (up from £37,000 the previous year). This brings the annual retention fee down to roughly £240 (with licence) or £86 (without), and a version of this discount can also apply to the registration application fee itself once you're fully registered. This threshold is reviewed annually, so always confirm the current figure on the GMC's discounts page before assuming eligibility.

Refugee and displaced doctor support

The GMC and partner organisations offer specific support — which can include fee assistance — for refugee and displaced doctors seeking UK registration, recognising that this group often cannot access standard document verification routes or afford fees while their status is unresolved. See our dedicated post on UKMLA for refugee doctors for the current support landscape and how to check eligibility.

Hidden and easy-to-miss costs around GMC registration

Beyond the headline GMC, EPIC, and English test fees, several smaller costs regularly catch candidates off guard. None of these individually are large, but together they can add several hundred pounds to your realistic budget.

Document notarisation and attestation

Some documents need notarisation, apostille, or attestation before they're accepted for verification, particularly if a medical school or licensing authority requires certified copies rather than originals sent directly. Notarisation costs vary significantly by country but commonly run £20-60 per document; if you need several documents notarised, this adds up quickly.

Certified translations

If your primary medical qualification, transcripts, or supporting certificates aren't in English, you'll need certified translations before they can go through EPIC verification. Certified translation costs vary by language and document length but commonly run £30-100 per document — a real cost that's easy to forget when budgeting only for the "official" fees.

Courier fees

As covered above, Intealth charges an optional US$15 (US institutions) or US$30 (non-US institutions) courier fee to send your verification request. If your institution requires original documents returned by courier rather than electronically, you may also face a separate courier cost arranged directly with your medical school — outside Intealth's fee structure entirely.

Travel and accommodation for the CPSA

The CPSA is a practical, in-person exam sat at specific UK test centres. For IMGs travelling from outside the UK, this means flights, accommodation, and possibly time off work on top of the £1,036 exam fee itself — often the single largest hidden cost in the entire journey, and one worth planning for well in advance given visa and travel booking lead times.

Re-verification or expired-result costs

If a credential verification fails, needs correction, or your English test result expires before you submit your registration application, you may need to pay again for verification or retesting. Building a realistic timeline — covered above — is the best defence against paying twice.

Rule of thumb: whatever your itemised total looks like on paper, add a 10-15% buffer for notarisation, translation, or a courier fee you didn't anticipate. Almost no one gets through the process without at least one of these extra costs.

How the cost of GMC registration compares by route

Not every applicant follows an identical cost path. UK medical graduates entering the Foundation Programme typically pay only the £26 provisional registration fee plus their AKT and CPSA costs, since these are usually embedded in their medical school's own assessment fees rather than paid separately. International medical graduates pay the full £481 registration fee (or £184 with the fixed-term discount) on top of independently booking and paying for the AKT and CPSA, plus EPIC/MyIntealth verification, which UK graduates do not need. Doctors applying through the Portfolio Pathway for specialist or GP registration face a different fee structure entirely, tied to that assessment route rather than the standard UKMLA fees covered here. Knowing which route applies to you before you start budgeting prevents the common mistake of assuming one applicant's total cost applies to everyone.

Currency and international payment considerations

Most GMC fees are charged in GBP regardless of where you live, while EPIC/MyIntealth verification fees are typically charged in USD. This means your actual out-of-pocket cost in your home currency will shift with exchange rates between when you plan your budget and when you actually pay, sometimes by a meaningful margin if your local currency is volatile. International card payments may also incur foreign transaction fees from your bank, typically 1-3% per transaction, which are easy to overlook across the several separate payments this process requires. Where possible, use a card with no foreign transaction fees, and build a small currency-movement buffer into your total budget rather than planning to the exact GBP or USD figure quoted at the time you first research costs.

Who pays what: IMGs, EU/Irish doctors, and refugee doctors

The GMC's fee schedule itself doesn't differentiate by nationality — everyone pays the same £26/£481 registration fee and the same annual retention fee. What differs by group is the verification route and eligibility for support schemes.

International medical graduates generally

Most IMGs from outside the UK and EU/EEA follow the standard EPIC verification route through Intealth described above. See our broader guide on GMC registration requirements for international doctors for the full eligibility picture beyond just cost.

EU and Irish doctors

EU and Irish doctors pay the same registration fees but may follow different verification or evidence pathways depending on their specific qualification and any relevant mutual recognition arrangements — this can change which documents you need verified, and therefore how many EPIC verification fees you pay. See GMC registration for EU and Irish doctors for the details specific to that route.

Refugee and displaced doctors

As noted above, dedicated support exists for refugee doctors who often face additional, non-standard barriers to standard document verification. See UKMLA for refugee doctors for current guidance.

Frequently asked questions

What is the total cost of GMC registration in 2026?

Budget roughly £700 to £1,400 for GMC registration itself once you add the application fee, EPIC/MyIntealth verification, and an English test. The GMC application fee alone is £26 for provisional registration or £481 for full registration (effective 1 April 2026). This figure excludes UKMLA exam fees (AKT £283, CPSA £1,036) and the separate annual retention fee, which are budgeted independently.

Is the GMC registration fee the same as the annual retention fee?

No. The registration application fee is a one-off charge paid when you first join the register (£26 provisional or £481 full). The annual retention fee is a separate, recurring charge paid every year afterwards to remain registered: £481 per year with a licence to practise, or £172 per year without one, from 1 April 2026. Confusing the two is one of the most common budgeting mistakes IMGs make.

Does the cost of GMC registration include the UKMLA exam fees?

No. The AKT (£283) and CPSA (£1,036) are examination fees paid to sit the UK Medical Licensing Assessment, entirely separate from the registration application fee. An IMG typically pays exam fees first, then the registration application fee only after passing both components and completing verification, so both costs need their own line in your budget.

How much does EPIC or MyIntealth verification cost for GMC registration?

Intealth (ECFMG), which runs the EPIC verification service the GMC requires for most international medical graduates, charges around US$110 to establish a MyIntealth account and confirm identity, US$35 to open an EPIC portfolio, and US$110 per credential verified. Most doctors verify at least two documents (primary medical qualification and an internship or good-standing certificate), so EPIC verification commonly totals US$250-450 (roughly £200-350) before any optional courier fees. Always check current pricing on ecfmg.org, since Intealth fees are set independently of the GMC and can change.

What English test do I need for GMC registration and how much does it cost?

Most IMGs satisfy the GMC's English language requirement with IELTS Academic (UKVI version) or OET Medicine. IELTS Academic UKVI typically costs around £200 in the UK or roughly ₹19,000-20,000 in India; OET Medicine costs approximately AUD $587 (all four sub-tests), which is roughly £290-320. These fees are paid to the test provider, not the GMC, and are not refundable if you fail to meet the required score.

Do I pay the GMC application fee before or after the UKMLA exams?

You pay AKT and CPSA fees when you book each exam, before sitting them. The GMC registration application fee is paid later, when you actually submit your registration application after passing both exams and completing identity and qualification verification through EPIC. Provisional registration (£26) typically applies to UK medical graduates going straight into the Foundation Programme; most IMGs apply directly for full registration (£481).

Are there any discounts on GMC registration fees?

Yes. The GMC offers a fixed-term discount on the full registration application fee (£184 instead of £481) for doctors moving from provisional to full registration, or who passed their primary medical qualification within the past five years. There is also a 50% income-based discount on the annual retention fee for doctors whose worldwide income is below the current threshold of £38,500, and support is available for refugee and displaced doctors. Always confirm current eligibility on gmc-uk.org, as discount rules and thresholds are reviewed annually.

What hidden costs should I budget for beyond the official GMC fees?

Common hidden costs include document notarisation or attestation, certified translations for non-English documents, courier fees for sending documents to your medical school (US$15-30 per shipment via ECFMG's courier option), travel and accommodation for the CPSA exam, and possible re-verification fees if a credential check fails or expires before you complete your application. These can add several hundred pounds beyond the headline GMC and EPIC fees, so build in a buffer.

Can I pay the GMC registration fee in instalments?

No, the GMC registration application fee is paid in full at the point of application, typically by card through GMC Online. The GMC does not offer instalment plans for the registration fee itself, though it does offer a direct debit option for the recurring annual retention fee once you are registered, which can spread that cost across the year.

How does the cost of GMC registration compare for provisional versus full registration?

Provisional registration costs only £26 and is designed for newly-qualified UK medical graduates entering their Foundation Year 1 under supervision. Full registration costs £481 (or £184 with the fixed-term discount) and is what almost all IMGs apply for directly, since it confers the right to practise independently once granted a licence. The gap in price reflects the more limited scope of provisional registration.

Do EU and Irish doctors pay the same GMC registration fees as other international doctors?

Yes, the GMC application fee schedule is the same regardless of where a doctor qualified. EU and Irish doctors pay the same £26 provisional or £481 full registration fee. However, the verification route can differ: Irish graduates and doctors with qualifications recognised under specific mutual recognition arrangements may follow different evidence requirements, so it is worth checking the GMC's guidance for EU and Irish doctors on which verification pathway applies before assuming the EPIC route is required.

Does the retention fee income discount apply to registration fees too?

Partially. The 50% income-based discount is most commonly associated with the annual retention fee, but the GMC also allows a 50% discount on the full registration application fee itself for doctors whose worldwide income is below the current £38,500 threshold, provided you are not already receiving another discounted fee. You can only apply for this once fully registered, so check the exact eligibility wording on the GMC's discounts page before assuming it applies at your first application.
