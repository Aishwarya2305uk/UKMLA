Urology in the UKMLA: High-Yield Topics for the AKT

Urology UKMLA questions turn up across the Applied Knowledge Test far more often than candidates expect, because urinary tract problems present in every setting from general practice to the emergency department. A working knowledge of urinary tract infections, renal colic, urinary retention, prostate disease, and testicular emergencies is essential for both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) sitting the exam. Unlike nephrology, which concerns itself with kidney function, glomerular disease, and electrolyte physiology, urology is the specialty of the structural and surgical urinary tract — stones, obstruction, infection, and the cancers and emergencies that affect the kidneys, ureters, bladder, prostate, and testes.

This guide works through the urology UKMLA syllabus systematically: urinary tract infections and when to investigate further, acute and chronic urinary retention with catheterisation basics, renal and ureteric colic, benign prostatic hyperplasia, prostate cancer red flags and the PSA testing controversy, testicular torsion as a classic surgical emergency, the haematuria investigation pathway, and bladder cancer red flags. Throughout, the emphasis is on the practical decision-making the AKT actually tests — when to reassure, when to refer routinely, and when to act as an emergency. Revising urology UKMLA content alongside the closely related renal medicine syllabus is a sensible strategy, since AKI, hydronephrosis, and electrolyte disturbance often appear as complications within an otherwise urological question stem, and examiners frequently expect candidates to move fluently between the two disciplines within a single scenario.

Preparing for this part of the exam should also sit within a wider revision timetable rather than being tackled in isolation — cross-referencing your AKT exam format knowledge and general UKMLA preparation strategy alongside a systems-based topic like this one helps consolidate both content and technique together.

Urology UKMLA at a Glance: The Quick Answer

Urology UKMLA topics test the recognition and initial management of structural and infective urinary tract disease rather than detailed operative technique. The AKT expects candidates to recognise red-flag symptoms (visible haematuria, testicular pain, acute retention, signs of urosepsis), select the correct first-line investigation (urine dipstick and culture, non-contrast CT KUB, PSA where appropriate, ultrasound), and choose safe initial management while knowing exactly when a case needs same-day surgical or urological referral.

Six clinical scenarios recur across past papers and question banks: an elderly man with lower urinary tract symptoms and a raised PSA, a young man with sudden severe testicular pain, a patient with loin-to-groin pain and haematuria, a woman with recurrent urinary tract infections, an older adult unable to pass urine after starting a new medication, and painless visible haematuria in a smoker over 60. Each of these maps onto a specific pathway covered in detail below, and each is designed to test whether the candidate can distinguish an urgent surgical emergency from a routine primary care presentation.

Urinary Tract Infections: Diagnosis and When to Investigate Further

Urinary tract infection (UTI) is one of the most frequently tested presentations in urology UKMLA questions, sitting at the interface of primary care, elderly medicine, paediatrics, and urology . A UTI is a bacterial infection anywhere along the urinary tract, most commonly caused by Escherichia coli, and is classified as lower (cystitis — bladder) or upper (pyelonephritis — kidney, with flank pain and systemic upset).

Recognising Uncomplicated Lower UTI

Classic symptoms are dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, and suprapubic discomfort, sometimes with visible or non-visible haematuria. In women under 65 with no red-flag features, NICE guidance supports empirical antibiotic treatment based on symptoms alone, without needing to send a urine culture in every case. A urine dipstick showing nitrites (suggesting Gram-negative bacteria such as E. coli) and leucocyte esterase supports the diagnosis, though a negative dipstick does not fully exclude infection if symptoms are convincing.

In men, UTIs are considered complicated by default because of the higher likelihood of an underlying structural or prostatic cause, so a urine culture should be sent and a longer antibiotic course considered. Pregnant women require urine culture at every presentation because even asymptomatic bacteriuria carries a real risk of progression to pyelonephritis and preterm labour, and asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy is treated actively, unlike in the non-pregnant population where it usually is not.

Recognising Upper UTI (Pyelonephritis)

Pyelonephritis presents with fever, rigors, loin pain, and costovertebral angle tenderness, often alongside lower urinary tract symptoms and systemic features such as nausea, vomiting, and tachycardia. Because pyelonephritis can progress rapidly to sepsis, any patient with systemic features alongside urinary symptoms needs prompt assessment against the sepsis six pathway, blood cultures, and admission if there are red flags such as hypotension, marked tachycardia, or an inability to tolerate oral fluids or antibiotics.

When to Investigate Further

Most uncomplicated UTIs do not need imaging. Further investigation of the urinary tract becomes necessary when a UTI is recurrent, when it occurs in a man, a child, or a pregnant woman, when there is visible haematuria persisting after treatment, when a patient fails to respond to appropriate antibiotics, or when there are features suggesting an underlying stone, obstruction, or structural abnormality. In these situations, a renal tract ultrasound or CT KUB is the standard next step, since imaging can reveal hydronephrosis, calculi, or an anatomical abnormality that is perpetuating infection.

Recurrent UTIs, defined as two or more episodes in six months or three or more in a year, warrant a search for an underlying cause: incomplete bladder emptying (from prolapse, prostatic obstruction, or a neurogenic bladder), stones, or, in postmenopausal women, vaginal atrophy from oestrogen deficiency, which is treatable with topical oestrogen. Recognising when recurrent infection reflects a fixable urological problem, rather than simply prescribing repeated antibiotic courses, is a common AKT discrimination point.

Catheter-Associated UTI

Catheter-associated UTI is a distinct entity tested frequently in the context of hospitalised or care home patients. A positive urine culture in an asymptomatic catheterised patient should not automatically be treated — this is catheter colonisation, not infection, and antibiotics are only indicated if the patient has systemic or localising symptoms. Cloudy or malodorous urine alone, in the absence of fever or systemic upset, is not an indication for antibiotics — a frequently tested trap given how often catheter samples appear abnormal on dipstick regardless of true infection.

Urinary Retention: Acute vs Chronic, and Catheterisation Basics

Urinary retention is one of the most operationally important urology UKMLA topics, since it is the inability to voluntarily void urine, and the AKT expects candidates to distinguish confidently between the acute and chronic forms, since the urgency and management differ substantially.

Acute Urinary Retention

Acute urinary retention presents with a sudden inability to pass urine, severe suprapubic pain, and a palpable, tender, distended bladder on examination. It is a urological emergency. The most common cause in men is benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), but other important precipitants include constipation, urinary tract infection, postoperative states (particularly after spinal or pelvic surgery, or under general/regional anaesthesia), and medications with anticholinergic effects (antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants) or sympathomimetic effects (decongestants) — a reminder that careful medication review belongs alongside the broader prescribing safety principles tested throughout the AKT. In women, retention is less common but can result from pelvic masses, prolapse, or neurological disease.

Management is urgent catheterisation to relieve the obstruction and pain, with the volume drained recorded (typically several hundred millilitres to over a litre). Bloods should include urea and electrolytes to assess for any element of post-renal AKI, and a digital rectal examination is performed to assess prostate size and exclude faecal impaction. If the residual volume is very large (over 1 litre), the patient should be monitored for post-obstructive diuresis, a brisk polyuria that follows decompression of a chronically obstructed system and can cause significant fluid and electrolyte shifts if not anticipated. Alpha-blockers such as tamsulosin are typically started before a trial without catheter (TWOC) to improve the chance of successful voiding, usually attempted after 24 to 72 hours.

Chronic Urinary Retention

Chronic urinary retention develops gradually and is frequently painless, which makes it easy to miss — the bladder can distend to hold very large volumes without the discomfort seen in the acute form, because the detrusor muscle and bladder wall adapt slowly over time. Patients may present instead with overflow incontinence, recurrent UTIs, or, in advanced cases, painless post-renal AKI (bilateral hydronephrosis from chronic outflow obstruction). Chronic retention is generally managed more cautiously than acute retention: decompression is sometimes staged, rather than immediate, in very large-volume chronic cases, because rapid decompression can occasionally provoke haematuria from sudden mucosal decompression, although the evidence for slow versus rapid decompression is not entirely settled and most departments now favour a single controlled drainage with close monitoring.

Catheterisation Basics

Urethral catheterisation is first-line in most cases of retention. A standard Foley catheter is passed under aseptic technique, with the balloon inflated once urine flow confirms correct bladder placement. If urethral catheterisation fails — commonly due to urethral stricture, a large prostate, or a false passage — a suprapubic catheter, inserted directly through the abdominal wall into the bladder under ultrasound guidance, is the alternative. Suprapubic catheterisation is contraindicated when the bladder cannot be confidently identified as distended, when there is known bladder cancer at the intended puncture site, or in the presence of a coagulopathy that has not been corrected.

Catheter complications tested on the AKT include: introduction of infection (the leading cause of hospital-acquired UTI), urethral trauma or false passage from forceful insertion, encrustation and blockage from long-term catheter use, and bladder spasm. Long-term catheterisation is also associated with a modestly increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder over many years, which is why any new haematuria or change in catheter drainage pattern in a long-term catheterised patient should prompt further assessment rather than being dismissed as routine catheter irritation.

Renal and Ureteric Colic: Kidney Stones

Renal and ureteric colic is one of the most classically described pain presentations in medicine and a frequent AKT scenario, usually featuring a patient describing sudden-onset, severe pain radiating from the loin to the groin, who cannot lie still — a useful discriminator from peritonitis, where patients typically lie motionless to avoid aggravating the pain.

Clinical Features

The pain of ureteric colic classically comes in waves as peristaltic contractions of the ureter attempt to move the stone distally, and is frequently accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and either visible or non-visible haematuria. The location of pain can hint at stone position: loin pain suggests a renal or upper ureteric stone, while pain radiating to the groin, testicle, or labium suggests a stone lower in the ureter, near the vesicoureteric junction, where it may also cause urinary frequency and urgency by irritating the bladder trigone.

Investigation

Non-contrast CT KUB (CT of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder) is the gold-standard first-line investigation in suspected renal colic, identifying almost all stone compositions, confirming the size and location of the stone, and showing the degree of any hydronephrosis. It requires no contrast and no bowel preparation, and has largely superseded the plain KUB X-ray, which misses radiolucent stones such as uric acid and most cystine stones and is unreliable for assessing obstruction. Ultrasound is preferred in pregnancy, where ionising radiation is avoided, and is also useful as a rapid bedside tool to detect hydronephrosis even though it is less sensitive for the stone itself.

Stone Types and Risk Factors

  • Calcium oxalate stones (around three-quarters of all stones): associated with low fluid intake, hypercalciuria, and high dietary oxalate.
  • Calcium phosphate stones: associated with renal tubular acidosis and primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • Uric acid stones: radiolucent on plain X-ray, associated with gout, high purine intake, and acidic urine; can sometimes be dissolved by alkalinising the urine.
  • Struvite (infection) stones: form in alkaline urine in the presence of urease-producing organisms such as Proteus mirabilis; can grow into large staghorn calculi filling the renal pelvis.
  • Cystine stones: rare, caused by the inherited condition cystinuria, presenting typically in younger patients with recurrent stones.

Management

Analgesia is the first priority — NSAIDs (such as diclofenac) are recommended first-line for renal colic pain, as trial evidence consistently shows they control pain at least as effectively as opioids while causing less nausea and vomiting; opioids are reserved for patients in whom NSAIDs are contraindicated or insufficient. Stones under 5mm pass spontaneously in the great majority of cases with conservative management and adequate hydration; alpha-blockers such as tamsulosin can be used as medical expulsive therapy to relax the ureteric smooth muscle and help stone passage for larger stones being managed conservatively. Stones between 5 and 10mm have a more variable spontaneous passage rate, and stones over 10mm usually require active intervention, most often ureteroscopy with laser fragmentation, extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy for renal stones, or percutaneous nephrolithotomy for very large renal or staghorn calculi.

The single most important emergency scenario within this topic is the infected, obstructed system — pyonephrosis. A patient with a ureteric stone, fever, and signs of sepsis has a surgical emergency requiring urgent decompression, typically with a nephrostomy or a retrograde ureteric stent, alongside broad-spectrum antibiotics; this cannot wait for the stone itself to be definitively treated, because pus under pressure in an obstructed kidney can progress rapidly to overwhelming sepsis and irreversible renal damage. Recognising this combination — stone plus fever plus obstruction — as distinct from uncomplicated renal colic is one of the highest-yield discrimination points in urology UKMLA questions.

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Benign prostatic hyperplasia is a core urology UKMLA topic and is non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland, extremely common with advancing age, and a leading cause of lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) and acute urinary retention in older men.

Clinical Presentation

LUTS from BPH are traditionally divided into voiding symptoms (hesitancy, poor stream, straining, terminal dribbling, a sensation of incomplete emptying) and storage symptoms (frequency, urgency, nocturia). Digital rectal examination in BPH typically reveals a smoothly enlarged, non-tender prostate with a preserved central sulcus, which is an important contrast to the findings expected in prostate cancer, discussed below. The International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) is used to grade symptom severity and guide management decisions, and can be repeated over time to track response to treatment.

Investigation

Initial assessment includes a urine dipstick to exclude infection or haematuria, renal function tests if there is any suspicion of upper tract involvement, and a frequency-volume chart to characterise the storage and voiding pattern. PSA testing is not routinely required for uncomplicated LUTS but is considered when there is diagnostic uncertainty about coexisting prostate cancer, particularly if the prostate feels asymmetrical, hard, or irregular on examination, since BPH itself can also modestly raise PSA.

Management

Conservative measures (reducing evening fluid intake, bladder training, reviewing medications that may worsen symptoms such as diuretics or anticholinergics) are first-line for mild symptoms. Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin, alfuzosin) relax prostatic and bladder neck smooth muscle and produce rapid symptomatic improvement, making them the usual first pharmacological step. 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride) shrink the prostate over months by blocking the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone, and are particularly useful in larger prostates; they can also lower PSA by around half, which must be accounted for when interpreting PSA results in men taking these drugs. Combination therapy is used for larger prostates with more severe symptoms. Surgical options, principally transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP), are reserved for refractory symptoms, recurrent acute retention, recurrent UTIs, bladder stones, or renal impairment secondary to obstruction.

Prostate Cancer: Red Flags and the PSA Testing Controversy

Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men in the UK and a heavily tested topic precisely because its diagnostic pathway involves genuine clinical nuance rather than a simple rule.

Red-Flag Features

Because early prostate cancer is frequently asymptomatic, clinicians rely on a combination of examination findings and risk factors rather than symptoms alone. On digital rectal examination, a hard, irregular, asymmetrical prostate, or one with a loss of the central sulcus, raises concern for malignancy, in clear contrast to the smooth, symmetrical enlargement typical of BPH. Red-flag symptoms that should prompt urgent assessment include unexplained weight loss, bone pain (suggestive of metastatic disease, since prostate cancer characteristically metastasises to bone), new lower urinary tract symptoms in combination with an abnormal prostate examination, and unexplained haematuria or haematospermia. Risk factors that should lower the threshold for investigation include increasing age, Black African or Black Caribbean ethnicity (which carries a notably higher lifetime risk), and a family history of prostate or certain other hereditary cancers.

The PSA Testing Controversy

Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) is a protein produced by both normal and cancerous prostate tissue, and its use as a screening tool is genuinely controversial — a point the AKT expects candidates to understand factually rather than dismiss. PSA lacks both sensitivity and specificity for prostate cancer: it can be raised by BPH, prostatitis, urinary retention, vigorous exercise, recent ejaculation, and instrumentation of the urinary tract (including catheterisation and digital rectal examination performed immediately beforehand), and a proportion of clinically significant prostate cancers occur with a normal PSA. This means PSA testing carries a real risk of both false positives, leading to unnecessary anxiety and invasive biopsy, and false negatives, providing false reassurance.

There is no national population-based PSA screening programme in the UK, reflecting evidence that mass screening has not been shown to clearly reduce prostate cancer mortality once the harms of overdiagnosis and overtreatment of indolent, non-life-threatening cancers are weighed against the benefit. Instead, the UK approach is informed choice: any asymptomatic man aged 50 or over who requests a PSA test after being counselled on its benefits and limitations is entitled to have it done, a policy sometimes referred to as the PSA informed-choice or risk-management programme. Where a man presents with symptoms or examination findings suggestive of prostate cancer, referral and investigation follow the NICE suspected cancer pathway rather than opportunistic screening logic.

Referral and Further Investigation

Under the NICE suspected cancer referral guideline, men with a hard, irregular prostate on examination, or an appropriately raised age-specific PSA together with clinical suspicion, should be referred on a suspected cancer pathway for urological assessment, typically leading to multiparametric MRI of the prostate before any biopsy is considered — a sequencing change from older pathways, where biopsy was often the immediate next step. MRI helps target biopsies to areas of genuine concern (reported using the PI-RADS scoring system) and can, in some men with a low-suspicion scan, avoid an unnecessary biopsy altogether. Where biopsy proceeds, results are graded using the Gleason score (or the newer Grade Group system), which is central to risk-stratifying treatment options, ranging from active surveillance for low-risk, localised disease to radical prostatectomy, radiotherapy, or hormonal therapy for higher-risk or metastatic disease.

Testicular Torsion: A Surgical Emergency

Testicular torsion is arguably the single most important urological emergency for the AKT, precisely because a missed or delayed diagnosis leads directly to loss of the testis, and exam questions are constructed to test whether candidates will act immediately rather than waiting for confirmatory imaging.

Classic Presentation

Testicular torsion typically affects adolescent boys and young men, though it can occur at any age, and presents with sudden-onset, severe unilateral testicular pain, often associated with nausea and vomiting. On examination, the affected testis is high-riding (retracted upward due to cord shortening), swollen, exquisitely tender, and may lie in an abnormal, horizontal orientation compared with the unaffected side. The cremasteric reflex — stroking the inner thigh normally causes the ipsilateral testis to rise — is characteristically absent on the affected side, and this is a frequently examined discriminating sign, though its absence alone should never be relied upon to exclude torsion if the clinical picture otherwise fits. A useful bedside test, Prehn's sign, describes relief of pain on elevating the testis in epididymitis but no relief (or worsening) in torsion, although this sign is unreliable enough that it should never be used to rule out torsion in practice.

Why Speed Matters

Torsion causes twisting of the spermatic cord, occluding testicular blood supply, and testicular salvage rates fall sharply with time: salvage is highest within six hours of symptom onset and drops substantially beyond twelve hours, with most testes non-viable by twenty-four hours. This time-critical physiology is exactly why the AKT rewards candidates who choose immediate surgical exploration over waiting for an ultrasound in a classic presentation — a boy or young man with sudden severe testicular pain and an absent cremasteric reflex needs emergency surgical referral for exploration, not a scan first, if that scan would meaningfully delay theatre.

Investigation and Management

Doppler ultrasound, which can demonstrate reduced or absent blood flow to the affected testis, is useful when the diagnosis is uncertain and surgery can be arranged without meaningful delay, but it should never be allowed to delay definitive surgical exploration in a clinically convincing case. Management is emergency surgical exploration, with detorsion of the affected testis and bilateral orchidopexy (fixation of both testes to the scrotal wall) to prevent recurrence on either side, since the anatomical predisposition (a "bell-clapper" deformity, where the testis is inadequately fixed within the tunica vaginalis) is frequently bilateral. If the testis is found to be non-viable at exploration, orchidectomy (removal) is performed.

Differential Diagnosis

The principal differentials are epididymo-orchitis (typically more gradual onset, often with urinary symptoms and a preserved cremasteric reflex, more common in sexually active young men where Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae are important causes), and torsion of a testicular appendage (the hydatid of Morgagni), which can mimic torsion but is usually less severe, sometimes showing a localised "blue dot sign" of the necrotic appendage visible through the scrotal skin. Because clinical differentiation between these conditions is imperfect, and the consequences of missing true torsion are severe, the guiding exam principle is: when in genuine doubt, treat as torsion and refer for emergency surgical exploration.

Haematuria: The Investigation Pathway

Haematuria is one of the highest-yield presenting symptoms across urology UKMLA scenarios and is classified as visible (macroscopic, sometimes called frank haematuria) or non-visible (microscopic, detected only on dipstick or microscopy), and the investigation pathway differs according to which type is present and the patient's risk profile.

Initial Assessment

The first step for any haematuria is to exclude a benign or transient cause: confirmed urinary tract infection, recent vigorous exercise, menstruation contaminating the sample, or a recently passed renal stone. Once these are excluded, or if haematuria persists after treatment of an apparent cause, further urological assessment is required. A history should specifically explore smoking status (the single most important modifiable risk factor for bladder cancer), occupational exposure to aromatic amines and dyes (historically linked to industries such as rubber, dye, and textile manufacture), painless versus painful haematuria, and any associated storage or voiding symptoms.

Red Flags Warranting Urgent Referral

Any visible haematuria without an obvious benign explanation warrants referral on a suspected cancer pathway, particularly in patients aged 45 and over, because painless visible haematuria is the classic presenting feature of bladder cancer and can also indicate renal or upper tract urothelial cancer. Non-visible haematuria is investigated more selectively: in patients aged 60 and over, unexplained non-visible haematuria together with either dysuria or a raised white cell count on a blood test also meets the threshold for urgent referral, reflecting the fact that non-visible haematuria can still herald urological malignancy even without visible blood.

Investigations

The standard urological work-up for haematuria combines upper and lower tract assessment: a CT urogram (or renal tract ultrasound where CT is contraindicated) to assess the kidneys and ureters for stones, masses, or upper tract urothelial cancer, and cystoscopy to directly visualise the bladder and urethra for tumours, stones, or inflammation, since bladder cancers arising from the urothelium are frequently missed on cross-sectional imaging alone. Urine cytology, looking for malignant cells shed into the urine, can add diagnostic value, particularly for high-grade tumours, though a negative cytology result does not exclude bladder cancer, given its variable sensitivity depending on tumour grade.

Bladder Cancer: Red Flags

Bladder cancer rounds out the malignancy component of urology UKMLA revision alongside prostate cancer. It is most commonly a urothelial (transitional cell) carcinoma, and is closely linked to the same haematuria pathway above but deserves specific attention because of its distinctive risk factors and staging implications.

Risk Factors and Presentation

Smoking is the dominant risk factor, roughly tripling the risk of bladder cancer and accounting for a substantial proportion of cases, making smoking cessation counselling directly relevant whenever this diagnosis is discussed. Occupational exposure to aromatic amines (historically in the dye, rubber, and printing industries), chronic schistosomiasis infection (particularly relevant for squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder in patients with a travel or residence history in endemic regions of Africa and the Middle East), and previous pelvic radiotherapy are additional recognised risk factors. The classic presenting symptom is painless visible haematuria, though some patients present instead with recurrent UTIs, new-onset storage symptoms (frequency, urgency) without infection, or, in advanced disease, pelvic pain or symptoms of ureteric obstruction.

Staging and Management Overview

Bladder tumours are broadly divided into non-muscle-invasive (confined to the mucosa or submucosa) and muscle-invasive disease, a distinction that fundamentally changes management and is usually established via transurethral resection of bladder tumour (TURBT), which is both diagnostic and, for non-muscle-invasive disease, potentially curative. Non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer is managed with TURBT followed by intravesical therapy (chemotherapy such as mitomycin C, or immunotherapy with BCG for higher-risk disease) and rigorous cystoscopic surveillance for recurrence, since recurrence rates are high even after apparently complete resection. Muscle-invasive bladder cancer is a substantially more aggressive disease requiring radical treatment — typically radical cystectomy with urinary diversion, or radical radiotherapy with chemotherapy as a bladder-preserving alternative — which is why prompt recognition and referral of red-flag haematuria, rather than a delayed diagnosis at a more advanced stage, has such a direct bearing on patient outcomes.

Acute Scrotal Pain: Wider Differentials Beyond Torsion

Although testicular torsion dominates revision of acute scrotal pain because of its emergency status, urology UKMLA questions also test the ability to recognise the more common, less dangerous causes of scrotal pain and swelling, precisely because distinguishing them from torsion is the real clinical skill being assessed.

Epididymo-Orchitis

Epididymo-orchitis is inflammation of the epididymis and testis, usually of infective origin, and typically presents with a more gradual onset of scrotal pain and swelling than torsion, often over a day or two rather than minutes. In sexually active men under 35, Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae are the leading causes, and a sexual history together with screening for other sexually transmitted infections is essential. In older men, and in those with recent urinary tract instrumentation or catheterisation, coliform organisms such as E. coli are more typical, often in association with an underlying UTI or prostatitis. Examination classically shows a tender, swollen epididymis with a preserved cremasteric reflex, and pain that may ease slightly on elevating the testis (a positive Prehn's sign), though this finding is not reliable enough to exclude torsion on its own if the history is otherwise concerning. Treatment is with appropriate antibiotics guided by the likely organism (a quinolone or doxycycline-based regimen for suspected chlamydial infection, following local sexual health guidance) alongside analgesia and scrotal support.

Acute and Chronic Prostatitis

Acute bacterial prostatitis presents with perineal or suprapubic pain, fever, urinary symptoms, and sometimes systemic sepsis, with a markedly tender prostate on digital rectal examination — a finding that should prompt caution, since vigorous prostatic massage in acute prostatitis risks precipitating bacteraemia and is avoided. Management requires a prolonged course of antibiotics (typically four to six weeks) because prostatic tissue is poorly penetrated by many agents, and admission is warranted if there are systemic features of sepsis. Chronic prostatitis/chronic pelvic pain syndrome is a distinct, more difficult entity, causing pelvic pain lasting three months or more without a consistently identifiable bacterial cause, and is managed with a multimodal approach (alpha-blockers, analgesia, physiotherapy, and psychological support) rather than repeated antibiotic courses, which are not warranted in the absence of confirmed infection.

Hydrocele, Varicocele, and Epididymal Cysts

Not every scrotal presentation in urology UKMLA questions is painful or infective. A hydrocele is a painless fluid collection within the tunica vaginalis that transilluminates brightly and can be palpated above (you can "get above" it, distinguishing it from an inguinoscrotal hernia); it is managed conservatively unless large or symptomatic. A varicocele is a dilated pampiniform venous plexus, classically described as feeling like a "bag of worms," almost always on the left side because of the drainage of the left testicular vein into the left renal vein at a right angle; a new, sudden-onset right-sided or non-reducing varicocele in an older man is a red flag for renal cell carcinoma obstructing venous drainage and warrants urgent renal imaging rather than routine reassurance. Epididymal cysts are smooth, well-defined, painless cystic swellings separate from the testis itself, again managed conservatively unless troublesome. Any solid, firm, non-transilluminating intratesticular mass, in contrast, should be treated as testicular cancer until proven otherwise and investigated urgently with scrotal ultrasound and tumour markers (AFP, beta-hCG, LDH).

Nocturia and Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms in Older Adults

Nocturia — waking one or more times at night to pass urine — is an extremely common complaint in older adults and a frequent source of overlap between urology UKMLA content and general internal medicine, since its causes extend well beyond the prostate.

Differentiating the Causes of Nocturia

In men, BPH is an obvious and common contributor, but nocturia is frequently multifactorial, and treating it as a purely prostatic problem is a common exam trap. Nocturnal polyuria — excessive urine production specifically overnight — can result from poorly controlled diabetes (osmotic diuresis), heart failure or venous insufficiency (daytime fluid redistributing to the circulation once lying flat at night), obstructive sleep apnoea (via natriuretic peptide release), or simply excessive evening fluid, caffeine, or alcohol intake. A frequency-volume chart recorded over several days is the key investigation for distinguishing true nocturnal polyuria from reduced bladder capacity or BPH-driven storage symptoms, and should be requested before assuming prostatic surgery is the answer.

Practical Assessment

A focused history should cover fluid intake patterns, diuretic timing (taking a diuretic in the evening rather than the morning is a frequently missed and easily corrected cause of nocturia), symptoms of sleep apnoea, and cardiovascular or diabetic history, alongside the standard urological review of voiding and storage symptoms. Basic investigations include a urine dipstick, renal function and glucose, and a bladder diary; escalation to specialist urological assessment is reserved for those with red-flag features (haematuria, suspected retention, abnormal prostate examination) or those who fail simple conservative and pharmacological measures.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is urology a heavily weighted topic in the UKMLA AKT?

Urology UKMLA questions are not confined to a single specialty block — they surface throughout the exam in the context of acute presentations, cancer red-flag recognition, and safe prescribing, which is exactly why a dedicated systems-based revision pass pays off. Candidates who have already worked through the wider eligibility and format requirements for the exam tend to find it easier to slot topic-specific revision, such as this one, into a realistic study timetable.

What is the most important thing to remember about testicular torsion for the AKT?

Testicular torsion is a time-critical surgical emergency, and a clinically convincing presentation (sudden severe testicular pain, high-riding testis, absent cremasteric reflex) should prompt immediate emergency surgical referral for exploration rather than waiting for a confirmatory ultrasound. Testicular salvage rates fall sharply after six hours and most testes are non-viable by twenty-four hours, so any delay directly risks losing the testis.

Do all men with a raised PSA need a prostate biopsy?

No — PSA is neither fully sensitive nor specific for prostate cancer, being raised by benign conditions such as BPH, prostatitis, and urinary tract infection, so an elevated result is interpreted alongside age, examination findings, and risk factors rather than acting alone. Current pathways typically move to multiparametric MRI before biopsy is even considered, helping avoid unnecessary invasive procedures in men whose raised PSA reflects a benign cause.

How do I distinguish acute from chronic urinary retention in an exam question?

Acute urinary retention is sudden and painful, with a tender, palpable bladder, and needs same-day catheterisation, while chronic urinary retention develops gradually and is often painless despite very large residual volumes, sometimes presenting instead with overflow incontinence or recurrent UTIs. The key exam clue is the presence or absence of pain relative to the degree of bladder distension described.

What is the first-line investigation for suspected renal colic?

Non-contrast CT KUB is the first-line and gold-standard investigation for suspected renal or ureteric colic in non-pregnant adults, since it identifies almost all stone types, confirms size and location, and shows any associated hydronephrosis without needing contrast. Ultrasound is used instead in pregnancy to avoid ionising radiation, and plain X-ray is not relied upon alone because it misses radiolucent stones such as uric acid calculi.

When does non-visible haematuria need urgent referral?

Non-visible haematuria warrants urgent suspected cancer pathway referral in patients aged 60 or over if it occurs alongside either dysuria or a raised white cell count on blood testing, since this combination can still indicate underlying urological malignancy even without visible blood in the urine. Persistent non-visible haematuria without an obvious benign cause in any adult should also prompt further urological assessment rather than being dismissed.

Why is there no national PSA screening programme in the UK?

PSA testing has not been adopted as a national population screening programme because trials have not shown a clear enough mortality benefit once the harms of overdiagnosis and overtreatment of low-risk, non-life-threatening prostate cancers are weighed against the benefits of early detection. Instead, the UK operates an informed-choice approach, where any asymptomatic man aged 50 or over can request PSA testing after being counselled on its limitations.

What examination finding distinguishes BPH from prostate cancer on digital rectal examination?

Benign prostatic hyperplasia typically produces a smoothly enlarged, non-tender prostate with a preserved central sulcus, whereas prostate cancer is more often suggested by a hard, irregular, or asymmetrical gland, sometimes with loss of the central sulcus. Neither finding is completely definitive on its own, which is why suspicious examination findings are followed by PSA testing and, where indicated, MRI and referral on the suspected cancer pathway.

Conclusion

Urology UKMLA questions reward candidates who can quickly separate genuine surgical emergencies — testicular torsion, an infected obstructed kidney, acute urinary retention — from the much larger volume of routine urinary tract presentations that can be safely managed or referred non-urgently. Building fluency in the red-flag pathways for haematuria, prostate disease, and testicular pain, alongside a clear grasp of first-line investigations such as CT KUB, PSA, and urine culture, covers the great majority of what the AKT actually tests in this specialty, and should translate directly into confidence when reviewing your AKT results and scoring against this topic area.
