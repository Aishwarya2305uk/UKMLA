Critical care UKMLA questions sit at the heart of the AKT because the GMC's overriding concern at the point of licensure is whether a newly qualified doctor can safely recognise a patient who is deteriorating on a general ward, begin the correct initial treatment, and escalate to more senior help at the right moment. Unlike a single-organ specialty, this content tests a way of thinking: a structured, repeatable approach to any acutely unwell adult, regardless of the underlying diagnosis. This guide sets out that approach in full — the ABCDE assessment, NEWS2 scoring and SBAR escalation, the two patterns of acute respiratory failure, acute kidney injury as a common critical-care-adjacent problem, the major categories of shock, and the concept of a ceiling of care — so that both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AKT can walk into a deterioration scenario with confidence.

It is worth being explicit from the outset about scope. This article is deliberately general: it covers the recognition and initial management of the deteriorating ward patient as a category, not any single diagnosis in depth. Sepsis, for example, has its own dedicated care bundle and its own article on this site — acute presentations and specific emergency conditions are covered separately, including the Sepsis Six in full detail. Here, sepsis is referenced only as one possible cause of distributive shock, in the same way that anaphylaxis, myocardial infarction, and pulmonary embolism are referenced as causes of other patterns of deterioration. The purpose of this guide is to teach the underlying skeleton — the "what do I do in the first five minutes, and who do I call next" — that applies whichever diagnosis eventually emerges.

Critical Care UKMLA: The Quick Answer

Critical care UKMLA questions test a structured five-step approach: assess using ABCDE, quantify severity objectively with the National Early Warning Score 2 (NEWS2), escalate using SBAR communication to a senior clinician or the critical care outreach team, treat the specific pattern of failure you find (respiratory, renal, or circulatory), and reconsider the patient's ceiling of care throughout. Every deteriorating-patient AKT question can be answered by working through this sequence rather than jumping straight to a diagnosis.

In practical terms, this means: any acutely unwell patient gets an ABCDE assessment before a differential diagnosis is even considered; a NEWS2 score of 5 or more, or any single parameter scoring 3, triggers an urgent review; a NEWS2 score of 7 or more is a medical emergency requiring senior review and consideration of critical care involvement; and every phone call to a senior colleague or the outreach team should use the SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) structure so that nothing important is omitted under pressure. These five elements — ABCDE, NEWS2, SBAR, pattern recognition, and ceiling of care — recur across almost every AKT item in this domain, and examiners reward candidates who apply them systematically rather than guessing the diagnosis first.

The ABCDE Approach to the Acutely Unwell Patient

The ABCDE approach is the internationally recognised framework for assessing and managing any acutely unwell or deteriorating patient, and it underpins essentially all critical care UKMLA content in both the AKT and the CPSA. It is deliberately sequential: each step is addressed and, where necessary, treated before moving to the next, and the whole sequence is repeated after every intervention because a patient's physiology can change within minutes. The single most common error candidates make in written and simulated assessments alike is skipping ahead to look for a diagnosis before airway, breathing, and circulation have been secured — the AKT rewards candidates who resist that temptation.

Airway

The first question is simply whether the airway is patent. A patient who is talking normally has, by definition, a patent airway — this is a quick and reliable bedside test. Concerning signs include stridor (a harsh inspiratory noise suggesting upper airway obstruction), gurgling (suggesting fluid or secretions in the airway), and paradoxically, complete silence in a patient making respiratory effort (suggesting complete obstruction). Initial airway manoeuvres are simple: a head-tilt chin-lift (avoided if cervical spine injury is suspected, in which case a jaw thrust is used instead) and suction of visible secretions or vomit. If these fail, airway adjuncts such as an oropharyngeal (Guedel) or nasopharyngeal airway can maintain patency in a semi-conscious patient. A definitive airway — endotracheal intubation, or in extremis a surgical airway — is a critical care and anaesthetic decision and should prompt immediate escalation rather than ward-level management.

Breathing

Assessment of breathing includes respiratory rate, depth and symmetry of chest movement, oxygen saturation, use of accessory muscles, and auscultation for added sounds (wheeze, crepitations, reduced air entry). High-flow oxygen (typically 15 L/min via a non-rebreathe mask) should be given to any acutely unwell hypoxic patient as an immediate first step, with one important caveat: patients with known chronic type 2 respiratory failure (most commonly severe COPD) should have oxygen titrated to a target saturation of 88–92% rather than being flooded with high-flow oxygen, because excessive oxygen can worsen hypercapnia in this group by removing the hypoxic drive to breathe and by altering ventilation-perfusion matching. This distinction between type 1 and type 2 respiratory failure, discussed in detail below, is one of the most frequently tested points in this entire content area.

Circulation

Circulatory assessment includes heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill time (normal is under two seconds, measured centrally on the sternum or peripherally on a fingertip held at heart level for five seconds), skin colour and temperature, jugular venous pressure, and urine output as a marker of end-organ perfusion. Intravenous access should be secured early, with blood taken for full blood count, urea and electrolytes, C-reactive protein, lactate, blood cultures if infection is suspected, and a venous or arterial blood gas. A 12-lead electrocardiogram and continuous cardiac monitoring are appropriate in most acutely unwell patients. If the patient is haemodynamically compromised — hypotensive, tachycardic, or has a prolonged capillary refill time — a fluid challenge is usually appropriate: typically 250–500 mL of crystalloid (such as balanced crystalloid solution) given as a bolus over 10–15 minutes in an adult, with reassessment of response before repeating. Fluid boluses should be given more cautiously, or avoided altogether pending senior input, in patients with known heart failure or significant renal impairment, since fluid overload in these groups can rapidly worsen respiratory status.

Disability

The Disability step assesses conscious level and neurological function. The AVPU scale (Alert, responds to Voice, responds to Pain, Unresponsive) is a rapid screening tool used at the bedside and within NEWS2 itself; the Glasgow Coma Scale (Eyes out of 4, Verbal out of 5, Motor out of 6, summing to a maximum of 15) gives a more granular assessment when needed. Blood glucose must be checked in every acutely unwell or confused patient, since hypoglycaemia is rapidly reversible and easily missed if assessment jumps straight to more complex differentials. Pupillary size and reactivity should also be assessed, particularly in any patient with reduced consciousness.

Exposure

The final step is a full exposure and examination, checking temperature, looking for rashes (a non-blanching purpuric rash raises concern for meningococcal sepsis; urticaria suggests an allergic process), examining for occult bleeding or injury, and checking calves for signs of deep vein thrombosis. Dignity should be maintained throughout and the patient kept warm, since exposure for examination should not itself precipitate hypothermia, which independently worsens coagulopathy and cardiac function.

NEWS2 and Structured Escalation

The National Early Warning Score 2 (NEWS2) is the standardised early warning scoring system recommended across NHS England, Wales, and Scotland for the routine clinical assessment of acutely ill adult patients on general wards. NEWS2 scores six physiological parameters — respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, any use of supplemental oxygen, systolic blood pressure, pulse rate, and level of consciousness (using AVPU, where a new onset of confusion scores 3 points) — plus temperature, each contributing 0 to 3 points, which are then summed into a single aggregate score. This system was developed and is maintained by the Royal College of Physicians, and it is designed to standardise the language used to describe a deteriorating patient across every ward, specialty, and hospital in the country, so that a NEWS2 score of 7 means the same thing whether it is called in from a surgical ward or a medical assessment unit.

How NEWS2 Scores Are Calculated and Escalated

An important nuance is the two SpO2 scales built into NEWS2. Scale 1 is used for most patients and rewards higher saturations; Scale 2 is used specifically for patients with documented hypercapnic (type 2) respiratory failure, and rewards a lower target range of 88–92%, reflecting the clinical reality that pushing these patients to saturations above 92% can be actively harmful. Using the wrong scale is a genuine and examinable pitfall — a patient with COPD and chronic type 2 respiratory failure who is deliberately kept at 90% saturation should not be scored as if that were dangerously low, provided Scale 2 has been correctly selected and documented.

The resulting aggregate score maps onto a clinical response scale. A score of 0–4 indicates a low risk of deterioration and prompts routine ward-based monitoring, typically 4–12 hourly depending on local policy. A score of 5–6, or a single parameter scoring 3 in isolation (sometimes called a "red score"), constitutes medium risk and should trigger an urgent review by a clinician competent to assess acutely ill patients, usually within 30 minutes, alongside consideration of increasing the monitoring frequency and involving the critical care outreach team. A score of 7 or above constitutes high risk and is treated as a medical emergency requiring emergency assessment by a team with critical care competencies, typically including a member of the critical care outreach service, and continuous monitoring of vital signs. These thresholds are the single most commonly tested numerical facts in this entire subject area, and candidates should be able to recall them precisely rather than approximately.

SBAR Communication to Seniors and Outreach Teams

Recognising deterioration is only half the task — the second half is communicating it effectively enough that the right help arrives quickly. SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) is the structured communication tool used across the NHS for exactly this purpose, and the UKMLA tests candidates' understanding of it both directly (naming the framework) and indirectly (choosing the most appropriate next action in an escalation scenario). Situation states, in one or two sentences, who the patient is and what is happening right now — for example, "I'm calling about Mr Patel in bed 14, who has become acutely breathless and hypoxic in the last twenty minutes." Background gives the relevant clinical context succinctly: admission diagnosis, comorbidities, relevant recent results or interventions. Assessment presents the caller's own clinical findings and interpretation, including the current NEWS2 score and vital signs, and what the caller believes is going on. Recommendation states explicitly what the caller wants — a review within a specific timeframe, a particular investigation, or immediate attendance — because a vague call that ends without a clear ask is one of the most common causes of delayed escalation in real clinical practice.

The critical care outreach team (sometimes called the medical emergency team, patient at-risk team, or similar depending on the trust) exists specifically to bridge the gap between ward-level care and the intensive care unit. Outreach nurses and doctors can be contacted proactively, before a patient reaches crisis point, whenever a junior doctor is concerned about a trend in observations even if the absolute NEWS2 score has not yet crossed a hard threshold — the AKT rewards candidates who recognise that a rising trend (for example, NEWS2 climbing from 2 to 4 over two hours) can warrant early outreach involvement even before the score itself mandates it, since early intervention in critical illness consistently improves outcomes compared with reactive crisis management. This principle — call early, not just when the numbers force you to — is one of the clearest, most examinable messages within the wider concept of critical care UKMLA teaching, and it is explicitly endorsed in NICE's guidance on recognising and responding to acute deterioration in hospital, which recommends that graded response strategies include the ability for any member of staff to escalate concerns regardless of the calculated score.

Acute Respiratory Failure: Type 1 versus Type 2

Acute respiratory failure is one of the most common reasons a ward patient triggers escalation, and distinguishing type 1 from type 2 respiratory failure is a core, heavily tested skill. Both are defined by arterial blood gas analysis, and both require a structured initial response rather than a single "one size fits all" oxygen prescription.

Type 1 Respiratory Failure

Type 1 respiratory failure is defined as hypoxaemia (PaO2 below 8 kPa) with a normal or low PaCO2. It reflects a problem with gas exchange at the alveolar-capillary interface — ventilation-perfusion mismatch or a diffusion defect — rather than a failure of ventilation itself. Common causes include pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, pulmonary embolism, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and pneumothorax. Because the problem is oxygenation rather than carbon dioxide clearance, the mainstay of initial management is supplemental oxygen titrated to achieve a target saturation of 94–98%, alongside treatment of the underlying cause. Escalation to critical care is warranted when hypoxaemia persists despite high-flow oxygen, when the patient is tiring, or when non-invasive or invasive ventilatory support is being considered — for example, continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) in cardiogenic pulmonary oedema, or invasive ventilation in severe ARDS.

Type 2 Respiratory Failure

Type 2 respiratory failure is defined as hypoxaemia (PaO2 below 8 kPa) together with hypercapnia (PaCO2 above 6 kPa). It reflects a failure of alveolar ventilation itself — the patient is not moving enough air to clear carbon dioxide — rather than (or in addition to) a problem with gas exchange. Common causes include an acute exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, severe asthma with exhaustion, opioid or sedative overdose, neuromuscular disease affecting the respiratory muscles, and chest wall deformity. The critical distinction in initial management is oxygen titration: uncontrolled high-flow oxygen in a patient with chronic type 2 respiratory failure risks worsening hypercapnia and precipitating a hypercapnic coma, so oxygen should be given via a controlled method (typically a Venturi mask delivering a precise fraction of inspired oxygen) with a target saturation of 88–92%, and an arterial blood gas repeated promptly to check the trend in PaCO2 and pH. If the pH is falling (indicating an acute, uncompensated respiratory acidosis) despite optimal medical therapy, non-invasive ventilation (typically bilevel positive airway pressure, BiPAP) is the next step, and this is a critical care or respiratory high-dependency unit decision that should prompt early referral rather than prolonged ward-based trial and error.

A useful way to remember the distinction for AKT purposes: type 1 respiratory failure is a "lung failure" pattern (the lungs cannot transfer oxygen efficiently, but the patient can still breathe fast enough to blow off carbon dioxide, and may even hyperventilate and have a low PaCO2), whereas type 2 respiratory failure is a "pump failure" pattern (the respiratory muscles, drive, or mechanics cannot sustain adequate ventilation, so carbon dioxide accumulates alongside hypoxaemia). Recognising which pattern is present changes the oxygen prescription, the urgency of specific interventions, and the threshold for escalating to critical care outreach or the medical registrar.

Acute Kidney Injury as a Critical-Care-Adjacent Problem

Acute kidney injury (AKI) deserves specific attention within any critical care UKMLA revision plan because it is both extremely common in deteriorating ward patients and frequently either the cause or the consequence of the same physiological insult that triggers a NEWS2 escalation — for example, a patient in hypovolaemic shock will typically also be in AKI, and recognising both processes together, rather than treating them as unrelated findings, is what the AKT is testing. AKI is defined as an abrupt deterioration in kidney function over hours to days, staged using the KDIGO criteria by the rise in serum creatinine and the fall in urine output. Stage 1 AKI is a creatinine rise to 1.5–1.9 times baseline, or a rise of 26.5 micromol/L or more within 48 hours, or a urine output below 0.5 mL/kg/hour for six to twelve hours. Stage 3, the most severe, involves a creatinine rise to three times baseline or a level above 354 micromol/L, or the initiation of renal replacement therapy, or a urine output below 0.3 mL/kg/hour for 24 hours or anuria for 12 hours. A full breakdown of AKI staging, pre-renal, intrinsic, and post-renal classification, and long-term CKD management sits in the site's dedicated nephrology revision guide, but the critical care perspective on AKI is worth summarising here because it is what the AKT tests in a deterioration context specifically.

Recognising and Initially Managing AKI on the Ward

The single most common cause of AKI in an acutely deteriorating ward patient is pre-renal — reduced renal perfusion secondary to hypovolaemia, sepsis-related vasodilation, or reduced cardiac output — and the single most important initial intervention is therefore the same ABCDE-driven fluid assessment described above, not a nephrology referral. Practical steps include: reviewing and, where possible, temporarily withholding nephrotoxic medications (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, and certain antibiotics such as gentamicin), ensuring adequate volume status with cautious fluid challenges guided by clinical response, catheterising to monitor hourly urine output accurately when AKI is significant, and checking for hyperkalaemia, which is the most immediately life-threatening complication of AKI and is discussed further below. Indications for urgent renal or critical care input include a failure to respond to fluid resuscitation, refractory hyperkalaemia, severe metabolic acidosis, fluid overload causing pulmonary oedema unresponsive to diuretics, or uraemic complications such as encephalopathy or pericarditis — any of these should prompt the same escalation pathway as a respiratory or circulatory emergency, using SBAR to a senior clinician and considering renal replacement therapy in the critical care setting.

Hyperkalaemia associated with AKI is managed with the same stepwise approach regardless of the underlying cause: intravenous calcium gluconate to stabilise the cardiac membrane where ECG changes are present (this does not lower the potassium level itself), an insulin-dextrose infusion and nebulised salbutamol to shift potassium intracellularly, and definitive removal of potassium via enhanced renal excretion, gut-binding agents, or dialysis in refractory cases. Any new ECG changes in a hyperkalaemic patient — peaked T waves progressing to a widened QRS complex — should be treated as a medical emergency and escalated immediately rather than awaiting a formal potassium result if the clinical suspicion is high.

Recognising and Managing Shock

Shock is defined as a state of circulatory failure resulting in inadequate tissue perfusion and oxygen delivery relative to metabolic demand. It is a final common pathway that can result from several distinct underlying mechanisms, and distinguishing between them is one of the highest-yield skills tested under the critical care UKMLA umbrella, because the correct initial treatment differs substantially between categories even though the end result — hypotension, tachycardia, and a rising NEWS2 score — can look superficially similar at the bedside.

Hypovolaemic Shock

Hypovolaemic shock results from a reduction in circulating volume, most commonly due to haemorrhage (gastrointestinal bleeding, trauma, ruptured aortic aneurysm, postpartum haemorrhage) or severe fluid losses (vomiting, diarrhoea, burns, inadequate intake). Clinically, patients are tachycardic, hypotensive (often a late sign, since compensatory vasoconstriction can preserve blood pressure until 30% or more of circulating volume is lost), cool and clammy peripherally, and have a prolonged capillary refill time. Initial management prioritises identifying and controlling the source of loss alongside volume replacement — crystalloid in the first instance, with blood products substituted early if haemorrhage is confirmed or strongly suspected, following a damage-control resuscitation approach that avoids over-diluting clotting factors.

Cardiogenic Shock

Cardiogenic shock results from the heart's failure to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's demands, most commonly following a large myocardial infarction, but also from arrhythmias, acute valvular failure, or myocarditis. Unlike hypovolaemic shock, patients are typically not fluid-depleted and may show signs of fluid overload — a raised jugular venous pressure, pulmonary crepitations, and peripheral oedema — alongside the same cool, poorly perfused peripheries and hypotension seen in other shock states. This is a crucial distinguishing feature: giving a large fluid bolus to a patient in cardiogenic shock, rather than to one who is hypovolaemic, can precipitate acute pulmonary oedema and rapid deterioration. Management centres on treating the underlying cause (urgent reperfusion for myocardial infarction, rhythm control for arrhythmia), judicious use of inotropes or vasopressors under critical care guidance, and in refractory cases, mechanical circulatory support.

Septic (and Other Distributive) Shock

Distributive shock arises from inappropriate vasodilation reducing systemic vascular resistance, and its most common cause on a general ward is sepsis — though the same haemodynamic pattern also occurs in neurogenic shock (spinal cord injury disrupting sympathetic tone) and, arguably, in anaphylaxis, which is often classified separately given its distinct trigger and treatment. Patients in early distributive shock are often warm and well-perfused peripherally despite hypotension — a "warm shock" pattern that can be misleadingly reassuring compared with the cold, clammy presentation of hypovolaemic or cardiogenic shock. As deterioration progresses, peripheral vasoconstriction can supervene and the patient may appear cold and mottled instead. Since this article deliberately does not duplicate the dedicated sepsis-specific care bundle, readers managing a patient in suspected septic shock should refer to this site's separate emergency medicine content for the full Sepsis Six pathway; the point relevant to this general framework is simply that recognising the distributive pattern — hypotension with a disproportionately warm periphery, often with a source of infection or another precipitant identifiable — should prompt the same urgent escalation and fluid-plus-vasopressor approach as any other form of shock, adapted to the specific cause.

Anaphylactic Shock

Anaphylactic shock is a severe, rapidly progressive form of distributive shock triggered by a systemic hypersensitivity reaction, typically to drugs, foods, insect stings, or latex. It is distinguished clinically by its speed of onset (minutes to a few hours after exposure) and its accompanying features in other organ systems — urticaria, angioedema, wheeze, and stridor alongside the cardiovascular collapse. Because the treatment is so specific and time-critical, anaphylaxis is managed differently from other distributive shock: intramuscular adrenaline into the anterolateral thigh is the immediate first-line treatment, repeated every five minutes if there is no improvement, given before, not after, antihistamines or steroids, which are adjuncts only. High-flow oxygen and an intravenous fluid bolus support the adrenaline. Any patient recovering from anaphylaxis should be observed for a minimum of six to twelve hours given the risk of a biphasic reaction.

Ceiling of Care and Treatment Escalation Plans

A ceiling of care, sometimes documented as a treatment escalation plan (TEP), is an agreed, documented limit on the level of intervention that is appropriate for a specific patient, based on their underlying condition, prognosis, comorbidities, frailty, and — crucially — their own wishes and values. It is not a single blanket decision but a spectrum: a ceiling of care might specify that a patient should receive full ward-based medical treatment but should not be considered for invasive ventilation or cardiopulmonary resuscitation, or it might specify that critical care admission is entirely appropriate if organ support is needed. The UKMLA tests this concept because recognising a deteriorating patient and knowing how to escalate is only useful if it is combined with an honest assessment of whether escalation is actually in that patient's best interests.

When and How to Establish a Ceiling of Care

Ideally, a ceiling of care or treatment escalation plan is discussed and documented proactively, at or soon after admission, for any patient in whom deterioration and the need for critical care input is a foreseeable possibility — frail older patients, those with advanced malignancy, severe chronic organ failure, or multiple significant comorbidities. In practice, it is often only considered reactively, at the point of acute deterioration, which is far from ideal since a rushed conversation during a crisis is more likely to be incomplete and more distressing for the patient and family. Good practice, and the standard the AKT expects candidates to recognise, is early, honest, and collaborative discussion: explaining realistically what interventions such as invasive ventilation, renal replacement therapy, or cardiopulmonary resuscitation would involve and what their likely outcome would be for this specific patient, then documenting the agreed plan clearly so that it is immediately available to any clinician assessing the patient overnight or at a weekend, when the usual team may not be present.

A ceiling of care is not a decision to withhold all treatment, and this distinction is worth stating explicitly because it is a common misconception both in clinical practice and in exam questions: a patient with a ceiling of care that excludes critical care admission should still receive full symptomatic treatment, appropriate ward-based interventions such as oxygen, antibiotics, and analgesia, and dignified, attentive care — the ceiling defines the upper limit of escalation, not the withdrawal of everything below it. Equally, a ceiling of care is not necessarily permanent: it should be reviewed if the patient's condition or prognosis changes, and it should always be discussed with the patient (where they have capacity) or an appropriate surrogate decision-maker, respecting the same principles of shared decision-making that apply throughout Good Medical Practice.

When to Involve Critical Care Outreach Early

The unifying message across every element of this guide is that involving critical care outreach, or a senior clinician more broadly, should happen earlier rather than later. Waiting until a patient meets a hard NEWS2 threshold of 7 before making the first phone call misses the window in which the least invasive interventions are most likely to be effective. Appropriate triggers for early outreach involvement include: a NEWS2 score trending upward even if the absolute value remains moderate, any single parameter scoring 3 (a "red score"), new confusion in a previously alert patient, a junior doctor's own clinical concern even where the numbers do not yet mandate review (a factor NEWS2 explicitly does not capture but which is consistently shown to predict deterioration), and any patient in whom a ceiling of care discussion has not yet happened but clearly should, given their trajectory. This is precisely the kind of judgement-based decision-making, layered on top of the numerical framework, that distinguishes a strong AKT candidate from one who can only recite the NEWS2 thresholds without understanding what they are for.

Frequently Asked Questions About Critical Care in the UKMLA

What NEWS2 score should trigger a call to critical care outreach?

A NEWS2 score of 5 or 6, or any single physiological parameter scoring 3 in isolation, should trigger an urgent clinical review, typically within 30 minutes, with outreach involvement considered at this stage. A score of 7 or above is a medical emergency requiring emergency assessment, usually including a member of the critical care outreach team, and continuous monitoring thereafter. Trends matter as much as the absolute number — a rising score, or a clinician's own concern, can justify contacting outreach before the formal threshold is reached.

How do I tell the difference between type 1 and type 2 respiratory failure at the bedside?

Type 1 respiratory failure is hypoxaemia (PaO2 below 8 kPa) with a normal or low PaCO2, typically seen in pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or pulmonary embolism, and is treated with oxygen titrated to 94–98%. Type 2 respiratory failure is hypoxaemia with hypercapnia (PaCO2 above 6 kPa), typically seen in COPD exacerbations or opioid overdose, and requires controlled oxygen titrated to 88–92% because uncontrolled high-flow oxygen can worsen carbon dioxide retention. An arterial blood gas is required to confirm which pattern is present, since the clinical picture alone is not always reliable.

Why does the type of shock matter if the initial treatment is always fluids?

The initial treatment is not always fluids, and this is precisely why distinguishing the type of shock matters. A patient in cardiogenic shock who is already fluid-overloaded can deteriorate rapidly if given a large fluid bolus, whereas the same bolus is exactly the right first step in hypovolaemic or early septic shock. Recognising features such as a raised jugular venous pressure and pulmonary crepitations (favouring cardiogenic shock) versus a low JVP and cool peripheries (favouring hypovolaemia) changes the immediate management plan, not just the eventual diagnosis.

What is the difference between a ceiling of care and a "do not attempt resuscitation" decision?

A "do not attempt cardiopulmonary resuscitation" (DNACPR) decision addresses only whether CPR should be attempted in the event of cardiac arrest. A ceiling of care, or treatment escalation plan, is broader and addresses the full range of possible escalations — critical care admission, invasive ventilation, renal replacement therapy, and inotropic support — and DNACPR status is typically just one component of a wider ceiling-of-care discussion. A patient can have a DNACPR decision in place while still being appropriate for other forms of active treatment, including critical care admission, depending on their individual circumstances.

Is acute kidney injury always caused by the same problem that is causing the patient's shock?

Not always, but there is frequently a shared underlying cause, since AKI is most commonly pre-renal in origin — driven by reduced kidney perfusion from the same hypovolaemia, sepsis, or cardiac dysfunction that is producing the wider shock picture. Correcting the circulatory problem using the same ABCDE-driven approach is therefore usually the first and most effective step in managing the AKI as well, rather than treating the kidney injury as a separate, isolated problem requiring its own distinct workup before circulation has been addressed.

How is SBAR different from just describing the patient to a senior colleague?

SBAR imposes a specific structure — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — that ensures nothing critical is omitted and that the call ends with a clear, actionable request rather than trailing off uncertainly. Unstructured handovers are well documented to omit key safety information under the pressure of an acute situation, particularly the "Recommendation" element, leaving the senior colleague unsure what is actually being asked of them. Using SBAR consistently, even for calls that feel informal, builds a habit that becomes reliable under genuine pressure.

Do international medical graduates need to know NEWS2 specifically, or is it a UK-only system?

NEWS2 is a UK-specific track-and-trigger system, developed and maintained by the Royal College of Physicians, and it is used as the standard early warning score across NHS hospitals in England, Wales, and Scotland (Scotland has since adopted its own related system in some settings, but the underlying principle is the same). IMGs preparing for the AKT should learn NEWS2 specifically, including its exact scoring bands and escalation thresholds, rather than relying on an equivalent scoring system used in their home country, since the exam tests knowledge of the UK system as it will be used in day-to-day NHS practice.

Conclusion

Critical care UKMLA questions reward candidates who apply a consistent structure rather than searching for a single diagnosis first: ABCDE assessment, an accurate NEWS2 score, SBAR communication that ends in a clear request, correct pattern recognition for respiratory failure and shock, and an honest, ongoing conversation about each patient's ceiling of care. Mastering this framework transfers directly to safe clinical practice as a newly qualified doctor, which is exactly why the GMC has built it so firmly into the AKT blueprint.
