Quick Answer: Gynaecological Cancers and Referral in the UKMLA

The core message the UKMLA tests across gynaecological cancers is this: postmenopausal bleeding means suspected endometrial cancer until proven otherwise, persistent bloating with early satiety means suspected ovarian cancer, abnormal cervical bleeding or an abnormal-looking cervix means suspected cervical cancer, and a vulval lump, ulcer or unexplained itch that persists means suspected vulval cancer. Each of these red flags triggers a specific referral pathway defined by NICE's suspected cancer referral guideline (NG12), most commonly the two-week-wait (2WW) suspected cancer pathway.

Endometrial Cancer: Postmenopausal Bleeding as the Classic Red Flag

Endometrial cancer is the most common gynaecological malignancy in the UK, and among gynaecological cancers UKMLA questions test it overwhelmingly through one symptom: postmenopausal bleeding (PMB). Any vaginal bleeding occurring twelve months or more after the last menstrual period is defined as postmenopausal bleeding, and around 90% of women with endometrial cancer present this way, which is precisely why the exam treats PMB as a red flag that cannot be dismissed without investigation.

Risk Factors and the Unopposed Oestrogen Model

Endometrial cancer is fundamentally an oestrogen-driven disease. Understanding this single mechanism — unopposed oestrogen stimulating endometrial proliferation without the protective effect of progesterone — explains almost every risk factor tested on the AKT. Obesity is the single biggest modifiable risk factor, because adipose tissue converts androgens to oestrogen via aromatase, creating a chronic unopposed-oestrogen state. Other risk factors that fit the same pattern include nulliparity, early menarche, late menopause, polycystic ovary syndrome (chronic anovulation means no progesterone-secreting corpus luteum), tamoxifen use (which has a paradoxical oestrogenic effect on the endometrium despite its anti-oestrogenic action in breast tissue — an example of the kind of drug-safety reasoning covered in the site's guide to recognising prescribing errors and medication safety), and oestrogen-only hormone replacement therapy given to a woman with an intact uterus. Type 2 diabetes and hypertension are also independently associated, largely because they cluster with obesity in metabolic syndrome.

Lynch syndrome (hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer) is the most important genetic risk factor the UKMLA expects candidates to recognise, since it confers up to a 60% lifetime risk of endometrial cancer alongside colorectal cancer, and women with a known or suspected Lynch syndrome diagnosis are offered surveillance or risk-reducing surgery outside the standard symptomatic referral pathway. Conversely, the combined oral contraceptive pill and multiparity are protective, because both increase progesterone exposure relative to oestrogen.

The Two-Week-Wait Referral Pathway for Endometrial Cancer

NICE NG12 is unambiguous about who needs urgent referral. Any woman aged 55 or over with postmenopausal bleeding should be referred using a suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW) for endometrial cancer. For women under 55 with postmenopausal bleeding, clinical judgement is used, but the threshold for referral remains low given the same underlying risk. NICE also recommends considering a direct-access transvaginal ultrasound scan for women aged 55 or over with unexplained symptoms of vaginal discharge, or with visible haematuria plus low haemoglobin, thrombocytosis or raised glucose, since these constellations can also indicate endometrial cancer even without overt bleeding.

The first-line investigation is a transvaginal ultrasound scan (TVUSS) to measure endometrial thickness. An endometrial thickness greater than 4 mm in a postmenopausal woman is considered abnormal and warrants further investigation with hysteroscopy and endometrial biopsy (pipelle sampling), since ultrasound alone cannot distinguish benign endometrial hyperplasia from malignancy. A thin endometrium (4 mm or less) has a high negative predictive value for excluding cancer, which is why it is used as the triage step before committing every woman to an invasive biopsy. Hysteroscopy allows direct visualisation of the endometrial cavity and targeted biopsy of any suspicious area, and is considered the gold-standard diagnostic test when ultrasound findings are equivocal or abnormal.

Staging follows the FIGO system and is primarily surgical: total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (TAH+BSO) is both the staging procedure and the definitive treatment for early-stage disease, often combined with pelvic lymph node assessment — the perioperative considerations around major gynaecological surgery are explored further in the site's guide to recognising surgical presentations. Adjuvant radiotherapy is added for higher-risk histology or more advanced stage, and chemotherapy is reserved for advanced or recurrent disease. While the UKMLA does not expect detailed oncological management, it does expect candidates to know that hysterectomy is the mainstay of treatment and that early diagnosis via the PMB red flag is directly linked to the excellent prognosis endometrial cancer carries when caught at stage I.

Because postmenopausal bleeding sits at the intersection of gynaecological oncology and the menopause transition itself, candidates should also be comfortable distinguishing pathological PMB from the irregular bleeding patterns that can occur around perimenopause — a distinction covered in more depth in the site's dedicated guide to managing the transition through menopause, which explains how hormone replacement therapy regimens themselves can cause bleeding that must still be worked up if it falls outside the expected pattern.

Ovarian Cancer: The "Silent" Cancer and the Role of CA-125

Ovarian cancer earns its reputation as a "silent" cancer because its symptoms are vague, common in the general population, and easily misattributed to irritable bowel syndrome, urinary tract infection, or simple ageing — which is exactly why the majority of cases are diagnosed at an advanced stage. Among gynaecological cancers UKMLA question writers test especially heavily, ovarian cancer stands out for this reason: candidates are expected to recognise that persistent, frequent, non-specific symptoms in a woman over 50 should trigger investigation for ovarian cancer rather than reassurance.

Recognising the Vague Symptom Cluster

NICE NG12 lists the symptoms that should prompt suspicion: persistent abdominal distension ("bloating"), feeling full quickly and/or loss of appetite (early satiety), pelvic or abdominal pain, and increased urinary urgency and/or frequency. The critical exam detail is frequency and persistence: these symptoms should raise suspicion particularly if they occur more than 12 times per month, especially in a woman aged 50 or over, since this is the threshold NICE uses to separate cancer-worthy symptoms from everyday gastrointestinal or urinary complaints. Other features that should prompt consideration of ovarian cancer include unexplained weight loss, fatigue, or changes in bowel habit, and any woman presenting with irritable bowel syndrome-type symptoms who is aged 50 or over should have ovarian cancer considered, because new-onset IBS after age 50 is unusual and ovarian cancer is a recognised mimic.

Risk factors follow the "incessant ovulation" hypothesis in the same way endometrial cancer follows the unopposed-oestrogen model: anything that increases the total number of ovulatory cycles across a lifetime increases risk. This includes early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, and not having used the combined oral contraceptive pill, while pregnancy, breastfeeding and the COCP are protective by suppressing ovulation. BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations are the most important genetic risk factors tested on the AKT, conferring a substantially elevated lifetime risk of both ovarian and breast cancer, and women with a strong family history are offered genetic counselling and, in confirmed mutation carriers, risk-reducing bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy once childbearing is complete.

CA-125 and the Risk of Malignancy Index

CA-125 is a tumour marker glycoprotein that is the first-line blood test NICE recommends for any woman with symptoms suggestive of ovarian cancer. It is important for candidates to understand what CA-125 is and is not: it is not a screening test for the general population, because it lacks the specificity required — CA-125 can be elevated by endometriosis, fibroids, pelvic inflammatory disease, menstruation, pregnancy, and even benign liver disease, meaning a raised result does not confirm cancer any more than a normal result excludes it. Its role in the UKMLA is specifically as the first investigation in a symptomatic woman, not as a population screening tool, which is a distinction the exam frequently probes.

If CA-125 is 35 IU/mL or greater, NICE recommends arranging an ultrasound scan of the abdomen and pelvis. If the ultrasound suggests ovarian cancer, the patient should be referred urgently on the suspected cancer pathway. This two-step process — CA-125 first, then imaging if raised, then urgent referral if imaging is abnormal — is the exact sequence the AKT expects candidates to reproduce when presented with a vignette describing bloating and early satiety in a postmenopausal woman.

The Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) is the next layer of risk stratification used once a woman reaches secondary care with a pelvic mass, and the UKMLA expects candidates to know the concept even if the exact multiplication is rarely tested numerically. RMI combines three components: the serum CA-125 level, the ultrasound score (based on features such as multilocular cysts, solid areas, bilateral lesions, ascites, and intra-abdominal metastases), and the menopausal status of the patient (a fixed multiplier, higher for postmenopausal women, since the pre-test probability of malignancy in a pelvic mass is higher after the menopause). The three values are multiplied together to give a composite score; a high RMI score indicates a mass that should be managed in a specialist gynaecological oncology multidisciplinary team setting rather than by a general gynaecologist, since ovarian cancer surgery — full staging laparotomy, omentectomy, lymph node assessment — is best performed by a specialist surgeon in one operation rather than needing repeat surgery. The core exam point is conceptual: RMI is a triage tool that decides which service a patient's pelvic mass is managed in, weighting the biochemical marker, the imaging appearance and the menopausal status together rather than relying on any single test in isolation.

Ovarian cancer's poor overall prognosis compared with endometrial and cervical cancer is a direct consequence of this "silent" presentation: without a reliable population screening test and with symptoms that mimic common benign conditions, the majority of cases are diagnosed at stage III or IV, when the tumour has already spread beyond the ovary. This is precisely why the vague-symptom-recognition skill is so heavily weighted in AKT questions — it is the one modifiable point in the pathway where earlier recognition genuinely changes outcomes.

Cervical Cancer: Screening, HPV, and Primary HPV Testing

Cervical cancer differs fundamentally from endometrial and ovarian cancer in one important respect: it has a well-established, effective, population-wide screening programme, and gynaecological cancers UKMLA content tests the mechanics of that programme just as heavily as it tests the red-flag symptoms of established disease.

HPV as the Causative Agent

Human papillomavirus (HPV) is the necessary cause of essentially all cervical cancer, and understanding this causative relationship is the foundation for every other fact tested about cervical cancer on the AKT. High-risk HPV types, particularly types 16 and 18, are responsible for around 70% of cervical cancers between them, with other high-risk types (including 31, 33, 45, 52 and 58) accounting for most of the remainder. HPV is transmitted through sexual contact and is extremely common — most sexually active people will acquire some HPV type during their lifetime — but the vast majority of infections are cleared by the immune system within one to two years without ever causing abnormal cells. Cervical cancer develops only when a high-risk HPV infection persists for years, allowing progression through the stages of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) to invasive cancer, which is why the screening programme is designed around detecting persistent HPV infection and pre-cancerous change long before invasive cancer develops.

The UK's HPV vaccination programme, offered to boys and girls at age 12 to 13 (school year 8) as part of the national childhood immunisation schedule, protects against the high-risk types responsible for the majority of cervical cancers as well as against the low-risk types (6 and 11) that cause genital warts. Candidates should understand that vaccination reduces but does not eliminate cervical cancer risk, which is why vaccinated women still require cervical screening.

The NHS Cervical Screening Programme and the Shift to Primary HPV Testing

The NHS cervical screening programme invites women and people with a cervix for a smear test at defined intervals: every three years for those aged 25 to 49, and every five years for those aged 50 to 64. This age-and-interval structure is a frequently tested fact in its own right, since candidates are expected to know both the starting age (25, not lower, because cervical cancer is rare below this age and overtreatment of transient HPV-related changes in young women carries its own harms) and the point at which the interval lengthens.

England moved from primary cytology screening to primary HPV testing in 2019, and this shift is one of the most important recent changes in the field for exam purposes. Under the older cytology-first model, every sample was examined under a microscope for abnormal cells first, with HPV testing used only as a triage step in specific circumstances. Under primary HPV testing, every sample is tested for high-risk HPV first; cytology (looking at the cells themselves) is only performed as a second step if HPV is detected. This reordering matters because HPV testing is significantly more sensitive at identifying women who are genuinely at risk, and because most cervical cell abnormalities occur in the context of an HPV infection, testing for the virus first allows far more women with no oncogenic virus present to be safely returned to routine recall without ever needing their cells examined.

The management pathway that follows primary HPV testing has a clear logic that the AKT expects candidates to reproduce: if HPV is not detected, the woman returns to routine recall at the standard interval, since her risk of cervical cancer in the near term is very low. If HPV is detected but the cytology is normal, the sample is repeated in twelve months rather than referring immediately, because the majority of HPV infections clear spontaneously within a year. If HPV is detected and cytology is abnormal, the woman is referred for colposcopy, where the cervix is examined under magnification and biopsies are taken from any visibly abnormal area. Any high-grade cytological abnormality is referred for colposcopy regardless of HPV status, since high-grade changes carry sufficient risk in their own right.

Beyond the screening pathway, the AKT also tests recognition of symptomatic cervical cancer, since screening only identifies pre-invasive and early asymptomatic disease — women who present with symptoms need the same red-flag recognition and referral reasoning used throughout this guide. Postcoital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, and postmenopausal bleeding are all red flags for cervical cancer, and any woman with these symptoms combined with an abnormal-looking cervix on examination should be referred using a suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW) for cervical cancer, irrespective of screening history or a recent normal smear result — a normal cytology result does not exclude cervical cancer in a symptomatic woman, because symptoms can arise from an invasive lesion that develops between screening rounds. Speculum examination revealing an unusual cervical appearance — ulceration, friability, or an obvious mass — is itself sufficient grounds for urgent referral even without confirmed bleeding.

This screening-and-referral logic connects directly to the broader statistical reasoning the UKMLA expects candidates to apply to any national screening programme — understanding sensitivity, specificity, and the difference between a screening test and a diagnostic test is covered in detail in the site's guide to interpreting diagnostic test statistics and evidence-based medicine, which explains exactly why CA-125 and cervical cytology are validated differently depending on whether they are being used to screen an asymptomatic population or to investigate a symptomatic patient.

Vulval Cancer: Red Flags Often Missed

Vulval cancer is far less common than endometrial, ovarian or cervical cancer, but it remains part of gynaecological cancers UKMLA revision because its red flags are frequently missed or attributed to benign causes such as thrush, eczema, or lichen sclerosus for months before a correct diagnosis is made — precisely the kind of diagnostic delay the exam wants candidates to prevent.

The most common red flag is a persistent vulval lump, ulcer or wart-like growth, particularly on the labia majora, that does not resolve with simple treatment. Persistent vulval itching (pruritus vulvae) or bleeding that cannot be explained by an obvious benign cause is equally significant, especially in a postmenopausal woman. Vulval skin changes — including colour change, thickening or plaque formation — should also raise suspicion, particularly when they occur in a woman with a background of vulval lichen sclerosus, since a small but clinically important proportion of women with untreated or poorly controlled lichen sclerosus go on to develop vulval squamous cell carcinoma, making adequate long-term management and follow-up of lichen sclerosus itself a form of cancer prevention.

NICE NG12 recommends an urgent suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW) for vulval cancer in any woman with an unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, or bleeding. Because the vulva is an external, visible and self-examinable site, delay in presentation is often the limiting factor rather than delay in referral once a patient does seek review — many women feel embarrassed discussing vulval symptoms, which the UKMLA implicitly tests through communication-focused CPSA-style scenarios as much as through pure red-flag recall. Diagnosis is confirmed by biopsy of the lesion, and management typically involves surgical excision (wide local excision or, for more extensive disease, vulvectomy), with groin lymph node assessment for invasive lesions given the vulva's rich inguinal lymphatic drainage. As with the other gynaecological cancers covered in this guide, the exam's real interest is not in surgical technique but in whether the candidate recognises the red flag quickly enough to trigger the referral in the first place.

Bringing It Together: How the AKT Actually Tests This Topic

Having covered each cancer individually, it is worth stepping back and describing the exact question format candidates should expect, since gynaecological cancers UKMLA questions rarely ask "what is the pathophysiology of endometrial cancer" in isolation — they present a short clinical vignette and ask for the single best next step.

A typical single-best-answer question describes a 58-year-old woman with one episode of light vaginal bleeding eight months after her last period, and asks for the most appropriate next step; the correct answer is a 2WW referral for suspected endometrial cancer, not reassurance, not "repeat in three months," and not starting HRT to see if bleeding resolves. Another common format describes a 54-year-old woman with three months of bloating, early satiety and increased urinary frequency occurring most days, and asks for the most appropriate initial investigation; the correct answer is CA-125, not a pelvic ultrasound as the very first step and not straight referral without any biochemical triage. A third pattern presents a woman with postcoital bleeding and a normal smear result twelve months ago, testing whether candidates incorrectly assume a recent normal screening result rules out current disease — it does not, and examination followed by referral if the cervix looks abnormal remains the correct approach.

Recognising these patterns is more valuable for exam preparation than memorising oncological staging systems, chemotherapy regimens or detailed surgical technique — a principle worth applying across the whole of UKMLA preparation, not just this topic — since none of these details sit within the expected knowledge base of a newly qualified doctor and none are heavily represented in the UKMLA content map's gynaecological oncology section. The exam is deliberately calibrated to what a foundation doctor needs to do safely: recognise the red flag, select the correct initial test if one exists, and route the patient onto the correct referral pathway without unnecessary delay.

It is also worth remembering that gynaecological cancer questions frequently overlap with early pregnancy and general women's health content, since abnormal bleeding is a symptom shared across many different conditions and ages. Distinguishing bleeding that requires cancer referral from bleeding that reflects a benign or pregnancy-related cause — covered in the site's guide to recognising and managing early pregnancy complications — is itself a tested skill, since a postmenopausal woman and a woman of reproductive age presenting with vaginal bleeding are worked up along entirely different pathways, and choosing the wrong pathway for the wrong age group is a common distractor built into AKT questions.

Finally, no discussion of gynaecological cancer red flags is complete without situating it inside the wider obstetrics and gynaecology curriculum. This guide deliberately narrows in on cancer red flags and referral pathways in isolation, but candidates should also be comfortable with the acute obstetric emergencies, contraception, and non-malignant gynaecological conditions covered in the site's broader obstetrics and gynaecology revision guide, since the AKT blends questions across the whole speciality rather than testing cancer topics in isolation.

Preparing for Gynaecological Cancers UKMLA Questions: UK Students vs IMGs

Although the underlying NICE guidance is identical for every candidate, UK medical students and IMGs typically arrive at gynaecological cancers UKMLA revision from different starting points, and it is worth addressing each explicitly rather than assuming one route through the material suits everyone.

UK medical students usually reach this topic having already sat through clinical placements in gynaecology and women's health, so the individual facts — endometrial thickness cut-offs, CA-125 thresholds, the cervical screening interval — are often already familiar in isolation. Where UK students most commonly lose marks is in the exam-specific framing: the AKT does not ask "describe the management of ovarian cancer," it presents a vignette and expects a single best next step chosen instantly from several plausible-sounding distractors. The most effective revision strategy for UK students is therefore not re-reading textbook chapters on gynaecological oncology, but working through single-best-answer questions repeatedly until the red-flag-to-referral mapping becomes close to automatic, then cross-checking any missed question directly against the relevant NICE NG12 criterion rather than a secondary source, since minor wording differences between question banks and the actual guideline are a common source of confusion.

IMGs preparing for the UKMLA often bring strong clinical knowledge of gynaecological cancer from training systems where diagnostic pathways, referral timeframes, or first-line investigations differ meaningfully from the NHS. A doctor trained in a system where a pelvic mass would routinely go straight to CT imaging, for example, needs to consciously relearn that the NHS pathway for a suspicious pelvic mass in primary care starts with CA-125 and pelvic ultrasound, with CT reserved for staging once malignancy is already suspected. Similarly, IMGs from countries where cervical screening is opportunistic rather than a structured national call-and-recall programme need to learn the specific UK age bands and intervals as testable facts in their own right, not just general principles. Because gynaecological cancers UKMLA questions are written against UK NICE guidance specifically, IMGs should treat this topic as one where prior clinical experience needs to be explicitly cross-checked against NG12 rather than assumed to transfer directly, and should pay particular attention to referral timeframes (2WW), which are an NHS administrative construct that may not have a direct equivalent in the health system they trained in.

Both groups benefit from the same core discipline: for every gynaecological cancer red flag encountered in practice questions, explicitly name the referral pathway out loud (2WW suspected cancer referral, or routine/non-urgent where appropriate) rather than leaving the answer as an implied clinical impression. This habit converts passive recognition of a red flag into the active decision-making skill the AKT is actually testing.

Frequently Asked Questions About Gynaecological Cancers in the UKMLA

What is the most important red flag for endometrial cancer in the UKMLA?

Postmenopausal bleeding is the single most important red flag for endometrial cancer, present in around 90% of cases at diagnosis. Any woman aged 55 or over with postmenopausal bleeding should be referred using a suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW), and a transvaginal ultrasound is the first-line investigation to measure endometrial thickness. A thickness greater than 4 mm is considered abnormal and warrants hysteroscopy with biopsy.

Why is ovarian cancer called a silent cancer?

Ovarian cancer is described as a silent cancer because its early symptoms — bloating, early satiety, pelvic pain and urinary frequency — are vague and common in the general population, so they are often misattributed to benign conditions until the disease has progressed. There is also no validated population screening test for ovarian cancer, unlike cervical cancer, meaning diagnosis relies almost entirely on symptom recognition and appropriate investigation once a patient presents.

What does a CA-125 result of 35 IU/mL or above mean?

A CA-125 level of 35 IU/mL or above in a symptomatic woman is the threshold NICE uses to trigger the next investigative step, which is an ultrasound scan of the abdomen and pelvis. CA-125 is not diagnostic on its own, since it can be raised by benign conditions such as endometriosis, fibroids and pelvic infection, so a raised result prompts imaging rather than an immediate cancer diagnosis. If the ultrasound findings suggest malignancy, the patient is then referred urgently on the suspected cancer pathway.

What is the Risk of Malignancy Index used for?

The Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) is a scoring system that combines a woman's CA-125 level, her ultrasound findings, and her menopausal status to estimate how likely a pelvic mass is to be malignant. A high RMI score means the mass should be managed by a specialist gynaecological oncology multidisciplinary team rather than a general gynaecologist, since ovarian cancer surgery is best performed as a single definitive operation by an appropriately trained surgeon. It functions as a triage tool rather than a diagnostic test.

Why did cervical screening move to primary HPV testing?

England moved to primary HPV testing in 2019 because testing for high-risk human papillomavirus first is more sensitive at identifying women genuinely at risk of developing cervical abnormalities than examining cell samples under a microscope first. Since virtually all cervical cancer is caused by persistent high-risk HPV infection, testing for the virus allows women with no HPV detected to safely return to routine recall, while cytology is reserved as a second step only for samples where HPV is found.

Does a normal smear result rule out cervical cancer if a woman later develops symptoms?

No. A recent normal cervical screening result does not exclude cervical cancer in a woman who subsequently develops symptoms such as postcoital, intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding. Any woman with these red-flag symptoms and an abnormal-looking cervix on speculum examination should still be referred using a suspected cancer pathway referral (2WW), regardless of her screening history, because symptomatic disease can develop between screening rounds.

What vulval symptoms should prompt an urgent cancer referral?

An unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, persistent bleeding, or a persistent unexplained skin change should prompt an urgent 2WW suspected cancer pathway referral for vulval cancer. Suspicion should be particularly high in a woman with a background of vulval lichen sclerosus, since a proportion of untreated cases progress to squamous cell carcinoma, and any new or changing lesion should be examined and biopsied rather than treated empirically for a prolonged period.

Conclusion

Gynaecological cancers UKMLA revision, in the end, rewards a consistent, learnable pattern: recognise the red flag, apply the correct initial test where one exists, and select the appropriate NICE NG12 referral pathway without delay. Postmenopausal bleeding for endometrial cancer, persistent vague symptoms plus CA-125 and the Risk of Malignancy Index for ovarian cancer, abnormal bleeding or cervix appearance layered on top of the HPV-driven cervical screening programme for cervical cancer, and persistent vulval lumps, ulcers or skin change for vulval cancer together cover the overwhelming majority of exam scenarios in this area. Mastering this red-flag-to-referral logic, rather than oncological staging or treatment detail, is what turns gynaecological cancer questions from a weak area into a reliably scoring one for both UK medical students and IMGs alike.
