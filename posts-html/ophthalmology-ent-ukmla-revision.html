Ophthalmology and ENT for the UKMLA AKT: Complete High-Yield Guide

Ophthalmology and ENT questions appear regularly in the UKMLA Applied Knowledge Test (AKT). While these specialties can feel peripheral during medical school, the AKT tests a defined core of high-yield presentations — red eye differentials, acute visual loss, chronic eye conditions, and the full spectrum of ear, nose, and throat emergencies. This guide walks through every high-priority topic systematically, with the clinical pearls and management principles you need to score well.

Part 1: The Red Eye — A Systematic Approach

The red eye is one of the most classic AKT scenarios. The key discriminators are: the pattern of redness, the nature of discharge, pupil shape and reactivity, visual acuity, and pain character. Always ask yourself: is this an emergency requiring same-day ophthalmology referral, or can it be managed in primary care?

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

This is the most important red eye emergency to recognise. Aqueous humour cannot drain because the iris physically occludes the trabecular meshwork at the angle of the anterior chamber. Intraocular pressure (IOP) rises rapidly, causing ischaemic damage to the optic nerve.

Classic presentation: Middle-aged to older patient (more common in women, hypermetropes, and those of East Asian descent) who develops sudden severe unilateral eye pain, headache, nausea, and vomiting. They describe seeing haloes or rainbows around lights — caused by corneal oedema. On examination: conjunctival injection, corneal clouding, a fixed mid-dilated oval pupil, and a rock-hard eye on gentle palpation. IOP is typically well above 21 mmHg (normal <21 mmHg).

Precipitants: Dim light (pupil dilates, iris bunches up), anticholinergic drugs (tropicamide eye drops, tricyclic antidepressants), adrenergic drugs — anything causing pupillary dilation in a susceptible anatomy.

Emergency management: This is an ophthalmic emergency. Give intravenous acetazolamide 500 mg (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor — reduces aqueous production), topical pilocarpine (constricts pupil to pull iris away from angle), topical beta-blocker (timolol) and alpha-agonist (apraclonidine). Lay the patient supine. Call ophthalmology immediately — definitive treatment is laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI), which creates a hole in the iris to allow aqueous to bypass the pupillary block.

Anterior Uveitis (Iritis)

Inflammation of the uveal tract — specifically the iris and ciliary body (anterior uveitis). This is the most common form of uveitis seen in clinical practice.

Presentation: Unilateral aching eye pain, photophobia (consensual photophobia — shining a light in the fellow eye also causes pain in the affected eye), blurred vision, and a ciliary flush (perilimbal redness, deepest at the limbus). The pupil is small and irregular due to posterior synechiae — adhesions between the iris and lens — a complication of recurrent or inadequately treated uveitis. Slit-lamp examination shows cells and flare in the anterior chamber, and keratic precipitates (KPs) on the corneal endothelium.

Associations: HLA-B27-associated conditions are the major association — ankylosing spondylitis (most common), reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, and inflammatory bowel disease. Other causes include sarcoidosis, Behcet's disease, and herpes viruses.

Management: Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone acetate) to suppress inflammation, and cyclopentolate (a cycloplegic/mydriatic) to dilate the pupil — this relieves spasm of the ciliary muscle (reducing pain) and prevents/breaks posterior synechiae.

Conjunctivitis — Distinguishing Types

Bacterial conjunctivitis is caused most commonly by Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, or Haemophilus influenzae. The defining feature is mucopurulent discharge — thick, yellow-green, causing lids to be stuck together on waking. It is typically bilateral (or starts unilateral and spreads). Treatment is topical chloramphenicol (drops or ointment). In neonates, consider gonococcal or chlamydial infection — a medical emergency requiring systemic antibiotics.

Viral conjunctivitis is usually caused by adenovirus and is the most common cause of infectious conjunctivitis overall. Watery discharge, often following an upper respiratory tract infection, frequently affects both eyes, and may be associated with preauricular lymphadenopathy. It is self-limiting — no antibiotic drops are needed. Highly contagious — advise hand hygiene and avoiding sharing towels.

Allergic conjunctivitis presents with bilateral itching as the dominant and defining symptom, watery discharge, and chemosis (conjunctival oedema). It is seasonal (tree/grass pollen) or perennial (house dust mite, animal dander). Treatment: avoid trigger, cold compresses, topical antihistamines (azelastine, olopatadine) or sodium cromoglicate (mast cell stabiliser — needs to be started before the season).

Episcleritis vs Scleritis

Episcleritis is inflammation of the episclera (the superficial layer between the conjunctiva and sclera). It appears as a sector of bright red redness that moves with the conjunctiva when you push on it gently. It causes mild discomfort rather than severe pain. Crucially, it blanches with 10% phenylephrine drops — differentiating it from scleritis. It is usually idiopathic and self-limiting.

Scleritis is far more serious. The redness is deeper, with a characteristic violaceous (blue-purple) hue. Pain is severe, boring, constant, and may wake the patient from sleep — it does not blanch with phenylephrine. It is strongly associated with systemic inflammatory disease — rheumatoid arthritis, granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), relapsing polychondritis, and lupus. Necrotising scleritis can cause scleral thinning and perforation (scleromalacia perforans in RA). Requires urgent ophthalmology referral and systemic immunosuppression.

Part 2: Acute Visual Loss

Acute visual loss is an emergency until proven otherwise. The key clinical question is: painful or painless? Unilateral or bilateral? Complete or partial?

Retinal Vascular Occlusions

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO) presents as sudden, complete, painless, unilateral visual loss. The fundus shows a pale, whitened retina (ischaemic oedema of the inner retinal layers) with a cherry-red spot at the fovea — the fovea is supplied by the choroidal circulation (via the choriocapillaris) and retains its colour while the surrounding ischaemic retina appears white. The optic disc is initially normal but later becomes pale. The afferent pupillary reflex is markedly reduced (RAPD on swinging torch test).

This is a true ocular emergency — the retina tolerates ischaemia for only 90-100 minutes before irreversible damage. Emergency measures attempted include ocular massage (to dislodge a thrombus), anterior chamber paracentesis (to lower IOP), and high-flow oxygen. Thrombolysis has a controversial evidence base. Investigate for systemic embolic sources: atrial fibrillation (ECG), carotid artery disease (Doppler ultrasound), hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes. Treat these underlying risk factors urgently.

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO) causes a sector of visual field loss corresponding to the territory of the occluded branch. Vision may be partially preserved. The same embolic workup applies.

Retinal Detachment

Separation of the neurosensory retina from the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). The classic sequence is:

  • Prodrome of flashes and floaters: Flashes (photopsia) result from traction on the retina by the vitreous. Floaters ("cobwebs", "shower of black dots") represent vitreous haemorrhage or cellular debris.
  • Shadow/curtain across the visual field: As the retina detaches, a dark shadow spreads — described as a curtain falling across the vision. If the macula detaches, central vision is lost (worse prognosis).

Risk factors: Myopia (elongated eye — retinal stretching), previous retinal detachment, trauma, posterior vitreous detachment (PVD — common with ageing), family history.

Management: Emergency ophthalmology referral. Advise the patient not to lie flat if the detachment is superior (gravity can extend it to the macula). Surgical options include: pneumatic retinopexy (gas bubble), scleral buckling, or vitrectomy.

Vitreous Haemorrhage

Bleeding into the vitreous cavity causes sudden onset of floaters, "cobwebs", or "red haze". The red reflex is absent on fundoscopy. Major causes include: proliferative diabetic retinopathy (new fragile vessels bleed), subarachnoid haemorrhage (Terson syndrome), retinal tear/detachment, and trauma. Management involves treating the underlying cause; vitrectomy if the haemorrhage fails to clear.

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

AMD affects the macula — the area of highest cone density responsible for central vision and fine detail. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness in the developed world in those over 50.

Dry AMD (atrophic): The more common form (~85-90%). Caused by accumulation of drusen (deposits between the RPE and Bruch's membrane), progressive RPE atrophy leading to geographic atrophy. Visual loss is gradual and bilateral. Central scotoma develops over years. There is no licensed treatment, but high-dose antioxidant supplements (AREDS2 formula — vitamins C, E, lutein, zeaxanthin, zinc, copper) slow progression in intermediate AMD.

Wet AMD (neovascular): Less common but responsible for ~90% of severe visual loss from AMD. Characterised by choroidal neovascularisation — abnormal new blood vessels grow from the choroid beneath the retina. These vessels leak fluid and blood, causing sudden central visual loss and metamorphopsia (distortion of straight lines — tested with an Amsler grid). The patient may notice that door frames or tiles appear wavy or bent.

Management of wet AMD: Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections — ranibizumab (Lucentis) or aflibercept (Eylea) — given monthly initially, then as needed. These block vascular endothelial growth factor, reducing neovascularisation and fluid leakage. Early treatment is critical. Monitoring: regular Amsler grid testing to detect new metamorphopsia.

Optic Neuritis

Inflammation of the optic nerve, typically demyelinating. This is a classic presentation in the context of multiple sclerosis (MS) — the presenting symptom in up to 25% of MS cases, and over 50% of patients presenting with optic neuritis will eventually develop MS.

Presentation: Subacute unilateral visual loss (over hours to days), pain on eye movement (the optic nerve sheath shares attachments with extraocular muscles — this is highly characteristic), colour desaturation (red objects appear "washed out"), and a central scotoma. On examination, the classic finding is a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) — on the swinging torch test, both pupils dilate when the light swings to the affected eye (because the afferent signal from that eye is reduced). The optic disc may appear swollen (papillitis) or normal (retrobulbar neuritis — "the patient sees nothing and the doctor sees nothing").

Management: MRI brain (look for demyelinating plaques — McDonald criteria). IV methylprednisolone (500 mg daily for 3-5 days) accelerates recovery but does not improve final visual outcome. Most patients recover significant vision spontaneously. Refer to neurology for MS workup.

Amaurosis Fugax

Transient monocular visual loss (TMVL) — patients describe it as a "curtain" or "shutter" coming down over one eye, lasting seconds to minutes, then resolving completely. It is caused by a transient ischaemic attack (TIA) of the retinal artery, usually due to an embolus from the ipsilateral carotid artery or the heart.

This is a TIA equivalent — carry the same stroke risk. Management: urgent TIA pathway (ABCD2 score, dual antiplatelet — aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days), carotid Doppler ultrasound (if ipsilateral >70% stenosis, carotid endarterectomy is indicated within 2 weeks), and cardiac monitoring for AF.

Part 3: Chronic Eye Conditions

Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

The most common form of glaucoma. The drainage angle is open (unlike acute angle closure) but trabecular meshwork function is impaired, leading to gradually rising IOP and optic nerve damage over years. Importantly, normal tension glaucoma exists — IOP can be statistically "normal" but still cause optic nerve damage in susceptible individuals.

Clinical features: The insidious onset is why it is called the "silent thief of sight." There are no symptoms until significant visual field is lost — peripheral visual field is lost first, progressing inward. Fundoscopy shows optic disc cupping: the cup-to-disc ratio (C:D ratio) is >0.6 (normal ~0.4), asymmetry between eyes, disc haemorrhages, or "notching" of the neuroretinal rim (especially inferotemporal).

Screening: Optometrist picks up elevated IOP, abnormal C:D ratio, or visual field defects on routine check. Offered to those over 40 with a first-degree family history (free NHS sight test).

Management (NICE guidelines):

  • First-line: Prostaglandin analogue eye drops (latanoprost, bimatoprost) — once daily, most effective at lowering IOP, increase uveoscleral outflow. Side effect: increased iris and periorbital pigmentation, eyelash growth.
  • Second-line: Beta-blocker (timolol) — caution in asthma/COPD, heart block; or carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (dorzolamide) topically.
  • Surgical: Laser trabeculoplasty or trabeculectomy (creates a new drainage pathway) for uncontrolled disease.

Cataract

Opacification of the crystalline lens. The most common cause of reversible blindness worldwide.

Risk factors: Age (most common), diabetes mellitus, prolonged corticosteroid use (posterior subcapsular cataract — classic for AKT), UV radiation exposure, smoking, trauma, and congenital causes (see below).

Presentation: Gradual, painless blurring of vision; glare and halos around lights (especially driving at night); monocular diplopia; frequent changes in spectacle prescription. The red reflex is diminished or absent on fundoscopy.

Management: Surgical — phacoemulsification (ultrasound fragmentation of the lens) under local anaesthetic as a day case, with insertion of a synthetic intraocular lens (IOL). There is no effective medical treatment. Refer when the cataract is affecting quality of life.

Diabetic Retinopathy

The most common cause of new blindness in working-age adults in the UK. Caused by microvascular damage from chronic hyperglycaemia. Staged as follows:

Background (Non-proliferative) Diabetic Retinopathy:

  • Microaneurysms — earliest change; small red dots on fundoscopy
  • Dot and blot haemorrhages — small retinal haemorrhages
  • Hard exudates — lipid deposits, sharp edges, yellow-white

Pre-proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy:

  • Cotton wool spots — soft, fluffy white lesions representing nerve fibre layer infarcts (focal ischaemia causing axoplasmic flow arrest). Important to distinguish from hard exudates: cotton wool spots have indistinct borders and are fluffy; hard exudates are sharp-edged.
  • Venous beading and venous loops
  • IRMA — Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy:

  • New vessels (neovascularisation) — on disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE). These fragile vessels bleed easily, causing vitreous haemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment (fibrovascular proliferation contracts and pulls the retina).
  • Treatment: Pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP) laser (ablates ischaemic retina, reducing VEGF drive) or intravitreal anti-VEGF.

Diabetic Maculopathy: Involvement of the macula — clinically significant macular oedema (CSME). Most common cause of visual loss in diabetics. Hard exudates at or near the macula, macular oedema. Treatment: intravitreal anti-VEGF (ranibizumab), laser, or intravitreal steroids. Screened annually via the NHS Diabetic Eye Screening Programme (7-field digital photography).

Optimise systemic risk factors: HbA1c control (target <48 mmol/mol in T2DM), blood pressure control (<130/80 mmHg), smoking cessation, and lipid management all slow retinopathy progression.

Hypertensive Retinopathy

Graded using the Keith-Wagener-Barker (KWB) classification:

  • Grade 1: Arteriovenous (AV) nicking — at arteriovenous crossings, the thickened arteriole compresses the venule underneath, causing it to appear to "nick" or narrow.
  • Grade 2: Silver wiring / copper wiring — increased light reflex from arteriolar walls due to thickening. Copper wiring is less severe than silver.
  • Grade 3: Flame haemorrhages (along nerve fibre layer), cotton wool spots, hard exudates — significant hypertension.
  • Grade 4: Papilloedema (blurred disc margins, swollen disc) — hypertensive emergency. Admit urgently; lower BP carefully (avoid precipitous drops that can cause watershed infarction).

Part 4: Paediatric Eye Conditions

Amblyopia

Amblyopia ("lazy eye") is reduced visual acuity in one (or rarely both) eye(s) due to abnormal visual development during the critical period (birth to approximately 7-8 years). If the brain receives a blurred or suppressed image from one eye during this time, it "learns" to ignore it, and the cortical pathways for that eye fail to develop normally. The eye itself is structurally normal.

Causes: Strabismus (misalignment means the deviated eye's image is suppressed), anisometropia (significant refractive difference between the two eyes), or deprivation (congenital cataract, ptosis, corneal opacity blocking visual development).

Treatment: Correct any refractive error with glasses (sometimes this alone resolves amblyopia). Then occlusion therapy (patching) the better eye — forces the amblyopic eye to be used, stimulating cortical development. Atropine drops to the good eye are an alternative. Treatment must occur within the critical period — after age 7-8 it is increasingly ineffective.

Strabismus (Squint)

Misalignment of the visual axes. Esotropia = convergent (eye turns in); exotropia = divergent (eye turns out); hypertropia = upward deviation.

Cover test: The definitive test in primary care. Cover the apparently good eye — if the other (suspected deviant) eye moves to take up fixation, a manifest squint (tropia) is present. Uncover the eye and watch — if the covered eye moves on uncovering (re-fixates), a latent squint (phoria) is present (common and usually asymptomatic).

Management: Refer to orthoptics if squint is constant or persists beyond 3-4 months. Correct refractive errors. Treat associated amblyopia. Surgery (strabismus surgery to align the eyes) may be needed. Never assume a squint is "just cosmetic" — amblyopia is the primary risk and must be detected and treated early.

Congenital Cataract

Detected on the newborn examination and the 6-8 week check via assessment of the red reflex. An absent, white, or asymmetric red reflex mandates urgent ophthalmology referral. Surgery (lens extraction) should occur within weeks of birth, followed by optical correction (contact lens or aphakic glasses) and amblyopia therapy — the window for visual development is narrow and treatment delays lead to irreversible deprivation amblyopia. Causes include idiopathic, maternal rubella (TORCH infection), galactosaemia, Down syndrome, and Marfan syndrome.

Otitis Externa

Inflammation of the external auditory canal. The most common bacterial cause is Pseudomonas aeruginosa, followed by Staphylococcus aureus. Fungal (Aspergillus, Candida) causes occur in immunocompromised patients or after prolonged antibiotic use.

Presentation: Ear pain (otalgia), itch, and purulent discharge. The hallmark on examination is pain on tragus pressure and/or movement of the pinna — this distinguishes otitis externa from otitis media (where the TM is the problem and pinna traction is pain-free). The canal is oedematous and erythematous; the TM may be obscured but is normal if visualised.

Precipitants: Swimming (water retained in canal — "swimmer's ear"), cotton bud trauma, hearing aid use, eczema/psoriasis of the canal.

Management: Keep ear dry (cotton wool with Vaseline when showering). Mild cases: acetic acid 2% drops (Earcalm) — acidifies canal, restoring normal flora. Moderate-severe: combined antibiotic + steroid drops (ciprofloxacin/dexamethasone — Cilodex; or neomycin/betamethasone). Aural toilet (microsuction) to clear debris. If canal is very swollen, an ear wick (Pope wick) is inserted to allow drops to penetrate. Oral antibiotics only if spreading cellulitis or systemic features. Malignant (necrotising) otitis externa — in diabetics/immunocompromised, Pseudomonas invades bone causing osteomyelitis of the skull base; presents with severe pain, granulation tissue on the floor of the canal, CN VII palsy; requires IV ciprofloxacin and urgent ENT/neurosurgery.

Acute Otitis Media (AOM)

Infection of the middle ear cleft, most common in children aged 6 months to 5 years. Pathogens: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis, and respiratory viruses. Often follows a URTI.

Presentation: Ear pain (young children may pull at the ear, be irritable, or have poor sleep), fever, and on otoscopy: red, bulging tympanic membrane with loss of light reflex. If the TM perforates, there is a purulent discharge and paradoxically the pain relieves.

Management (NICE): Most cases resolve without antibiotics within 3 days. Use the National Guidance (NICE 2008 / 2023 update):

  • No antibiotic or delayed prescription for most children — provide safety-netting advice
  • Immediate amoxicillin if: severely unwell, under 2 years with bilateral AOM, otorrhoea (perforated TM), immunocompromised, or failure to improve after 2-3 days. Clarithromycin if penicillin-allergic.
  • Analgesics (paracetamol/ibuprofen) for pain relief in all cases
  • Perforated TM: heals spontaneously in most cases; amoxicillin to prevent secondary infection

Complications: Mastoiditis (most common; post-auricular swelling, tenderness, pinna displaced forwards — ENT emergency, IV antibiotics +/- mastoidectomy), meningitis, intracranial abscess, facial nerve palsy, labyrinthitis.

Otitis Media with Effusion (OME / Glue Ear)

Persistent middle ear effusion without acute inflammation. The most common cause of conductive hearing loss in children. Often asymptomatic apart from hearing loss — parents notice the child turning up the TV, not responding when called, or having poor academic progress.

Examination: The TM appears dull, retracted, or amber-coloured; a fluid level or air bubbles may be visible. Tympanometry shows a flat (type B) trace — no compliance peak, indicating fluid in the middle ear.

Management: Watchful waiting for 3 months — most OME resolves spontaneously. After 3 months with persistent bilateral hearing loss >25 dB: refer to ENT. Treatment: grommets (ventilation tubes inserted through the TM under GA; equalise pressure and allow drainage) are effective but tubes typically extrude spontaneously after 6-18 months. Adenoidectomy may be performed at the same time. Antibiotics and antihistamines are not recommended.

Cholesteatoma

An epidermal (keratinising squamous epithelium) cyst in the middle ear or mastoid that progressively enlarges, destroying surrounding bone. Despite the name, it is not a true tumour — it is a destructive accumulation of desquamating keratin. It can be congenital (rare; white mass behind intact TM in a child) or acquired (from a retraction pocket in the pars flaccida — "attic" region).

Red flag signs on otoscopy:

  • Attic perforation — a defect in the pars flaccida (superior TM), often with a crust
  • White pearly debris behind or at the TM (keratin accumulation)
  • Recurrent, offensive-smelling (foul) otorrhoea — does not resolve with antibiotics

Why it is DANGEROUS: Cholesteatoma is a locally aggressive, destructive lesion. It erodes the ossicles (causing conductive hearing loss), can invade the mastoid, erode the tegmen (dura) causing meningitis/brain abscess, erode the lateral semicircular canal causing vertigo and SNHL, erode the facial nerve canal causing facial nerve palsy, and cause sigmoid sinus thrombosis. Always refer to ENT — surgical excision (mastoidectomy) is the only definitive treatment.

Presbycusis

Age-related sensorineural hearing loss. Affects high frequencies first (difficulty hearing consonants — "s", "sh", "f", "th") — hence the classic complaint of hearing people speak but not being able to understand them, especially in noisy environments. Bilateral and symmetric. Audiogram shows a sloping high-frequency SNHL. Management: hearing aids (NHS Digital Hearing Aid Service). No medical treatment.

Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)

Defined as >30 dB SNHL across three consecutive frequencies over 72 hours or less. Most cases are idiopathic (thought to be viral or vascular). A minority have a structural cause — acoustic neuroma, Meniere's disease, autoimmune, ototoxic drug exposure.

This is an otological emergency. Treatment within 24-48 hours gives the best chance of recovery: oral prednisolone (high dose, e.g., 60 mg/day for 5 days then taper). Urgent ENT referral and audiology. MRI internal auditory meati (IAMs) to exclude acoustic neuroma. Consider hyperbaric oxygen in specialist centres for those who fail to recover.

Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)

A benign tumour arising from the Schwann cells of the vestibular division of CN VIII, growing in the internal auditory meatus and cerebellopontine angle. Most are sporadic; bilateral acoustic neuromas are pathognomonic of neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2).

Classic triad: Unilateral SNHL (progressive, often starting with high frequencies), unilateral tinnitus, and imbalance/vertigo (usually mild, as the brain compensates for the gradual vestibular loss). As the tumour enlarges: ipsilateral CN V (facial numbness, absent corneal reflex) and CN VII (facial weakness) involvement; and cerebellar signs if brainstem compression occurs.

Investigation: MRI with gadolinium of the IAMs — the gold standard; shows an enhancing lesion in the IAM or CPA. Pure tone audiometry shows unilateral SNHL. ABR (auditory brainstem response) is abnormal.

Management: Depends on tumour size and patient factors: observation with serial MRI (small tumours in elderly patients), microsurgical excision, or stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife). The goal is to preserve facial nerve function and residual hearing.

Meniere's Disease

A disorder of the inner ear caused by endolymphatic hydrops (accumulation of endolymph fluid distending the membranous labyrinth). The exact aetiology is unknown.

Classic tetrad — all four are required for diagnosis:

  • Episodic vertigo — severe, rotatory, lasting 20 minutes to several hours (not seconds as in BPPV; not days as in vestibular neuritis)
  • Fluctuating unilateral SNHL — affects low frequencies initially (unusual — most SNHL affects high frequencies first)
  • Unilateral tinnitus — described as roaring or low-pitched
  • Aural fullness — a pressure sensation in the affected ear

Management:

  • Acute attacks: prochlorperazine (vestibular sedative/antiemetic) buccally or IM
  • Prevention: low-salt diet (reduces endolymphatic fluid retention), betahistine (histamine analogue — increases microvascular blood flow in the cochlea and vestibular labyrinth, though evidence is debated), diuretics
  • Refractory cases: intratympanic gentamicin (chemical labyrinthectomy — destroys vestibular function on the affected side; trades vertigo control for residual hearing loss risk), endolymphatic sac decompression, labyrinthectomy

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

The most common cause of vertigo. Caused by dislodged otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals — "canaliths") floating freely in the posterior semicircular canal (most commonly), causing inappropriate stimulation of the ampullary cupula with head movement.

Presentation: Brief (seconds to <1 minute), positional vertigo triggered by specific head movements — rolling over in bed, looking up, bending forward. Resolves completely between episodes. No hearing loss or tinnitus.

Dix-Hallpike test: Gold standard. Patient sits on the couch; examiner rapidly takes the patient to lying with the head turned 45° to the affected side and extended 20-30° below horizontal. A positive test is geotropic rotatory nystagmus (beating towards the ground/affected ear) with a brief latency (~5-10 seconds), fatiguability on repeated testing, and reproduction of vertigo. The nystagmus reverses on sitting up.

Treatment: Epley manoeuvre — a series of head positioning steps that move the canalith from the posterior canal back into the utricle, where it is reabsorbed. Curative in approximately 80% of patients after one or two treatments. Can be performed in primary care once BPPV is confirmed by Dix-Hallpike. Vestibular suppressants (prochlorperazine, betahistine) are not recommended for BPPV long-term — they suppress central compensation and delay recovery.

Part 6: ENT — Nose

Epistaxis (Nosebleed)

Divided anatomically into anterior (more common, less severe) and posterior (less common, more serious).

Anterior epistaxis: 90% of nosebleeds. Source is Little's area (Kiesselbach's plexus) on the anterior nasal septum — the anastomotic site where four arteries converge: the anterior ethmoidal artery, posterior ethmoidal artery, sphenopalatine artery (branch of maxillary artery), and the superior labial artery (branch of facial artery). Common in children. Precipitated by trauma (nose picking), dry air, and upper respiratory infections.

First aid: Sit the patient upright, lean forward (prevents blood trickling down the throat and being swallowed — causing nausea/vomiting), and pinch the soft part of the nose (lower cartilaginous third) continuously for 10-20 minutes. Do not pack the bony nose or tilt the head back.

Definitive treatment: If the bleeding point is visible and the patient cooperative: silver nitrate cautery (chemical cauterisation) under topical anaesthesia. Do not cauterise both sides of the septum simultaneously (risk of septal perforation). Nasal packing (Merocel sponge or Rapid Rhino) if cautery fails.

Posterior epistaxis: Originates from branches of the sphenopalatine artery deeper in the nasal cavity. More common in older patients, associated with hypertension and atherosclerosis. More serious — can be life-threatening. Blood may flow into the pharynx even when the patient is sitting forward. Requires ENT referral, posterior nasal packing, or surgical/radiological intervention (sphenopalatine artery ligation or embolisation).

Allergic Rhinitis

IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal mucosa. Seasonal (hay fever — grass pollen, tree pollen) or perennial (house dust mite, mould, animal dander). Symptoms: nasal obstruction, watery rhinorrhoea, sneezing (especially in the morning — "first thing" sneezing), itchy nose/palate/eyes, and post-nasal drip.

NICE stepwise management:

  • Mild intermittent: oral antihistamine (loratadine, cetirizine — non-sedating) or intranasal antihistamine (azelastine)
  • Moderate-severe or persistent: intranasal corticosteroid (INCS — beclometasone, fluticasone, mometasone) is the most effective single treatment; needs to be started 2 weeks before the season. INCS is preferred over oral antihistamine as first-line for persistent symptoms.
  • Moderate-severe persistent (where INCS alone inadequate): add oral antihistamine; consider oral leukotriene receptor antagonist (montelukast) especially if concurrent asthma
  • Refractory: allergen immunotherapy (subcutaneous or sublingual — desensitisation)

Acute Rhinosinusitis

Inflammation of the nasal mucosa and paranasal sinuses. Viral (the great majority) or bacterial. Presentation: facial pain/pressure (worse on bending forward), nasal congestion, purulent nasal discharge, hyposmia, and fever.

Distinguishing viral from bacterial: The "double sickening" pattern — initial improvement followed by worsening after 5 days — or symptoms persisting beyond 10 days without improvement suggest bacterial superinfection (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae).

NICE (2017) antibiotic guidance:

  • <10 days: no antibiotic — advise saline irrigation, analgesia, intranasal decongestant (short-term xylometazoline)
  • >10 days or severe (high fever, very unwell): phenoxymethylpenicillin for 5 days (co-amoxiclav if first-line fails)

Dangerous complications:

  • Orbital cellulitis — periorbital oedema, proptosis, limited eye movement, reduced vision; from ethmoid sinusitis spreading to the orbit. CT head and orbits, IV antibiotics, urgent ophthalmology + ENT. Cavernous sinus thrombosis is a feared complication.
  • Meningitis, subdural/epidural abscess, frontal bone osteomyelitis (Pott's puffy tumour)

Part 7: ENT — Throat

Tonsillitis

Inflammation of the palatine tonsils, most commonly viral (EBV, adenovirus, enteroviruses) or bacterial (Group A beta-haemolytic Streptococcus — Streptococcus pyogenes). Presentation: sore throat, odynophagia, fever, cervical lymphadenopathy, and tonsillar exudate (pus on the tonsils — does not reliably distinguish viral from bacterial).

Clinical scoring tools to guide antibiotic prescribing:

FeverPAIN score (NICE-preferred for UK primary care):

  • Fever (>38°C in past 24 hours) — 1 point
  • Purulence (pus on tonsils) — 1 point
  • Attend rapidly (within 3 days of symptom onset) — 1 point
  • Inflamed tonsils (severe redness) — 1 point
  • No cough or coryza — 1 point
  • Score 0-1: no antibiotic
  • Score 2-3: delayed antibiotic prescription
  • Score 4-5: immediate antibiotic or delayed prescription

Centor criteria (older, still used): Tonsillar exudate, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, fever, absence of cough (1 point each). Score 3-4: antibiotic may be beneficial.

Antibiotic of choice: Phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) for 10 days — must be 10 days to eradicate Group A Strep and prevent rheumatic fever. Clarithromycin if penicillin-allergic.

Glandular fever (infectious mononucleosis — EBV): Must be considered, especially in teenagers and young adults. Features suggesting EBV: severe tonsillitis with white exudate, posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hepatitis, widespread lymphadenopathy, a maculopapular rash. Do NOT give amoxicillin or ampicillin — causes a widespread maculopapular rash in ~90% of cases of EBV infection (though the mechanism is not a true allergy). Diagnose with Monospot (heterophile antibody) test or EBV-specific serology. Advise: rest, adequate fluids, avoid contact sports for 4 weeks (splenic rupture risk).

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

A collection of pus between the tonsillar capsule and the superior pharyngeal constrictor muscle, complicating acute tonsillitis. The classic presentation is:

  • Unilateral peritonsillar swelling — the soft palate and anterior pillar bulge on one side
  • Uvular deviation to the contralateral side
  • Trismus — difficulty opening the mouth (spasm of the pterygoid muscles)
  • Drooling and dysphagia
  • Hot potato (muffled) voice
  • Systemic features: high fever, toxicity

Management: IV benzylpenicillin (or co-amoxiclav) plus needle aspiration (first-line drainage) or incision and drainage (I&D) under local anaesthetic. IV dexamethasone to reduce oedema and pain. Tonsillectomy ("quinsy tonsillectomy" at the time or interval tonsillectomy 6 weeks later) is considered for recurrent episodes. Urgent ENT referral.

Epiglottitis

Life-threatening inflammation and swelling of the epiglottis, causing upper airway obstruction. Classically caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) — now rare in the UK since Hib vaccination in the childhood immunisation schedule, but still occurs in unvaccinated individuals, adults, and can be caused by other organisms (S. pneumoniae, S. aureus, Group A Strep, viruses).

Classic presentation: A child (or adult) with sudden high fever, toxic and severely unwell appearance, tripod/sniffing posture (sitting forward, neck extended, hands on knees — adopts this position to maintain their own airway), muffled voice (not the hoarse/barking cough of croup), drooling (too much pain to swallow), and dysphagia. Stridor is an ominous sign of impending airway compromise.

Critical management rule: DO NOT examine the throat and DO NOT lay the patient flat. Any attempt to look in the throat with a tongue depressor or laryngoscope, or any distressing procedure, can precipitate complete laryngospasm and sudden airway obstruction. The child must be kept calm, with a parent present, in the position of comfort.

Management: Call anaesthetics and ENT immediately — this requires a skilled airway team. Take the patient to theatre for controlled intubation under inhalational anaesthesia (gas induction), with a surgeon scrubbed ready for surgical airway (tracheostomy) if intubation fails. Once the airway is secured, take a "cherry-red" swollen epiglottis view (the classic endoscopic appearance) and start IV cefuroxime or cefotaxime. If the diagnosis is uncertain and the patient is stable enough, a lateral neck X-ray shows the "thumbprint sign" (swollen epiglottis).

High-Yield Revision Checklist

Before your AKT, ensure you can confidently answer questions on:

  • The distinguishing features of each red eye cause — especially acute angle-closure glaucoma (emergency) vs uveitis vs conjunctivitis
  • Cherry-red spot on fundoscopy = central retinal artery occlusion
  • RAPD on swinging torch test = optic neuritis (or severe retinal disease on the same side)
  • Anti-VEGF injections for wet AMD; supplements only for intermediate dry AMD
  • Latanoprost (prostaglandin analogue) as first-line for open-angle glaucoma
  • Cotton wool spots = nerve fibre layer infarcts (not lipid deposits — those are hard exudates)
  • Proliferative diabetic retinopathy: new vessels + risk of vitreous haemorrhage = laser photocoagulation or anti-VEGF
  • Hypertensive retinopathy grade 4 (papilloedema) = hypertensive emergency
  • Cholesteatoma: attic perforation + foul discharge + DANGEROUS bone erosion = ENT surgery
  • Meniere's tetrad: episodic vertigo (hours) + low-frequency SNHL + tinnitus + aural fullness
  • BPPV: seconds-duration positional vertigo + positive Dix-Hallpike + Epley manoeuvre = cure
  • Epiglottitis: DO NOT examine the throat — call anaesthetics immediately
  • Glandular fever: avoid amoxicillin
  • FeverPAIN score guides antibiotic prescribing in tonsillitis; penicillin V 10 days
  • SSNHL = otological emergency; oral prednisolone within 24-48 hours

Ophthalmology and ENT may feel like niche areas, but the AKT tests a well-defined core. Mastering the differentials, recognising the emergencies that require same-day specialist referral, and knowing first-line management will carry you confidently through these questions.
