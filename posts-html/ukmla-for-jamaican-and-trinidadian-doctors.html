UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors is a route that many graduates of the region's medical schools research seriously, given the UK's long-standing historical ties with the English-speaking Caribbean and the sizeable Caribbean-heritage medical community already established across the NHS. Doctors trained in Jamaica, Trinidad and Tobago, and at the other campuses of the University of the West Indies (UWI) sit in a somewhat distinctive position among international medical graduates (IMGs): their medical education is conducted entirely in English, their curricula draw heavily on Commonwealth clinical traditions, and generations of Caribbean-trained doctors have already crossed into UK practice before them. None of that removes the formal requirements, but it does shape which parts of the pathway are genuinely straightforward and which still demand careful planning.

This guide sets out the full route into GMC registration specifically as it applies to doctors qualified in Jamaica or Trinidad and Tobago, and more broadly across the wider English-speaking Caribbean through UWI's regional campuses. It covers how the UKMLA replaced PLAB as the gateway exam, how a Caribbean primary medical qualification is verified through EPIC and the World Directory of Medical Schools, why English-medium Caribbean training may make some candidates eligible for the GMC's English language exemption route, realistic test-centre access across the Caribbean and Americas region, typical costs in USD and GBP with the necessary hedge on JMD/TTD conversion, and a step-by-step timeline from application to a first NHS post.

The historical relationship between Caribbean medical training and UK medical practice is not incidental. The University of the West Indies was founded in 1948, at Mona in Jamaica, as a college of the University of London, and Caribbean doctors have practised within British and then NHS medicine for over seven decades since. That heritage means the structural compatibility between Caribbean training and UK registration requirements is generally strong — but, as with every IMG route, eligibility is still assessed qualification by qualification and applicant by applicant, and this guide sets out exactly how that assessment works.

UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian Doctors: The Quick Answer

UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors means sitting the two-part UK Medical Licensing Assessment — the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) and the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment (CPSA) — as part of the same registration pathway now used by every international medical graduate seeking a UK licence to practise, alongside primary source verification of a Caribbean medical degree and evidence of English language proficiency. The UKMLA replaced the old PLAB test as the single UK-wide licensing assessment from 2024/2025 onward, and functionally it plays the same role PLAB 1 and PLAB 2 used to play for IMGs: a knowledge test (AKT) and a practical clinical skills test (CPSA), both of which must be passed before a doctor can apply for full GMC registration with a licence to practise.

For a Jamaican or Trinidadian doctor, the pathway runs through six broad stages: confirming that your primary medical qualification is listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools and accepted by the GMC; opening a GMC Online account and starting EPIC verification of your degree; providing evidence of English language proficiency, either through a formal test or a possible exemption; booking and passing the AKT, which can often be sat within the Caribbean and Americas region; travelling to the UK to sit the CPSA, which is only available at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester; and finally submitting a full application for GMC registration once every requirement is verified. Each of these stages is explored in detail below, together with the practical cost and timeline planning that matters most for candidates budgeting in Jamaican dollars (JMD) or Trinidad and Tobago dollars (TTD).

  1. Confirm eligibility — verify your medical school's listing on the World Directory of Medical Schools and check the GMC's current guidance on accepted primary medical qualifications.
  2. Create a GMC Online account and begin primary source verification of your degree through EPIC.
  3. Establish your English language position — sit IELTS Academic or OET Medicine, or gather the specific documentary evidence needed to claim an exemption or alternative-evidence route.
  4. Book and pass the UKMLA AKT, sat at a Pearson VUE test centre, potentially within the Caribbean or wider Americas region.
  5. Book and pass the UKMLA CPSA, which must be sat in person in Manchester.
  6. Apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise, then move into NHS job applications.

Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors researching this pathway typically want to know three things above all else: whether their degree will actually be recognised without complication, how much the whole process realistically costs once flights and Manchester travel are included, and how long it genuinely takes from a standing start to a first NHS pay cheque. The sections below answer each of these in turn, grounded in the same GMC rules that apply to every IMG route, alongside the region-specific detail that makes the Caribbean route distinctive.

Eligibility: How the GMC Recognises Jamaican and Trinidadian Medical Qualifications

Eligibility for the UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors starts with the same basic test the GMC applies to every IMG: your primary medical qualification (PMQ) must be listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools (WDOMS), the FAIMER-maintained global register of recognised medical schools, for the year in which you graduated. The University of the West Indies — which operates its principal Faculty of Medical Sciences at Mona, Jamaica, and a second major faculty at St. Augustine, Trinidad and Tobago, alongside smaller campuses at Cave Hill in Barbados and Nassau in the Bahamas — appears in WDOMS under its own FAIMER school listing, which candidates and administrators can verify directly through the WDOMS search portal. Graduates of other GMC-recognised medical schools within Jamaica or Trinidad and Tobago should carry out the same check for their specific institution and graduation year before proceeding, since WDOMS listings are assessed by school and by period, not as a blanket national approval.

Because verifying your own school's listing takes only a few minutes and materially affects every later stage of planning, it is worth doing before committing to exam bookings or travel plans. A UWI-trained doctor whose degree year is confirmed on WDOMS, and whose subsequent internship year is properly documented, generally clears this first hurdle without complication — but the confirmation step itself should never be skipped or assumed.

Beyond the medical school listing itself, the GMC also expects evidence of the supervised clinical training that follows graduation. Jamaican and Trinidadian medical graduates typically complete a one- to two-year rotating internship, supervised by the relevant national medical council — the Medical Council of Jamaica or the Medical Board of Trinidad and Tobago, depending on where the internship was undertaken — before being granted full local registration. This internship period broadly mirrors the kind of supervised-practice requirement the GMC looks for from IMGs generally, but as with every country route, the GMC assesses the specific documentation on a case-by-case basis rather than applying an automatic equivalence. Candidates should request a certificate of good standing or registration status from their national medical council early, since this document is issued by a separate body from the university and can carry its own processing timeline.

Primary Source Verification via EPIC

Once basic eligibility is confirmed, the GMC requires primary source verification of a Jamaican or Trinidadian medical degree through EPIC (Electronic Portfolio of International Credentials) — the same global platform used to verify every IMG's primary medical qualification, regardless of country of origin. Verification requests are sent directly to the medical school's registrar, bypassing the candidate entirely, which is precisely why EPIC processing time depends on institutional responsiveness rather than the applicant's own effort. UWI's constituent campuses generally respond to EPIC requests within a reasonably predictable window, though candidates should still expect this step to take anywhere from four weeks to three months depending on the time of year, since university administrative offices slow down considerably around examination periods and the academic calendar's transition points.

Full detail on how primary source verification works, what supporting documents are required, and how the GMC uses the resulting evidence is set out directly by the regulator at the GMC's registration and PLAB information hub, which covers the full international registration process including how EPIC fits into it. This process runs identically for Jamaican and Trinidadian applicants as it does for candidates from any other country — the site's detailed walkthrough of how GMC registration works for doctors already inside the European regulatory sphere and the guide to the UKMLA pathway for South African doctors both describe the same underlying EPIC mechanics, even though the specific national verifying bodies differ.

Documents Caribbean Candidates Should Gather Early

A consistent set of supporting documents applies across almost every Jamaican or Trinidadian application, and gathering them in parallel rather than one at a time saves considerable calendar time:

  • MBBS or equivalent degree certificate and full academic transcripts from UWI or your specific medical school, covering every year of study.
  • Internship completion certificate, confirming your supervised rotating internship year(s) under the Medical Council of Jamaica or the Medical Board of Trinidad and Tobago.
  • Certificate of registration or good standing from your national medical council, confirming your current registration status and disciplinary record.
  • Valid passport, ideally with several years of remaining validity to cover the full pathway and any subsequent visa application.
  • Evidence of any name changes — marriage certificates or statutory declarations — where documents show a different name to your current passport.
  • English language test certificate, or the specific documentary evidence needed to support an exemption claim (covered in detail below).

Requesting the national medical council certificate early is particularly worthwhile, since — much like the equivalent regulatory-body certificates required from other Commonwealth IMG routes — it is issued by a separate national administrative body operating on its own timeline, distinct from the university's own EPIC response. Candidates who request both in the same week rarely find either becomes an unexpected bottleneck later in the process.

The English Language Exemption Angle for Caribbean-Trained Doctors

English is the sole language of instruction, clinical placement, and examination throughout Jamaican and Trinidadian medical education, and this is precisely the situation the GMC's alternative-evidence and exemption framework is designed to address. Unlike a general assumption that "my degree was in English so I don't need to test," the GMC operates a specific, evidence-based ground for exactly this circumstance: where a primary medical qualification was taught and examined entirely in English at a recognised institution, within a defined recent period before the registration application, a doctor may not need to sit a fresh IELTS or OET test at all. This ground is examined in full detail, including the recency requirement and the clinical-interaction threshold that applies alongside the language-of-instruction test, in the site's dedicated guide to the GMC English language exemption for IMGs, which is essential reading before assuming this route applies automatically.

The practical implication for Jamaican and Trinidadian candidates is this: because UWI and other Caribbean medical schools teach and examine entirely in English, including clinical placements conducted in English throughout, many recent graduates are realistic candidates for this specific exemption ground — but qualifying still requires a formal, verifiable statement from the medical school confirming both the language of instruction and examination and, where the GMC requires it, the proportion of clinical interaction conducted in English. This is not something a candidate can self-certify; it typically requires a purpose-specific letter from the university registrar or dean's office, requested separately from the standard EPIC verification and sometimes carrying its own processing delay.

Candidates who graduated some years ago, or who are uncertain whether their specific programme meets the GMC's current recency and clinical-interaction thresholds, should not assume the exemption will apply and should not delay their overall pathway waiting to find out. The safer planning approach is to request the documentary evidence needed for an exemption claim at the same time as beginning EPIC verification, while simultaneously registering interest in an IELTS Academic or OET Medicine test date as a fallback. This way, if the exemption claim is ultimately unsuccessful or the required documentary evidence cannot be obtained in time, no calendar time has been lost waiting to find out. Where a formal test is required, the GMC accepts IELTS Academic, with an overall score of 7.0 and no individual component below 7.0, or OET Medicine, with a minimum of Grade B across all four sub-tests — both are widely available at test centres across Jamaica and Trinidad and Tobago. Full detail on accepted scores and evidentiary requirements is available directly from the regulator at the GMC's page on evidence of knowledge of English.

It is also worth noting that certificates for both IELTS and OET carry a defined validity window from the test date, so sequencing matters: sitting a test too early relative to the rest of the pathway risks the certificate expiring before a registration application is actually submitted, forcing an unnecessary and costly re-sit. Candidates should time their English language evidence — whether a test certificate or an exemption application — to remain valid comfortably past their expected registration date, not merely past their expected exam dates.

Test Centre Access for the Caribbean and Americas Region

One practical advantage for candidates pursuing the UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors is that the AKT, delivered through the global Pearson VUE test centre network, does not necessarily require travel outside the Caribbean and wider Americas region. Pearson VUE maintains authorised test centres across the Caribbean, including established centres in Trinidad and Tobago and testing arrangements available through partner organisations in Jamaica, alongside a substantial network of centres throughout North America that candidates able to travel slightly further afield can also consider. Because exact centre availability, opening hours, and specific testing capability shift between booking windows, candidates should always confirm current options directly through the Pearson VUE test centre locator linked from their GMC Online account rather than relying on a fixed list, and should register interest in a testing window as early as possible once eligible, since popular regional dates can fill quickly.

The picture is materially different for the CPSA. Unlike the AKT, the CPSA cannot be sat anywhere in the Caribbean, or indeed anywhere outside the UK — it is held exclusively at the GMC's purpose-built Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London sittings during periods of high demand. This means every Jamaican or Trinidadian candidate must, at minimum, make one trip to the UK to complete the UKMLA pathway, even where the AKT itself is passed entirely from a regional Caribbean or Americas test centre without ever leaving home. Budgeting for this single mandatory UK trip — flights, a UK visitor visa if required, several nights of accommodation, and living costs for the preparation period immediately before the exam — should be built into financial and calendar planning from the very beginning of the process, not treated as an afterthought once the AKT is already passed.

Many candidates choose to combine the Manchester trip with a short period of UK clinical observation or shadowing in the days beforehand, which helps with the specific communication style and structured consultation format the CPSA examines, and which is a genuinely useful adjustment for doctors trained entirely within Caribbean clinical settings. Full detail on the exam format itself, station structure, and marking criteria is covered in the site's guide to the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment.

The Route to GMC Registration: Step by Step

With eligibility, English language evidence, test-centre logistics, and costs all understood, the practical application process for the UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors follows a defined sequence. Each step below should generally be actioned in the order given, though several can and should run in parallel to save calendar time.

Step 1: Create Your GMC Online Account

The pathway begins with creating a GMC Online account through the GMC's official website. This account becomes the single hub for the entire process — tracking EPIC verification status, booking both the AKT and CPSA, submitting English language evidence, and eventually completing the registration application itself. Use a personal email address you expect to retain for several years, not a hospital or university address that may lapse after you leave a post, since correspondence with the GMC can span well over a year for most Caribbean candidates.

Step 2: Begin EPIC Verification Immediately

As covered above, EPIC verification of a UWI or other Jamaican or Trinidadian medical degree is usually the longest single step in the entire pathway, precisely because it depends on institutional response time rather than the candidate's own effort. Start this step as early as possible — ideally before finalising an English test date or an AKT booking — since it runs quietly in the background and does not require active involvement beyond following up if there are delays. Keep a written record of every communication with your medical school's registrar's office, since this becomes valuable evidence if escalation is later needed.

Step 3: Resolve English Language Evidence

In parallel with EPIC verification, determine whether you have a realistic claim to the GMC's English language exemption route based on your Caribbean medical training, or whether you need to sit IELTS Academic or OET Medicine. Request any documentary evidence an exemption claim would require from your medical school at the same time as registering interest in a formal test date as a fallback, so that no time is lost regardless of which route ultimately applies to your specific circumstances.

Step 4: Book and Prepare for the AKT

Once your GMC Online account is active and your eligibility is confirmed, you can book the AKT through the linked Pearson VUE booking system. Booking windows typically open several months ahead of each testing period, and centre dates within the Caribbean and Americas region can fill quickly, so book as soon as a suitable slot appears. Preparation should draw on the official MLA content map, which defines the full range of examinable conditions, presentations, and professional knowledge areas, supported by a structured question bank — see the site's guide to choosing a good AKT question bank and mock test resource for a comparison of the main preparation options.

Step 5: Book and Prepare for the CPSA

The CPSA can only be booked once the AKT has been passed. Because the CPSA is only available in Manchester, Jamaican and Trinidadian candidates need to plan a UK trip around this stage well in advance, factoring in visa arrangements, flight routing (which for many Caribbean candidates involves a connection through a US or European hub), and several days of pre-exam preparation and acclimatisation. Full detail on the exam format itself is covered in the site's guide to the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment, and the general knowledge test equivalent is covered in the guide to the Applied Knowledge Test (AKT).

Step 6: Submit Your GMC Registration Application

Once both the AKT and CPSA are passed, and EPIC verification, English language evidence, and all other supporting documents (national medical council certificate, internship completion documentation, identity documents) are confirmed, the full application for GMC registration with a licence to practise can be submitted. Full step-by-step detail on this final stage, including document checklists and how to respond if an application is queried, is available in the site's GMC registration process guide.

Life and Career for Caribbean Doctors in the NHS After Registration

Once GMC registration is secured, Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors join a long and well-established Caribbean medical presence within the NHS — a presence dating back generations, closely tied to the broader history of Windrush-era and post-independence Caribbean migration to the UK. This established diaspora means new arrivals typically find peer networks, informal mentorship, and Caribbean professional and cultural associations already active in several UK regions, particularly London, Birmingham, and Manchester, which have historically had substantial Caribbean communities.

Career progression after registration generally follows one of several familiar routes: a trust-grade or clinical fellow post, offering FY2-equivalent clinical responsibility without requiring a Foundation Programme place and the most common entry point for newly registered IMGs; a Specialty Training application once sufficient UK clinical experience and portfolio evidence are in place; or locum work through NHS staff banks for those wanting flexibility while building a UK track record. Many Caribbean-trained doctors entering through clinical fellow posts progress into core or specialty training within two to three years, particularly in specialties experiencing sustained workforce demand such as emergency medicine, general internal medicine, psychiatry, and anaesthetics.

The broad shape of this journey — degree recognition, primary source verification, English language evidence, a knowledge exam, a clinical skills exam sat in the UK, then registration — mirrors the route followed by IMGs from many other Commonwealth and non-Commonwealth countries alike. It closely parallels, for instance, the equivalent pathway followed by South African-trained doctors, another country with deep historical medical ties to the UK and a well-established diaspora presence in the NHS, even though the specific verifying bodies, typical processing windows, and regional test-centre access differ between the two routes. It is a useful comparison for understanding just how consistent the underlying UKMLA structure is across very different source countries and training systems — including, further afield, the route taken by doctors trained in Vietnam and Indonesia, where English is not the primary language of instruction and the practical considerations therefore differ considerably from the Caribbean route, despite an otherwise identical formal structure. Smaller Caribbean and Southeast Asian source countries too low-volume to warrant an individually dedicated guide are covered together in the site's round-up guide to other international medical graduate country routes, which is a useful cross-reference for candidates comparing notes with colleagues from neighbouring regions.

Day-to-day adjustment for newly registered Caribbean doctors in the NHS tends to be lighter than for IMGs coming from non-English-medium training systems, given the shared language and substantial overlap in clinical terminology and Commonwealth-derived medical culture. The most common early adjustments instead relate to specific NHS documentation conventions — structured handover formats such as SBAR, electronic prescribing systems, and a strongly codified, multidisciplinary approach to ward-level decision-making — rather than any fundamental gap in clinical knowledge or communication style. Most Caribbean doctors who complete the pathway describe the structured nature of UK postgraduate training, with clearly defined competency frameworks and protected training time, as comparing favourably with less formalised training structures elsewhere, which is part of what continues to draw graduates of Jamaican, Trinidadian, and other regional Caribbean medical schools toward NHS careers.

Common Mistakes Jamaican and Trinidadian Doctors Make on This Route

Several avoidable errors recur consistently among candidates pursuing the UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors, and almost none of them are clinical in nature — they are planning and administrative errors, yet they are responsible for a disproportionate share of the delays and unexpected costs Caribbean candidates actually experience.

  • Starting EPIC verification too late. Because EPIC processing time depends on your medical school's administrative speed rather than your own study effort, this step should begin before, not after, serious AKT preparation. Candidates who wait until they feel exam-ready before starting EPIC frequently lose two to three months they could have used productively in parallel.
  • Assuming English-medium Caribbean training automatically waives the language test. Being taught entirely in English is a genuine, relevant factor for the GMC's exemption route, but it is not self-certifying — it requires specific documentary evidence from the medical school, assessed against defined recency and clinical-interaction criteria. Candidates who assume an automatic waiver, without gathering the required evidence, sometimes find themselves needing to sit a test later than planned, under greater time pressure.
  • Underestimating the true cost of the Manchester CPSA trip. The exam fee itself is only part of the cost. Flights from the Caribbean (often routed through a US or European hub), several nights of Manchester accommodation, local transport, and a short UK preparation period beforehand can together add well over £1,000 on top of the core fee. Candidates who budget only for the headline figure frequently scramble for additional funds close to their exam date.
  • Not accounting for JMD or TTD exchange rate movement. Because every UKMLA and GMC fee is charged in pounds sterling, a candidate who calculates a JMD or TTD budget many months before actually paying can find the real local-currency cost has shifted meaningfully by the time payment falls due. Build a buffer of at least 15–20% above an initial budget calculation, and check current rates close to each actual payment date rather than relying on an old estimate.
  • Letting English test certificates expire. Both IELTS and OET certificates carry a defined validity window from the test date. Candidates who sit an English test early in the process, before EPIC verification or AKT preparation is complete, sometimes find the certificate has expired by the time a full registration application is ready to submit, forcing an unnecessary and costly re-sit.
  • Treating national medical council paperwork as an afterthought. The certificate of registration or good standing from the Medical Council of Jamaica or the Medical Board of Trinidad and Tobago is issued on its own administrative timeline, separate from the university. Requesting it only once every other document is ready is a common, avoidable source of last-minute delay.
  • Compressing AKT and CPSA preparation into the final few weeks. Both assessments draw on a specific, defined content base distinct from undergraduate Caribbean medical curricula in places, and candidates who begin serious, structured preparation only shortly before their booked date typically underperform relative to their actual underlying clinical knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a UWI medical degree from Jamaica or Trinidad accepted by the GMC?

The University of the West Indies is listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools, and its degrees are generally accepted as meeting the GMC's basic eligibility requirement, provided the specific campus and graduation year are confirmed against the current WDOMS listing. Acceptance also depends on completing primary source verification through EPIC and providing evidence of your supervised internship year, so eligibility should always be confirmed against your own specific circumstances rather than assumed automatically.

Do Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors need to sit an English language test?

Not necessarily, but it should not be assumed either way. Because Caribbean medical education is conducted entirely in English, some candidates may qualify for the GMC's English language exemption or alternative-evidence route, provided their medical school can supply a formal statement confirming the language of instruction, examination, and clinical interaction within the GMC's recency requirements. Candidates who cannot obtain this evidence, or whose training falls outside the required timeframe, still need to sit IELTS Academic (7.0 overall, no component below 7.0) or OET Medicine (Grade B minimum in all four components).

Can the AKT be sat in Jamaica or Trinidad and Tobago without travelling abroad?

In many testing windows, yes. Pearson VUE maintains authorised test centres and testing arrangements across the Caribbean and wider Americas region, including established centres in Trinidad and Tobago and partner testing arrangements in Jamaica. Candidates should always confirm current centre availability directly through the Pearson VUE test centre locator linked from their GMC Online account, since exact locations and open dates shift between testing windows.

Do Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors have to travel to the UK for the CPSA?

Yes, without exception. The CPSA is held exclusively at the GMC's Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, with occasional additional London dates during periods of high demand. There is no option to sit the CPSA remotely or anywhere within the Caribbean, so every candidate on this pathway must budget for at least one trip to the UK.

How much does the UKMLA pathway cost in USD for Caribbean candidates?

The core GMC and exam fees — AKT, CPSA, and initial registration — total approximately £1,800, or roughly $2,286 at a broadly indicative exchange rate, though this figure moves with the market and should always be rechecked before paying. Once English testing, EPIC verification, Manchester travel and accommodation, and study materials are included, a realistic total budget is closer to £3,300–£4,800, or approximately $4,200–$6,100. Candidates budgeting in JMD or TTD should convert from the USD or GBP figure at the current rate close to each actual payment date, since both currencies carry independent exchange-rate exposure against sterling.

What is the realistic timeline from applying in Jamaica or Trinidad to working in the NHS?

Most candidates should plan for approximately 16 to 28 months from beginning EPIC verification to starting a first NHS post, assuming first-attempt passes at both the AKT and CPSA. This accounts for national medical council document processing, EPIC verification delays, English language evidence, exam preparation for both assessments, and the GMC registration application itself. Candidates needing to re-sit either exam, or facing slower-than-average verification, should expect this timeline to extend.

How does the historical UK-Caribbean medical relationship affect this route today?

The University of the West Indies was established in 1948 as a college of the University of London, and Caribbean-trained doctors have practised in British and NHS medicine for over seven decades since, creating a well-established diaspora and professional network within the UK. This history does not change the formal GMC requirements — every candidate still completes the same UKMLA and registration pathway as any other IMG — but it does mean Jamaican and Trinidadian candidates typically find substantial informal support, mentorship, and community already in place once they arrive.

Conclusion

The UKMLA for Jamaican and Trinidadian doctors follows the same formal structure applied to every international medical graduate, but the region's English-medium medical education, its University of the West Indies degrees already listed on the World Directory of Medical Schools, and a genuinely long historical relationship with UK medicine make several stages of the journey more straightforward than for many other IMG routes. Candidates who start EPIC verification early, resolve their English language position — exemption or formal test — well ahead of exam bookings, plan realistically for the mandatory Manchester trip, and budget with a currency buffer for JMD or TTD volatility consistently move through the pathway faster and with fewer costly setbacks.
