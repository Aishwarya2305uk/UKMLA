Toxicology UKMLA questions sit at the intersection of pharmacology and emergency medicine, and they are tested precisely because a foundation doctor is expected to recognise a poisoned patient, start safe initial treatment, and know when to call for expert help without hesitating. Overdose and poisoning scenarios appear across the Applied Knowledge Test as single-best-answer questions built around a history, a set of observations, an ECG, or a set of blood results, and they appear in the Clinical and Professional Skills Assessment as a station in which a candidate must take a focused history, interpret an early warning score, and communicate a management plan. This guide works through the poisonings and overdoses that recur most often in UKMLA-style questions — paracetamol, opioids, benzodiazepines, tricyclic antidepressants, salicylates, carbon monoxide, and alcohol-related presentations — and sets out the initial assessment and antidote knowledge that both UK medical students and international medical graduates (IMGs) preparing for the AKT need to have automatic recall of.

Unlike a broader acute-presentations revision note, this guide deliberately narrows in on the toxicology and overdose curriculum: the specific antidotes, the treatment thresholds, the ECG changes, and the practical safety-netting advice that the GMC's Medical Licensing Assessment content map expects every newly registered doctor to apply on day one, whether they trained in Manchester, Mumbai, or Manila. It also covers the single most important piece of real-world safety information for this topic — that no foundation doctor is ever expected to manage a poisoned patient from memory alone, because the UK has a dedicated 24-hour clinical toxicology service that exists precisely for this purpose.

Quick Answer: The Toxicology UKMLA Essentials

Toxicology UKMLA content tests recognition of a specific poison or overdose from a clinical picture, plus the correct first-line antidote or supportive treatment, plus safe escalation. The seven poisonings covered here — paracetamol, opioids, benzodiazepines, tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), salicylates (aspirin), carbon monoxide, and alcohol — account for the overwhelming majority of toxicology single-best-answer questions, because they map directly onto the poisonings UK emergency departments see most frequently and because each has a clear, testable antidote or treatment algorithm.

The single most important safety fact to hold in mind for every toxicology scenario, in the exam and in real practice, is that the UK has a dedicated National Poisons Information Service (NPIS), and its clinical database, TOXBASE, is the authoritative first port of call for any clinician managing a poisoned patient. TOXBASE is not an optional extra resource — it is the primary reference NHS clinicians are expected to consult for the specific agent, dose, and timing involved in a real case, and UKMLA questions are written on the assumption candidates know this service exists and know when to escalate to it or to the NPIS telephone advice line rather than guessing.

The General Approach to the Unknown Poisoning

The correct first step for any suspected poisoning, regardless of the agent, is a structured ABCDE assessment — because an unstable airway, breathing, or circulation kills faster than any delay in identifying the exact poison ever will. This principle underpins almost every toxicology UKMLA question stem: the correct "next step" answer is very often airway and breathing support before diagnostic reasoning about which substance is responsible.

A — Airway: is the airway patent, and is the patient protecting it? A reduced Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS below 8) is the classic threshold at which airway protection becomes a serious concern and anaesthetic input for a definitive airway should be considered early, since aspiration is a major cause of morbidity in poisoned patients who vomit while obtunded. B — Breathing: respiratory rate, depth, and oxygen saturation, remembering that opioid and benzodiazepine toxicity both cause primary respiratory depression, and that pulse oximetry can be falsely reassuring in carbon monoxide poisoning (discussed below). C — Circulation: heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill, and a 12-lead ECG in essentially every poisoning case, because several of the substances covered here (tricyclic antidepressants, salicylates in overdose, and some recreational stimulants) cause specific and dangerous arrhythmias. D — Disability: blood glucose (hypoglycaemia can mimic or coexist with poisoning and must never be missed), pupil size and reactivity (pinpoint pupils suggest opioids; dilated pupils suggest anticholinergic or sympathomimetic toxicity), and GCS. E — Exposure: temperature (both hyperthermia and hypothermia occur in overdose), needle marks, medication patches, and any tablets or packaging found with the patient.

Taking a Toxicology UKMLA History

Wherever the patient can communicate, or collateral history is available from paramedics, family, or a suicide note, the key details to establish are: what was taken (specific drug, formulation, and whether modified-release), how much, when, whether anything else was co-ingested (including alcohol), and whether there has been any vomiting since ingestion. In deliberate self-poisoning, a brief risk assessment for ongoing suicidal intent is essential once the patient is medically stable, and every such patient requires psychiatric liaison assessment before discharge — this is tested directly in CPSA-style stations and is a patient-safety principle the UKMLA expects every candidate to apply without prompting.

When to Call TOXBASE and the National Poisons Information Service

For any poisoning that is uncertain, unusual, involves multiple agents, involves a paediatric patient, or simply falls outside a clinician's day-to-day experience, the correct action is to consult TOXBASE, the NPIS's online clinical toxicology database, which is free to access for NHS clinicians and provides agent-specific management advice updated by UK-based consultant clinical toxicologists. For cases needing direct discussion with a specialist — an unusual re-presentation, a deteriorating patient, or a genuinely novel agent — the NPIS operates a telephone advice line for healthcare professionals, reachable via npis.org. UKMLA questions that describe an atypical or complex poisoning very often expect "consult TOXBASE / contact the National Poisons Information Service" as either the correct answer itself or the safety-netting step that should accompany any other correct clinical action — always state the limits of memorised knowledge honestly and defer to this real, checkable UK resource rather than guessing at an unfamiliar antidote regimen.

Paracetamol Overdose

Paracetamol overdose is the single most commonly tested toxicology UKMLA topic because it is the most common presentation to UK emergency departments among all deliberate and accidental poisonings, and because its management follows a precise, numerically-defined algorithm that lends itself directly to single-best-answer questions. Paracetamol in overdose depletes hepatic glutathione stores, allowing a toxic metabolite (NAPQI) to accumulate and cause centrilobular hepatocyte necrosis; if untreated, this progresses to acute liver failure over several days.

Acute Staggered vs Acute Overdose

The distinction between an acute overdose (all tablets taken within one hour, or treated as such if timing is unclear) and a staggered overdose (tablets taken over a period of more than one hour, sometimes over many hours or days) is one of the most frequently tested discriminators in this topic. An acute overdose can be plotted against the paracetamol treatment nomogram using a single timed serum paracetamol level; a staggered overdose cannot be reliably plotted on the nomogram at all, because the assumptions behind the nomogram (a single ingestion with known timing) do not hold. For staggered overdoses, or where the time of ingestion is unknown or unreliable, current UK guidance is to treat with N-acetylcysteine (NAC) regardless of the paracetamol level or apparent dose, because the risk of missing a clinically significant overdose outweighs the relatively low risk of unnecessary treatment.

The Treatment Nomogram Concept

For a genuine single acute ingestion, a serum paracetamol concentration is taken no earlier than four hours after ingestion (levels taken before four hours may still be rising and are unreliable for nomogram interpretation) and plotted on the paracetamol treatment graph against time since ingestion. If the level falls on or above the treatment line, NAC is started immediately. If it falls below the line and the patient is asymptomatic with normal liver function, treatment can usually be withheld, though many UK protocols now treat a wider population empirically given the low harm profile of NAC and the catastrophic consequences of an undertreated genuine overdose. Patients presenting more than eight hours after a significant ingestion, or with any evidence of hepatotoxicity, should generally be started on NAC empirically while further results are awaited, rather than delaying treatment until a level is available — the exact opposite of what a candidate's instinct might suggest, and a recurring exam trap.

N-Acetylcysteine Treatment

N-acetylcysteine works by replenishing hepatic glutathione, which conjugates and detoxifies the NAPQI metabolite before it can damage hepatocytes; it is most effective when started within eight hours of ingestion, though it still provides benefit later and should never be withheld solely because of a delayed presentation. NAC is given as a weight-based intravenous infusion; contemporary UK regimens increasingly use a two-bag protocol given over a total of around twenty hours rather than the older three-bag regimen, reflecting evidence that a slower initial infusion rate reduces the incidence of anaphylactoid reactions. Anaphylactoid reactions to NAC (flushing, urticaria, wheeze, hypotension) are relatively common, particularly with rapid infusion, and are managed by slowing or briefly pausing the infusion and treating symptomatically with antihistamines, rather than by permanently stopping treatment, since the reaction is not a true IgE-mediated allergy in most cases.

Monitoring and Liver Injury

Baseline and repeat blood tests in paracetamol overdose include liver function tests, INR/clotting, urea and electrolytes, venous or arterial blood gas, and glucose. Markers of a poor prognosis include a rising INR, worsening acidosis, rising creatinine, and encephalopathy — the combination captured by the King's College Hospital criteria for liver transplant referral, which candidates should recognise by name even if the precise numerical thresholds are not required for most UKMLA questions. Any patient with a significant paracetamol overdose and evolving hepatotoxicity should be discussed early with a liver unit, since transplantation, when indicated, has a narrow window in which referral gives the best outcome.

Opioid Overdose

Opioid overdose is a core toxicology UKMLA topic, characterised by the classic triad of respiratory depression, pinpoint (miotic) pupils, and reduced consciousness, and it is tested heavily in the UKMLA both as a recreational/illicit-drug scenario and as an iatrogenic complication of prescribed opioid analgesia on a hospital ward — a patient-safety angle the exam favours because it is entirely preventable with careful prescribing and monitoring.

Recognition

Beyond the pupillary and respiratory findings, opioid toxicity commonly presents with bradypnoea (a respiratory rate below eight to ten breaths per minute is a major red flag), hypotension, hypothermia, and reduced bowel sounds. In the ward setting, opioid toxicity should be considered in any patient who becomes difficult to rouse and has a falling respiratory rate after receiving opioid analgesia, particularly in patients with renal impairment (where opioid metabolites accumulate) or in opioid-naive patients given a dose intended for someone with existing tolerance.

Naloxone

Naloxone is a competitive opioid receptor antagonist and is the definitive treatment for opioid-induced respiratory depression, reversing the effect within one to two minutes of intravenous administration. In the hospital setting, a typical approach is small, titrated intravenous doses (for example 100 to 400 micrograms, repeated every two to three minutes) aiming to restore adequate respiratory rate and consciousness without precipitating acute, severe withdrawal in an opioid-dependent patient — over-aggressive naloxone dosing is a recognised exam trap, since abruptly reversing all opioid effect in a dependent patient can cause agitation, vomiting, and even acute pulmonary oedema. Naloxone can also be given intramuscularly or intranasally, which is particularly relevant in the community setting (take-home naloxone kits distributed to people who use opioids and their contacts are a genuine and expanding UK public-health intervention). Because naloxone's duration of action (roughly thirty to ninety minutes) is often shorter than that of the opioid causing toxicity, especially long-acting or modified-release opioids and methadone, patients require a period of monitoring after naloxone reversal and may need repeat doses or an infusion, and should not be discharged immediately after a single dose simply because they appear to have woken up.

Ward-Based Prevention

On the wards, opioid-induced respiratory depression is largely a preventable patient-safety failure, and the UKMLA tests this angle directly under its patient-safety and prescribing-errors curriculum: regular respiratory rate and sedation-score monitoring after opioid administration, appropriate dose reduction in renal or hepatic impairment, and caution with concurrent sedatives (benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids) are all expected knowledge, and this topic connects directly to the broader theme covered in the site's review of prescribing errors and patient safety.

Benzodiazepine Overdose

Benzodiazepine overdose is another regularly examined toxicology UKMLA scenario, though in isolation it is usually far less dangerous than the topics above, because benzodiazepines have a relatively wide therapeutic margin and pure benzodiazepine overdose rarely causes severe respiratory depression in an otherwise healthy adult — the exam frequently tests this reassuring fact in contrast to the much greater danger when benzodiazepines are combined with alcohol, opioids, or other sedatives, which is a common and genuinely high-risk real-world combination.

Clinical Features

Features include drowsiness, slurred speech, ataxia, nystagmus, and in more severe cases, hypotonia and respiratory depression, particularly in the elderly, in patients with existing respiratory disease, or where co-ingestion with other central nervous system depressants has occurred. Most patients require nothing more than supportive care and close observation of conscious level and respiratory rate until the drug is metabolised and cleared.

Flumazenil — Used With Caution

Flumazenil is a competitive benzodiazepine receptor antagonist that can reverse sedation and respiratory depression, but it is used far more cautiously than naloxone is in opioid overdose, and knowing why is a frequently tested discriminator. In a patient who is benzodiazepine-dependent, or who has co-ingested a pro-convulsant drug (most importantly tricyclic antidepressants), flumazenil can precipitate acute, severe, treatment-resistant seizures by abruptly removing the benzodiazepine's GABA-mediated anticonvulsant protection. For this reason, flumazenil is generally reserved for specific circumstances such as reversing iatrogenic over-sedation during a procedure in a patient with no history of chronic benzodiazepine use or seizure risk, and is not given as a routine or first-line treatment in self-poisoning of unclear or mixed cause. The correct exam answer for an undifferentiated benzodiazepine overdose is very often "supportive care and monitoring" rather than flumazenil, precisely because of this risk profile.

Tricyclic Antidepressant Overdose

Tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) overdose is one of the highest-stakes topics in toxicology UKMLA revision and one of the most dangerous poisonings covered in this guide, causing a combination of anticholinergic effects, sodium-channel blockade, and cardiotoxicity that can progress rapidly to life-threatening arrhythmia, seizures, and cardiovascular collapse — this is precisely why the UKMLA tests it so heavily, since recognising the ECG changes early and starting treatment before full cardiovascular collapse is the difference between a good and a catastrophic outcome.

ECG Changes

The hallmark ECG finding in TCA toxicity is QRS widening (a QRS duration above 100 milliseconds indicates significant toxicity and a substantially increased risk of seizures and arrhythmia; above 160 milliseconds indicates a high risk of ventricular arrhythmia), reflecting sodium-channel blockade in cardiac myocytes. Other recognised ECG changes include sinus tachycardia (very common and often the earliest sign), a prolonged QT interval, right axis deviation of the terminal 40 milliseconds of the QRS complex, and a specific finding — a terminal R wave in aVR greater than 3 millimetres — that is a well-recognised marker of significant TCA toxicity and is increasingly tested directly in UKMLA-style ECG questions. Serial ECG monitoring is essential in any suspected TCA overdose, since a patient with a normal or only mildly widened QRS at presentation can deteriorate over the following hours as absorption continues.

Clinical Features

Anticholinergic features include dilated pupils, dry mouth, urinary retention, hot dry skin, tachycardia, and agitation or delirium progressing to coma; the classic teaching phrase "hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, and mad as a hatter" captures this anticholinergic toxidrome, which is also relevant to other anticholinergic-drug overdoses. Seizures occur in a significant minority of serious TCA overdoses and are a poor prognostic sign, and hypotension develops as alpha-adrenergic blockade and myocardial depression progress.

Sodium Bicarbonate Treatment

Intravenous sodium bicarbonate is the definitive first-line treatment for cardiotoxic TCA overdose, and understanding why it works is a favourite exam angle: bicarbonate increases extracellular pH and sodium concentration, both of which help overcome the sodium-channel blockade responsible for QRS widening and arrhythmia — it is a targeted physiological antidote, not simply a buffer for acidosis. Indications for giving IV sodium bicarbonate include a widened QRS (generally above 100 milliseconds), ventricular arrhythmia, and significant hypotension in the context of confirmed or strongly suspected TCA overdose; it is typically given as repeated boluses aiming for a target arterial pH (commonly 7.45 to 7.55), with careful monitoring given the risk of hypernatraemia and metabolic alkalosis from repeated dosing. Benzodiazepines are used to control seizures; antiarrhythmics that further block sodium channels (particularly class Ia and Ic agents, such as flecainide) are avoided as they can worsen cardiotoxicity, and this is itself a frequently tested "which drug should be avoided" style question.

Salicylate (Aspirin) Overdose

Salicylate overdose is another staple of toxicology UKMLA revision and produces a distinctive and frequently examined acid-base picture, because aspirin directly stimulates the respiratory centre while simultaneously uncoupling oxidative phosphorylation and causing a build-up of organic acids — the combination of these two mechanisms is exactly why this topic recurs so often in UKMLA blood-gas interpretation questions.

Clinical Features and the Classic Acid-Base Picture

Early features include tinnitus, deafness, nausea, vomiting, sweating, and hyperventilation. The direct stimulation of the medullary respiratory centre causes a primary respiratory alkalosis (low CO2 from hyperventilation) which, particularly in adults, is followed by a superimposed high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis from accumulating salicylate, lactate, and ketones — producing the classically tested mixed respiratory alkalosis and metabolic acidosis picture on an arterial blood gas. In children and in very severe adult poisoning, the metabolic acidosis can dominate and mask the respiratory alkalosis entirely, which is another commonly tested nuance. As toxicity progresses, features include agitation, confusion, hyperthermia, and eventually seizures, coma, and non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema in severe cases.

Urinary Alkalinisation and Haemodialysis

Salicylate is a weak acid, and its renal elimination can be substantially increased by alkalinising the urine, which favours ionisation of the drug in the renal tubule and traps it for excretion rather than allowing reabsorption — the pharmacological principle ("ion trapping") behind why this treatment works is itself a frequently tested concept. Urinary alkalinisation is achieved with intravenous sodium bicarbonate, titrated to a target urine pH (commonly around 7.5 to 8.0), with careful potassium monitoring and replacement, since hypokalaemia impairs the kidney's ability to excrete an alkaline urine and must be corrected for alkalinisation to succeed. Haemodialysis is the treatment of choice for severe salicylate poisoning — indicated for very high serum salicylate concentrations, severe metabolic acidosis unresponsive to bicarbonate, renal failure, pulmonary oedema, or significant central nervous system features such as confusion, seizures, or coma — and should be arranged urgently via renal and critical care input rather than delayed while alkalinisation alone is trialled in a clearly severe case.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning is a dangerous, frequently missed diagnosis and a recurring toxicology UKMLA favourite precisely because its early symptoms — headache, nausea, dizziness, fatigue, and confusion — are non-specific and easily mistaken for a viral illness, which is exactly why the UKMLA tests the contextual clues (a faulty gas appliance, a house fire, multiple household members or even pets becoming unwell simultaneously, or symptoms that improve when the patient leaves the house and recur on return) as the key to making the diagnosis.

Why Pulse Oximetry Is Misleading

One of the most important and most heavily tested facts in this topic is that standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish carboxyhaemoglobin from normal oxyhaemoglobin, because both absorb light at similar wavelengths to the device's sensor — as a result, a patient with severe CO poisoning can show a falsely reassuring, normal-looking oxygen saturation reading on a standard pulse oximeter despite being critically hypoxic at the tissue level. The correct investigation is a carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) level, measured on an arterial or venous blood gas sample with co-oximetry, not a standard pulse oximeter reading — recognising this discrepancy is one of the single highest-yield "spot the trap" facts in the entire toxicology curriculum.

Treatment: High-Flow Oxygen and Hyperbaric Oxygen

The mainstay of treatment is high-flow 100% oxygen via a non-rebreathe mask, which dramatically shortens the half-life of carboxyhaemoglobin compared with breathing room air (roughly four to six hours on room air, reduced to under ninety minutes on 100% oxygen), accelerating displacement of carbon monoxide from haemoglobin and from cytochrome oxidase in tissues. Oxygen should be continued until the patient is asymptomatic and the COHb level has normalised, which in practice usually means several hours of treatment even after symptoms have started to settle. Hyperbaric oxygen therapy, which delivers 100% oxygen at increased atmospheric pressure, is reserved for more severe cases — features such as loss of consciousness at any point, significant neurological or cardiac abnormality, pregnancy with a significantly elevated COHb (because fetal haemoglobin binds carbon monoxide even more avidly than adult haemoglobin, putting the fetus at particular risk), or a very high COHb level — and should be discussed urgently with the National Poisons Information Service or a regional hyperbaric unit, since access to hyperbaric facilities in the UK is limited to specific centres.

Safety-Netting and Source Identification

Every confirmed or suspected case of accidental CO poisoning should trigger practical safety advice and, in the UK, involvement of the Health and Safety Executive or the Gas Safe Register where a faulty domestic appliance is suspected, since other household occupants remain at risk until the source is identified and fixed — this consumer-safety dimension is a recurring theme the UKMLA rewards candidates for including in a complete answer, not just the immediate medical treatment.

Alcohol-Related Presentations

Alcohol-related presentations round out the core toxicology UKMLA curriculum and are involved in a very large proportion of poisoning and overdose presentations to UK emergency departments, both as the primary intoxicant and as a common co-ingestant that worsens the risk profile of other overdoses (particularly benzodiazepines and opioids, through additive central nervous system and respiratory depression), and alcohol withdrawal is itself a distinct and dangerous medical emergency that the UKMLA tests separately from acute intoxication.

Acute Alcohol Intoxication

Acute alcohol intoxication causes dose-dependent central nervous system depression, ranging from mild disinhibition and slurred speech through to profound respiratory depression, aspiration risk, hypoglycaemia (alcohol impairs gluconeogenesis, and hypoglycaemia can be masked by or mistaken for ongoing intoxication), and hypothermia, particularly in patients found outdoors. Management is largely supportive — airway protection in the recovery position if consciousness is reduced, monitoring of blood glucose and correction of hypoglycaemia, warming, and monitoring for aspiration or head injury, which must always be considered and actively excluded in an intoxicated patient found with a reduced conscious level, since head injury can otherwise be wrongly attributed entirely to alcohol.

Alcohol Withdrawal

Alcohol withdrawal occurs in patients with established physical dependence when blood alcohol levels fall, typically beginning six to twenty-four hours after the last drink with tremor, anxiety, sweating, tachycardia, and hypertension. Withdrawal seizures, when they occur, typically happen within the first twelve to forty-eight hours. Delirium tremens is the most severe manifestation, usually developing forty-eight to seventy-two hours after the last drink, and is characterised by severe confusion, agitation, hallucinations (classically visual, sometimes tactile), marked autonomic hyperactivity, and significant mortality if untreated — this progression from early tremor through seizures to delirium tremens over a predictable timeframe is a frequently tested sequence.

Chlordiazepoxide and Thiamine

The mainstay of alcohol withdrawal treatment in the UK is a reducing regimen of a long-acting benzodiazepine, most commonly chlordiazepoxide, given on a fixed reducing schedule over several days (or symptom-triggered using a validated withdrawal scale such as CIWA-Ar in some settings), which substitutes for alcohol's effect on GABA receptors and is then tapered to prevent withdrawal while avoiding the harms of ongoing alcohol dependence. Every patient at risk of alcohol withdrawal, or with a history suggesting chronic heavy alcohol use, should also receive parenteral thiamine (vitamin B1), because chronic alcohol use causes thiamine depletion and places patients at risk of Wernicke's encephalopathy — classically presenting with the triad of confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia, though the full triad is often incomplete in practice. A critical, frequently tested prescribing-safety point is that thiamine should be given before or alongside any glucose administration in a patient at risk of Wernicke's, because giving glucose first can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy by rapidly consuming the patient's already-depleted thiamine stores. Untreated or inadequately treated Wernicke's encephalopathy can progress to Korsakoff's syndrome, an often-irreversible amnestic condition with confabulation — a devastating and largely preventable outcome that underlines why thiamine replacement is treated as a genuine medical emergency in this population, not a routine afterthought.

How Toxicology UKMLA Content Is Tested Across the AKT and CPSA

For UK medical students, toxicology questions in the Applied Knowledge Test typically present as a clinical vignette with a history of ingestion, a set of vital signs or an ECG, and ask for the immediate next management step or the correct antidote — success depends on rapid, confident pattern recognition built through repeated practice with SBA-style questions rather than trying to memorise every possible poison in isolation. For IMGs, the same content is tested identically, but candidates who trained in health systems with different formularies, different first-line antidote availability, or a different structure of poisons information services should specifically make sure they know the UK-specific service (NPIS/TOXBASE) and UK-specific drug names (for example, chlordiazepoxide rather than a locally-preferred alternative for alcohol withdrawal) that the exam and, more importantly, UK clinical practice actually uses.

In the CPSA, toxicology-relevant stations are more likely to focus on history-taking from an intoxicated or recently-overdosed patient, safe prescribing communication (for example, explaining a NAC infusion or a chlordiazepoxide reducing regimen to a patient), or a risk-assessment conversation following deliberate self-poisoning, rather than testing pharmacological detail directly — the same ABCDE-first, safety-netting-conscious approach that underpins the AKT content applies equally to how a candidate should structure a spoken answer or a real patient interaction in this station format. This overlap between structured emergency assessment and toxicology-specific knowledge is also why it is worth revising this topic alongside the wider approach to the acutely septic or deteriorating patient, since the initial ABCDE steps, escalation thresholds, and safety-netting principles transfer directly between the two topics.

Toxicology and Perioperative Medicine

Toxicology also intersects with perioperative care in ways the UKMLA occasionally tests — for example, a patient presenting for emergency surgery after a deliberate overdose, or a patient who develops opioid-induced respiratory depression in the recovery period after receiving intraoperative analgesia. Candidates preparing for questions that blend these two areas should also review the site's guide to anaesthesia and perioperative medicine, since sedation scoring, airway management principles, and opioid titration overlap substantially between the two topics.

Toxicology and Safe Prescribing

A meaningful proportion of toxicology-adjacent UKMLA content is really a prescribing-safety question wearing a toxicology costume — iatrogenic opioid overdose from an excessive dose or inadequate monitoring, drug interactions that precipitate serotonin syndrome or QT prolongation, and the dangers of abruptly stopping or reversing a sedative in a dependent patient are all, at heart, prescribing-safety lessons. It is worth revising this guide alongside the site's dedicated overview of pharmacology and prescribing safety for the AKT, since many toxicology questions are really testing whether a candidate understands why a drug becomes dangerous in overdose or in combination, not just what antidote reverses it.

Frequently Asked Questions About Toxicology in the UKMLA

What is the most commonly tested overdose in UKMLA toxicology questions?

Paracetamol overdose is the most frequently tested toxicology UKMLA topic, reflecting its status as the most common poisoning seen in UK emergency departments. Candidates should know the difference between acute and staggered overdoses, when a serum paracetamol level can and cannot be plotted on the treatment nomogram, and the mechanism and administration of N-acetylcysteine. Questions frequently test the trap of trying to use the nomogram for a staggered or uncertain-timing overdose, where empirical treatment is the safer and correct approach.

Why is a normal pulse oximetry reading misleading in carbon monoxide poisoning?

Standard pulse oximeters cannot distinguish carboxyhaemoglobin from normal oxyhaemoglobin because the two absorb light at similar wavelengths, so a patient with significant carbon monoxide poisoning can show a falsely normal oxygen saturation despite being severely hypoxic at the tissue level. The correct investigation is a carboxyhaemoglobin level taken on a blood gas sample with co-oximetry, not reliance on the pulse oximeter reading. This is one of the highest-yield safety facts in the toxicology curriculum precisely because it contradicts the intuitive assumption that a normal saturation reading rules out significant poisoning.

When should flumazenil be avoided in benzodiazepine overdose?

Flumazenil should be avoided in patients who are benzodiazepine-dependent or who may have co-ingested a pro-convulsant drug such as a tricyclic antidepressant, because abruptly reversing the benzodiazepine's protective, anticonvulsant GABA effect can precipitate severe, treatment-resistant seizures. Most cases of isolated or unclear-cause benzodiazepine overdose are managed with supportive care and monitoring rather than flumazenil. Flumazenil is reserved for narrower situations, such as reversing iatrogenic over-sedation in a patient with no dependence or seizure risk factors.

What ECG change indicates severe tricyclic antidepressant toxicity?

QRS widening is the hallmark ECG finding in tricyclic antidepressant toxicity, with a QRS duration above 100 milliseconds indicating significant toxicity and a QRS above 160 milliseconds indicating a high risk of ventricular arrhythmia. Other recognised changes include sinus tachycardia, a prolonged QT interval, and a terminal R wave in lead aVR. Serial ECGs are essential because a patient can deteriorate over several hours as the drug continues to be absorbed, even if the initial ECG looks reassuring.

Why must thiamine be given before glucose in patients at risk of Wernicke's encephalopathy?

Chronic heavy alcohol use depletes the body's thiamine stores, and administering glucose to a thiamine-deficient patient rapidly consumes the small remaining thiamine reserve as it is used in carbohydrate metabolism, which can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy. For this reason, parenteral thiamine should be given before or at the same time as any glucose in a patient with suspected chronic alcohol use or malnutrition, not afterwards. Untreated Wernicke's encephalopathy can progress to the often-irreversible amnestic Korsakoff's syndrome, making this a genuine medical emergency rather than a routine vitamin supplement.

What should a doctor do if they are unsure how to manage an unusual or unfamiliar poisoning?

The correct and expected action is to consult TOXBASE, the National Poisons Information Service's online clinical database, which provides agent-specific management guidance for healthcare professionals, or to telephone the NPIS directly for cases needing specialist discussion. No foundation doctor is expected to manage an unfamiliar poisoning from memory alone, and UKMLA questions describing an atypical or complex overdose frequently expect this safety-netting step as part of a complete answer. Relying on TOXBASE rather than guesswork is itself the professionally and clinically correct behaviour being tested.

Is aspirin overdose a respiratory alkalosis or a metabolic acidosis?

Salicylate overdose classically produces both: an early primary respiratory alkalosis from direct stimulation of the respiratory centre causing hyperventilation, followed by a superimposed high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis as salicylate, lactate, and ketones accumulate. In severe poisoning, particularly in children, the metabolic acidosis can dominate the picture. Recognising this mixed acid-base disturbance on an arterial or venous blood gas is one of the most frequently tested aspects of salicylate toxicity.

How quickly does naloxone reverse opioid overdose, and why might repeated doses be needed?

Intravenous naloxone typically reverses opioid-induced respiratory depression within one to two minutes, but its clinical effect usually lasts only thirty to ninety minutes, which can be considerably shorter than the duration of action of the opioid responsible, particularly long-acting or modified-release formulations and methadone. As a result, patients can relapse into respiratory depression after an initial response and require repeat doses or a continuous infusion, and should be monitored for a period after apparent recovery rather than discharged immediately. Titrated, small incremental doses are also preferred over a single large dose to avoid precipitating acute, severe withdrawal in an opioid-dependent patient.

Conclusion

Toxicology UKMLA questions reward candidates who can pair rapid recognition of a poisoning's clinical picture with confident recall of its specific antidote or treatment algorithm, all built on the same ABCDE foundation that underpins every acute presentation in the exam. Whether the scenario involves a paracetamol nomogram, a widened QRS from tricyclic overdose, a misleadingly normal oxygen saturation in carbon monoxide poisoning, or the timing of thiamine relative to glucose in alcohol withdrawal, the safest and most exam-consistent habit is to treat TOXBASE and the National Poisons Information Service as the real, authoritative backstop they are in UK clinical practice, not just a fact to memorise for a single-best-answer question. Building this topic into a wider, structured revision plan across the full Clinical Systems syllabus will help both UK medical students and IMGs turn isolated antidote facts into durable, exam-ready clinical judgement.
